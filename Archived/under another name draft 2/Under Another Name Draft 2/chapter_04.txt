Thomas was scheduled to inspect a fabrication yard in Sumgait. Rauf assigned Olesya as the interpreter. 

They drove the company Land Cruiser. The landscape was a grey, industrial wasteland. 

At the yard, the foreman met them near a stack of steel beams. He spoke aggressively in Russian. Olesya translated, faltering. "He says the welds are complete, but the inspection team has not arrived."

"Tell him the welds fail visual inspection," Thomas said. "We need ultrasound testing."

The foreman laughed, pointing a thick finger at Olesya's chest. "He says... a woman from the office cannot tell him how to build a rig."

Thomas stepped between them. "Tell him the signature on the payment release is mine. If the ultrasound is not done, the payment is withheld."

The foreman glared, spat on the ground, and walked away.

"Get back in the car," Thomas told Olesya.

They drove in silence. "He knew I am Russian," she said quietly. "It matters here."

Olesya’s phone rang. She answered, her voice pleading. She spoke rapid Russian, repeating the word "dengi"—money.

"Turn left here," she said suddenly. "Please. I need to stop."

Thomas turned into a dense, older residential district and parked outside a utility payment centre. Olesya went inside. After ten minutes, Thomas followed. 

She was arguing with the clerk, holding papers. 

"What is the problem?" Thomas asked.

"The electricity," she whispered. "They are shutting it off. The bill is late."

Thomas looked at the clerk. "How much?" The clerk showed a figure on a calculator. Thomas pulled out the cash, paid, and handed the receipt to Olesya.

They walked back to the car. "I will pay you back," she said.

"You cannot work efficiently if you are worried about your electricity. It is a practical solution," Thomas said.

She understood. He was extending the system from the office into her life. He was providing the provision she craved. 

In her mind, this was the beginning of a foundation. She was accepting these fragments of care, assembling them, waiting for the moment they would solidify into the complete, singular life she desired—a home, a husband, stability. 

Thomas drove back to the office, unaware of the structural inversion he was initiating. He believed he was simply maintaining a parallel node. He did not realize she expected the parallel lines to eventually converge.
