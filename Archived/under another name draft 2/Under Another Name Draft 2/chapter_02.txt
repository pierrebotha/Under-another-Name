During his office rotations, Olesya’s presence became the fixed point in his day. Her incompetence with corporate systems was absolute, yet she arranged his calendar, his coffee, and the physical files with the meticulous devotion of a caretaker maintaining an estate.

Thomas absorbed her failures. He built buffer times into his schedule to accommodate the corrections.

A shipment of specialized drill bits was delayed. Rauf was demanding answers. Olesya had been tasked with the customs clearance. Thomas found her staring at a stack of forms, completely still.

He walked over and picked up the top form. "What is the status?"

"They require a secondary stamp," Olesya whispered. "From the Ministry of Taxation. But the man there says the invoice is incorrect."

"Let me see." Thomas reviewed the invoice. "It's a clerical mistake on the vendor's end."

"Rauf is angry," she said.

"I will handle it."

He drafted a letter of correction and called the vendor. The clearance was approved. He handed the document to Olesya.

"Give this to Rauf," he said.

She looked at the document, then at Thomas. "You fix everything," she said.

The next day, Olesya did not arrive at eight. She arrived at eleven. Her hair was damp from rain.

"I was at the clinic," she said, holding a pharmacy receipt. "I did not have enough for the taxi back. I walked from the metro."

Thomas looked at the receipt. He did not ask what was wrong. He reached into his desk drawer and pulled out two fifty-manat notes.

Olesya looked at the money.

"For the taxi next time," Thomas said. 

She took the notes. "Thank you."

She returned to her desk. He had identified a point of friction and applied a resource to eliminate it. 

Thomas's wife, Sarah, was currently in London, negotiating a consulting contract. Sarah loved the movement, the independence. She thrived in airport lounges and corporate suites. She abhorred the idea of a fixed, domestic routine. Thomas loved Sarah deeply for her competence and her independence. 

But as he watched Olesya meticulously arrange the pens on her desk, aligning them perfectly parallel to the edge, he felt an equal, parallel pull. He believed, with an engineer's strange logic, that love was not a zero-sum equation. He could love Sarah's velocity, and he could equally love the quiet, desperate domesticity that Olesya projected into the sterile office. 

Two weeks later, he noticed she was wearing a warmer coat. He knew the taxi money had gone toward it. 

He began leaving small amounts of cash in the top drawer of her desk. He never mentioned it. She never mentioned it. The money would simply disappear. 

They communicated through these silent adjustments. He fixed her spreadsheets; she brought him coffee and maintained the pristine order of the office. He left cash in the drawer; she arrived exactly on time, wearing the warm coat. It was a system. It worked perfectly.
