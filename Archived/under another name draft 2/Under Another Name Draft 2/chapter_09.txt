The rhythm of the distance solidified over the next eighteen months. Thomas deployed to Iraq. The environment was hostile—searing heat and dust storms. 

The system with Olesya adapted. It became less about emergencies and more about structural support. 

*The new job pays cash weekly,* she messaged him. *But they do not provide health insurance. Anya's asthma is worse.*

*I will send the funds for the private clinic,* he replied.

He had become her primary infrastructure. He managed her life from three thousand miles away. 

One afternoon, a dust storm grounded all flights. His personal phone rang. 

"She is in the hospital," Olesya’s voice was frantic. "Anya. The asthma attack... The state hospital is terrible, Thomas. There are no beds."

The panic in her voice was absolute. 

"Listen to me," Thomas said. "Go back to the private clinic. Tell them a wire transfer is coming from London."

"They will not believe me."

"Tell them," Thomas repeated. "I will handle it."

He initiated an expedited wire transfer directly to the clinic's account. Two hours later, Olesya messaged him.

*They admitted her. She is in a room.*

The next morning, she sent a photograph. It showed Anya lying in a clean hospital bed. 

Thomas stared at the photograph. It was a tangible piece of the reality he was financing. He was sustaining their existence. The realization gave him a profound sense of control. He poured resources in, and stability was produced. He was providing the protection a husband would provide, without the physical presence. 

Meanwhile, Sarah was finalizing her travel to join him. She emailed him her flight itinerary and a list of consulting clients she would manage remotely from the compound. 

He reviewed Sarah's itinerary, admiring her logistical precision, and then checked the exchange rate to ensure Olesya's hospital funds would cover the final bill. He was functioning perfectly across both domains.
