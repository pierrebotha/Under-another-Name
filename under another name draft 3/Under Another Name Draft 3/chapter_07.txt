London was damp and grey, a different texture of cold than Baku. Thomas resumed his life with Sarah in their narrow, expensive terrace house in Fulham. 

Sarah was organized, capable, and embedded in her own career. They operated as a highly functional unit. They shared a calendar, managed their investments jointly, and communicated efficiently. There was no friction, no desperate phone calls. Sarah desired movement, independence, and a life not confined to domestic expectation. She possessed the title of wife, but operated entirely outside the traditional confines of the role.

Thomas loved her for this. He loved the clean, frictionless architecture of their marriage. 

Yet, he found himself checking his phone constantly. The void left by the Baku system was palpable. 

Three weeks after his return, his phone vibrated. 

*Davies is very angry. He shouts.* 

It was Olesya. The new engineer was dismantling her carefully maintained order. 

*Ignore him,* Thomas typed back. *Focus on the data entry.*

*He fired me today,* the reply came. 

Thomas called her. "Tell me what happened."

"I have no job," she said softly. "Anya needed a dentist."

The structure had collapsed entirely. 

"I have a long weekend next month," Thomas said suddenly. "Can you travel? To Istanbul?"

"I... I would need to check my passport," she said. 

Two days later, she messaged him.

*I cannot go. It is the exit restriction.*

"What restriction?" Thomas asked when he called her.

"I have a bank debt. From before Anya's father left. The court placed a block on my passport."

"How much is the debt?"

"Ten thousand manat."

It was a structural blockage. Thomas did not offer to pay it. Clearing a massive, ancient debt was a systemic entanglement he was not prepared for. 

"I am trapped here," she whispered. 

"We will find a solution," Thomas said. 

She hung up. 

Thomas sat in his study. Sarah was downstairs, packing for a conference in Geneva. Her life was defined by unrestricted movement. Olesya's life was defined by absolute restriction. 

Thomas believed he loved both women equally. He saw no contradiction in this. He loved Sarah's flight, and he loved Olesya's rooted, desperate need for a home. But the distance was absolute. The Istanbul plan had failed. The tether had snapped under the weight of the restriction.
