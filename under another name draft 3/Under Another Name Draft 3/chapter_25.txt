Thomas remained in Istanbul for three days. He did not leave the hotel room.

He watched the rain streak the windows. The bridge had collapsed; the nodes were permanently disconnected.

On the third day, he packed his bags. He left the stack of cash in the hotel safe. 

Before he left for the airport, he opened the secure messaging app. He initiated a test ping. 

The app returned an error message. 

*User not found.*

She had deleted the account. She had scrubbed him from her reality entirely.

The block was absolute. He could not route money. He could not message her. 

He flew back to Stavanger. The Norwegian air was violently cold. 

Sarah was in the living room, reading a brief. "How was Istanbul?" 

"Productive," Thomas said. "We resolved the supply chain bottlenecks."

"Good," she smiled. 

Thomas walked upstairs to his home office. He checked the joint accounts. The numbers were robust. 

He then opened the portal for the secondary account. It was nearly empty. He clicked the button to permanently close it. The system was closed. 

Months passed. The Norwegian winter gave way to a brief, bright spring. 

Thomas sat in his office late one evening. He opened a browser window in incognito mode. He navigated to TikTok. He did not have an account in his own name. He logged in using a burner email, a string of randomized characters he had created weeks ago. 

He typed Anya's name into the search bar, adding the name of the northern village in Cyrillic. 

A profile appeared. It was public. 

He clicked on the most recent video. 

It was a short, shaky recording. Anya was standing in a small, bare kitchen, laughing at something off-camera. She turned the camera. 

Olesya was at the stove, stirring a pot. She looked older, the lines around her mouth deeply etched by the cold and the debt. But she was smiling—a brief, genuine smile directed at her daughter. The kitchen was poor, the walls unpainted, but the surfaces were immaculate. The small space was ordered, maintained, kept. 

She had found her home. It was broken, impoverished, and entirely disconnected from him, but it was whole. It was singular. 

Thomas watched the six-second loop play again. And again. 

He did not feel grief. He felt the cold truth of the governing paradox. He had believed love could be equal, distributed across systems and distance. But reality demanded it be chosen, lived, and structurally singular. No one was wrong. But their truths could not exist in the same system.

He did not click the 'like' button. He did not comment. 

He sat in the dark of his Norwegian office, watching the woman he loved stir a pot in a kitchen he could never enter. 

Just access.

Under another name.
