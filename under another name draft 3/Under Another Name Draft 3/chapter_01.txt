The wind off the Caspian Sea carried the distinct scent of salt and sulphur, settling over the city of Baku in a thin, invisible film. Thomas stood at the window of his rented apartment in the Port Baku complex, watching the traffic unspool along the Neftchilar Avenue. Down below, the grey water chopped against the concrete promenade. 

He was here for the offshore installations, the deep-water rigs that sat like steel islands on the horizon. His job was to ensure the systems operated without failure. Baku was simply another node in a long career of nodes.

The local operations manager, Rauf, suggested hiring an administrative assistant. "Someone to handle the permits, the translation, the local procurement," Rauf had said. "It smooths things over."

Thomas had agreed. 

The interviews took place in a glass-walled meeting room. The fourth candidate was Olesya.

She sat with her hands folded in her lap, her posture rigid. She wore a dark blue dress, slightly frayed at the cuffs, and sensible, scuffed black heels. Her hair was pulled back tightly. She did not smile to fill the silences.

"You have experience with logistics?" Thomas asked.

"Yes," she said. Her voice was quiet. "I organise things."

Her surname ended in '-ova', marking her out as an ethnic Russian in a city reclaiming its Turkic roots. She sat as if expecting to be dismissed.

"The work requires precision," Thomas said. "Tracking equipment manifests, filing government permits."

"I understand," Olesya said. She looked at him, her pale eyes steady. 

Thomas hired her. 

Olesya started the next morning. By the end of the week, Thomas noticed the first discrepancies in the transport logs. 

He walked out of his office. Olesya was staring at her screen. 

"The casing logs," he said gently. "The tonnage is mismatched."

She stood up, smoothing her skirt. "I copied the numbers from the driver's slips."

"The slips are in kilograms. The spreadsheet requires metric tonnes," Thomas explained. 

"I see," she said. "I will fix it."

She returned to her desk. When she sent the file back, it was still wrong. Thomas did not call her back in. He highlighted the column, applied the correct formula, and saved the file. 

The pattern established itself quietly. Olesya would attempt a task; Thomas would quietly repair the fractures before submitting the final documents. 

He noticed, however, what she did excel at. The physical space around her desk was immaculate. She wiped down the surfaces every morning. She arranged the stationery with exact, deliberate care. She brought a small, potted fern and placed it near the window, watering it precisely at noon. She was not a natural administrator of data; she was an administrator of spaces. She possessed a deep, unarticulated drive to manage a home, diverted awkwardly into a corporate office.

Every morning, she arrived exactly at eight. She would place a cup of black coffee on his desk. 

"Good morning," she would say.

"Morning, Olesya," he would reply.

She was a quiet presence. When the office became loud with the arguments of the local engineers, she shrank slightly into her chair. 

One afternoon, the air conditioning failed. The heat pressed against the glass. 

"You are from England," Olesya said suddenly, wiping her forehead. 

"Yes."

"You travel for the oil."

"I travel for the work."

She nodded, looking at the distant rigs. "It is a transient life."

She turned back to her desk. Her screen displayed a spreadsheet riddled with errors. Thomas watched the tense set of her shoulders. 

"Leave the permit forms on my desk," Thomas said. "I will finalise them tonight."

She looked at him. She placed the file on his desk and nodded.

When she left, Thomas opened her files. He corrected the margins. He updated the formulas. He saved the documents. The system held. He locked the office, appreciating the quiet order she had brought to the physical room, even as he repaired the digital one.
