Chapter 12
The dream was not a dream. It was a memory that had been edited, corrupted by a hand he could not see.
Thomas stood in the souk in Marrakesh. The air smelled of cumin, dust, and hot metal, a thick, spicy haze that coated the back of his throat. The light was golden, filtering through the reed mats overhead, casting striped shadows on the cobblestones that shifted like living things. He was holding Olesya’s hand. Her palm was sweaty, slippery against his own. She was laughing at something he had said, her head thrown back, eyes crinkled at the corners, the sound bright and sharp as broken glass. It was a perfect moment. Frozen in amber. A snapshot of a life that felt real, solid, attainable.
Then, the image glitched.
A digital tear ran vertically down the centre of the vision, jagged and violent, like a corrupted video file buffering on a slow connection. Olesya’s face froze mid-laugh, her mouth open in a silent scream, pixelating into blocks of sickly green and static grey. The sound of her laughter stretched, slowing down, warping until it became a low, demonic groan that vibrated in his teeth. The cobblestones beneath his feet turned to liquid, black and oily, swallowing his shoes, pulling him down into the depths.
‘Check your email, Tom,’ a voice said. Not hers. His own. Flat. Dead. Mechanical.
He woke with a jolt, his heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped bird beating its wings against a cage. The room was pitch black. The silence was absolute, heavy, pressing against his eardrums with physical weight. He lay still, waiting for his eyes to adjust, for the darkness to resolve into shapes he recognized. The wardrobe, a tall shadow in the corner. The door, a rectangle of slightly less dense dark. The window, a blind eye.
But the darkness felt different tonight. It felt occupied. It felt like someone was standing just out of sight, breathing in time with him, waiting for him to move.
He reached for the phone on the bedside table. His fingers brushed the cold glass. It was silent. No buzzing. No glowing screen. Just a dead slab of technology, a brick of plastic and silicon. He had left it there, face down, hours ago. Or maybe days. Time had become a fluid thing, leaking out of the clock and pooling on the floor with the rest of the mess, mixing with the stains until minutes and hours were indistinguishable.
He sat up. The mattress creaked, a loud, complaining sound in the stillness, a betrayal of his presence. His back was stiff, his neck cramped, muscles knotted from sleeping in a position that offered no rest. He had slept in his clothes again. Jeans, jumper, socks. They smelled of stale sweat and rain, of the city and the park and the bench. He didn’t care. Showering felt like a performance for an audience that wasn’t there. Or maybe an audience that was everywhere. An audience of one, watching through lenses he couldn’t find.
Phase Two, Olesya had said. Surveillance from the inside.
He swung his legs over the side of the bed. His feet found the floorboards. Cold. Bare. The chill seeped into his soles, a sharp, grounding sensation. He remembered the stains. Nine of them. A constellation of failure mapped out on the laminate of the living room. He navigated the short distance to the door by memory, stepping carefully to avoid the imaginary lines connecting them, dancing around the minefield of his own undoing. One step. Two. Hop. Turn. One step.
He opened the bedroom door. The hallway was darker than the bedroom, a tunnel of shadow leading to the living room. He walked down it, his hands brushing the walls for balance. The plaster was rough, peeling in places, flaking under his fingertips like dead skin. He could feel the texture, the grit, the imperfection. Real. Solid. Unlike the rest of his life, which had dissolved into a haze of paranoia and digital ghosts, where nothing could be trusted and every surface was a potential screen.
He entered the living room. The grey dawn light was just beginning to leak through the curtains, stripping the room of colour, leaving only tones of charcoal and ash. The world outside was waking up, but in here, time stood still. The stains were visible now. Dark patches on the floor. Nine of them. He had counted them a thousand times. He knew their shapes, their sizes, their origins. The wine. The rain. The spilled water. The coffee. The mud. Each one a chapter in the story of his collapse.
He counted them again, aloud this time, his voice rough with disuse. ‘One. Two. Three. Four. Five. Six. Seven. Eight. Nine.’
Wait.
He frowned. Squinted through the gloom, leaning forward, eyes straining.
There was a tenth.
It was small. Faint. Near the leg of the sofa. A drop of something dark. Wine? Water? Blood? Tea? He couldn’t tell. It looked old. Dried. Crusted into the grain of the wood.
He hadn’t made that one. Had he?
He walked over to it, movements slow, deliberate, as if approaching a wild animal. Kneelt down. Inspected it. The smell hit him first. Metallic. Sharp. Like old copper. Or dried blood. Or maybe just rust from a pipe. He reached out, hovered his finger over it. Didn’t touch. If he touched it, it would become real. Confirmed. Irreversible.
It was dry. Flaky. It looked like it had been there for days. Weeks. Maybe longer. But he would have remembered spilling something here. He would have remembered the sound of the drop, the splash, the cleanup. Wouldn’t he? He remembered everything. Or he thought he did. Memory was supposed to be a record. A tape. Playable. Rewindable.
Unless he hadn’t cleaned it. Unless he had just… forgotten. Unless the act of spilling it, the moment of creation, had been erased from his mind, leaving only the evidence behind.
Memory was a sieve. That’s what she had said. You can’t trust your own head, Tom. Not anymore.
Panic rose in his chest, a cold tide. If he couldn’t trust his memory, what could he trust? The floor beneath him? The air in his lungs? The name he answered to? If she could edit his memories, insert stains he didn’t make, moments he didn’t live… then where did he end and she begin? Was there a boundary left? Or had she dissolved it, molecule by molecule, until he was just an extension of her will?
He stood up. His knees cracked, a dry, brittle sound. He walked to the window. Pulled the curtain back a fraction. Just enough to see.
The street below was empty. The white van was gone. Or maybe it had never been there. Maybe the two men drinking coffee were just figments of his fraying mind, projections of his own fear onto the blank canvas of the city. Maybe the van was real, but had moved. Maybe they were watching him right now, from a rooftop, from a drainpipe, from a satellite orbiting the earth.
He let the curtain fall. Turned away from the window. The room plunged back into semi-darkness.
The flat was silent. But the silence was different today. It wasn’t empty. It was watching. It was holding its breath.
He walked to the kitchen. The kettle sat on the counter, unplugged. The fridge hummed, a solitary sound in the dead room, a mechanical heartbeat. He opened the cupboard. Tea bags. Coffee jars. Sugar. Biscuits. All in their places. Neat rows. Order imposed on chaos. But they looked wrong. Alien. Objects from a life he used to live, belonging to a man who believed in mornings, in routines, in the simple act of making coffee.
He took out the coffee jar. The one she had bought. ‘Proper coffee, Tom. None of that instant sludge.’ He remembered her voice, bright, teasing. He had drunk two cups since she left. The rest had sat there, slowly losing its aroma, oxidizing, turning into bitter dust. A relic.
He spooned granules into a mug. The sound of the spoon against the glass was loud. Clink. Clink. He added boiling water from the tap. Cold. He didn’t bother to boil it. He didn’t have the energy. He didn’t have the will. He stirred. The smell was sharp, chemical, stale. He drank it standing up. It tasted of nothing. Or rather, it tasted of everything he had become: functional, bland, enduring. A substance with no flavour, no purpose, just occupying space.
He needed to do something. But what?
The spreadsheet was locked in the drawer. A useless document. A list of passwords that didn’t matter. The laptop was buried under a pile of wires, a tomb of technology. The phone was dead. A brick. He was cut off. Analog. Invisible.
Or so he thought.
If she could put a stain on his floor without him knowing, what else could she do? Could she move objects? Change the time on the clock? Swap his clothes while he slept? Could she rewrite the narrative of his life in real time, editing the footage as he lived it?
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa. The springs groaned under his weight, a familiar complaint. He leaned forward, elbows on knees, head hanging. He stared at the floor. At the ten stains.
Just access. Under another name.
The phrase drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted. A refrain from a song he couldn’t forget, a hook lodged in his brain.
Was it a threat? Or an instruction?
If he became nobody, would she lose interest? If there was no Thomas Ward to track, no history to weaponize, no passwords to crack… would she let him go? Would she get bored and move on to the next subject, the next experiment, the next sleeping man to wake up?
Or would she just follow him into the nothingness? Would she be the only thing left, the only definition he had? If he erased Thomas Ward, would he become just… Access? A user ID? A ghost in her system?
He closed his eyes. Tried to visualize her. Where was she? In a dark room in Istanbul, surrounded by screens? A café in London, sipping tea while watching his feed? Or was she nowhere? Just a voice, a presence, a ghost in the machine, a virus in his code?
‘You’re more connected to me now than you ever were when you were texting me goodnight.’
The words echoed in his mind. Was it true? Before, their connection had been voluntary. A choice. A text sent, a text received. A conscious act of communication. Now, the connection was involuntary. Constant. Subconscious. He carried her in his head, in his fear, in the way he scanned every window, every shadow, every reflection. She had colonized his consciousness. She was the operating system, and he was just the hardware running her code, executing her commands without even knowing he was doing it.
He stood up. Paced the room. One step. Two steps. Turn. One step. Two steps. Turn.
A caged animal. A rat in a maze. Running on a wheel, going nowhere.
He stopped. Looked at the door.
He needed to leave. The flat was shrinking. The walls were pressing in, inch by inch, closing the gap between them, suffocating him. The air was too thick, too heavy to breathe.
He grabbed his coat. Put it on. Didn’t check the pockets. Nothing there anyway. No phone. No wallet. No keys? Wait. Keys. He needed keys.
He found them on the table. Cold metal. Heavy. A tangible weight in his hand. The only real thing left.
He opened the door. Stepped out into the stairwell.
The smell of boiled cabbage and damp concrete hit him. Familiar. Comforting, even. A smell that hadn’t changed, hadn’t been edited. A smell of the building, of other people, of a world that continued regardless of his internal collapse.
He walked down the stairs. One flight. Two flights. Three.
Pushed open the front door.
The air outside was crisp. Cold. Smelling of exhaust and wet pavement. The sky was a uniform sheet of grey cloud, low and heavy. It looked like rain. Again. Always rain.
He started walking.
No destination. Just movement. Motion was life. Stasis was death.
He walked towards the high street. Past the bakery. The smell of yeast and sugar was still there, warm and sweet, but it didn’t tempt him. It smelled like a memory of hunger, not hunger itself. Past the newsagent. The headlines screamed about wars and scandals, politicians and celebrities. Worlds collapsing in print. He didn’t stop to read. None of it mattered. None of it was real compared to the ten stains on his floor. Past the off-license. The neon sign buzzed, even in the daylight, a flickering blue light that promised oblivion in a bottle. He didn’t stop.
He kept walking. Legs moving automatically. Left. Right. Left. Right.
Ended up in the park.
The grass was muddy. Churned up by dogs and children, a brown slurry of earth and water. The trees were bare skeletons against the grey sky, branches reaching up like claws, scratching at the clouds.
He found a bench. Sat down.
It was the same bench as before. The wood was still damp, cold seeping through his trousers. He didn’t care. The discomfort was proof of existence.
He watched the dogs. Running. Chasing. Barking. Tails wagging. Tongues lolling.
Simple lives. Simple joys. Throw stick. Fetch stick. Repeat.
No hidden agendas. No digital leashes. No ghosts.
A dog ran up to him. A terrier mix. Rough fur. Bright eyes. Sniffed his hand. Wet nose. Warm breath.
Thomas reached out. Stroked the rough fur behind the ears. The dog leaned into his hand, trusting. Innocent.
‘Good boy,’ he said. His voice sounded strange. Rusty. Unused.
The owner called the dog. A woman. Young. Smiling.
The dog ran off, yapping, tail high.
Thomas watched it go. Felt the absence of the warmth.
He felt a pang of something. Loneliness? Envy? Grief?
It was hard to name. Emotions were distant things, seen through fogged glass.
He sat there for an hour. Maybe two. Time had lost its meaning. The sun didn’t move. The clouds didn’t shift. Just the grey. The damp. The silence inside his head.
People walked past. Joggers. Mothers with prams. Old men feeding pigeons. Couples holding hands.
None of them looked at him. He was invisible. A ghost on a bench. A man who had already ceased to exist, waiting for his body to catch up.
Eventually, he stood up. Legs stiff. Joints creaking. Blood flowing sluggishly.
He started walking back.
Towards the flat. Towards the silence. Towards the stains. Towards the tenth mark that he didn’t remember making.
He passed the tech store again.
The screens in the window were still bright. Still scrolling their lies.
Secure. Private. Encrypted.
Words meant to reassure. To sell safety. To promise control.
He stopped. Stared at them.
A reflection moved in the glass. Behind him.
He turned.
A woman. Walking past. Looking at her phone. Typing. Smiling.
She didn’t look at him. She was in her own world. Safe. Secure. Encrypted.
He turned back to the window.
His own reflection stared back. Pale. Gaunt. Eyes hollowed out by lack of sleep, by fear, by the endless scanning of horizons. Hair messy. Clothes wrinkled.
He looked like a stranger.
Like a man who had already disappeared. Like a ghost haunting his own life.
Delete yourself, Olesya had said.
Cease to exist.
Was that the only way out?
To become nobody?
To walk away from the name, the history, the pain, the ten stains?
To shed Thomas Ward like a snake sheds its skin, leaving the old self behind to rot?
Under another name.
The phrase echoed in his mind. Louder now. Insistent.
Just access. Under another name.
Was it a threat? Or a promise?
If he became nobody, would she lose interest? Would the experiment end?
Or would she just follow him into the nothingness? Would she be the only thing left, the only definition he had? The only one who knew he had ever existed?
He turned away from the window. Walked on.
The sky was darkening. Clouds gathering. Heavy with rain. The air pressure dropped, making his ears pop.
He walked faster.
Wanting to get inside. To hide. To curl up in the corner and wait for it to end.
He reached his building. Climbed the stairs.
Three flights. Legs burning. Breath short. Lungs screaming for air that felt too thin.
Unlocked the door. Stepped inside.
The flat was exactly as he had left it. Silent. Cold. Dark. The air stale, recycled, thick with the scent of his own anxiety.
He took off his coat. Dropped it on the floor. Didn’t hang it up.
Took off his shoes. Kicked them into the corner. Didn’t line them up.
Walked to the kitchen.
The stains were still there. Ten of them.
He stared at them.
They looked darker now. Deeper. Permanent. Etched into the soul of the flat.
Had the tenth one grown? Or was it just his perception shifting?
He couldn’t tell. He couldn’t trust his eyes.
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa.
Closed his eyes.
Waited.
For what?
For the call? For the knock? For the glitch to happen in real life? For the world to pixelate and dissolve?
He didn’t know.
He just waited.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of names. Thousands of them. Floating in the air. Written on slips of paper. Blowing in the wind.
He tried to catch them, but they dissolved in his hands, turning to dust, to water, to smoke.
He dreamt of Olesya. Standing in a room full of mirrors. Infinite reflections.
Each mirror showed a different version of him. Young. Old. Happy. Sad. Alive. Dead.
She was pointing at one. Laughing.
That one, she said. That’s the real one.
But which one? He couldn’t see. He was everywhere and nowhere.
He woke up gasping.
Pitch black.
Silence.
No phone buzzing. No calls. No glitches.
Just the sound of his own breathing. Shallow. Rapid.
He lay there. Listening.
Wondering if she was out there. Watching. Waiting. Editing.
Wondering if he was still Thomas.
Or if he had already become someone else. Someone new. Someone with no history. No stains. No name.
Someone under another name.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted. A loop. A cycle. A trap.
He pushed it away. Tried to think of nothing. Failed.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless. A mercy.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh. Cutting through the curtains, illuminating the dust motes dancing in the air.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was silent.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up. The taste bitter. Metallic.
Looked at the ten stains on the floor.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him. The click final.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day. Into the noise. Into the world that didn’t care.
Into the life that was no longer his own.
Into the identity he was about to lose.
