Chapter 12

The compound made privacy a matter of angles.

There were no private rooms, not truly. Only beds behind thin doors, bathrooms with pipes that carried sound, offices with glass panels beside the handles, corridors where every footstep arrived before the person did. The place had been built to count people and move them. Badge in. Badge out. Convoy. Meal. Briefing. Return. Sleep. Repeat. Even silence had procedure.

Thomas had once found that useful.

By April, it had become a geometry problem with no stable answer.

Sarah was still there. Her extension had become another extension, then a second workstream, then an arrangement everyone spoke about with the casual tone of something already approved. Her name sat on security lists. Her badge opened the office door now without the receptionist looking up. Her laptop lived beside his. Her toiletries occupied the better half of the bathroom shelf. Her shoes multiplied under the chair in ways that suggested a supply line he had failed to detect.

She had not invaded the room.

She had made it habitable.

That was the difficulty.

On the morning the email arrived, the wind came from the south and carried dust low across the compound. It moved like smoke along the paths, gathering at door frames and the base of walls, working its way through any gap left open. The sky had turned the colour of old metal. Men came into the dining hall wiping grit from their eyelids. The coffee tasted of the urn and the air. Even the oranges had a film on them.

Sarah sat opposite him with her laptop open and a scarf over her hair.

'I look ridiculous,' she said.

'You look prepared.'

'Prepared is ridiculous with better stationery.'

She pushed a document across the table with two fingers. 'Can you read this before nine?'

'What is it?'

'The implementation note. Two pages. It grew overnight, like mould.'

'That happens when you feed consultants after midnight.'

'You should put that in a site bulletin.'

He took the document. The paper had already collected dust at the corners. Sarah held her coffee with both hands and watched the serving line through the steam. She looked tired in the practical way, tired with work still moving through her, not the hollow kind that emptied a face.

His phone lay beside the tray.

O admin was due the next day.

He had moved the reminder from 06:00 to 05:20, before Sarah woke, then moved it back after noticing the change made the thing look like concealment. He had considered deleting the reminder completely and using memory, then rejected that as vanity. Memory failed under heat. Systems did not.

At 08:07, he walked to the operations office with Sarah's document folded under his arm.

The office was already crowded. Dust had found its way in through the old seals around the windows and settled on keyboards, monitors, cups, the tops of men's boots. The air conditioners rattled with a new dry rasp. Someone had taped a cloth over the worst vent. The cloth ballooned and collapsed with each breath of machine air.

Thomas opened his laptop.

No connection.

He tried again.

The network icon spun, failed, spun again.

'External mail is down,' Nisha called from the finance desk.

Three men groaned in different languages.

'Internal still works,' she said. 'Try the old terminal.'

The old terminal stood against the wall near the printer, a company machine with a cracked keyboard and a monitor that made everything look faintly green. It was used for permits, printing boarding passes, emergency approvals, anything no one wanted to own. The browser remembered too much. Men logged in and out badly. The machine carried the residue of every rushed password in the building.

Thomas did not like using it.

At 08:23, he used it.

Sarah's document needed comments before her nine o'clock call, and his own laptop had become a sealed box. He logged into webmail, waiting while the screen loaded line by line. Inbox. Site reports. Pump seal confirmation. Travel update. Security weather advisory. Sarah's document sat under the wrong subject line, forwarded by her to him at midnight.

He downloaded it, opened it, and began marking the file.

The terminal keyboard stuck on the letter e. Each correction required a harder strike. Dust gritted under the keys. The printer beside him warmed itself with a smell of hot plastic.

At 08:41, the inbox refreshed.

One new message.

From: Olesya Petrova

Subject: March and April

Thomas's hand stopped over the keyboard.

The message line sat in the middle of the screen, ordinary as any other mail. Black text. White background. Sender. Subject. Time. The whole arrangement exposed by format.

He had told her to use messages.

Months ago, after the first transfer, he had said email was fine for receipts when messaging failed. Then the monthly rhythm had moved elsewhere, and email had fallen out of use. Old instructions had a way of waiting in the walls.

Nisha was speaking to someone behind him. The printer clicked. A chair rolled. Outside, a gust pushed dust against the window with a dry hiss.

He closed Sarah's document.

The inbox stayed open.

He moved the cursor to the message and did not click.

Sarah came in carrying her laptop.

'Tell me you have destroyed my note elegantly.'

He hit the browser close button.

The terminal froze.

Sarah stopped beside him. 'Ah. The old machine. Brave.'

'Network is down.'

'Naturally. The one morning London remembers Iraq exists.'

The browser window remained on screen, pale and unresponsive. Inbox visible. Olesya's name visible. Subject visible.

Thomas pressed close again.

Nothing.

Sarah leaned closer to see what the machine was doing.

He shifted his body in front of the monitor.

'Give it a second.'

'It never deserves a second.'

'I have the document.'

'Good. Where?'

'On the desktop.'

'Then move, engineer.'

She reached past him for the mouse.

He caught it first.

Too fast.

Her hand paused in the air.

The office noise thinned around them. Not stopped. Never stopped. Printer. Air conditioner. Phones. Men. Dust at the window. Still, the small space between Sarah's fingers and the mouse filled with something neither of them named.

'Thomas.'

'It is frozen.'

'I can see it is frozen.'

'Let me restart it.'

'I need the file.'

'I will print it.'

'From a frozen machine?'

He pressed the power button and held it down.

The screen went black.

For a second, both of their reflections sat on the dead glass.

Sarah's face beside his shoulder. His own behind the dust, less clear.

'That seemed dramatic,' she said.

'It was doing nothing.'

'It was showing your inbox.'

'Yes.'

'And?'

'And now it is off.'

She looked at him. The scarf had slipped back from her hair. A fine line of dust had gathered along one eyebrow.

'Was there something in your inbox I was not meant to see?'

He took his hand from the power button.

'No.'

The answer came too clean.

Sarah's mouth changed, not into a smile. The office door opened behind her, and the HSE supervisor came in asking for the incident form from last week. Nisha answered. Someone laughed near the printer. The ordinary world rushed back into the space and saved him badly.

Sarah stepped away.

'Email me the comments when your machines recover.'

'Sarah.'

'Nine o'clock call.'

She left with her laptop under one arm.

The terminal began restarting.

Thomas stood in front of it until the login screen returned.

He did not log in.

At 09:12, his phone vibrated.

Olesya.

Did you see email?

He walked out through the side door before answering. The dust hit his face and went into his mouth. He crossed the service path to the narrow strip between the operations building and the generator yard, where the noise covered speech and the cameras faced the gate, not the wall.

What email?

He sent it and could not bear the shape of it after it left.

Her reply came fast.

Receipts. March and April. School letter also.

Do not email.

There was a pause.

Then:

You told me email if files big.

Not now.

The three words looked smaller than the force behind them.

Olesya did not answer.

He looked back towards the office. Through the dusty glass, he could see Sarah in the conference room, headset on, one hand moving across the air as she spoke to London. Her posture was exact. Her mouth made shapes no one in the room could hear.

His phone vibrated again.

Why not now?

He typed:

Shared machines here.

Then:

Use messages only.

Olesya answered:

It has school stamp. Message lowers quality.

He turned the phone in his hand. The generator sound pressed against his ribs.

Send later.

No email.

Another pause.

Then:

You need paper but not path.

He read the line twice.

Dust moved against the wall in little strikes.

No email, he typed.

This time there was no reply.

The day did not give him a place to put the event.

Sarah's call ran late. The network came back. The old terminal sat with its login screen open, blank and accusatory. Nisha printed boarding passes. The HSE supervisor found the form he had asked for. A maintenance planner spilled tea near the plotter and blamed the table. Dust entered the office, was wiped away, entered again.

Sarah's document returned to him at 10:06 with no note, only the file attached and the words revised version in the subject line. She had taken his silence and worked around it. That was worse than a question. Questions still expected an answer. Workarounds accepted the shape of an obstruction and built a path around it.

He opened the file.

Her changes were clean. His earlier comments had been folded into the margins, not quoted, not credited. She had removed his phrasing where it sounded like field defence and replaced it with language London could repeat without injury. The document was better. It had passed through her hands and come back less argumentative, more usable, more exact.

He should have written:

Good changes.

He should have written:

Sorry about the terminal.

He wrote neither.

He corrected one figure in a table and sent it back with:

Pressure number updated.

The reply came three minutes later.

Thanks.

No punctuation after it.

He looked at the single word until the screen dimmed.

At 10:32, the old terminal printed a boarding pass for someone from drilling. The paper came out with one edge crumpled. The man tore it free and cursed the machine with affection, the way people spoke to objects that failed in known ways. Thomas watched the screen return to its login prompt. Anyone could have seen the inbox. Anyone could have asked who Olesya was. Anyone could have read the subject and forgotten it, or remembered it later while standing beside Sarah at the tea urn.

That was the new problem.

Not exposure as an event.

Exposure as residue.

A name left on a screen. A subject line caught by a passing eye. A download in the wrong folder. A notification brightening at the wrong second. A printer tray. A cache. A list of recent files. All the places where a system handled a thing after the person had stopped touching it.

The compound did not need to know secrets.

It only needed to keep traces.

At 10:47, Nisha came to his desk with a finance query.

'Do you know what cost code this should sit under?'

She placed a form beside his keyboard. Her fingernails were painted a dark red that had chipped at the tips. The form carried three signatures and a coffee ring.

'Which job?'

'Basra pump parts. Or maybe not pump parts. The invoice says general materials, which is how vendors confess to sin.'

Thomas read the line items. Seal kit. Coupling. Freight. Expedited handling. Documentation fee.

'Pump.'

'You are sure?'

'Documentation fee is nonsense, but the rest is pump.'

'Everything has a nonsense fee now.'

'That should be a separate cost code.'

She laughed and took the form back.

'Sarah is looking for you.'

He kept his face on the invoice.

'Now?'

'After her call. She said no rush, which means rush, but with manners.'

'Where is she?'

'Conference room.'

Nisha looked at him a fraction longer. Not prying. Not quite. People in compounds learned the difference between information and trouble, and they learned it early.

'Dust is making everyone strange today,' she said.

'That is one diagnosis.'

'Best I can do without medical cover.'

She left.

Thomas saved his work, closed the browser, checked the downloads folder again, and stood. The movement had become procedural. Close. Clear. Lock. Pocket. Walk. He could feel the order forming in his muscles.

The conference room door was half open.

Sarah sat alone at the far end of the table, headset beside her laptop, notebook open to a page filled with square boxes and arrows. The room smelled of stale coffee and dry marker ink. Dust had gathered along the sill behind her. Someone had drawn a smiley face in it with a finger.

'Nisha said you wanted me.'

'I did.'

'About the note?'

'No.'

She turned the notebook around.

At the top of the page she had written:

Baku school support.

Beneath it:

How much?

How long?

Who knows?

Any risk to us?

Thomas looked at the four questions.

'This is efficient.'

'I am predictable.'

'That was not my word.'

'No. It was mine.'

She did not sound wounded. That would have been easier. She sounded like a person opening a file in the correct place.

'I do not have all the answers here.'

'Start with one.'

'How much?'

'That seems traditional.'

He gave her the monthly figure.

Sarah wrote it down without looking up.

'Plus extras?'

'Sometimes.'

'How sometimes?'

'School. Medicine. One competition.'

'Competition?'

'English.'

Her pen paused at the paper.

'She came third.'

'I remember.'

Sarah wrote another note.

'How long?'

'I do not know.'

'That is not a system.'

'No.'

'It is an open-ended commitment.'

He said nothing.

She drew a line beneath the questions. The pen left a small dot where she stopped.

'Who knows?'

'Olesya. Me.'

'The child?'

'Some of it.'

'And now nearly anyone who walked past a shared machine.'

The air conditioner ticked dryly above them.

'I shut it down.'

'You shut down the machine. You did not shut down what happened.'

Her pen moved once between her fingers, end over end.

'Any risk to us?'

'Financially, no.'

'Thomas.'

'I do not know how to answer the larger version.'

'Try without engineering it.'

He looked at the dust-smiley on the window sill. One eye had already begun to collapse where the grit slid down the glass.

'If I stop, it damages them.'

'That is not what I asked.'

'If I continue badly, it damages us.'

'Better.'

She closed the notebook.

'I am not asking you to stop today.'

The words struck the table softly and stayed there.

'What are you asking?'

'To know before I am made part of something by accident.'

'You are not part of it.'

'I was standing in front of a frozen inbox this morning.'

He had no reply to that.

Sarah put the pen down.

'I have another call in twelve minutes.'

'All right.'

'And I need you to decide whether this is help, or whether it is a structure you built so you never have to decide anything.'

She opened the laptop.

The meeting was over.

Thomas logged into his own laptop at 11:20.

The email was there.

From: Olesya Petrova

Subject: March and April

He opened it.

The body was short.

Thomas,

Attached March receipts and April school letter. Also confirmation for extra notebook and competition. Monthly money was enough. Thank you.

O.

There were four attachments.

Rent.

Medicine.

School letter.

Competition certificate.

No confession. No heat. No sentence that said too much unless the reader knew how to read it. Monthly money was enough. A plain line. A wire under carpet.

He downloaded the attachments and saved them to the O folder. Then he deleted the email.

The message vanished from the inbox.

He opened deleted items.

It sat there.

He deleted it again.

The system asked if he wanted to remove it permanently.

He clicked yes.

Then he cleared the downloads folder.

Then the browser cache.

Then he opened the O folder and renamed the attachments:

M-03-A.

M-03-B.

A-04-S.

C-04.

The names looked like maintenance codes. He took a breath through his nose and tasted dust.

At 12:04, Sarah sent him a message.

Lunch?

He looked through the glass. She was still in the conference room, laptop open, head bent over the screen.

He typed:

Five minutes.

She answered:

Make it four. Fish day.

He wrote:

That is not an incentive.

She sent:

For speed, it is.

In the dining hall, she spoke about the call. London had found new concerns. London always found new concerns in the part of the work that belonged to someone else. She had three follow-up notes and one request so foolish she had written it down exactly for future prosecution.

Thomas listened.

He did the right things with his face.

The tray between them held rice, chicken, cucumber, yoghurt, flatbread. Sarah tore bread into pieces while she spoke, then forgot to eat them. Dust had found its way into the corners of the windows and drawn pale lines along the frames.

'You are quiet,' she said.

'I am listening.'

'Those are different.'

'I know.'

'The machine this morning.'

He picked up the yoghurt spoon and put it down again.

'I should not have shut it off like that.'

'No.'

'I handled it badly.'

'No.'

He looked at her.

Sarah's hands had gone still on the bread.

'You were guarding something.'

The sentence stayed on the table.

He could have denied it. The shape of denial was ready. Work. Confidential. Personal account. Old colleague. Messy. Each part fitted the next. A small bridge could be built from one half-truth to another if a man did not look down.

'It was an email from Baku,' he said.

Sarah waited.

'The school receipts. For the girl I told you about.'

'The old colleague's daughter.'

'Yes.'

'Why would that need guarding?'

'It has names. Amounts. Documents.'

'And?'

'It is not my story to put on a company machine.'

That was true.

Not all true.

True enough to stand.

Sarah looked at him across the tray. The dining hall noise moved around them with its forks, chairs, voices, trays sliding over metal rails.

'You are paying regularly.'

He did not ask how she knew.

'Yes.'

'Monthly?'

'Yes.'

'For how long?'

'Since the hospital.'

She looked down at the torn bread.

'You said a tight patch.'

'It became more than that.'

'You did not update the briefing.'

'No.'

A corner of her mouth moved at the phrase, then settled.

'Is this person in trouble?'

'They have limited options.'

'That is not quite an answer.'

'No.'

Sarah leaned back. A man at the next table laughed too loudly and struck his cup with a spoon. The sound rang out, bright and out of place.

'Are we in trouble?' she asked.

'No.'

'That was quick.'

'Financially, no.'

'I did not ask only financially.'

He looked at the bread in her hands, torn into pieces she had not eaten.

'I have kept it separate.'

She breathed out through her nose.

'That is also not quite an answer.'

He had no better one that would not break the room.

Sarah gathered the bread pieces into a neat pile on the edge of her tray.

'Do not use joint money without telling me.'

'I have not.'

'Do not make me discover something by accident.'

'I will not.'

She watched him for another second, then stood.

'I need to go back.'

He stood too.

'Sarah.'

'Not here.'

She took her tray to the return rack.

He followed after a delay long enough to look accidental.

The afternoon narrowed.

Each hour made a smaller room.

The office became too bright. The bedroom too full. The corridor too exposed. His own laptop screen carried reflections from behind him. Sarah's phone lay on the desk while she worked, face up, every notification innocent and visible. His phone stayed in his pocket until it heated against his leg.

At 15:36, Olesya sent a message.

I cannot send school letter properly in message.

He was in the control room beside the senior operator, who was explaining a pressure fluctuation with a pencil against a printed graph. Thomas nodded, saw nothing. The phone pulsed again.

Olesya:

Email is normal.

Then:

You make me do wrong thing then say wrong.

The operator tapped the graph.

'Here,' he said. 'You see?'

'Yes.'

Thomas did not see.

At 16:05, he went to the bathroom at the end of the corridor and locked himself in the last cubicle. The floor was wet near the drain. Someone had left boot prints in grey arcs across the tiles. The extractor fan made a rough metallic buzz overhead.

He opened the app store.

The connection struggled, then caught.

He searched for the app he had used once for a contractor in Basra who would not send photographs through company channels. Green icon. Encrypted messages. Disappearing options. No email, no subject lines, no accidental preview on a shared terminal.

He downloaded it.

The progress circle moved slowly.

Outside, someone came in, washed hands, left. Water ran. Door closed. The circle reached the end.

He opened the app, registered, adjusted every setting he could find. No previews. Lock screen hidden. Archive unknown contacts. Disappearing messages after seven days. Then one day. Then he changed it back to seven. One day looked like alarm. Seven looked like housekeeping.

He sent Olesya the invitation link.

Use this from now.

Her reply in the old message thread:

Another app?

Yes.

Why?

Cleaner.

That is not reason.

It is enough.

No answer.

He waited in the cubicle until someone tried the door handle. The metal latch jumped under the pressure.

'Occupied,' he said.

The person outside muttered and moved away.

The new app lit with a request.

Olesya Petrova wants to connect.

He accepted.

Her first message arrived there.

I do not like smaller places.

He sat on the closed toilet lid, phone in both hands. The fluorescent light buzzed above him. The wet floor reflected the door in broken strips.

It is only for documents.

No.

For hiding.

He typed:

For keeping things contained.

The word looked precise and insufficient.

Olesya answered:

Contained for who?

He closed the app.

The phone remained in his hand.

At dinner, Sarah spoke to Nisha and did not mention the email. Thomas sat opposite them and watched the space her silence left. Nisha described a payroll error involving three men with the same surname and one bank account that belonged to none of them. Sarah laughed in the right places. She ate rice, drank water, asked one sharp question, then another.

Thomas could have mistaken it for pardon if he had been a more careless man.

He was careful now.

Careful had become the smallest enclosure.

After dinner, Sarah walked back to the room ahead of him. Her keycard worked on the second try. The lock blinked red, then green. She pushed the door open and went in without holding it for him.

The room had changed in his absence, though nothing had moved.

Her laptop on the desk.

His laptop beside it.

Two chargers in the wall.

Two phones on two different surfaces.

The bed made badly in the joint style they had developed: her side pulled tight, his side mostly persuaded into order. A towel over the chair. Dust along the window rail. The kettle beside the socket that did not spark.

Sarah sat on the bed and took off her shoes.

'I am not asking tonight,' she said.

He closed the door.

'All right.'

'That does not mean it has gone away.'

'I know.'

'Good.'

She placed one shoe beside the other with more care than the act required.

'I need sleep.'

'Yes.'

'And tomorrow I need you to read the revised note without destroying company property.'

'I can do that.'

'Good.'

She went into the bathroom.

The shower started.

Thomas stood in the room with the sound of water in the pipes and opened the new app.

Olesya had sent three files.

School letter.

Competition certificate.

April receipts.

Then one message:

Here. Smaller.

He saved the files to the hidden folder.

Then he changed the app name on his phone to Notes.

The icon remained green.

He moved it into a folder with bank apps, airline apps, utility apps, all the small machinery of an adult life. It sat there without complaint.

Sarah came out of the bathroom in a T-shirt, hair wet at the ends, face clean of the day. She placed her phone on the bedside table, screen up.

Thomas placed his face down.

She saw it.

Neither of them spoke.

Later, after the light was off and the air conditioner had settled into its night rattle, his phone buzzed once against the wood.

Not loud.

Enough.

Sarah did not move.

Thomas waited until the room returned to itself. Then he reached for the phone and opened the new app beneath the sheet, brightness lowered until the words seemed printed under water.

Olesya:

Do not make me disappear from places.

He typed nothing.

The message sat in the smaller dark.
