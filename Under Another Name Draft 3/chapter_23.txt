Chapter 23

The hazard lights counted down into winter.

On.

Off.

On.

Off.

Thomas sat in the Stavanger office until the port emptied of ordinary movement. The crane lights remained. The lorry left. Rain struck the window in small hard points, each one catching the office reflection before sliding into the dark. On his screen the secure app remained open.

Tell me you arrived.

Sent.

No blue ticks.

At 22:17 he wrote:

Olesya.

Sent.

No blue ticks.

At 23:03:

Please confirm Anya has medicine.

Sent.

No blue ticks.

He did not send another. Three messages sat there like knocks on a door after the house had gone empty.

At home, Sarah had left a plate for him in the fridge. Fish, potatoes, cucumbers with dill. The plate was covered with cling film and labelled with a small square of masking tape.

Yours.

He took it out, stood with it in his hand, then put it back without lifting the film.

The house looked out over black water. Across the fjord, lights marked the opposite shore in a broken line. The windows held his reflection inside the room: coat still on, phone in one hand, the fridge light making the kitchen bright behind him. He looked like a man who had arrived somewhere and forgotten what for.

Sarah came downstairs just after midnight.

She had put on a jumper over her nightclothes. Her hair was loose. She stopped at the last stair and looked at him through the open kitchen.

'No message?'

'No.'

She came down the remaining steps.

'Did you eat?'

'Not yet.'

'That means no.'

He closed the fridge.

'They may be on the road.'

'They may be.'

'There may be no signal.'

'There may not be.'

She did not argue. That was worse.

Thomas opened the app again. Nothing had changed.

'Come to bed,' she said.

'Soon.'

Sarah looked at the phone, then at the black window.

'Soon has become a room you live in.'

She went back upstairs.

The first transfer after the move went out four days later.

Olesya had not asked. Anya had not sent an invoice, a photograph, or a list. They had both gone quiet. The transfer went out when the month turned and the old structure reached its date. Thomas woke before dawn, opened the payment folder, and prepared the amount by touch, the way a hand finds a switch in a familiar room.

Murad needed a recipient.

Thomas wrote:

Old card closed. Need alternative.

Murad replied:

No card no payout.

Cash pickup north?

Need town and name.

Have name.

Need town.

Thomas stared at the line.

Need town.

He wrote to Anya:

I can send support if you tell me which town.

No answer.

He wrote to Olesya:

I am not asking for address. Only town for transfer.

Sent.

No blue ticks.

He waited until noon. Then he sent the money to Murad anyway, instructing him to hold it.

Murad replied:

Holding costs after three days.

Fine.

Fine, Thomas wrote, and paid the cost.

On the third day, Murad messaged:

Woman contacted from old number. She gave pickup location. Paid.

Which location?

Cannot share.

I am sender.

She said no.

Send proof.

Murad sent a photograph of a payout slip with most of the branch information covered by a thumb. Olesya's initials were visible. Amount. Date. Signature line, scrawled too quickly to read.

Thomas enlarged the signature until the pixels tore.

He wrote to Olesya:

Received?

No blue ticks.

He wrote to Anya:

Received?

No reply.

The money moved.

The line did not.

The second month was easier.

That made him close the laptop and sit back from it.

He no longer expected a message before sending. He opened the exchange, bought the amount in smaller parts, moved it to Murad, paid the holding fee, waited for proof. The proof came late, photographed from a counter somewhere with the branch name covered. A woman's sleeve appeared at the edge of the frame, dark wool, not enough to identify anyone.

He saved it.

Month 2.

The file name looked obscene.

The third month arrived with snow against the study window and Sarah packing for Oslo. She had meetings with a shipping insurer and a woman she knew from Delhi who had reappeared in Norway with a different surname and the same bracelets. Sarah folded a navy dress into a small suitcase, then unfolded it and hung it back in the cupboard.

'You should come,' she said.

'To Oslo?'

'Two nights. A hotel. People who do not know us.'

'I have Hammerfest calls.'

'You have calls everywhere.'

He looked at the phone.

'Not this week.'

Sarah sat on the edge of the bed.

'When, then?'

He did not answer.

She zipped the suitcase.

'I am not asking for a holiday now. I am asking whether there is still a version of you that can leave the house without waiting for a message.'

The phone lay face down on the bedside table. No sound came from it.

'There is no message,' he said.

'That is the message.'

She lifted the suitcase from the bed and set it on the floor.

After she left for Oslo, he sent the third payment. Murad confirmed pickup the following morning. No one wrote from the north.

Thomas added a tick to the spreadsheet.

The tick sat in the row, green and meaningless.

After that, the month learned a new rhythm.

He sent. Murad held. Someone, sometimes Olesya, sometimes Anya, sometimes a third person whose name Murad would not give, collected or redirected the money. Proof arrived with pieces covered: branch name, city, transaction code, sometimes the payer reference. Thomas paid fees for not knowing. Each payment reached a place he could not name.

In the spreadsheet, the confirmation column became useless.

He replaced Received with Proof.

Then with Paid.

Then with a tick.

The tick offended him less.

Stavanger moved into deeper winter. Mornings came late and with reluctance. The fjord changed colour by the hour, steel, slate, pewter, black. The house held heat and silence. Sarah bought heavier curtains for the bedroom. The room was warm. The glass still left her exposed at night.

'It's like sleeping in a display case,' she said, standing on a chair to clip the curtain ring.

'I can do that.'

'I know.'

She clipped the last ring herself.

They spoke more about the house than about anything else. Heating. Insurance. Car tyres. Whether to buy a proper dining table or continue with the company one. The conversation had become a way of walking across ice without looking down.

At dinner, Sarah asked:

'Have they answered?'

'No.'

'But the money is still going?'

'Yes.'

'How?'

'Broker.'

'Do you know where?'

'No.'

She set her fork down.

'Then you are funding a shape.'

He looked at his plate. Salmon, boiled potatoes, green beans. Food that made no demand.

'They need it.'

'Do you know that?'

'They collect it.'

'That is not the same thing.'

He had no answer that would stand.

Sarah did not press. She had stopped pressing. Pressing required a belief that something might open.

In February, Anya's email account replied.

Thank you.

No greeting. No explanation. No name beneath it.

Thomas answered within thirty seconds.

Are you both well?

The reply did not come.

He stared at the two words for so long that Sarah walked behind him and saw them.

'That is from Anya?'

'The account.'

'The account.'

'Yes.'

Sarah remained behind his chair.

'You don't know if she wrote it.'

'No.'

'You will treat it as hers.'

He closed the laptop.

'What would you have me do?'

Sarah moved away from the chair.

'I don't know anymore.'

Her honesty left no surface to strike. He preferred it when she had a position. Positions could be argued with, adjusted, worked around. This was only a person standing in the room with him, refusing to become an instrument.

The next morning he searched for private investigators in Azerbaijan.

He did it at the office before anyone arrived, sitting at his desk whilst the harbour lay under a colourless sky. The search results came in English, Russian, Azerbaijani. Matrimonial checks. Corporate due diligence. Debt tracing. Asset searches. Missing persons. Discreet enquiries. Photographs of men in suits, hands folded over desks, flags behind them, office plants with glossy leaves.

He closed three sites immediately.

One had a London number and a Baku partner. One listed corporate clients. One asked for a retainer before naming services. He called the London number.

A man answered on the second ring.

'Field enquiries in Azerbaijan?' Thomas asked.

'Depends on subject.'

'Two adults. Mother and daughter. No contact. Location unknown. Need welfare check without approaching.'

'Relationship?'

Thomas looked at the harbour.

'Financial sponsor.'

The words sounded wrong and exact.

'Legal matter?'

'No.'

'Police matter?'

'No.'

'Consent?'

'No.'

The man did not react.

'We can do passive location assessment if there is a lawful basis and no harassment. Open sources, local enquiries, address confirmation. No confrontation.'

'No contact.'

'All right.'

'No contact,' Thomas repeated.

'I heard you.'

The retainer was high. Thomas paid from the secondary account and sent what he had: old Baku addresses, Olesya's full name, Anya's full name, old card numbers, hospital name, partial debt papers, photographs of the old flat door, the bus window, the payout slips with covered branches, medicine packets, aunt reference, north.

North was not evidence.

He sent it anyway.

The intake form asked for purpose.

Thomas wrote:

Welfare confirmation.

Then it asked for limitations.

No direct contact.

No approach.

No disclosure of client.

No involvement with collectors.

The form asked for known risks. He wrote debt pressure, recent hospitalisation, possible relocation, and stopped before adding himself. The cursor waited at the end of the line. He moved to the next field.

Relationship to subject.

He sat with the form open for twenty minutes.

Financial sponsor would be read as charitable, perhaps transactional. Former employer contact would be too small and too false. Partner would create a claim Olesya had not accepted, and the word would not survive Sarah standing in the kitchen with the invoice in her hand. Family was a door in a different house.

He wrote:

Long-term personal contact.

The phrase looked like something written by a man avoiding a document.

He sent it.

The investigator replied:

Please confirm you do not require address release if subjects are located.

Thomas typed:

I require location confirmation only.

He did not press send.

He knew that if the exact address arrived, he would open it. He would map it, measure the distance to the bakery, to the nearest clinic, to the road south. He would turn it into a plan before the file had finished downloading.

He changed the line.

Do not release exact address unless urgent welfare issue observed.

Then he sent it.

The investigator's first update arrived after eight days.

Subject not at prior Baku address.

He already knew that.

The second update:

Former neighbour confirms departure after dispute with collectors. Direction north stated but no precise location given.

The third:

Possible connection through sister of subject's late mother. Village outside Qusar district. Checking.

Thomas read Qusar on the screen and opened a map.

North.

Mountains. Border roads. Villages along pale lines. Rivers thin as wires. The Caspian far behind, Baku reduced to a name at the edge of the map.

He did not write to Olesya.

The restraint cost him more than the retainer.

Sarah found the investigator invoice by accident.

It came through the printer when she was printing insurance documents. The printer tray held both sets of papers together. She brought the invoice to the kitchen island and placed it in front of him without folding it.

'What is this?'

He looked at the letterhead.

'Investigator.'

'For Olesya.'

'Passive. No contact.'

'You hired someone to find her.'

'To know she is alive.'

Sarah's face did not change. Her hand remained on the paper.

'Did she ask not to be found?'

'She asked me to stop.'

Sarah nodded once.

'And this is stopping?'

'I am not contacting her.'

'You are still entering the room.'

'There is no room.'

'Exactly.'

The invoice sat between them.

Outside, wind moved across the fjord and struck the glass with fine sleet. The house made one small sound as the weather pressed against it.

'If I do nothing,' he said, 'I have no way to know.'

'And if you know?'

He looked at the invoice.

Sarah waited.

'Then I know,' he said.

She lifted her hand from the paper.

'That may be all you get.'

For two days after that, he did not open the investigator portal.

He told himself this was restraint. The portal sent automated updates anyway, small grey notices that arrived in his inbox and sat above company mail.

Case active.

Field enquiry pending.

Local contact assigned.

He archived each one without opening it. Then he opened the archive and read the subject lines again.

At work, the Russian package consultancy began sending recovered files. Vendor names translated poorly. Equipment tags matched old Baku lists he had corrected years before. He saw Olesya's old file naming habits in some of them: dates reversed, Russian abbreviations left inside English folders, a space before a dash that no system would have inserted by itself.

He clicked through one archive after another.

For three seconds, in a folder from 2017, he found her initials in a metadata field.

OB.

Not her initials now, after all the naming and renaming. The name she had used in the office then. He copied the field into a note, then deleted it. The consultancy email waited for technical approval. He gave it.

Sarah began sleeping with the curtains fully drawn.

In the morning she opened them with a hard pull, letting the fjord back into the room all at once. Some days she dressed for the office she did not have, a blouse, trousers, earrings, then worked at the dining table on investment papers, travel documents, and committees that seemed to find her through every country. Other days she walked for hours and came back with wet hair, cheeks marked by wind, and no account of where she had gone.

'You should come with me,' she said one Saturday.

'Where?'

'Out.'

He looked at the laptop.

'I have something.'

'You always have something.'

She put on her coat.

'Does it ever finish?'

He nearly answered yes. The word was ready, trained by years of projects, milestones, handovers, punch lists, close-out reports.

He said nothing.

Sarah left.

After the door closed, he opened the investigator portal.

No final report yet.

Only a note:

Subject two observed in district centre. Employment likely informal.

He read it until the words lost their order.

The final report arrived three weeks later.

It came in a password-protected file at 06:38, whilst the house was still dark and Sarah slept. Thomas carried the laptop downstairs and opened it at the kitchen island. The password had been sent in a separate message.

The file contained twenty-two pages.

Executive summary.

Subject one located.

Subject two located.

Current residence: village outside Qusar district, exact address withheld pending client instruction due to sensitivity.

No direct contact made.

Observation period: five days.

Thomas scrolled slowly.

Photographs followed.

First, the house.

Cinderblock walls, one storey, metal roof patched in two colours. A wooden gate leaning inward. Snow gathered in dirty ridges along the path. A satellite dish fixed to the side wall with wire. Two windows, one with plastic taped across a cracked pane.

Second, Anya.

She stood outside a bakery entrance carrying a sack of flour against her hip. Her hair was tied back, face turned away from the camera. Flour dust marked the front of her dark coat and one cheek. She had grown thinner or the coat made her look so. Behind her, a man in a white apron held the door open with his foot.

Third, Olesya.

She stood at a washing line behind the house, lifting a sheet with both hands. The line sagged. Snow lay in patches near her shoes. Her hair was covered with a scarf. The cardigan hung loose on her shoulders. The photograph had been taken from a distance, through branches perhaps, the foreground blurred by something close to the lens.

Thomas stopped scrolling.

The kitchen lights reflected in the laptop screen. For a second Olesya's image lay under the reflection of his hands, pale on the black keys.

He zoomed in.

The image softened. Her face was turned downward, not visible. One hand held the sheet. The other reached for a peg. On the ground beside the basket lay a strip of cloth or bandage, white against wet earth.

He zoomed out.

The next photograph showed Anya at the bakery counter. A customer reached across with coins. Anya's hand was extended, palm up. The sleeve of her coat had flour at the cuff.

The report notes were brief.

Subject two appears employed informally at local bakery, early morning shifts.

Subject one observed mostly at residence, limited outside movement.

No visible male co-resident during observation period.

No collection activity observed during period.

Residence shared with older female thought to be relative.

Thomas read the lines twice.

No collection activity observed.

No visible male co-resident.

Older female thought to be relative.

The words gave him what he had asked for. They were alive. They had shelter. Anya had work. Olesya could stand at a washing line. There was no man at the door in the photographs, no hospital corridor, no visible collector.

The file should have released him.

It did not.

The photographs made a new enclosure. They proved distance. They proved ordinary damage. They proved that a life could continue outside his reach and still carry the marks of what had happened. They gave him a village without an address, a house without a door he could knock on, a daughter behind a counter, a woman under a scarf hanging sheets in snow.

Sarah came downstairs at seven.

She stopped when she saw the laptop.

'Is that it?'

'Yes.'

'Are they alive?'

'Yes.'

She stood on the other side of the island.

'May I see?'

He did not answer at once.

Then he turned the laptop.

Sarah looked through the photographs without touching the trackpad. Her eyes moved slowly, pausing at Anya with the flour, at Olesya with the sheet, at the house.

'She looks cold,' Sarah said.

It was the only thing she said.

Thomas turned the laptop back.

The report ended with recommendations.

Continue passive monitoring if required.

Obtain exact address with further field work if authorised.

Potential discreet courier route available through district centre.

He closed the file.

Then opened it again.

He went to the photograph of Olesya at the washing line and sat with it. Outside, Norway lightened by degrees. The fjord appeared, flat and colourless. A ferry moved on schedule. The house heating clicked somewhere beneath the floor.

Sarah made coffee.

The machine ground beans, louder than it needed to be. She placed a cup beside him and stood there, one hand on the island.

'What will you do with it?' she asked.

He looked at the report.

'Nothing.'

'Thomas.'

He closed the file.

'I said nothing.'

Sarah took her own cup to the window. Her reflection joined the morning glass, thin over the fjord.

He opened the secure app.

Olesya's last message remained:

Stop.

Below it, his own:

Tell me you arrived.

Sent.

No blue ticks.

He did not write.

He opened a blank message anyway.

I saw that you are working.

He deleted it.

I know where you are.

He deleted that faster.

I can still help if you let me.

The sentence stayed longer than the others. It looked modest on the screen. It was not. It carried a door, a route, a transaction, a man in a rented car, an office, a file, a search history, every concealed thing he had built around the word help until help had become another pressure.

He deleted it.

The app returned to Stop.

At the office, he opened the report twice more.

At the office, he opened the report twice more.

Once before the morning risk meeting.

Once after lunch, when the Russian package consultancy sent a sample translation and asked for approval.

Each time the photographs gave him the same facts.

House.

Bakery.

Washing line.

Snow.

No answer.

By evening, the file had become a place he entered.

He copied it into a folder named Q.

Then renamed the folder Archive.

Then moved it into Norway, under Debt, then moved it out again.

At home, Sarah had not made dinner. She had left bread, cheese, and a note.

Walking. Back later.

He stood at the island with the investigator file open and the note beside it.

The house was warm. The fjord was black. Somewhere in the walls, water moved through pipes with a low domestic rush.

He selected the file.

Delete.

The system asked:

Are you sure you want to move this item to the recycle bin?

He clicked yes.

The file disappeared.

For a minute, the folder was empty.

Then he opened the recycle bin and restored it.

He sat down.

The restored file appeared in the folder with its original name. The system did not ask why he had brought it back. It did not mark the hesitation. It kept no visible record of the minute in which he had attempted to remove what he had paid to know.

He opened the report again.

Potential discreet courier route available through district centre.

He had avoided that line before. It had sat at the bottom of the recommendations like a small hook. Courier route. District centre. A way to place something into their hands without requiring an account, a card, a branch name, a message read in blue. A way around the refusal that still looked enough like distance to let him call it respect.

He clicked the investigator's contact thread.

What does courier route mean?

The reply came the next morning.

Local delivery through trusted intermediary. Package only. No discussion. Subject may refuse.

Can money be delivered?

Cash not recommended. Medicine, documents, ticket, phone possible.

Ticket.

The word entered the room differently from money. It had direction built into it.

He wrote:

International ticket?

Possible if subject agrees. Needs identification details and route.

He did not answer.

For the rest of the day, he carried ticket through the house.

It sat with him while he emptied the dishwasher. It sat with him while Sarah spoke on the phone in the study, her voice low and efficient. It sat with him while snow slid from the roof in a soft collapse and broke apart on the deck.

At dinner, Sarah asked:

'Did the report give you what you wanted?'

He looked at her across the table. She had made lentils and rice, something warm and plain, and had opened a bottle of red wine she did not finish.

'It gave me where they are.'

'Not the address.'

'Enough.'

'Enough for what?'

He did not answer.

Sarah put down her glass.

'Thomas.'

'I don't know yet.'

'That is not true.'

He looked at the window. Their reflections sat in it, divided by the pendant light above the table.

'There may be a way to get her to Istanbul.'

Sarah did not move.

'Her and Anya?'

'Olesya first.'

The words came out and showed their damage.

Sarah stood and took her plate to the sink. She rinsed it carefully, though there was food still on it.

'You are doing it again.'

'No.'

'You are separating the hard part and calling it sequence.'

'If I can get Olesya out, Anya can follow.'

'Later.'

He closed his eyes.

'Do not use my words against me.'

'I am using your pattern.'

Water ran over the plate. Sarah turned it off.

'Has she asked for Istanbul?'

'No.'

'Has she answered you?'

'No.'

'Then who is this for?'

He stood.

'For the chance.'

Sarah dried her hands on a towel.

'A chance requires another person reaching for it.'

The house settled around the sentence.

The final intervention did not arrive as a thought. It arrived as an action.

He opened a flight search.

Stavanger to Istanbul.

Then he opened a second tab.

Baku to Istanbul.

He did not enter dates yet.

Outside, the deck light came on by motion sensor, though nothing visible had moved.
