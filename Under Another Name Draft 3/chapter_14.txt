Chapter 14

The lockdown arrived by email before breakfast.

No siren this time. No thud through concrete. No shelter door closing with people on the wrong side of lunch. Only a message from regional security, copied to operations, logistics, medical, travel, and anyone senior enough to be made responsible by inclusion.

Effective immediately.

Movement restricted to essential personnel.

All outbound travel suspended pending review.

Rotations paused.

Visitors to remain on site.

Do not proceed to airport unless instructed.

The words came in black type on a white screen while the compound was still washing itself awake. Sprinklers clicked across the grass. The dining hall extractor fans started one by one. Men walked towards breakfast with towels around their necks, not yet briefed into the new rules. The sun sat low behind a veil of dust, flattening the accommodation blocks into pale shapes with hard edges.

Thomas read the email twice.

Then he read the recipient list.

Sarah was on it.

She stood in the bathroom, brushing her teeth with the door open. The mirror was fogged at the edges. Her phone lay on the bed, face up, charging from the socket that did not spark. It lit once, then again. The same email. A calendar alert beneath it. Travel review. Pack. Convoy to airport.

None of that belonged to the day any more.

'What?' she said from the bathroom.

He looked up.

'Travel is suspended.'

She spat into the basin and rinsed her mouth.

'Suspended how?'

'All outbound. Pending review.'

'Today?'

'Effective immediately.'

She came to the doorway with the toothbrush still in one hand. Her hair was wet from the shower and dark against her neck.

'My flight is tonight.'

'Not now.'

'I know what suspended means.'

She stepped past him, picked up her phone, and read the email without sitting down. Her face did not alter much. That was how he knew the thing had landed. Sarah moved first in the body, later in the face. Her left foot pressed harder into the floor. Her thumb stopped on the screen. A drop of water ran from her hair to the collar of her T-shirt and stayed there.

'How long?'

'No date.'

'There is always a date, even if it is a lie.'

'Not here.'

She scrolled, found the attachment, opened it, closed it.

'Regional?'

'Global wording.'

'That is worse.'

He said nothing.

She sat on the edge of the bed and placed the phone beside her. Her suitcase stood open under the window, half-packed. Two shirts folded. One pair of shoes wrapped in a laundry bag. A stack of documents in a plastic sleeve. A packet of oat biscuits she had bought for the flight, already softened by the heat.

'I was ready to go home,' she said.

The line did not ask for comfort.

He gave her none.

Outside, someone laughed in the corridor, unaware of the new size of the world.

By eight, the compound knew.

It knew through the official briefing in the dining hall, through travel coordinators with red eyes and printed lists, through men refreshing airline pages until the pages failed, through supervisors calling wives from stairwells, through the sudden value of cigarettes, instant coffee, spare chargers, extra medication. The compound had always been temporary. That had been one of its promises. Hard beds, bad food, dust in the teeth, but with a departure date attached.

The date had been removed.

Everything changed weight.

Sarah did not cry. She did not shout. She made three calls before breakfast and two after. London first. Then her mother. Then the travel desk. Then London again. Then a friend in Edinburgh who was supposed to collect her from the airport and now received instructions about heating, post, and the basil plant on the kitchen sill, already condemned by distance.

Thomas sat at the desk with his laptop open and no useful work inside it.

The official lockdown tracker appeared in the shared drive at 09:12.

Rows of names.

Nationality.

Role.

Location.

Room.

Medical notes.

Travel status.

Sarah's name sat amongst contractors, engineers, finance staff, visiting auditors, two trainers, and one vendor whose paperwork had been wrong even before the world closed.

Visitor.

On site.

Travel suspended.

Thomas looked at the word visitor until it lost shape.

Sarah stood behind him.

'Do not look at me like a row in a spreadsheet.'

He closed the file.

'Sorry.'

'I know it is your native language.'

'Still.'

She reached over him for her laptop. Her arm brushed his shoulder. The contact was ordinary and not ordinary.

'I have calls.'

'What do you need?'

'A room that is not this room.'

'Conference room?'

'Booked. Everyone wants to be trapped professionally.'

'Quiet office?'

'Nisha has it.'

'Operations side office.'

'Does it smell of printer toner and old socks?'

'Yes.'

'Perfect. No one in London will notice the difference.'

He gave her the side-office key and watched her leave with laptop, notebook, charger, phone, water bottle, scarf, and the careful expression she used when the thing requiring management was herself.

His own phone vibrated.

Olesya.

It is closed here also.

He opened the secure app.

School closed.

Packing closed.

Buses less.

Owner says no visits to flat without notice.

Anya cough again in night.

The messages had arrived in a stack, each one a smaller room than the last.

Thomas typed:

Medicine?

Olesya replied:

Two days.

Then:

Pharmacy expensive now.

Then:

My work says no shifts this week.

He looked towards the open door. Sarah's suitcase under the window. Sarah's phone charger still in the wall. Sarah's slippers beside the bed, one upright, one fallen sideways.

Send list, he wrote.

Olesya answered:

List is longer.

Send it.

The dots appeared and disappeared.

Then:

You are inside with her?

He held the phone still.

Yes.

No answer.

At ten thirty, the compound moved into lockdown rhythm.

Meal times split by block. Temperature checks outside the dining hall. Laundry reduced. Gym closed. Visitors instructed to stay near accommodation unless authorised. All non-essential meetings remote, which meant the same people sat in rooms beside each other and called into screens that failed every third sentence. The corridors filled with taped arrows, hand-written signs, plastic chairs placed at odd intervals to enforce space between bodies that had already breathed the same air for weeks.

Sarah returned from the side office at midday carrying her laptop against her chest.

'London wants me to keep working.'

'Can you?'

'That was not the question they asked.'

'What did they ask?'

'Whether I had sufficient connectivity to maintain delivery.'

'And?'

'I said sufficient is a theological term.'

She placed the laptop on the desk and looked at the suitcase.

'I should empty the suitcase.'

'Maybe wait.'

'For what? A miracle flight with legroom?'

He did not answer.

She knelt and took out the shoes in the laundry bag. Then the shirts. Then the plastic sleeve of documents. She laid each item back into the room with a deliberateness that made unpacking look more violent than packing had been.

'Do you know what the strangest part is?'

'What?'

'I am not stuck somewhere unfamiliar.'

She slid the suitcase under the bed with her foot.

'I am stuck in your life.'

The kettle clicked off.

Neither of them moved to pour it.

By afternoon, the first money problem arrived.

Olesya sent a photograph from the pharmacy window. The queue stood outside on the pavement, each person separated by a square of tape. The sign on the glass was in Azerbaijani and Russian. Restricted entry. Masks required. No credit for medicine. A small shelf near the door held bottles of hand gel priced like imported perfume.

Below the photograph came the list.

Inhaler.

Nebuliser liquid.

Cough syrup.

Paracetamol.

Masks.

Soap.

Rice.

Oil.

Eggs.

Internet for school.

The last item sat there with no price.

Thomas added the amounts in the monthly sheet.

The total was not impossible.

That no longer comforted him.

The cost itself had stopped being the point. The point was route. Bank. Reference. Recipient. Pattern. Sarah in the next room. Olesya in the app. The landlord who now knew money came from outside. The bank that had blocked him once and would learn from its own suspicion. Every transfer left a line. Every line wanted a name.

He opened the banking app.

Then closed it.

He opened the spreadsheet.

Then closed it.

He looked at the suitcase under the bed.

Then at Sarah's laptop on the desk, still warm from use.

The room had become too full of evidence.

He took his phone and went to the laundry block.

The laundry smelled of detergent, hot lint, and damp cotton. Machines turned behind scratched glass. Two dryers were out of order, though one still spun with nothing inside it. A hand-written roster had been taped to the wall, revised three times in different ink. Someone had left a sock on top of the payment box. Blue. Child-sized, though no children lived here.

Thomas stood between the machines and opened the banking app again.

Amount.

Recipient.

Reference.

Purpose.

He stopped at the reference line.

April correction had already been used.

Family support had been spoken aloud to the bank through a satellite handset with Sarah standing outside.

Rent looked like rent.

Medicine looked like medicine.

Other looked like what he had made of his life.

He typed:

Supplies.

Then deleted it.

Maintenance.

Deleted.

Household.

Deleted.

He left the reference blank.

The app would not allow it.

He typed:

April.

Purpose: Other.

The bank asked for additional verification.

Not blocked. Not yet. A softer gate. A code sent by text. A warning about fraud. A reminder never to transfer money under pressure.

Thomas stared at that line beside the washing machines.

Never transfer money under pressure.

A dryer changed direction with a dull internal thump.

He entered the code.

The transfer went through.

He sent the screenshot to Olesya.

For medicine and food.

Her reply:

This is too much.

Use it.

She wrote:

You say use like cupboard opens when you say.

He stood in the laundry block with the smell of hot lint in his throat and did not answer.

When he returned to the room, Sarah was sitting on the floor with the suitcase open again.

Not packing.

Rearranging.

She had divided the contents into piles.

Clean.

Laundry.

Work.

Home.

The home pile was smallest: oat biscuits, one book, a scarf, the plastic sleeve of documents, two wrapped gifts for people in Edinburgh, bought too early and now stranded. She held one of the gifts in her lap, turning it over.

'My mother will think I did this deliberately,' she said.

'Get trapped in Iraq?'

'To avoid lunch with my aunt.'

'That is an elaborate plan.'

'My family will respect the commitment.'

He sat on the bed.

'Did you get through to her?'

'Yes. She did the thing where she becomes calm and then sends eight messages afterwards.'

'Good.'

'One of them says basil dead.'

'I am sorry for your loss.'

'It died doing what it knew. Being neglected by me.'

She placed the gift in the home pile.

'Where did you go?'

'Laundry.'

'Without laundry?'

He looked at the empty chair.

'Needed space.'

'There is no space.'

'Less room, then.'

She nodded once. Not accepting. Marking.

'Baku?'

'Yes.'

'The child?'

'Needs medicine.'

'Did you send money?'

'Yes.'

Her fingers flattened the gift wrapping at one corner.

'From your account?'

'Yes.'

'Good.'

Then, after a pause:

'Do not make me ask every time.'

'I will not.'

'That means tell me before I ask.'

'I know.'

'Do you?'

He had no answer large enough for the room.

Sarah put the wrapped gift into the suitcase and closed the lid without fastening it.

That evening, the compound served dinner in paper boxes.

Block B between six and six thirty. Block C after that. Thomas and Sarah queued under the dining hall awning with taped marks on the ground keeping everyone apart by regulation, not by instinct. The wind had dropped. Heat held the dust low. A man ahead of them wore a mask below his nose and kept touching it with the hand that held his phone. Sarah watched him for three seconds, then looked away with visible effort.

'This will end badly,' she said.

'The food or the system?'

'Yes.'

They received rice, chicken, boiled vegetables, and an orange each. The boxes softened at the corners before they reached the room.

They ate on the bed, cross-legged, the desk occupied by laptops. Sarah's phone lay face up between them. His lay face down beside his knee.

She looked at it.

He turned it over.

No notifications showed. He had hidden them all.

'That is not better,' she said.

'What?'

'Blankness.'

'It is quieter.'

'For whom?'

He put the phone beside the food box, screen up.

The new app icon still sat inside the utilities folder, renamed Notes. No preview. No name. No text. A door without a handle.

Sarah watched him do it and said nothing.

The phone stayed dark until after dinner.

When it lit, it showed only:

Notes.

1 notification.

Sarah's eyes went to it before his hand did.

'Is that her?'

'Yes.'

'Then read it.'

He opened it.

Olesya had sent a photograph of Anya at the kitchen table. School on the screen of a small phone propped against a mug. The red notebook open. An inhaler beside her hand. A packet of masks on the table. Rice and oil behind it. The flat was theirs again, but the door still seemed capable of rejecting them.

Message:

She is inside.

Then:

Medicine bought.

Then:

Thank you.

Sarah saw the photograph.

Thomas did not move quickly enough to hide it. Or perhaps, by then, hiding had become the more visible act.

'That is Anya?'

'Yes.'

Sarah leaned closer, careful not to touch the phone.

'She is small.'

'She is ten.'

'That is small.'

He looked at the photograph again. Anya's shoulders drawn in. The red notebook under her hand. The inhaler close enough to be reached without standing.

'Yes.'

Sarah sat back.

'And the mother?'

'What about her?'

'What does she need?'

He looked at her.

'Sarah.'

'I am asking practically.'

'Everything is practical until it is not.'

'That sounds like something you say when you want to stop the question.'

The air conditioner rattled above them, caught, rattled again.

He closed the app.

'She needs the flat to stay hers. She needs school to continue. She needs work to reopen. She needs the landlord to stop pressing. She needs a country that does not make her smaller at every counter.'

Sarah looked down at the rice box in her lap.

'And you?'

'What about me?'

'What do you need?'

His phone screen went dark.

'I need the routes to keep working.'

Sarah's mouth tightened. She placed the lid on her food box.

'That was a very thin answer.'

He did not say the word back to her. Sentiment would have used air the room no longer had.

By the third day, lockdown had made the compound smaller than its fence.

The gym stayed closed. The dining hall served boxes. The smoking area became a place of rumours. The clinic called room by room for temperature checks. The internet slowed while everyone tried to keep lives alive elsewhere. Men who had once complained about flights now checked flight maps for planes that did not move. Sarah's world shrank to the desk, the side office, the bed, the corridor, the dining hall queue, and the square of sky visible through the dusty window.

Olesya's world shrank too.

Flat.

Pharmacy.

Queue.

Landlord.

School on a phone.

Messages at odd hours.

Receipts when shops still printed them.

Photographs when they did not.

The monthly sheet no longer fitted the month.

Rent had become rent plus penalty plus cash demand.

School had become school plus internet.

Medicine had become medicine plus masks plus delivery.

Food had become food before the prices changed again.

Contingency had become everything.

By the fifth day, the compound began rationing certainty.

Official updates arrived at nine and five, whether or not anything had changed. Travel remained suspended. External movement remained restricted. Local regulations remained under review. Medical screening remained mandatory. The repetition was meant to calm people. It did the opposite. It made language feel like furniture pushed against a door.

Sarah stopped asking when she could leave.

She asked better questions.

'Who signs off visitor medication?'

'Medical.'

'Who gets priority on laundry?'

'Field crew.'

'Who decides whether guests can use the office printer?'

'No one until someone complains.'

'Good. Then I am using it.'

She adapted with visible competence and invisible cost. She built a second version of herself from what the compound allowed. Calls before breakfast. Work in the side office. Food boxes at six. Ten minutes outside after dinner when the guards permitted it. A towel washed by hand and hung over the wardrobe door. A list taped inside the cupboard: coffee, soap, toothpaste, paracetamol, patience. The last word was crossed out by the second afternoon and replaced with batteries.

Thomas found the list whilst looking for a clean shirt.
