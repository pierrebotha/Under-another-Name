Chapter 14
The train did not slow. It carved through the countryside, a silver needle stitching the green and grey landscape together, but Thomas felt no movement. He was suspended in the carriage, a specimen in a jar, watching the world blur past the window. The rhythm of the wheels—clack-clack, clack-clack—had synchronized with the pounding in his temples. A metronome for a mind coming undone. The sound was not external; it was internal, a vibration rattling the fillings of his teeth, resonating in the hollows of his sinuses. It was the sound of time passing, of distance accumulating, of a life receding into a dot on a map that someone else was watching.
He stared at his hands again. They rested on his knees, pale and still, like two dead animals left out in the cold. The lines on his palms seemed to have shifted since he last looked, mere minutes ago. The life line was shorter? Fractured? Or was it just the flickering fluorescent light above, casting dancing shadows that played tricks on his retinas, rewriting his biology in real time? He turned them over, examining the back of the hand. Veins blue and prominent under thin, translucent skin. Whose hands were these? They looked like the hands of a stranger who had borrowed Thomas Ward’s body for a journey to nowhere, a temporary vessel piloted by a ghost.
Just access. Under another name.
The phrase looped in his head, a scratched record skipping on the same groove, wearing down the vinyl of his sanity. He tried to silence it. Tried to focus on the physical sensation of the seat beneath him. Hard plastic. Rough fabric. Real. But even that felt distant, mediated by a layer of static. Was the seat real? Or was it a haptic simulation fed directly into his nervous system? If she could put a phone in his bag without him seeing, if she could edit his memories of stains, insert a tenth mark overnight while he slept, why not this? Why not the whole train? The whole world? Perhaps the countryside rushing by was just a screensaver. Perhaps the other passengers were NPCs, scripted entities designed to populate his personal hell.
He looked up. Scanned the carriage with slow, deliberate movements, afraid that a sudden jerk might trigger something.
The businessman two rows ahead was still typing. The click-clack of his keyboard was rhythmic, precise. Report. Deadline. Profit. A life of meaning. Or was it just noise? Random keystrokes generating gibberish?
The students were laughing now, sharing earbuds, heads bobbing to a beat Thomas couldn’t hear. Were they laughing at him? Did they know?
The mother was asleep, the baby quiet in her arms. A picture of peace. Or a tableau of control?
Normalcy. A performance of normalcy. And he was the only one who knew the script had been rewritten.
And the man in the grey coat.
He was still there. Two rows ahead. Facing backward now? No, he had turned. Or had he moved seats while Thomas blinked, while he was lost in the labyrinth of his own hands?
Thomas squinted, forcing his eyes to focus through the haze of fatigue and fear.
The man was looking at him. Not casually. Not the glance of a fellow passenger checking the time or assessing a threat. A stare. Direct. Unblinking. Predatory.
Grey coat. Black umbrella resting against his leg like a weapon sheathed. Face obscured by the brim of a hat Thomas hadn’t noticed before. When had he put it on?
Security? Thomas thought again, the old reflex kicking in. Or one of hers? An agent of the experiment?
If it was security, what had he done? Bought a ticket with cash? Looked suspicious? Carried a bag that smelled of damp canvas and fear?
If it was one of hers… then the experiment had entered Phase Three.
Surveillance from the outside, Olesya had implied. Physical manifestation. The digital leashing was no longer enough; now she needed flesh and blood to corroborate the data.
Thomas felt a cold sweat prickle his hairline, trickling down his spine like an insect. He wanted to stand up. To walk to the other end of the train. To confront the man. Who are you? What do you want? Is she paying you? Are you real?
But he couldn’t move. His legs felt heavy, anchored to the floor by an invisible weight, as if gravity had been dialed up specifically for him. Paralysis. The classic fear response. Freeze. Play dead. Maybe if he didn’t move, the predator would lose interest. Maybe if he stopped transmitting, the signal would fade.
He looked away. Focused on the window. Forced himself to breathe. In. Out. Shallow. Ragged.
The reflection showed only himself. Pale. Gaunt. Eyes wide with terror, dark circles bruising the skin beneath them.
Where was she? In the glass? Behind his own shoulder?
He leaned closer. Peered into the dark depths of the reflection, searching the black void behind his image.
For a second, he thought he saw her. Behind his own shoulder. Smiling. That tight, unreadable smile that used to mean love and now meant ownership.
Then the train rushed out of a tunnel into bright sunlight. The reflection vanished, replaced by the glare of green fields, hedgerows whipping past in a blur of chlorophyll and mud.
He blinked. Rubbed his eyes until sparks danced in his vision.
When he looked back, the seat opposite was empty. Just empty blue fabric. Dust motes dancing in a shaft of light.
Had she been there? Or was the hallucination spreading? Was the virus in his brain now, replicating, overwriting his visual cortex?
You’re running in a circle. Inside my head.
Her voice echoed. Clear as a bell. Not a memory. A live feed.
He gripped the armrests. Knuckles white. Tendons standing out like cables.
I am not inside your head, he thought fiercely, projecting the words like a shield. I am on a train. I am going to Carlisle. I am leaving. I am real.
But the doubt was a worm, eating its way through his certainty, hollowing him out from the inside.
What if Carlisle didn’t exist? What if the train never stopped? What if he rode this loop forever, a ghost on a steel track, watched by a man in a grey coat and a woman in his own mind, trapped in a recursive algorithm of flight and capture?
The intercom crackled. Static. Then a voice. Distorted. Artificial. Sounding like it was coming from underwater.
‘Next station: Peterborough. Change here for services to the North and East. Please mind the gap between the train and the platform.’
Peterborough. Not Carlisle yet. A stop. A junction.
He could get off.
The thought sparked a sudden surge of adrenaline, a chemical jolt that cut through the paralysis.
Get off. Lose the man in the grey coat. Disappear into the crowd. Break the pattern.
It was a chance. A small window of opportunity in the wall of his prison.
He stood up. Legs shaky. Muscles trembling. He grabbed his bag. The canvas felt heavy. Did it weigh more than before? Was there something else inside now? Another phone? Another tracker? A brick of lead to anchor him to this reality?
He didn’t open it. Didn’t dare. To look inside was to invite the unknown in.
He moved into the aisle. Stumbled slightly as the train swayed around a bend.
The businessman glanced up. The students paused their music. The mother stirred, shielding her baby.
And the man in the grey coat.
He stood up too. Smoothly. Deliberately. No rush. No panic. Just purpose.
He picked up his black umbrella. Held it like a cane. Like a truncheon. Like a sceptre.
He began to walk towards the door. Towards Thomas.
Thomas froze.
The aisle was narrow. Only one person could pass at a time. A choke point.
The man was coming closer. Step by step. Slow. Measured. The click of his shoes on the floor was loud, distinct.
Thomas could see his face now. Under the brim of the hat.
It was blank. Featureless. Like a mannequin. Or a face blurred by motion, by bad resolution.
No. Focus. Breathe.
It was a normal face. Middle-aged. Clean-shaven. Unremarkable. Except for the eyes. Cold. Dead. Empty sockets filled with static.
Don’t look at him, Thomas told himself. Look at the door. Look at the exit. Look at freedom.
He shuffled sideways. Pressed his back against the seats. Let the man pass. Made himself small. Invisible.
The man walked past him. Close. Too close. Thomas smelled him. Stale tobacco. Wet wool. And something else. Something chemical. Antiseptic? Formaldehyde? The smell of a hospital morgue.
The man didn’t look at him. Didn’t acknowledge him.
He just walked past. Towards the connecting door. Towards the next carriage.
Thomas let out a breath he didn’t know he was holding. A long, shuddering exhale.
Just a passenger, he thought. Just a coincidence. Just paranoia. Just a man in a grey coat going to the toilet.
But as the man reached the connecting door, he stopped. Turned his head. Just slightly. Just enough.
‘Carlisle is a long way, Tom,’ he said. Voice low. Gravelly. Not Olesya’s voice. But familiar. Like a voice from a dream. Like his own voice, recorded and played back. ‘You might not make it.’
Then he pushed the door open. Stepped through. Gone.
Thomas stood there. Trembling. Violently.
He knew my name.
How?
Had Thomas said it aloud? Had he muttered it in his sleep? Had he written it on his forehead?
Or was the man part of the script? An actor planted in the play, hired to deliver the line, to break the fourth wall and remind him that he was the protagonist of a tragedy?
The train slowed. Brakes squealing. A high-pitched shriek that set his teeth on edge, vibrating through his skull.
Peterborough.
The doors hissed open. Pneumatic sigh.
A rush of cold air. Noise. Voices. Announcements.
‘Mind the gap…’
Thomas stepped onto the platform.
The crowd swallowed him immediately. Commuters rushing to connect. Families reuniting. Tourists confused by maps. A sea of faces, none of them looking at him.
He merged into the flow. Kept his head down. Clutched his bag tight against his chest, protecting it, protecting himself.
He didn’t look back. Didn’t check if the man in the grey coat was following. He couldn’t. To look was to invite the gaze.
He just walked. Towards the exit. Towards the street. Towards the unknown.
He needed to lose the trail. Change direction. Buy another ticket. Go somewhere else. Anywhere else. Leeds. Newcastle. Timbuktu. It didn’t matter. Just move.
He reached the ticket hall. A vast space of echoing announcements and flashing boards. High ceilings. Acoustic tiles.
Departures. Arrivals. Cancelled. Delayed.
Chaos. Perfect cover.
He headed for the machines again. The same ritual. The same desperation.
Destination:
He typed: Newcastle. No. Too obvious.
Edinburgh. No. Too far.
Somewhere random. Somewhere small. Somewhere off the grid.
Leeds.
He fed in more coins. Clunk. Clunk. Clunk. The sound of money buying time.
The machine whirred. Printed the ticket. A small slip of paper. No name. Just a time. A platform. A seat.
He took it. Folded it. Put it in his pocket.
He turned to leave.
And stopped.
His heart skipped a beat. Stopped. Restarted.
On a bench near the exit, sitting calmly, was the man in the grey coat.
He wasn’t walking. He wasn’t chasing. He hadn’t taken the stairs. He hadn’t run.
He was waiting.
He had the black umbrella across his knees. His hands folded on top of it. Patient. Eternal.
He looked up as Thomas approached.
Smiled.
A small, tight smile. No warmth. No humanity. Just recognition.
‘Wrong train, Tom,’ the man said. Loud enough for nearby passengers to hear. But they didn’t react. They walked past, oblivious. Blind. Deaf. ‘You’re already there.’
Thomas backed away. Heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped bird, beating its wings against the bone cage.
‘Who are you?’ he shouted. His voice cracked. Echoed in the high ceiling, bouncing off the tiles. ‘Who sent you?’
Passengers glanced at him. Frowned. Moved away. Giving him space. The space of the madman.
The man didn’t answer. Just pointed.
Pointed at the departure board. The giant screen hanging from the ceiling.
Thomas looked up.
The board flickered. Changed. Glitched.
All the destinations vanished. The times. The platforms. The companies.
Replaced by a single line of text. Over and over. Row after row. Filling the screen.
THOMAS WARD. DELETE COMPLETE.
THOMAS WARD. DELETE COMPLETE.
THOMAS WARD. DELETE COMPLETE.
Green text on a black background. Terminal code. Final verdict.
He stared. Blinking. Rubbing his eyes. Pinching the bridge of his nose.
Was it real? Or another glitch? Another projection? Another layer of the simulation?
He looked back at the bench.
Empty.
The man was gone.
The umbrella was gone.
Just an empty bench. And the departure board screaming his erasure to the world.
Delete Complete.
But he was standing there. Breathing. Feeling the cold floor through his shoes. Feeling the sweat on his brow.
If he was deleted… who was this?
Who was feeling the panic? Who was hearing the announcements? Who was holding the ticket to Leeds?
A ghost? A glitch? A residual file?
‘The train now standing at platform 4 is the 14:00 service to Leeds…’
He turned. Ran.
Out of the station. Into the street.
The city was grey. Industrial. Unfamiliar. Brick buildings. Steel girders. Rain starting to fall. Light. Misty. Coating everything in a slick film.
He walked. Fast. Aimless. Legs pumping. Lungs burning.
He needed to hide. To find a corner. To curl up and wait for the deletion to finish. To wait for the system to purge him.
He turned a corner. Found an alleyway. Narrow. Dark. Smelling of bins and wet brick. Urine. Rotting cardboard.
He stepped in. Leaned against the wall. Slid down to the ground.
Sat in the damp. In the dirt.
Opened his bag.
Needed to check. Needed to know what was inside. Needed to find the source of the infection.
He unzipped it. Hands shaking.
Looked inside.
No cash. No envelope. The three hundred pounds. Gone. Vanished.
Just a phone.
Black. Slim. Unknown model. No logo. No brand.
And a piece of paper.
He picked up the paper. Unfolded it. Wet fingers tearing the edge.
Handwritten. Familiar handwriting. Loopy. Sharp. Aggressive.
Olesya.
The note read:
‘Phase Three: Displacement.
You left the flat. You left the city. But you didn’t leave the system.
You are still Thomas Ward. Still trackable. Still mine.
To delete yourself, you must stop running.
Stop being the data.
Become the noise.
Come home, Tom.
The stains are waiting.’
He dropped the paper.
It landed in a puddle. Ink bleeding. Running. Smearing into illegibility.
Come home.
Home? To the flat? To the ten stains? To the broken smoke detector?
Or was home wherever she was? Wherever the signal was strongest?
He looked at the phone.
It buzzed.
Screen lit up. Bright. Blinding.
Unknown Number.
He stared at it.
Didn’t answer.
Let it buzz.
Once. Twice. Three times.
It stopped.
Silence.
Then a message appeared. Pop-up. Bold.
‘See you soon.’
He threw the phone against the brick wall.
Hard. Violent.
It shattered. Plastic and glass scattering into the puddle.
Sparks. Smoke. A small explosion of lithium.
Dead.
He sat there. Shaking. Convulsing.
Rain falling harder now. Soaking his clothes. Dripping from his hair.
He closed his eyes.
Become the noise.
How?
How do you become noise when you are the signal? How do you disappear when you are the centre of the universe?
He didn’t know.
He just sat.
Waiting.
For the next instruction.
For the next phase.
For the end.
The rain washed over him. Cold. Relentless.
Washing away the dirt. The sweat. The fear?
No. Not the fear.
The fear was internal. Permanent. Etched into his DNA.
He opened his eyes.
Looked at his hands.
They were clean now. Washed by the rain.
But the lines…
Had they changed again?
He couldn’t tell.
He just sat there.
In the alley.
In the rain.
In the noise.
Waiting to become nobody.
Waiting for the system to crash.
Waiting for the screen to go black.
Waiting for the final delete.
But the screen stayed on.
And the rain kept falling.
And Thomas Ward sat in the mud, deleted and complete, existing and erased, a ghost in the machine, a signal in the noise, a man under another name, or no name at all.
Just access.
Always access.
