Chapter 19

The word stayed where she had left it.

Received.

For three days Thomas did not write again.

He kept the app open in the background and let it show the last thing Olesya had given him. He moved through the Delhi flat, through office calls, through supplier notes and vehicle arrivals, through Sarah's packing for London, with the word resting beneath everything like a coin under a plate.

The app showed no new typing mark.

No missed call.

No photograph.

No small correction from Baku, no complaint about bank fees, no line from Anya using her mother's phone. Only the receipt, the delivery tick, the finished transaction.

Sarah left for London on the fourth morning.

Her suitcase stood beside the hall table at six, zipped and tagged. A black coat lay folded over the handle, absurd in the Delhi heat. She had dressed for the aircraft rather than the city: dark trousers, white shirt, scarf light enough to pass for decoration and warm enough to survive Heathrow. The driver waited downstairs, engine running too long in the compound until the guard knocked on the bonnet and told him to switch it off.

'You have the dinner with Rajiv on Thursday,' Sarah said.

'I know.'

'And the engineer from Stavanger arrives Friday.'

'Yes.'

She looked at him over the open lid of her handbag.

'You are saying yes too quickly.'

He checked the time on his watch.

'I have it in the calendar.'

'That is not the same thing.'

The driver rang once from downstairs. Sarah ignored it. She put her passport into the inside pocket of the handbag, then took it out again and checked the photo page, the visa pages, the boarding pass folded behind the cover.

'How long is London?'

'Six nights.'

'I thought four.'

'I told you six.'

He must have nodded. He had no memory of it.

Sarah closed the handbag.

'Try to sleep properly.'

'I will.'

'And eat something that is not from the office canteen.'

'All right.'

She waited, perhaps for him to step forward, perhaps only for the lift bell that had not yet sounded. Then she lifted the scarf from the suitcase handle and wrapped it once around her neck.

'Goodbye, Thomas.'

'Message when you land.'

'I always do.'

The door shut behind her with the soft hydraulic pull of expensive hinges.

The flat changed at once.

Her perfume remained near the hall mirror. The air-conditioning pushed it into the living room, where it thinned among the smells of cooled dust, old coffee, and the lemon cleaner the housekeeper had used on the table. A single earring back lay near the ceramic dish. He saw it only after the lift had taken her down.

He picked it up and placed it in the dish.

Then he opened the secure app.

Received.

He wrote:

Anything else?

The message delivered.

No answer.

By late afternoon he had checked the app eleven times. On the twelfth, the ticks turned blue.

Read.

No reply.

He placed the phone face down, then turned it over again. The blue ticks remained. There was a particular insult in their neatness, in the little mark the system gave when it could prove a message had arrived and leave untouched what came after. He had paid Murad. Murad had paid someone else. Someone had paid Olesya's bank or card or hand. The system had closed. She had seen the question and left it open.

He made coffee in the kitchen and forgot to put a cup under the machine.

The first run splashed into the drip tray, dark and thin, then overflowed onto the counter. He stood watching it reach the edge and fall in slow drops to the floor. The smell filled the kitchen with burned bitterness.

He cleaned it with paper towels.

The towels came away brown.

The next transfer was due on the eleventh.

He began the process early, partly to avoid the bank holiday Murad had warned him about, partly to give the money more time to surface if one of the pieces stalled. The sequence had become physical by then. Laptop open. Exchange account. Card code. Wallet address copied into a blank file. First six characters. Last six. Paste. Check again. Small send. Confirmation. Larger send. Screenshot. Message to Murad. Message to Olesya only after the broker had proof.

He did it from the dining table whilst rain gathered over Delhi and refused to break.

The sky lowered all afternoon. The compound trees turned their leaves pale side out. Air sat against the balcony glass. Down below, the guard dragged a chair beneath the overhang, then dragged it back out again when no rain came. The city seemed to wait without moving.

At 17:42 Murad wrote:

Paid.

He sent a screenshot. Blurred bank interface. Olesya's initials. Amount. Time stamp.

Thomas forwarded the amount to Olesya.

Sent.

No reply.

At 21:10 he wrote:

Confirm when received.

The message delivered.

No answer.

He ate at the table with the laptop open. Rice from a foil container, chicken gone soft in a sauce that had separated. Sarah had left two yoghurts in the fridge. He ate one standing by the sink and rinsed the spoon straight after.

At midnight the ticks turned blue.

Read.

No reply.

He called.

The app rang six times and ended.

He called again.

Declined.

He looked at the word until the screen dimmed.

The next morning he wrote:

Did the money arrive?

Blue ticks after seven minutes.

No reply.

Murad confirmed again:

Paid yesterday. Ask bank.

Thomas sent:

Need proof. Recipient not confirming.

Murad answered with a voice note.

Thomas did not play it. He wrote:

Text only.

A long pause.

Then:

Brother, paid. I cannot make woman answer phone.

Thomas set the phone down.

Outside, Delhi finally broke open. Rain struck the balcony in hard sheets, turned the opposite block into a grey wall, filled the gutters so quickly that water began to leap from the roof corners. The compound path vanished beneath a shallow skin. Gardeners ran for cover under a stairwell. A plastic chair tipped over and travelled a little before lodging against a drain.

His phone lay silent on the table.

He spent the day inside a pressure he could not name. The office call at ten ran through valve specifications. At eleven, a procurement dispute over storage charges. At twelve, a Norwegian engineer asked whether Delhi was always this loud in the background and laughed at his own line. Thomas answered each question. He made decisions. He corrected one calculation in a load sheet. He rejected a supplier deviation with two sentences.

The app stayed open beside the work.

Read.

No reply.

By the second day, the silence had shape.

It was not the old withdrawal, not the flat economy of Received. This silence had a boundary around it. Olesya had seen the messages. She had declined a call. She had not asked for more. She had not accused him. She had given him nothing to repair.

He began writing messages and deleting them.

Are you all right?

Deleted.

Please answer.

Deleted.

I need to know if the transfer arrived.

Deleted.

On the third night, Sarah called from London.

He had forgotten the time difference and answered with the laptop still open on the broker spreadsheet. It was early evening there, late in Delhi. Behind her, through the hotel room window, he could see the dull London sky and the pale reflection of a lamp.

'You look awful,' she said.

'Bad light.'

'It is always bad light with you now.'

He moved the phone away from the spreadsheet.

'How is London?'

'Wet. Efficient. Overpriced.'

'Meetings?'

'Useful enough.'

She was sitting on a hotel bed with her shoes still on. A room-service tray rested on the desk behind her, silver lid lifted, a folded napkin beside it. Visible life. Delivered food. Receipts that matched the room.

'What is happening?' she asked.

'Work.'

'Thomas.'

He rubbed his thumb along the edge of the phone case.

'Baku has gone quiet.'

Sarah's face changed only in the eyes. The rest held.

'How long?'

'Three days.'

'After a payment?'

'Yes.'

'Can you contact the daughter?'

'I have an old email.'

'Use it.'

'She is a child.'

'No, she is not.'

The correction struck cleanly. Anya had moved through the years whilst he had kept her fixed in school fees, asthma medicine, exam registration, shoes, winter clothes, all the categories that let a person remain smaller in the ledger than in life.

Sarah shifted on the bed. The tray rattled softly behind her.

'Use the email,' she said.

He did not answer.

'Thomas.'

'Yes.'

'Use it tonight.'

After the call ended, he searched his archive.

Anya's email sat inside an old school invoice from Baku, copied into the payment note field. He had used it once to send a scanned textbook when Olesya's connection failed during lockdown. The address had a string of numbers after her name. He copied it into a new message.

Subject:

Your mother.

He deleted the subject.

Subject:

Checking in.

He deleted that too.

In the end he left the subject blank.

Anya,

I have not been able to reach your mother. Please tell me if everything is in order.

Thomas.

He looked at the email for several minutes before sending it.

The reply arrived at 03:17.

He had fallen asleep at the table with the laptop open. The sound woke him. Not fully. Enough to lift his head and see the blue glow of the screen, the empty yoghurt pot near his elbow, the balcony glass gone black with his own reflection.

The email was from Anya.

Mr Thomas,

My mother is in hospital. Her heart. They took her first to state hospital. Now she is in intensive care. I did not know if I should write to you.

A.

He read it once.

Then again.

The words did not move.

He stood and the chair fell backwards onto the floor.

The sound reached the bedroom, then returned from it unchanged. Sarah was not there. The flat had no one in it to answer the noise.

He lifted the chair and sat down.

Which hospital?

He sent the email, then opened the secure app and called Olesya.

No answer.

He called again.

No answer.

He wrote to Anya:

Which hospital? Who is the doctor? What do they need?

The reply came after nine minutes.

State hospital first. Now cardiology centre. I am in corridor. They say no visitors at night. I will send name.

A photograph arrived.

It showed a corridor under greenish light. A row of plastic chairs bolted together. A vending machine with no water in the front row. A door at the end with a sign in Azerbaijani and Russian. The floor had been mopped recently, and the wet marks caught the fluorescent strips in long broken lines. In the lower corner of the image, Anya's shoe appeared. White trainer, scuffed near the toe.

No Olesya.

Thomas enlarged the door sign until the letters broke apart.

Anya sent the hospital name.

He searched it. State-run cardiology unit, private wing attached, mixed reviews, an address near a part of Baku he had driven through once without stopping. Photographs online showed a low building with narrow windows, a cracked step, a flag mounted above the entrance. Another image showed a polished reception desk in the private wing, bright enough to belong to a different country.

He called the number on the website.

The first call failed.

The second rang into a switchboard where a woman answered in Azerbaijani. Thomas asked for English. The line rustled, then another woman came on, older by the sound of her breathing, each word placed with care.

'Patient name.'

He gave Olesya's full name.

'Relation?'

The question arrived before he had prepared for it. It kept arriving. Bank, platform, card desk, hospital. A small gate, always the same size, always set at the point where money stopped being enough.

'I am paying for her care.'

'Relation?'

'I am overseas contact.'

'Family?'

He looked at the blank wall above the dining table. Sarah had wanted to buy a print for it. Something local, she had said. Something with colour. The wall remained empty.

'I am the contact.'

'We speak family only.'

'Her daughter is there. Anya.'

'Daughter can speak doctor morning.'

'Can you confirm she is alive?'

The line went quiet.

Not silence. He could hear movement near the receiver, a drawer closing, a voice further away, wheels crossing a hard floor.

'Patient in intensive care,' the woman said at last.

'Stable?'

'Doctor morning.'

'What does she need now?'

'Doctor morning.'

Then the line ended.

Thomas kept the phone to his ear after the call had gone dead. The flat around him seemed to have enlarged. The dining table, the laptop, the yoghurt pot, Sarah's empty chair, all of it suddenly too arranged, too ready for a life that could prove itself on request.

Anya wrote again.

They say I cannot stand near door.

Where are you now?

On stairs.

Send picture.

The photograph showed concrete steps with a metal rail painted brown. The paint had worn off where hands had touched it for years. A paper cup lay crushed near the wall. Someone had written a phone number in black marker beside a patch of flaking plaster. Anya's bag sat between her feet, unzipped, a charger cable hanging from one pocket.

He wrote:

Do you have food?

She answered:

Later.

Now?

No.

Buy something.

I don't want leave.

Ask nurse if there is cafeteria.

She did not reply for twelve minutes.

Then:

Tea only.

Buy tea. Stay where lights are.

The instruction sounded foolish after he sent it. Stay where lights are. She was eighteen, alone on hospital stairs, and he was telling her where to stand from a dining table in Delhi.

He opened a map of the hospital area and looked for cafes, pharmacies, ATMs. He marked them in a note and sent the names to Anya, each one with distance from the hospital entrance. She replied to none of them.

He wrote:

Can they move her to private wing?

They say deposit first. But doctor says not now. Maybe morning.

How much?

Anya sent the number.

Then:

There is also money owed downstairs.

What money?

A pause.

Mr Thomas, the men came again.

Which men?

Debt men.

He sat very still.

The rain had stopped. Somewhere outside, water dripped from an awning in steady intervals. The air-conditioning hummed above the silence.

Anya wrote again:

They found the flat. They knew when money came. Mama paid them. Then more came. They said old interest. They said they will come to school.

Thomas read the lines with both hands on the table.

The debt had not been outside the system.

It had been waiting inside it.

He asked her to send what she had.

The first photograph was of a folded paper pushed under a door. The edges were softened from being handled. The writing was in Russian and Azerbaijani, numbers circled, dates beneath them, a signature that looked less like a name than a scratch made with a blunt instrument.

The second was a message from an unknown number.

Today.

No delay.

School know address.

The third was a photograph of their living room taken from the doorway. Not the whole room. A corner. The place where the television had been, now marked by a rectangle of cleaner wall and two loose cables hanging down. On the floor beneath it sat a small bowl, a slipper, and a stack of school papers tied with string.

Thomas remembered the first time he had entered that flat in Baku. Olesya standing by the kitchen counter with her hands damp from washing cups. Anya smaller then, watching him with the solemn suspicion of a child trained not to ask adult questions. The television had been on low volume, a Russian cartoon or music channel, something bright that filled the room without entering it. He had thought of it as a poor object, not part of their breathing.

Now the wall showed where it had been taken.

The fourth photograph was not meant for him. Anya sent it and then wrote:

Sorry.

It showed Olesya from behind, months earlier perhaps, standing at the flat door in a cardigan, one hand on the handle, the other pressed flat against the metal to keep it shut. The chain was on. A man's shoe was visible beyond the threshold.

Thomas enlarged the photograph until the door grain broke into squares.

The shoe remained.

He wrote:

Who took this?

Me.

Why did you not tell me?

Mama said no.

Why?

She said you send and then men know.

He looked back at the spreadsheet.

The months were arranged without protest. Each transfer had landed and announced itself somewhere. Each arrival had put a mark on the water for people he had not seen.

He typed:

How did they know?

I don't know.

Then:

Maybe bank. Maybe neighbour. Maybe they watch.

The answer covered every route and no route. It was not a solvable fault. It was weather.

He typed:

How much has she paid them?

I don't know. Many times.

From the money I sent?

Yes.

The word landed without force. That made it worse. Yes. No defence. No apology. No attempt to soften the shape of it.

He opened the broker spreadsheet. Month after month arranged in rows. Rent. School. Utilities. Medical. Winter clothes. Exam registration. Emergency. Each label had been clean. Each label had hidden a mouth behind it, open and taking.

He wrote:

Does your mother owe them now?

Yes.

How much?

Anya sent a number, then another line.

Maybe more. They change.

He stood and went to the kitchen.

The tiles were cold under his feet. He opened the fridge and looked inside. Sarah's yoghurts. Bottled water. Half a lime in cling film. A glass container of rice he would not eat. The light inside the fridge made everything appear chosen, stored, preserved.

He closed it.

His phone vibrated on the table.

Doctor says medicines need bought outside. Pharmacy near hospital. I have list.

Send photograph.

The photograph came at an angle. A doctor's handwriting, pharmacy stamps, names he did not know, dosages, ampoules, one line circled twice. At the bottom, the hospital stamp cut into the page in blue ink.

He copied the medicine names into a search window and found little that helped.

He tried the hospital again.

This time a man answered, then transferred him to another extension where no one picked up. On the third attempt he reached someone in the private wing. The English was better there. The voice belonged to a receptionist, young, formal, reading from a line he had used often enough himself.

'For admission we require deposit and passport copy.'

'The patient is already in intensive care.'

'For transfer to private wing, deposit and passport copy.'

'Her daughter is there.'

'Daughter can present documents.'

'Can I pay directly by card?'

'Payment link only local.'

'Bank transfer?'

'Takes time.'

'She does not have time.'

'Sir, I am reception.'

The line held that last sentence between them. Not refusal. Boundary. He had reached a person whose job was to say where her job ended.

'What can be done now?'

'Medicines from outside pharmacy. Doctor morning. Deposit when office opens.'

'Can you tell the doctor payment is arranged?'

'Sir, I am reception.'

He thanked her.

After the call, he wrote the hospital's bank name, account number, and opening time in the notepad. The page already held exchange rates, card limits, Murad's fee, Anya's card number, the medicine amount, the debt number with a question mark beside it. In the margin, without planning to, he had drawn a small box around the word relation.

He tore the page out, folded it once, then unfolded it again.

How much?

Anya sent a photograph of a calculator screen.

He replied:

I am sending money to you directly. Your account.

I have only student card.

Send details.

It may not take international.

Send details.

The details arrived as a photograph of a card held against denim. Anya's thumb covered part of the number. The nail was bitten down to the quick. She sent a second photograph without him asking.

He opened every route he had.

The European platform was closed.

The bank transfer route still held its old refusal.

The broker would deposit, but only to Olesya's saved card unless he paid a higher commission for a new beneficiary.

The crypto exchange wanted review for a larger purchase.

His secondary account balance sat lower than he had expected. Fees had taken their own share for months. Sarah's London trip had not touched it, yet the number looked thinned, rubbed down by repeated charges.

He wrote to Murad:

New beneficiary. Student card. Urgent hospital. Amount large.

Murad answered:

New card 15 fee. Hospital no problem. Send.

Thomas wrote:

Need full paid today.

Then send fast.

He bought USDT with the credit card. Declined.

He opened the bank app.

Fraud warning.

He confirmed.

Tried again.

Declined.

He called the fraud desk and listened to music at 03:52, standing barefoot in the living room while the balcony glass cooled from the night rain. The agent asked him to confirm the last three transactions.

'Yes.'

'And this new attempted transaction is also yours?'

'Yes.'

'It is a high-risk merchant category.'

'Clear it.'

'I need the purpose of transaction.'

He looked at Anya's photograph of the hospital corridor.

'Medical support.'

'For yourself?'

'No.'

'For a family member?'

The word had become a toll booth.

'For a dependent.'

The agent typed.

'Relationship?'

Thomas pressed his fingers against the bridge of his nose.

'Personal dependent.'

'I will record medical support to dependent.'

'Clear it.'

'The hold is removed. Please allow ten minutes before retrying.'

The purchase went through.

He sent Murad the first tranche. Then the second. The third stuck pending network confirmation. He sent Anya the transaction plan in short lines, each amount and expected arrival.

She replied:

I am at pharmacy.

Then:

They want cash.

Murad can send cash pickup.

No men.

Bank then.

Pharmacy wants cash.

Thomas looked at the screen.

Where are you?

She sent a location pin.

Female recipient at pharmacy. No meeting in car. No private place. Cash handover inside pharmacy or bank deposit only.

Murad replied:

You want rules, use bank.

Inside pharmacy.

Fee 20.

Fine.

The word came too quickly. Fine. Twenty percent to place cash across a counter in a city where a young woman waited with a medicine list and a mother behind a closed door.

He sent the amount.

Murad wrote:

Thirty minutes.

At 04:41 Anya wrote:

A man called. He said outside pharmacy.

Inside only.

I told.

Wait near counter.

I am.

Her messages arrived without the formal Mr Thomas now. The corridor had stripped that away.

At 04:58:

Got cash.

Buy medicines now.

I am.

Nurse took.

At 05:23:

Doctor says wait.

Thomas sat with the laptop open, both phones on the table, charger cables crossing over Sarah's place mat. Dawn lifted behind the curtains. The compound began in fragments: a gate chain, a broom on wet concrete, a kettle in another flat, the first horn from the road beyond the trees.

He called Sarah at 05:31 Delhi time, 01:01 in London.

She answered on the fourth ring.

'What happened?'

Her voice was thick with sleep, but the question was already awake.

'Olesya is in hospital.'

Sarah sat up. The hotel lamp came on. Its yellow light cut across her face.

'How bad?'

'Cardiac. Intensive care.'

'Are you going?'

He had not asked himself that yet. The word going opened too many doors at once: visa, flights, company notice, Sarah, the hospital corridor, Anya waiting with a phone in her hand, Olesya behind a door he had no right to enter and every claim to pay for.

'I don't know.'

Sarah closed her eyes once, then opened them.

'Is Anya alone?'

'Yes.'

'Then treat her as the person in front of you.'

'I am sending money.'

'That is not what I said.'

He looked at the table. Four cables. Two devices. The old notepad full of numbers. The ceramic dish holding Sarah's earring back.

'It is what I can do from here.'

Sarah was silent.

From London came the distant sound of a door closing in a corridor, someone rolling luggage over carpet, the hotel continuing around her.

'What did the money do?' she asked.

He looked up.

'What?'

'Before hospital. What did it do?'

He did not answer.

'Thomas.'

'Debt collectors.'

The word entered the room and stayed there.

'How much?'

'I don't know.'

'But your money?'

'Yes.'

Sarah pressed her hand over her mouth, then lowered it.

'And now?'

'Now I am sending more.'

Neither of them spoke for a while.

'I have to go,' he said.

'No,' Sarah said. 'You have to decide what going means.'

He ended the call before the line could ask anything else of him.

Anya wrote at 06:12.

They let me see her for one minute.

Some.

The number in the secondary account went close to zero.

He sent the money through Murad in two pieces and a bank deposit directly to Anya's card. The fees looked grotesque by then, too large to trust, too regular to be accidental. He stopped calculating them.

At eight, the office called.

He let it ring.

Thomas wrote:

Unavailable.

Medical emergency.

The project manager replied with a thumbs-up and no question.

Deposit.

Private wing pending.

Doctor says wait.

Hospital_Deposit_Anya.

Receipt.

Where husband.

Thomas sat in the Delhi flat with the air-conditioning running and the curtains half drawn against the morning heat. Outside, the compound had dried in patches. Gardeners swept leaves into wet heaps.
