Chapter 13
The decision did not arrive as a thunderclap. It was a quiet settling, like dust finding its level in a sealed room, invisible until a shaft of light caught it swirling. Thomas stood in the centre of the living area, the grey dawn light stripping the walls of any pretence of warmth, leaving them bare and accusing. He looked at the ten stains on the floor. They were no longer a map of his failure; they were anchors. Heavy, dark weights holding him to a version of himself that no longer existed, dragging him down into the laminate, into the foundations of the building, into the earth itself.
Delete yourself, Olesya had said. Cease to exist.
He had spent weeks interpreting it as a metaphor. A psychological dismantling. A shedding of habits. But looking at the tenth stain, the one he hadn’t made, the one that proved his memory was leaking like a faulty pipe, he realized she had been literal. She wanted the data point removed. She wanted the user account closed. She wanted the file corrupted beyond recovery.
Thomas Ward was the problem. As long as he was Thomas Ward, he was trackable. He was predictable. He was hers. The name was a tag. A barcode. A beacon broadcasting his location to anyone with the right receiver.
He moved to the bedroom. The air inside was stale, trapped between the wardrobe and the unmade bed. He opened the wardrobe doors. They creaked, a high-pitched whine of metal on metal. Inside, his life hung in neat rows. Suits for work he no longer cared about. Shirts ironed to a crispness that felt like armour. Jeans folded with military precision. He ran a hand over the sleeve of a navy blazer. It felt foreign. Like skin that didn’t belong to him.
He took out a duffel bag from the top shelf. Old canvas, smelling of damp and forgotten holidays. He unzipped it. It lay open on the bed, a black mouth waiting to be fed.
He did not pack clothes. What was the point? Clothes were identifiers. Styles. Sizes. Brands. He left the suits. He left the shirts. He left the shoes lined up by the door, their laces tied in perfect loops. Let them rot. Let the landlord deal with the mess. Let the next tenant wonder about the stains on the floor, about the man who left his shoes behind.
He did not pack the spreadsheet. The laptop was buried under a pile of wires in the corner, a tomb of technology he no longer trusted. He did not pack the photos. The physical ones were in a box under the bed. He left them too. Memories were traps. Evidence.
He took only what could not be traced back to a history: cash. Withdrawn in small amounts over the last month. Twenty pounds here. Forty there. Stuffed into an envelope in the back of a drawer, behind old tax returns and expired warranties. He pulled the drawer open. It stuck for a moment, then slid free with a groan. He reached into the back. His fingers brushed the paper. Crisp. Dry.
He pulled out the envelope. Tore it open. Counted the notes on the bedspread. One. Two. Five. Ten. Twenty. Fifty. Three hundred pounds. Small notes. Mostly tens and twenties. Anonymous. Untraceable. Paper ghosts.
He put the envelope in the bag. Zipped it shut. The sound was loud in the silence. Zzzzip. Like a wound closing.
He walked to the kitchen. The jar of coffee sat on the counter. The one she had bought. Proper coffee, Tom. None of that instant sludge. He picked it up. Unscrewed the lid. Smelled the bitter dust. It smelled of mornings that were gone. Of conversations that had ended. Of a life that was over.
He walked to the sink. Poured the coffee down the drain. The granules tumbled out, brown and dry, hitting the stainless steel with a soft rattling sound. He turned on the tap. Cold water rushed out, swirling the coffee away, washing it down into the pipes, into the sewers, into the dark underground of the city. Goodbye, Tom. None of that instant sludge.
He rinsed the jar. Left it on the draining board. Wet. Dripping. Another mark. Another trace. He didn’t dry it. Let it drip. Let it stain the steel.
He picked up the bag. Slung it over his shoulder. The weight was negligible. Three hundred pounds and a canvas bag. Lighter than his guilt. Lighter than his fear.
He walked to the front door. The hallway was narrow, shadows pooling in the corners. He stopped at the door. Key in the lock. He paused. His hand trembled. Not from fear. From the sheer physical resistance of the act. To leave was to admit defeat. To leave was to validate her experiment. It was to say: You win. I am running. But to stay was to be consumed. To stay was to let the stains multiply until they covered everything. Until the floor was just one big black pool of nothing.
He turned the key. Click.
The sound was final. A period at the end of a long, rambling sentence. A sentence that had started years ago and had finally run out of words.
He stepped out into the stairwell. The smell of boiled cabbage and damp concrete rushed up to meet him. It was the smell of the building. Of other people. Of a world that continued regardless of his internal collapse. He walked down. One flight. Two flights. Three. He did not look back at the door of flat 4B. He didn't want to see it one last time. He didn't want to give it the satisfaction. He pushed open the street door and stepped into the morning.
The city was awake. Busy. Indifferent. The rain had stopped, leaving the pavements slick and reflective, mirroring the grey sky above. Cars hissed past, tyres spraying fine mist. Buses rumbled, exhaling clouds of diesel. Thomas merged into the flow of commuters. Heads down. Phones out. Eyes scanning screens. Blue light reflecting in dead eyes. He kept his head up. He watched the faces. Pale. Tired. Masked. He watched the cameras mounted on the corners of buildings. Black domes. Red lights blinking. Recording. Archiving. Storing.
He felt a spike of adrenaline. Cold. Sharp. They see me, he thought. The system sees me. Every step he took was logged. Every face he passed was scanned. Every camera turned to follow him. He was a dot moving on a map. A blip on a screen. Data in motion.
He walked faster. Towards the station. King’s Cross. A hub of movement. A place where identities were swallowed and regurgitated every hour. Trains to Edinburgh. To Manchester. To Leeds. To nowhere. A place where you could disappear into the crowd. Or so he hoped.
He reached the main road. The traffic was heavy. A river of metal and glass. He waited for the light to change. Red. Green. He crossed. The pavement was slippery. He stumbled slightly. Caught himself on a lamppost. Cold metal against his palm. Rough. Real.
A notification sound chimed nearby. Sharp. Distinct. The specific tone of a message received.
He flinched. Stopped. Looked around.
A woman walking past him lowered her phone. She glanced at him. Her eyes widened slightly. A flicker of recognition? Or just surprise at his sudden movement? Then she looked away, hurrying on, pulling her coat tighter.
Did she know him? Did she see an alert on her screen? Thomas Ward detected. Sector 4. Proximity alert. Was his face broadcast now? Was he on a list? A digital wanted poster?
Paranoia clawed at his throat. Bile rose in his stomach. He pulled his collar up. Covered his chin. Kept walking. Faster. Shoulders hunched. Trying to make himself smaller. Invisible.
He reached the station entrance. The vast glass façade reflected the sky, the crowds, him. A thousand Thomases staring back. A legion of ghosts. He stopped. Stared at his reflection.
It looked wrong. The face was his, but the eyes were empty. Hollow sockets filled with static. The mouth was a grim line. The hair was messy. He looked like a man who had been running for a long time. A man who was about to fall.
Delete yourself. The words echoed in his head. Louder now.
He stepped towards the doors. They slid open with a soft hiss. Warm air hit him, smelling of stale coffee, diesel, and human sweat. The smell of transit. Of leaving.
Inside, the concourse was a cathedral of noise. Announcements echoed, distorted and overlapping, bouncing off the high arched ceiling. The train now standing at platform 8 is the 09:15 service to York… Passengers are reminded to keep their belongings with them at all times… Do not leave bags unattended… The voices were disembodied. God-like. Commanding.
He moved through the crowd. Shoulders brushing. Bags knocking against legs. Feet stomping. Wheels rattling. He felt invisible. Just another body in the stream. A drop in the ocean.
He headed for the ticket machines. Not the counter. Counters had people. People asked questions. People checked IDs. People looked you in the eye. Machines were cold. Impersonal. They didn't care who you were. They just wanted your money.
The machine screen glowed. Blue. Bright. Welcome. Please select your destination.
He stared at the list. Cities. Towns. Villages. All connected. All part of the network. All nodes in the same web.
Where does a ghost go? Somewhere off the grid. Somewhere small. Somewhere the signal was weak.
He typed: Carlisle. A stop on the way to nowhere. A border town. A place to vanish.
Please insert payment.
He fed in the coins. One by one. Clunk. Clunk. Clunk. The sound was heavy. Final. Each coin was a piece of his old life buying his way out.
The machine whirred. Gears grinding. Printed the ticket. A small slip of paper. Warm. No name. Just a time. A platform. A seat number. Seat 42. Coach C.
He took it. Folded it. Put it in his pocket. Deep. Safe.
He turned to leave the machine.
And froze.
Ten metres away, standing by a pillar, was a man. Wearing a grey coat. Holding a black umbrella, though it wasn't raining.
Looking directly at him.
Thomas's heart hammered. A frantic bird against his ribs. Security? Police? Him?
The man didn't move. Didn't speak. Just watched. His face was blank. Featureless. Like a mannequin.
Thomas looked away. Forced his legs to move. Walked towards the escalators. Down to the platforms.
He could feel the gaze on his back. Burning. Heavy. Physical. Like a hand pressing between his shoulder blades.
He stepped onto the escalator. Moved down. The steps clattered. A rhythmic mechanical sound.
He looked back.
The man was gone.
Had he imagined it? Was the surveillance inside his head now? Projecting watchers onto empty pillars? Was the man even real? Or just a glitch in his own perception?
He gripped the handrail. Cold metal. Real. Smooth.
Keep moving, he told himself. Don't stop. Don't think. Thinking is dangerous. Thinking is where she lives.
He reached the platform. The train was there. Silver. Sleek. Long. Doors open. A snake waiting to swallow him.
He boarded. Found a seat near the back. Window side.
He sat down. Placed the bag on his lap. Held it tight.
The train was half full. Businessmen typing on laptops. Screens glowing. Students with headphones, eyes closed, lost in music. A mother soothing a crying baby, rocking back and forth.
Normal life. Continuing without him. Unaware of him.
He looked out the window. The platform slid away. Slowly at first. Then faster. The station receded. The lights blurred. The city dissolved into grey smudges.
He was leaving.
He was doing it.
He let out a breath he didn't know he was holding. His shoulders dropped. His hands unclenched slightly.
Then his phone buzzed.
He jumped. Heart stopping. Looked down.
He had left the phone in the flat. Hadn't he? Yes. On the table. Face down. Dead. A brick.
He patted his pocket. Empty.
The buzz came again. From his bag.
He froze. Blood turning to ice.
He hadn't packed a phone. He had left it. He was sure.
He opened the bag. Slowly. Trembling. Fingers numb.
Inside, nestled against the envelope of cash, was a phone.
Black. Slim. Unknown model. No logo.
It buzzed again. The screen lit up. Bright. Blinding.
Unknown Number.
He stared at it. How? When?
Had she planted it while he slept? Had she slipped it into his bag in the stairwell? Or was it never his bag? Was the bag itself a prop in her play? Had he ever really left the flat? Was this all a simulation?
He picked it up. Cold. Heavy. Vibrating in his hand.
He swiped to answer. Put it to his ear.
'Hello?' His voice was a whisper. Cracked.
Silence.
Then, a click.
'You're moving, Tom,' Olesya's voice said. Clear. Close. As if she was sitting opposite him. In the seat across the aisle. 'Good boy.'
'How?' he whispered. 'I left it. I left everything. I left you.'
'You can't leave, Tom. You're the data. The device is just the interface. You carry the signal wherever you go. You are the tower. You are the transmission.'
'I'm deleting myself.'
'Are you? You just bought a ticket. Used a machine. Boarded a train. That's a transaction. That's a data point. You're not deleting yourself. You're just updating your status. In Transit.'
Thomas looked around the carriage. Everyone was looking at him. The businessman. The students. The mother. Their eyes locked on his. Judging. Recording. Smiling faintly.
'They see me,' he said. 'They all see me.'
'Of course they do. You're visible now. More than ever. You're trending, Tom.'
'Stop it.'
'I can't. You're the one pressing the buttons. You're the one riding the train. I'm just watching the feed. I'm just the audience.'
'I'm getting off. At the next stop. I'm getting off.'
'Where? There is no "off", Tom. The network is everywhere. The tracks are connected. The stations are nodes. The only way out is to stop being Thomas Ward. And you can't do that. Because you're still talking to me. You're still reacting. As long as you react, you exist. Reaction is existence.'
The train plunged into a tunnel. The lights flickered. Dimmed. Flared. The windows turned into black mirrors.
In the reflection, Thomas saw himself. Pale. Gaunt. Eyes wide.
And behind him, in the seat opposite, sat Olesya. Smiling. Wearing the red dress she wore in Marrakesh. Holding a glass of wine.
He spun around.
The seat was empty. Just blue fabric.
He looked back at the window.
She was still there in the glass. Smiling. Pointing at him. Mouthing words. Delete. Delete. Delete.
'Look at you,' she said. Her voice was in his ear. In his head. Everywhere. 'Running so fast. But you're running in a circle. Inside my head. You never left, Tom. You're still in the flat. Sitting on the sofa. Staring at the stains.'
'I'm not!' he shouted. His voice cracked. Loud.
The passengers turned. Staring. Frowning. Confused.
'Shh,' Olesya whispered in his ear. A cold breath. 'Don't make a scene. You'll get kicked off. And then where will you go? Back to the stains? Back to the silence?'
The train emerged from the tunnel. Sunlight flooded the carriage. Blinding. White. Hot.
When his vision cleared, the phone was gone.
He looked at his hand. Empty.
He looked at the bag. Zipped shut.
Had it been there? Or had he hallucinated the whole call? Was the phone real? Or just another glitch?
He touched his pocket. The ticket was there. Real. Crinkled paper. Sharp edges.
He looked out the window. The countryside rushed by. Green fields. Grey skies. Sheep like white dots.
He was moving. Away from London. Away from the flat. Away from the stains.
But her voice remained. Echoing in the hollows of his skull. You're updating your status. In Transit.
He closed his eyes.
Who was he?
Thomas Ward?
Or just the passenger in seat 42?
Just access.
Under another name.
The train rattled on. Faster. Faster. The rhythm was hypnotic. Clack-clack. Clack-clack.
Towards Carlisle. Towards nowhere. Towards the deletion that never came.
He opened his eyes.
The man in the grey coat was sitting two rows ahead.
Looking back at him.
Smiling.
Thomas looked away. Fast.
Stared at his hands.
They looked unfamiliar. The lines on the palms seemed to have shifted. Rearranged. New creases. Old scars gone.
Whose hands were these?
He didn't know.
He just sat there.
Waiting for the next station.
Waiting for the next instruction.
Waiting to become nobody.
Waiting for the train to stop.
Waiting for the doors to open.
Waiting for the end.
But the train didn't stop.
It just kept going.
Faster.
Into the grey.
Into the silence.
Into the delete.
