Chapter 13

The siren began during the lunch queue.

Not loudly at first. A thin mechanical rise above the clatter of trays, the scrape of serving spoons, the low talk of men deciding between chicken and fish with the seriousness of people who had surrendered choice elsewhere. It climbed once, fell, then climbed again. This time every head lifted.

The dining hall changed without anyone speaking.

Forks stopped. A tray slipped sideways on the rail and struck the metal edge with a flat crack. The television above the drinks station continued showing a football replay to no one. A man from procurement stood with tongs in his hand, one piece of bread held in the air, waiting for a day that had already moved on.

Then the compound alarm found its full voice.

Incoming.

The word did not come from the siren. It came from men already moving.

Security entered through both doors. Black shirts. Radios. Hands out, not touching anyone until they had to. The dining hall staff stepped back from the counter. Steam rose from the rice. The smell of cumin, bleach, oil, and dust seemed to tighten inside the room.

'Shelter,' someone called.

Sarah was three places ahead of Thomas in the queue, holding a tray with salad, flatbread, and a cup of water. She turned once to find him.

He saw her face register the room before it registered him.

Then she put the tray down.

Good.

They had practised this twice in briefings and once in a drill no one had taken seriously enough. Move to the internal corridor. Stay low if outside. Do not return for belongings. Do not use lifts. Do not stand near glass. Wait for all-clear. The words had sat in welcome packs and laminated notices, useful in the abstract.

Now the dining hall doors looked too wide.

Thomas reached Sarah as the first thud arrived.

It was distant, or seemed distant, though distance changed inside a body. The sound did not crack like thunder. It struck the compound through the ground, a blunt pressure that moved through the soles of his boots and into his teeth. The lights flickered once. Someone swore. A woman near the drinks station dropped her phone, bent for it, and was pulled upright by a security guard.

'Move,' the guard said.

Not shouted.

No room for shouting.

Thomas took Sarah's wrist.

She did not resist.

They moved with the others through the side exit and into the internal service corridor that ran behind the dining hall towards the hardened shelter. The corridor was narrow, painted beige halfway up and white above, with old scuff marks where equipment trolleys had struck the walls. The air was hotter there. Bodies pressed close. The overhead lights hummed. A radio hissed against someone's shoulder.

Sarah's wrist was dry in his hand.

'Are you all right?' he asked.

'Walking.'

'Good.'

'Do not ask me questions with obvious answers.'

Her voice came level, clipped. He held onto that.

Another impact, closer.

The corridor compressed. A man ducked and struck his head against a fire extinguisher box. The metal rang. Someone laughed once, a short impossible sound, then stopped. Dust lifted from the top of the pipes and fell through the fluorescent light like sifted flour.

The shelter door stood open at the end.

Inside: concrete, benches, water crates, laminated instructions, a first-aid cabinet, three battery lights, a smell of stored plastic and old air. People entered too quickly and then had nowhere to go. Security pushed them along the walls. Sit. Sit. Keep the middle clear. Phones away unless instructed. Do not block the door.

Thomas and Sarah ended up on a bench near the back wall.

His shoulder touched hers. Her knee touched his. Across from them, Nisha sat with both hands flat on her thighs, staring at the floor between her shoes. The finance clerk from payroll counted people under her breath. A young contractor held a sandwich in its plastic wrapper, still unopened, the bread pressed flat in his grip.

The shelter door closed.

The sound of the compound changed.

Outside noise became something handled by walls. The alarm thinned. The impacts arrived without clear direction. Above them. Beside them. Somewhere beyond the concrete. Each one loosened small grains from the ceiling. The battery lights made everyone's skin the same colour.

Sarah leaned back against the wall.

'This is unpleasant,' she said.

'Yes.'

'I am making that my official incident statement.'

'Concise.'

'I know my audience.'

Her hand found his under the bench. Fingers first, then palm. She held him hard enough for the bones to meet.

He held back.

His phone was in his right pocket.

He had placed it there after lunch, face inward, new app hidden in the utility folder, notifications set to show no text. A soundless object. Managed. Contained.

It vibrated once.

Not a call. A message.

Sarah's hand tightened at the next impact. The vibration ended while he was still deciding whether she had noticed it.

He kept his eyes on the shelter floor.

Grey paint. Chips. Boot marks. A small dark screw near the base of the opposite wall, fallen from somewhere and ignored by everyone until now.

The phone vibrated again.

Then again.

Sarah turned her head a fraction.

'Is that you?'

'Yes.'

'Work?'

'Likely.'

'They can wait.'

'Yes.'

The third vibration came longer.

Not work.

He knew it with the old animal knowledge that had no need of proof.

Across the shelter, the security supervisor listened to his radio, head bent. Static. Numbers. A location code. A request repeated twice. No one in the room breathed loudly enough to compete with it. The shelter made every small sound public: the click of a swallow, the rub of fabric, a shoe sole shifting on concrete.

Thomas did not take out the phone.

He sat beside his wife and watched dust fall.

The shelter found its own order after the first ten minutes.

Strain did not announce itself. It entered through behaviour. A man who had spoken too loudly at lunch folded and unfolded the same paper napkin until it came apart in layers. The security guard nearest the door kept touching the radio clipped to his vest though it had not moved. Nisha wrote one name twice and crossed the second out so hard the pen tore the paper. Sarah's thumb counted the bones across the back of his hand, one at a time, then started again.

Thomas knew the compound systems well enough to picture the layers above them.

Perimeter cameras recording dust.

Operations freezing non-critical work.

Security logging all heads.

Medical standing by.

Admin waiting to send the official phrase once the phrase became available.

No immediate threat to personnel.

No casualties reported.

Routine precautions.

Words designed to travel cleanly to families, managers, insurers, regional offices, anyone whose job depended on distance staying legible. The words would leave out the sandwich flattened in the contractor's hand, the screw on the floor, Sarah's fingers tightening under the bench, the way impact entered bone before sound found language.

His pocket stayed quiet.

That gave him too much room to think.

Olesya's messages did not arrive like Sarah's. Sarah's were part of the visible world: calendar changes, meeting links, jokes about fish, a photograph of a ridiculous sign near the laundry. Olesya's messages came from below the floor of things. Rent. Medicine. Door. School. A child's breath. Problems that did not wait for the right hour.

He had moved her into the smaller app to contain the route.

Now the route had narrowed into a single dark channel in his pocket, and he was sitting on his hand while the channel called.

Sarah leaned closer.

'Your jaw is doing that thing.'

'What thing?'

'The thing where you become a locked filing cabinet.'

'It is probably the alarm.'

'Probably.'

She did not ask again.

The mercy in that was starting to cost both of them.

In Baku, Olesya stood in the stairwell with Anya's schoolbag in one hand and the grocery sack cutting into the fingers of the other.

She had not meant to be late.

The bus had stalled near Narimanov. The driver had opened the doors and half the passengers had stepped into traffic, preferring exhaust and shouting to the dead heat inside the bus. Olesya had waited three minutes, then followed with Anya close behind her. They had walked the last blocks past building sites, fruit sellers, parked cars with film over the windows, men smoking under balconies, the wind from the sea carrying dust and damp concrete.

Anya had talked about the English competition for two streets, then stopped when the grocery bag began to tear.

Now the key did not turn.

Olesya tried it once more, slowly.

The lock held.

From inside the flat came the thin sound of a television.

Not theirs.

She stepped back and looked at the number on the door.

Same door. Same scratch near the handle. Same cheap brass digit hanging slightly crooked. Same strip of old tape where the previous tenant had fixed a New Year decoration and torn paint away removing it.

Anya stood one step below, still in her school shirt. Sweat had flattened the hair at her temples. The red notebook stuck out of the schoolbag.

'Mama?'

'Wait.'

Olesya tried the key again.

No movement.

The neighbour's door opened.

Madina from the second floor looked out in slippers, one hand on the frame, the other holding a cigarette between two fingers. Her hair was wrapped in a towel. She looked at Olesya, then at Anya, then at the grocery bag.

'They changed it.'

Olesya kept the key in the lock.

'Who?'

'Owner's nephew came. With a man.'

'When?'

'Morning.'

'Why?'

Madina lifted one shoulder. 'Ask him.'

'Did they go inside?'

'They went inside. How to change lock from outside?'

Anya moved up one step until her shoulder touched Olesya's coat.

Olesya smelled metal from the key, onion from the torn grocery bag, old smoke from Madina's doorway.

'Did they take anything?'

'I did not stand and watch.'

Madina looked down the stairwell. A child was crying somewhere below. The building smelled of fried oil, damp plaster, drains, and someone's perfume sprayed too heavily in the morning.

'He said rent late.'

'Rent is paid.'

'Then call him.'

Madina closed her door halfway, not all the way. Enough to remove herself. Not enough to miss anything.

Olesya took out her phone.

No signal in the stairwell.

She climbed to the landing window, held the phone against the glass, watched one bar appear, disappear, return. She called the landlord. No answer. She called again. The line cut. She sent a message.

The rent is paid. Why lock changed?

No delivery tick.

Anya stood by the door with the grocery sack at her feet. A wet mark spread beneath it. Tomatoes, probably. Or yoghurt. Something soft giving up under pressure.

'Can we go inside?'

'Soon.'

'My uniform is inside.'

'I know.'

'My medicine.'

Olesya looked at the door.

The asthma medicine was in the kitchen drawer, beside the spoons. Spare inhaler. Nebuliser rental due back in three days. School uniform hanging from the balcony line. Passport copies in the blue folder. The transfer confirmations in the biscuit tin, under the old receipts.

All of it behind a door that now belonged to someone else's key.

She opened the secure app.

Thomas had not answered her earlier message about the school letter. The last thing in the thread was her line from the night before:

Do not make me disappear from places.

She typed:

Lock changed. We are outside.

The message sent.

No reply.

She typed:

Owner says rent late. It is not late.

No reply.

Then:

Anya medicine inside.

No reply.

In the shelter, Thomas kept his hand under Sarah's and counted the vibrations.

One.

Two.

Long gap.

Three.

Then nothing.

The all-clear did not come.

People had begun to arrange themselves into endurance. Someone passed water bottles from a crate. The young contractor finally opened the sandwich and took one bite without looking at it. Nisha wrote names on the back of a printed agenda, checking staff from finance by memory. The payroll clerk had stopped counting under her breath and started again on paper.

Sarah's hand loosened.

'If they keep us here long enough,' she said, 'I am charging this to the client.'

'Shelter hours?'

'Hostile working environment.'

'Accurate.'

'Billable trauma.'

He looked at her.

'Sorry,' she said. 'Bad joke.'

'No.'

'It was.'

She reached for the water bottle at her feet, twisted the cap, drank, then handed it to him. He drank from the same place her mouth had been. The water tasted of plastic and storage.

Another thud, farther this time.

The room stilled.

The radio came alive in fragments.

Impact outside perimeter.

No casualties confirmed.

Stand by.

Stand by.

Sarah's thumb moved over the back of his hand.

'This is close enough,' she said quietly.

'When we get out, I want to call home.'

'Good.'

'And you should message your people.'

'My people?'

'Office. Field team. Whoever needs to know you still have a head.'

'I will.'

His pocket stayed still.

That was worse.

Still meant messages waiting. Still meant the phone had finished telling him and now required him to choose.

The courtyard was a square of broken paving, parked cars, washing lines, and one mulberry tree whose roots had lifted the concrete into ridges. Children were playing near the bins with a punctured ball. The line ended.

Anya stood under the mulberry tree with the schoolbag against her chest. The grocery sack had finally split. Two tomatoes rolled under a car. The yoghurt tub had cracked and spread white across the paving. A stray cat came near, then thought better of it.

Olesya took a photograph of the lock.

Then of Anya's schoolbag by the door.

Then of the broken grocery sack.

She sent all three to Thomas.

No reply.

She called him in the new app.

It rang without sound on his side.

No answer.

Olesya ended the call before it could make her feel foolish.

The courtyard made witnesses of everything. Madina's half-open door upstairs. The old man by the opposite block. The children with the punctured ball. The cat returning now that the yoghurt had spread thin enough to lick. Even the mulberry tree seemed to lean over the scene, leaves dull with dust, its roots lifting the paving in slow public damage.

Anya had gone quiet.

That was worse than questions.

Olesya crouched beside the grocery sack and rescued what could be rescued. Bread in its plastic sleeve. One tin dented at the side. A packet of tea. The tomatoes under the car were gone, or else hidden beyond reach. The yoghurt had no second life.

'We can go to Marina auntie,' Anya said.

'She is at work.'

'Madina?'

'No.'

Madina was listening behind her door. That was not the same as opening it.

Olesya stood. Her knees made a small dry click. She brushed yoghurt from the side of the tea packet with her thumb and put it back in the torn sack.

The landlord's nephew arrived in a white car with one black replacement panel on the front wing. He was younger than Olesya expected, wearing sunglasses on top of his head and a shirt with a false designer logo printed too large across the chest. A locksmith came with him, carrying a canvas tool roll.

'You changed already,' Olesya said.

'My things are inside.'

'Bank says no.'

The regular transfer had failed somewhere between Thomas's account, the landlord's bank, and the system of names that made every foreign payment slightly suspect. Or the landlord had found a way to make it fail. Or the nephew had a screenshot from another month. 'Pay.'

'The child needs inhaler.'

'Her inhaler is inside.'

The compound after an alert had its own disorder. Not chaos. Never that. The place was too disciplined for chaos. It was procedure with dust in its mouth. Security men at junctions. Radios alive. Supervisors counting heads again after they had already counted them. Men trying to sound casual on calls to wives, parents, managers.
