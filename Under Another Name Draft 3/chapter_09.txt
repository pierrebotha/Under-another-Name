Chapter 9
The city had changed. Or perhaps Thomas had. The lens through which he viewed the world had cracked, distorting the familiar streets of London into a labyrinth of potential threats. He walked with his head down, collar turned up against a wind that carried the scent of exhaust and damp wool. Every passerby was a suspect. Every parked van a surveillance unit. Every window an eye.
He had left the flat in a state of suspended animation. The laptop remained in the locked drawer, a black box of treason. The phone was in his pocket, switched off, a dead weight of plastic and glass. He felt naked without it, yet terrified to turn it on. If Olesya had root access, powering it up was an invitation. A handshake with the enemy.
He needed to move. To burn energy. To outrun the feeling of being watched. But you cannot outrun a signal. You cannot hide from a ghost in the machine.
He walked for miles. Through Soho, where the neon signs buzzed like angry insects. Through Covent Garden, where street performers mimed silence to crowds that laughed. He didn’t laugh. He couldn’t remember the last time he had. Laughter felt like a language he had forgotten, a dialect of a country he no longer visited.
He ended up near the Thames. The river was grey, choppy, sliding under the bridges with a indifferent heaviness. He leaned against the railing, gripping the cold metal until his knuckles turned white. The water below looked thick, oily. Something dead floated past, a plastic bag or a bird, tumbling in the current.
‘You look like you’re about to jump.’
The voice came from his left. A woman. Middle-aged. Walking a dog. A terrier, same as the one in the park.
Thomas blinked. Pulled back from the railing. ‘No,’ he said. ‘Just looking.’
‘It’s a good view,’ she said. ‘Even on a day like this.’
‘Is it?’
She frowned. Tugged the dog’s lead. ‘Come on, Buster.’
They walked away. The dog looked back at him, head tilted. Judging.
Thomas pushed off the railing. Continued walking.
He needed a plan. A real one. Not the spreadsheet nonsense. Not the password changes. Those were games. She was playing chess; he was moving pawns at random.
Phase Two. Surveillance from the inside.
If she controlled his devices, then the devices were compromised. Irretrievably. He couldn’t fix them. He couldn’t clean them. He had to destroy them. And he had to start over. Analog. Offline. Invisible.
But how do you live in 2024 without a digital footprint? You don’t. You cease to exist. You become a ghost before you’re dead.
Maybe that was the point. Maybe she wanted him to disappear. To delete himself.
He stopped at a newsagent. Bought a disposable phone. Cash. No registration. The assistant, a teenager with piercings and bored eyes, didn’t ask for ID. Just took the ten pounds and handed over the plastic-wrapped package.
‘Cheers,’ Thomas muttered.
He walked to a bin two streets away. Unwrapped the phone. Slotted in the SIM. Turned it on.
It beeped. Connected to the network.
A blank screen. No apps. No history. No Olesya.
He felt a surge of relief. Pure, chemical relief. This was clean. This was safe.
Then he dialed his old number.
It rang once. Twice.
Voicemail kicked in. His own voice. ‘You’ve reached Thomas. Leave a message.’
He hung up.
Did she hear the call? Did the old phone ring in the drawer? Did she see the missed call notification from a burner?
Probably. If she had access to the account level, not just the device.
He tossed the burner into the bin. Covered it with a coffee cup and a newspaper.
Walked away.
Paranoia was a hunger. It ate everything. Logic. Reason. Hope.
He returned to the flat as dusk fell. The building loomed ahead, a silhouette against the bruised sky. His window was dark. Or was it? Did he see a flicker of light? A shadow moving behind the curtain?
He stopped at the door. Watched the windows for ten minutes. Nothing. Just the reflection of the streetlamp.
He went inside.
The flat was exactly as he had left it. The chair still overturned. The smoke detector shattered on the floor. The pile of unplugged devices on the sofa. The stains on the laminate. Eight of them. A dark constellation.
He righted the chair. Sat down.
Didn’t turn on the light.
Sat in the gloom.
What now?
He couldn’t work. Couldn’t communicate. Couldn’t bank. He was cut off. A modern man stranded in a pre-digital age.
He stood up. Went to the kitchen. Opened the cupboard. Took out a bottle of wine. The same one from weeks ago. Half empty.
He uncorked it. Poured a glass.
Drank it standing up.
The wine tasted sour. Oxidized. Gone off.
He drank it anyway.
Needed the blur. The numbness.
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa.
Closed his eyes.
Thought about her.
Where was she? Really?
London? Istanbul? Nowhere?
Was she sitting in a café, watching his location dot freeze because the phone was off? Was she smiling? Frustrated? Bored?
Or was she here? In the building? In the stairwell? Listening at his door?
He opened his eyes. Looked at the door.
Locked. Chain on.
But locks were for physical intruders. She was already inside. In the walls. In the wires. In his head.
His phone buzzed.
He jumped. Spilled wine on his trouser leg. A new stain. Ninth one.
He stared at the device.
It was the burner. He had put it in his pocket. Forgotten.
Unknown Number.
Of course.
He let it ring.
It stopped.
Started again.
Persistent. Relentless.
He picked it up. Answered.
‘Hello?’
Silence.
Then, a click.
‘Tom.’
Her voice. Again. Clear. Close.
‘How?’ he whispered. ‘This is a new number. Disposable. I bought it an hour ago.’
‘Numbers are just numbers, Tom. They route calls. I don’t need the number. I need you to answer.’
‘You’re everywhere.’
‘I’m nowhere. I’m just a voice in your ear. You’re the one carrying me around.’
‘What do you want?’
‘To finish the experiment.’
‘There is no experiment. You’re just hurting me.’
‘Pain is data, Tom. It tells us where the limits are. Where the breaking point is.’
‘I’m broken. Happy?’
‘Not yet. You’re still fighting. Still trying to hide. Still buying burners. That’s resistance. That’s life.’
‘I’m going to stop. I’m going to disappear.’
‘You can’t. You’re connected. By biology. By habit. By fear. You check your pockets for a phone that isn’t there. You look at windows. You flinch at noises. You’re more connected to me now than you ever were when you were texting me goodnight.’
Thomas gripped the phone. ‘Why? Just tell me why.’
‘Because I needed to know if you were real. If any of us are. Or if we’re just characters in a story we tell ourselves. And you… you’re very real, Tom. You’re screaming inside. I can hear it.’
‘I hate you.’
‘I know. Keep hating. It’s better than nothing.’
‘Where are you?’
‘Close.’
‘Are you coming?’
‘Maybe.’
‘When?’
‘Soon.’
The line went dead.
Thomas lowered the phone.
Soon.
The word hung in the air. A promise. A threat.
He looked around the flat.
Shadows lengthened in the corners. The silence deepened.
She was coming.
Or she wasn’t.
It didn’t matter. The anticipation was the torture. The waiting was the punishment.
He finished the wine. Poured another glass.
Drank it.
Lay down on the sofa.
Closed his eyes.
Waited.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of doors. Thousands of them. Lining a endless corridor. He tried each handle. Locked. Locked. Locked.
At the end of the corridor, one door was open. Light spilling out.
He walked towards it.
Inside, a room. Empty. Except for a chair. And a phone. Ringing.
He woke up gasping.
Pitch black.
Silence.
He fumbled for the burner.
No calls. No messages.
Just the time. Ticking forward.
He put the phone down.
Lay back.
Tomorrow he would leave. Pack a bag. Go somewhere remote. No signal. No internet. Just woods and silence.
But tonight, he just lay there.
In the dark.
Listening to the silence.
Wondering if she was outside the door. Hand on the handle. Waiting for him to sleep.
Wondering if he was safe. Or if safety was just a myth he had invented to get through the night.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind.
He pushed it away.
Sleep took him.
When he woke, the sun was up.
Bright. Harsh.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was silent.
He got up.
Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up.
Looked at the nine stains on the floor. The broken detector. The pile of electronics.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day.
