Chapter 09

Iraq did not begin with heat.

It began with dust.

Dust on the charter steps before Thomas had both feet on the ground. Dust lifting behind the baggage truck. Dust caught in the teeth of the perimeter fence, in the seams of the transport seats, in the damp at the corner of his eye. The airport buildings sat low and beige under a white sky. Men in uniforms moved between barriers with rifles slung across their chests. A convoy coordinator read names from a clipboard and ticked them off with a red pen.

Basra did not receive people. It processed them.

Thomas stood with the other arrivals beside a row of hard cases and watched his name become a tick. The air tasted of diesel and metal. Somewhere beyond the wire, a generator laboured with a thick, irregular cough.

'Ward,' the coordinator said.

'Here.'

'Majnoon compound. Vehicle three.'

Thomas lifted his bag.

'Helmet on before gate.'

'Yes.'

The helmet had been issued in Dubai, along with body armour, emergency card, radio instructions, and a laminated sheet explaining what to do if the convoy stopped unexpectedly. Do not exit the vehicle unless instructed. Keep head below window line. Follow security personnel. Do not improvise.

The final instruction had been printed in bold.

Inside the armoured vehicle, the windows were thick enough to tint the road. Basra passed in interrupted fragments: concrete walls, shopfronts, satellite dishes, a boy pushing a cart of gas cylinders, water pooled in tyre ruts, posters peeling from a blast wall. The driver did not speak. The security man in the front passenger seat watched the road with his rifle resting between his knees.

Thomas held his phone in his hand though there was no signal.

At the compound gate, two barriers lifted and one closed behind them before the next opened. Movement happened in boxes. Gate. Check. Barrier. Mirror under vehicle. Badge. Search. Barrier. Release.

The compound had lawns.

That was the first obscenity of it. Green strips under sprinklers. White modular buildings. Gravel paths. A dining hall with tinted windows. A gym. A clinic. A noticeboard advertising quiz night, yoga, convoy timings, and a warning about scorpions in boots.

His room was narrow and clean. Bed, desk, wardrobe, metal chair, small refrigerator, air conditioner already rattling at the wall. A laminated evacuation map was pinned beside the door. The window did not open.

He unpacked in the order the room allowed.

Boots under the bed. Field shirts on hangers. Laptop on desk. Passport in the metal lockbox. Blue folder in the top drawer.

He put it there, then took it out again.

There was no reason to keep Olesya's folder in Iraq.

That was why it stayed.

The first week ran on induction.

Security briefing. Site orientation. Permit system. Radio protocol. Emergency muster. Hydration requirement. Heat injury prevention. Access badge. Vehicle schedule. Rig familiarisation. The language of the place was restriction softened into procedure.

He learned the daily pattern. Breakfast before six. Convoy at six thirty. Operations office by seven. Field checks before the heat rose. Lunch in the dining hall under cold air and fluorescent lights. Calls with London in the late afternoon. Reports after dinner. Sleep in short pieces beneath the air conditioner.

At night, the compound hummed.

Generators. Water pumps. Air-conditioning units. Distant engines beyond the wall. Men laughing outside the smoking shelter. Doors opening and closing along the corridor with the same tired click.

Sarah called on the fourth night from London.

'You look boiled,' she said.

'I am indoors.'

'Then Iraq has found a way in.'

He had propped the laptop on two technical manuals to improve the camera angle. Behind Sarah, their Fulham kitchen was bright and narrow, the kettle steaming behind her shoulder. She had her hair tied up and a notebook open beside the laptop.

'How is the compound?'

'Secure.'

'That is not a description.'

'It has lawns.'

'Lawns?'

'Yes.'

'That is sinister.'

'Very.'

She wrote something in her notebook. 'Company housing sent me the arrival options. If I come after Lisbon, I can route through Dubai and land on the Tuesday convoy. They want my passport scan again.'

'They already have it.'

'Everyone always has it and no one can find it.'

She held the passport up to the camera, then put it beside her notebook.

'I will send the scan tonight.'

'Are you sure you want to come?'

'Want is a strong word. I want a great many things. Some of them involve a bath and a door that locks. But if you are there six months, I can work from there for a while.'

'It is restricted.'

'So is everywhere. Some places are just honest about it.'

Thomas looked at the window that did not open.

'Send the dates when confirmed,' he said.

'I will. And you send proof that you are eating something green.'

'There are lawns.'

'Do not eat the lawns.'

Her smile broke up slightly as the connection stuttered.

After the call, he opened the spreadsheet.

Restriction.

Debt.

Unknowns.

Transfers.

The last row still held February. Rent two months. School balance. Same-day. Receipts received.

Olesya had not asked again. She sent small practical updates, each one pared down before it reached him.

Anya back at school full week.

Clinic says inhaler morning and night.

New work possible. Cash weekly.

The job was at a stationery wholesaler near the bus depot. She packed boxes, checked delivery slips, and reconciled handwritten stock lists at a counter with no chair. It paid every Friday in cash. No contract. No insurance. No sick pay. No questions.

Thomas read the message twice.

He typed:

Do they give receipts?

Olesya replied:

For boxes?

He deleted his next question.

For two months, the system held at the edges.

He sent small amounts when a defined need appeared. Inhaler refill. School workbook. Winter electricity balance. Shoes after Anya's pair split at the sole. Each transfer had a note in the spreadsheet, each receipt arrived flattened on Olesya's table with the same care as before.

It should have been smaller than the first wire.

It did not.

Repetition made the line stronger.

In May, the compound clinic issued him a laminated card for heat stress.

He was meant to carry it in his shirt pocket during field visits. The front listed symptoms in block capitals. Dizziness. Confusion. Weakness. Collapse. The back gave actions. Move to shade. Remove excess clothing. Cool skin. Seek medical assessment.

He kept it beside his access badge and forgot it twice.

The clinic nurse stopped him at the dining hall door on the third morning.

'Card,' she said.

Thomas touched his empty pocket.

'Desk.'

'Your body is not on your desk.'

'Noted.'

'Noted does not lower core temperature.'

She handed him a spare card with the briskness of someone who had seen clever men become burdens.

That night, Olesya sent a photograph of Anya's inhaler box.

The dosage label had been torn at one corner. Beside it lay a pharmacy receipt and a strip of tablets Thomas could not read.

Doctor changed medicine.

He enlarged the label until the letters blurred.

Why?

Chest tight after school.

Clinic?

Yes.

He waited for the next line.

Cost more.

There it was. Not a request. A fact placed where a request would have been if she trusted need to arrive cleanly.

Send receipt, he typed.

Already did.

He looked again. The amount was on the pharmacy slip, bottom right, half hidden by the inhaler box. He entered it in the spreadsheet under medical. Then he sent the sum, plus enough for the next refill. The platform fee appeared, neat and shameless.

Olesya wrote:

Too much.

He answered:

Next refill.

For a long time there was no reply.

Then:

She has sports day next week. She cannot run.

Thomas looked at the heat-stress card beside his keyboard. Dizziness. Confusion. Weakness. Collapse. A body reduced to symptoms and action steps.

Ask doctor for note.

School wants official.

Then get official.

They charge for official.

He sent more.

The reply arrived after midnight.

Receipt tomorrow.

No thanks. No protest. A procedure accepted under pressure.

In the morning, Sarah emailed from London with her Iraq travel packet.

Subject: If I am going to live behind walls, I want decent coffee.

Attached were passport scan, vaccination record, work schedule, client call windows, and a list of things she intended to bring if the company housing allowed them: French press, running shoes, small speaker, two linen shirts, one black dress for whatever counted as formal inside a compound, and a framed photograph from the hall. Even modular prisons deserve evidence of prior civilisation, she wrote.

Thomas opened the files before breakfast.

Everything had a name. Sarah_Ward_Passport.pdf. Sarah_Ward_Vaccinations.pdf. Client_Calls_April_May.xlsx. Her documents behaved when summoned. They came clean, labelled, legible, complete.

Olesya's receipts arrived as photographs taken under kitchen light.

Rent paper beside a cup.

Clinic slip beside Anya's workbook.

Pharmacy bill held under Olesya's thumb.

Doctor note with a corner folded where it had been carried in a bag.

Thomas saved them in a folder called Baku medical, then changed it to Baku support, then changed it again to O admin.

The third name stayed.

On the first Friday of May, Olesya's new job paid her late.

She did not say this directly.

She sent a photograph of the bus card balance. Then:

Can walk Monday if not too hot.

He was in the field office when he read it. Outside, a mechanic was washing dust from a valve skid with a hose that turned the ground to paste. The thermometer near the door read forty-one.

Do not walk.

No reply.

He sent a small sum marked transport.

She replied:

This is foolish.

He wrote:

Heat is foolish.

Three dots appeared.

Then:

I will tell Anya you said this.

He left the message unanswered. It had a warmth he did not know where to put.

The bus receipt came the next day. Two trips. One for Olesya. One for Anya. The balance remaining was visible on the slip.

He entered it.

Transport.

By then the spreadsheet had changed character.

It was no longer a record of exceptions. It had begun to resemble a service log. Date. Category. Amount. Fee. Receipt. Notes. Rent. School. Medicine. Transport. Clinic. Refill. Official letter. Each line closed when paper arrived.

At work, he trusted closed lines.

A valve opened or it did not. A pressure test passed or failed. A cargo cleared or remained held. Men could argue, but the system recorded state.

Olesya's life began to present itself to him in the same grammar.

Need.

Transfer.

Receipt.

State changed.

The danger was not hidden. It was there in every clean row.

He liked rows that closed.

One evening, after a long call about corrosion allowance, Rauf messaged him from Baku.

I hear you are enjoying Iraq.

Thomas replied:

Enjoying is not the word.

Rauf:

Good. Then you understand it.

Thomas watched the message for a while, then typed:

How is the office?

Rauf answered after several minutes.

Less tidy. Your replacement prints emails.

Then:

Olesya left?

Thomas sat back.

Left what?

The dots appeared, vanished, appeared again.

Office. She is not with us now.

He read it twice.

When?

End of March. Temporary contract ended. You did not know?

Thomas looked at the spreadsheet. Inhaler. School note. Transport. The entries after March had no salary beside them, no office structure beneath them, no Rauf within reach.

No, he wrote.

Rauf replied:

Ah.

Then nothing.

The compound air conditioner rattled above him. Down the corridor, someone laughed at a film playing too loudly.

Thomas opened Olesya's thread.

Are you working today?

The answer came the next morning.

Yes.

Where?

Packing boxes.

Contract?

Cash.

He typed:

Why did you not tell me about the office?

The reply did not come until evening.

It ended.

He waited.

Then:

I found other work.

There was no apology in it. No invitation to ask what had happened in the gap. She had removed the missing month from the conversation as neatly as she removed an old receipt from a file.

Thomas opened the spreadsheet and added a new column.

Employment.

For every line after March he entered:

Cash.

The word looked insufficient.

By April, Thomas could read the shape of Olesya's messages before opening them. Thomas meant the matter had already passed the point where she could contain it. A receipt first meant closure by her own hand. Anya without a receipt meant something waiting.

On a Sunday evening, she wrote:

Anya's breathing not good.

Thomas was in the operations office with the night-shift superintendent, reviewing a stuck valve on a water injection line. Outside, the dust had risen during sunset and left a film on the windows. The office smelled of printer heat, old coffee, and the faint sourness of sweat trapped in work shirts.

He stepped into the corridor before replying.

Clinic?

No answer for six minutes.

Then:

Tomorrow.

He typed:

Today.

The dots appeared, vanished, appeared again.

It is Sunday.

Private clinic emergency.

Too expensive.

He wrote:

Go. I will send.

The reply came fast.

No.

Then:

If needed tomorrow.

He looked through the corridor glass at the superintendent leaning over the valve report, red pen in hand. A problem waiting for a decision.

Thomas typed:

It is needed today.

He sent an amount before she answered.

The payment platform showed the fee, the exchange rate, the recipient name. He pressed confirm. The screen turned white, then returned with a reference number.

He sent the screenshot.

Clinic today.

Olesya did not reply for twenty minutes.

When she did, the message was only:

We are going.

The next morning, he woke before five to a dust storm.

The world outside the sealed window had disappeared. The compound lights glowed in a brown fog. Fine grit hissed against the building. In the corridor, doors opened and closed as men checked convoy status on their phones.

By six, all movement was suspended.

The announcement came over the radio network and then through email. No road travel until visibility improved. Site teams to remain in accommodation blocks. Essential operations only by internal shuttle.

Thomas dressed anyway.

In the dining hall, the air-conditioning fought dust that came in on boots and shoulders. Men sat over eggs and tea, looking at the windows. Someone joked about being trapped in a snow globe built by a refinery.

Thomas's phone rang at six forty-two.

Olesya.

He answered before the second vibration.

'Tell me.'

Her breath was in the phone first. Not crying. Not words. Air failing to become either.

'Anya is worse.'

'Where are you?'

'State hospital.'

'Why state?'

'The private clinic sent us. They said attack too strong. Need hospital.'

The dining hall noise thinned around him.

'Is she admitted?'

'They put her in corridor. No beds.'

Something clattered behind her. Wheels. Metal. A voice shouting numbers.

'Can she breathe?'

'They gave oxygen. It stops, then starts. Thomas, they say wait.'

He stood. The chair legs scraped the floor.

'Go back to the private clinic.'

'They sent us away.'

'Call them. Tell them I will pay for a room and respiratory doctor.'

'They will not take her if hospital started.'

'Then ask what deposit changes that.'

She said something away from the phone. Russian, then Azeri, then Russian again. The languages crossed each other in the receiver.

Thomas walked out of the dining hall and into the corridor. Dust had gathered along the bottom of the door in a thin brown line.

'Olesya.'

'They say maybe private hospital, not clinic.'

'Name.'

'Caspian Medical.'

'Send address. Send billing contact. Send whatever they give you.'

'I do not have paper.'

'Then photograph the wall.'

The line stayed open. He heard footsteps, a cough, the squeak of wheels, Anya's name once, spoken away from the phone.

Then the call ended.

Thomas stood in the corridor with the phone in his hand.

At seven ten, Olesya sent three photographs.

A hospital corridor with green walls and metal chairs.

A signboard listing departments in Azeri, Russian, English.

A handwritten number on the back of an old receipt.

Below them:

They say deposit first.

He opened the payment platform. The amount she sent was not large by corporate standards. It was large in the way a door is large when it will not open.

The platform rejected the transfer.

Daily limit.

He stared at the message. Then tried a lower amount.

Rejected. Limit reached by previous transfer.

He opened his bank. International transfer. New recipient: Caspian Medical Services. Country: Azerbaijan. Purpose: medical.

The form asked for invoice number.

He had none.

He typed Anya Petrova deposit.

The form asked for patient relationship.

He sat still for one second too long.

Other.

The bank asked for supporting documentation.

He uploaded the photograph of the signboard, then the handwritten number, then the corridor.

The submit button stayed grey.

Required document missing.

He went to the operations office.

The compound outside had become a moving wall of dust. Men crossed between buildings with scarves over their mouths. The office generator had kicked into a higher pitch. Inside, only half the desks were occupied. The night superintendent looked up from a radio log.

'Roads are shut,' he said.

'I need the satellite line.'

'For work?'

'Medical.'

The man looked at him for one beat, then pointed to the small side office. 'Dial nine first.'

Thomas closed the door.

He called the number from the receipt.

The first time, no one answered.

The second time, a woman answered in Russian.

'English,' Thomas said. 'Please. English.'

The phone moved. Voices. A hand over the receiver. Then a man came on.

'Caspian Medical.'

'My name is Thomas Ward. I am calling about Anya Petrova. She is being transferred from the state hospital. I need to pay the deposit.'

'Family?'

'I am responsible for payment.'

'Family?'

The question returned with the same weight.

'No.'

'Then we need family.'

'Her mother is there.'

'Mother signs. Payment before admission.'

'Send invoice.'

'We cannot make invoice before admission.'

'You cannot admit before payment.'

'Correct.'

Thomas pressed his free hand flat against the desk. Its surface was gritty.

'Then give me bank details.'

The man gave them slowly. Thomas wrote each line in block letters. Bank name. Account number. Tax number. Reference.

'How fast can you see payment?'

'When it arrives.'

'I can send confirmation.'

'Confirmation is not funds.'

The same sentence as the landlord, dressed in another voice.

'If I send confirmation, will you move her from the corridor?'

The man covered the receiver again. Thomas heard the room on the other side, phones and voices and one long electronic beep.

'Send confirmation to WhatsApp,' the man said. 'Doctor will decide.'

Thomas took the number.

He could not make the bank form accept the hospital without an invoice, so he used the payment platform in pieces. Three transfers. Different references. Medical deposit. Treatment. Oxygen. Each fee cut into the amount. Each confirmation arrived as its own PDF.

He sent all three to the WhatsApp number.

Then to Olesya.

Then he called the hospital again.

'Received confirmation,' the man said.

'Move her.'

'Doctor will decide.'

'Get the doctor.'

'Sir.'

'Get the doctor.'

The man's voice cooled. 'This is not England.'

'No,' Thomas said. 'It is a hospital.'

The line went quiet.

When the man came back, he said, 'Mother is signing.'

Thomas put the phone down carefully.

His hands had left damp marks in the dust on the desk.

For the next two hours, nothing worked except waiting.

The dust storm held the compound closed. The operations team argued over pressure readings from a field they could not reach. The satellite internet flickered. Sarah sent a message from London.

Housing confirmed Tuesday convoy if I come next month. Need your compound address exactly.

Thomas copied the address from his induction pack and sent it.

Sarah replied:

That sounds like a prison pretending to be a hotel.

He wrote:

Accurate.

Then the site manager came in looking for him.

'Valve meeting. Five minutes.'

'I need ten.'

'The well does not.'

Thomas looked at his phone. No message from Olesya. No change on the payment platform. No second call from the hospital.

He followed the site manager to the conference room.

The video link to the field team was poor. Dust turned every face into a brown smear. Men spoke over one another from three locations. The stuck valve had become a production issue, then a scheduling issue, then a cost issue. Someone in Dubai wanted a decision about whether to defer the line until visibility improved. Someone at the field wanted permission to send a mechanic before the dust settled.

Thomas listened with his phone face down beside his notebook.

'We wait,' he said.

The Dubai manager frowned through the screen. 'That costs us twelve hours.'

'Sending a mechanic blind costs us more.'

'We have pressure margins.'

'We also have people.'

The room quietened for one beat.

The site manager looked at him. 'So we hold?'

'We hold until visibility is back above threshold. Then we inspect before restarting.'

The decision moved into the minutes. Action assigned. Time stamped. Accepted.

His phone vibrated.

He did not touch it until the meeting ended.

Olesya had sent:

They ask previous medicine.

Then a photograph of the inhaler box from the flat, taken at an angle in poor light. The label was visible. The dosage was not.

He went back to his office and searched the O admin folder. Pharmacy receipt. Clinic note. Doctor note for school. Inhaler refill. Each photograph had been saved for accounting and now became medical history.

He sent four images.

Show doctor.

She replied:

He wants name.

Thomas zoomed in until the prescription line became legible.

Budesonide. Salbutamol.

He typed both words slowly.

Then:

Tell him refill every month. Worse with dust and running.

She did not answer.

He looked out at the compound. Dust had sealed itself to the glass. Men moved outside as pale shapes. The lawn sprinklers had stopped.

At ten thirty, the hospital number called.

'Mr Thomas?'

'Yes.'

'Doctor wants to know if child has allergy.'

'Ask her mother.'

'Mother says no. We ask payer for file.'

Payer.

The word entered the room without knocking.

'I do not have a full file.'

'You sent medicines.'

'Those are all I have.'

'Okay.'

'How is she?'

'Doctor reviewing.'

'Is she breathing better?'

The line carried a pause, a hand covering the receiver, a question passed to someone else.

'Oxygen continues.'

'That is not an answer.'

'It is answer now.'

The line ended.

Thomas wrote oxygen continues in the notebook under the valve decision. Then he drew a line through it. Then he wrote it again on a clean page.

At eleven, Olesya sent a photograph of her hand holding a consent form. Only the lower part was visible. Her signature sat near the bottom in a script he had seen on courier books, salary forms, receipts, school slips. A same hand, moved through too many kinds of permission.

He typed:

What did you sign?

Treatment.

Which treatment?

Nebuliser. Observation. Maybe injection.

Ask name.

I asked.

And?

She sent a photograph of a doctor's handwriting. Thomas could read none of it.

He forwarded it to the compound clinic nurse.

Can you read this?

Her answer came five minutes later.

Steroid. Bronchodilator. Standard for asthma. Why?

He wrote:

Friend's child.

The nurse replied:

Then tell friend child needs a doctor, not WhatsApp medicine.

He put the phone down.

By then, the conference-room valve decision had already become an email chain. Men copied him on objections, confirmations, modified schedules. The line could wait. It had pressure margins. It had procedures. It had people standing around it with radios and authority.

In Baku, Olesya waited beside a bed that did not yet belong to Anya.

Olesya sent:

They moved her.

Then:

Room.

Then a photograph.

Anya lay in a narrow hospital bed with a white sheet pulled to her chest. A clear mask covered her nose and mouth. Her hair had been tied back, but strands stuck to her forehead. The wall behind her was clean, painted pale blue. A monitor stood beside the bed. On the small table, a bottle of water, a folded jumper, and Olesya's phone charger lay in a line.

Thomas enlarged the photograph.

Anya's eyes were closed.

He looked at the oxygen tube. The clip on her finger. The clean sheet. The space around the bed where no one else waited on a chair.

The system had produced a room.

He saved the photograph, then immediately unsaved it, then saved it again under the hospital folder he had not meant to create.

Olesya wrote:

Doctor says night observation.

He answered:

Send invoice when they give it.

The reply came after several minutes.

Yes.

Then:

She is sleeping.

He looked at the words.

The dust scraped against the window.

At lunch, the dining hall served rice, chicken, cucumber, and oranges cut into quarters. Thomas ate standing near the coffee machine, phone on the counter beside his tray. The superintendent came in, saw his face, and did not make the joke he had been forming.

'Medical sorted?'

'For now.'

'Good.'

The man poured coffee and left.

For now. The only honest time period in the oil fields.

By late afternoon the storm began to thin. The compound emerged in pieces: the fence, the gravel path, the clinic sign, a row of boots left outside the accommodation block. Vehicles remained grounded. Dust lay over the lawns and turned them the colour of old canvas.

Sarah called at five.

'I have ten minutes before a client dinner,' she said. 'Tell me whether I should pack anything civilised for Iraq.'

'Define civilised.'

'Shoes that are not trainers.'

'Bring one pair.'

'One pair. This really is prison.'

'A prison with lawns.'

'The worst kind.'

She laughed. Behind her, he heard restaurant noise, cutlery, English voices, a door opening to traffic.

'You sound tired,' she said.

'Dust storm.'

'Are you inside?'

'Inside the wire.'

'That is not the same answer.'

'It is the answer I have.'

There was a pause.

'I sent the passport scan again,' she said. 'Housing says the Tuesday convoy is possible. I may arrive two days before your next field rotation.'

'Send me the itinerary.'

'Already did.'

An email appeared while she spoke. Sarah Ward travel pack. Dubai to Basra. Convoy Tuesday. Accommodation request attached.

'Got it.'

'Efficient wife.'

'Very.'

She smiled at him through the screen. 'Do not sound so surprised.'

'Never.'

'I have to go. Eat something. You look like the air has been sandpapering you.'

'Good dinner.'

'Unlikely.'

The call ended.

Thomas opened Sarah's itinerary.

Everything was present. Passport scan. Visa approval. Flight details. Company hotel in Dubai. Convoy number. Accommodation block request. Emergency contact. Dietary note, none. Her movement had formed itself into a packet the company could process without hesitation.

He downloaded it and saved it under Sarah Iraq.

Then he opened Olesya's thread.

The invoice arrived at six twelve.

The hospital had itemised the first night. Admission. Oxygen. Nebuliser. Medication. Respiratory consultation. Monitoring. Deposit applied. Balance due before discharge.

At the bottom was a stamp.

Blue.

The balance was higher than the money already sent.

Thomas opened the exchange-rate page.

Manat to pound.

Pound to manat.

Fees.

Same-day limits.

He moved from Sarah's itinerary to the hospital invoice and back again, two documents open side by side on the compound desk. One brought his wife through official channels towards him. The other kept a child breathing in a room he had paid into existence.

Outside, the last of the dust moved against the window in a thin brown veil.

He entered the new amount into the spreadsheet.

Hospital balance.

Then he checked the exchange rate again.
