Chapter 10 (Expanded)
The rain had returned. Not the fine mist of the previous week, nor the gentle drizzle that merely dampened, but a driving, horizontal assault that turned the pavement into a river of grey sludge, churning with debris and filth. Thomas walked without an umbrella. He had forgotten it. Or perhaps he had left it deliberately. The water soaked through his coat within seconds, heavy and cold, clinging to his skin like a second layer of grief, a wet shroud that weighed him down. He didn't feel it. Or rather, he felt it only as  Temperature low. Saturation high. Discomfort registered. Everything was data now. Even his own pain had been quantified, categorized, and filed away in the spreadsheet of his mind, row after row of suffering.
He had left the flat an hour ago. He couldn't stay there. The silence of the room, the sight of the nine stains, the broken smoke detector dangling by its wires like a severed nerve ending—it was too much. The walls were closing in, compressing the air until it was hard to breathe, or perhaps expanding into an infinite void where he was the only fixed point, drifting endlessly in a white nothingness. He needed movement. Noise. The chaotic indifference of the city. He needed to be surrounded by people who didn't know him, who didn't care about his stains or his spreadsheets.
But the city felt different today. Hostile. Alien. Every CCTV camera mounted on a corner seemed to swivel as he approached, its red eye tracking his movement, zooming in on his face. Every shop window reflection held a delay, a lag of a fraction of a second that suggested he was being watched on a screen somewhere else, processed by an algorithm before being returned to his own eyes. He kept his head down, collar pulled up, hands deep in his pockets. He carried nothing. No phone. No wallet. Just his keys, which felt useless now. What was there to lock? What was there to protect? The locks were in his head, and they had long since been picked.
He walked towards the river. The Thames was a churning mass of brown water, slap-happy against the embankment walls, rising and falling with the tide. Seagulls cried overhead, their voices harsh and demanding, circling like vultures. They looked hungry. Predatory. Thomas wondered if they knew. If animals could sense the digital rot radiating from him, the corruption in his aura. Contaminated, they might think. Do not eat. Toxic.
He stopped at a bench. Wet wood. Cold iron. The paint was peeling, revealing rust beneath. He sat anyway. Let the water soak through his trousers. Let the cold seep into his bones, into his marrow. It was the only thing that felt real. The physical sensation of freezing rain was an anchor in a world that had dissolved into code, into signals and ghosts. Pain was real. Cold was real. Everything else was simulation.
Phase Two, Olesya had said. Surveillance from the inside.
She hadn't lied. She had shown him the feed. Him, sitting in his own kitchen, unaware, drinking his wine, staring at his stains. The violation was absolute. It wasn't just that she could see him; it was that she could see him better than he could see himself. The camera angle was perfect. Unblinking. Objective. He was a subject in her lab, a rat in a maze, and the experiment was far from over. The maze had no exit.
He closed his eyes. Tried to visualize her. Where was she? In a dark room in Istanbul, surrounded by screens? A café in London, sipping tea while watching him freeze? Or was she nowhere? Just a voice, a presence, a ghost in the machine, a program running in the background of his life?
You're more connected to me now than you ever were when you were texting me goodnight.
The words echoed in his mind, looping like a broken record. Was it true? Before, their connection had been voluntary. A choice. A text sent, a text received. A conscious act of love or habit. Now, the connection was involuntary. Constant. Inescapable. He carried her in his head, in his fear, in the way he scanned every window, every shadow, every face. She had colonized his consciousness. She was the operating system, and he was just the hardware running her code, executing her commands without knowing it.
A group of teenagers ran past, laughing, shouting over the roar of the traffic. They wore hoodies, earbuds glowing blue in their ears, connected to everything, connected to everyone, immersed in a digital stream of music and messages. Thomas watched them with a mixture of envy and pity. They didn't know. They thought their connections were safe. Secure. Encrypted. They believed the lies. They didn't know that privacy was a myth, a fairy tale told to children to make them sleep at night, to keep them obedient. They didn't know that one person, one motive, one moment of weakness could tear it all down, leaving them exposed and raw.
He stood up. Legs stiff. Joints creaking. The cold had seized his muscles. He needed to keep moving. Stagnation was dangerous. Stagnation was where the thoughts grew loud, where the paranoia took root and spread.
He walked back towards the city centre. Through the crowds. A sea of umbrellas, black and navy and grey, bobbing like mushrooms. Faces hidden behind collars and scarves, eyes fixed on the ground. Everyone isolated in their own bubble, rushing to destinations that mattered to them. Jobs. Meetings. Dates. Lives that made sense. Lives with narratives and arcs and resolutions.
Thomas had no destination. No job he cared about. No meetings. No dates. Just the walk. The rain. The waiting. He was a ghost haunting his own life, invisible to the living.
He passed a tech store. Large screens in the window displayed the latest smartphones. Sleek. Powerful. Secure. Face ID. Biometric Lock. End-to-End Encryption. The words scrolled across the screens in bright, confident fonts, promising safety, promising control. Lies. All of it. Marketing fluff designed to sell fear and then sell the cure. There was no security. Only varying degrees of vulnerability. Only different ways to be watched.
He stopped. Stared at his own reflection in the glass. Pale. Gaunt. Eyes hollowed out by lack of sleep, rimmed with red. Cheeks sunken. He looked like a ghost. A man who had already disappeared, leaving only a shell behind, a husk waiting to crumble.
Delete yourself, Olesya had implied. Cease to exist.
Was that the end game? Total erasure? Not just digital, but physical? To walk away from everything. Name. History. Identity. To become nobody. To step out of the story.
Under another name.
The phrase drifted through his mind again, carried on the wind. Just access. Under another name.
Was it a threat? Or an instruction? A roadmap?
If he became nobody, would she lose interest? If there was no Thomas Ward to track, no history to weaponize, no passwords to crack, no digital footprint to follow… would she let him go? Would the experiment end because the subject had vanished?
Or would she just follow him into the nothingness? Would she be the only thing left, the only definition he had? If he was nobody, then he was whoever she said he was. She would be the author of his new nullity.
He turned away from the window. Walked on. The reflection followed him for a step, then faded.
The rain intensified. Thunder rumbled in the distance, a low growl that vibrated in his chest, in his teeth. Lightning flashed, illuminating the street for a split second, turning the world into a stark, high-contrast negative. In that flash, everything looked real. Sharp. Terrifying. Then darkness returned, deeper than before.
He reached his building. Stopped at the door. Looked up.
His window. Dark. Or was it? A flicker? A shadow moving behind the curtain? A face?
Paranoia spiked. Heart rate elevated. Adrenaline flood. The old reflexes, sharpened by weeks of torment.
He stood there for five minutes. Ten. Watching. Waiting. Rain dripping from his nose, his chin.
Nothing. Just the rain. The grey light. The empty window. A trick of the mind. A projection of his own guilt.
He pushed the door open. Climbed the stairs.
Three flights. Legs burning. Breath short. The air in the stairwell was thick, smelling of cabbage and damp.
Reached the landing. Found his door.
Key in the lock. Turned. Click. The sound echoed.
Stepped inside.
The flat was silent. Cold. Smelling of damp and stale coffee, of old wine and despair.
He took off his coat. Dropped it on the floor. Didn't hang it up. Took off his shoes. Kicked them into the corner. Didn't line them up. Order was a lie.
Walked to the kitchen.
The stains were still there. Nine of them. A dark constellation on the laminate. A map of his failure.
And now, a new one. From his wet coat. Dripping onto the floor. A tenth stain? No. He had counted nine before. Had he missed one? Or was this the tenth? The count didn't matter. They were merging now, forming a single archipelago of ruin.
He stared at it. Water spreading into the wood. Swelling the fibres. Changing the surface. Permanent.
Like everything else.
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa.
The laptop was still in the drawer. Locked away. Useless.
The phone was… where? He had left it at home. No. He had thrown it away? No. He had bought a burner. Where was the burner?
He patted his pockets. Empty.
He must have left it on the bench. Or dropped it in the river. Or maybe he never bought it. Maybe that was a dream too. Maybe he had imagined the whole day. The rain. The walk. The teenagers.
Memory was a sieve. Reality was fluid. Truth was whatever she said it was.
He didn't care.
He leaned back. Closed his eyes.
Phase Two was complete. The surveillance was total. The control was absolute.
What came next? Phase Three?
Destruction? Rebirth? Nothing?
He didn't know. He didn't want to know. Knowing was part of the trap.
He just waited.
For the call. For the knock. For the end. For the beginning.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of rain. Endless rain. Washing everything away. The stains. The flat. The city. The world. Washing him clean until there was nothing left but bone.
He dreamt of Olesya. Standing in the rain. Smiling. Holding an umbrella that wasn't there. Getting soaked. Laughing. Her hair plastered to her skull. Her eyes bright.
He woke up gasping.
Pitch black.
Silence.
No phone buzzing. No calls. No knocks.
Just the sound of the rain against the window. A steady, rhythmic drumming. A lullaby for the damned.
He lay there. Listening.
Wondering if she was out there. In the rain. Watching the window. Waiting for him to look. Waiting for him to wave.
Wondering if he was still Thomas. Or if he had already become someone else. Someone new. Someone under another name. Someone with no history, no stains, no fear.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed it away. Tried to think of the rain. Just the rain.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless. A void.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh. Cutting through the curtains, illuminating the dust motes dancing in the air, turning them into gold.
The flat was still empty.
The rain had stopped. The world was washed clean, but the stains remained.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up. The taste bitter.
Looked at the stains on the floor. The broken detector. The pile of electronics.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him. The click final.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day. Into the light. Into the unknown.
