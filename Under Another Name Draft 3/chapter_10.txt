Chapter 10

The first monthly transfer did not look like a decision.

It looked like housekeeping.

Thomas made it on a Tuesday evening after the dust had settled and the compound had resumed its ordinary noise. Air conditioners rattled along the accommodation block. Men came back from the dining hall carrying paper cups and bruised fruit. Outside, the gravel paths held the day's heat. The lawns had been washed clean by sprinklers and already carried a new skin of dust.

In his room, Sarah's suitcase stood open beside the wardrobe.

She had arrived two days earlier on the Tuesday convoy, exactly as her itinerary had promised. Her passport had passed through three desks, two scanners, one armoured vehicle, and the compound reception without argument. She had been issued a badge, a room key, a site briefing, and a welcome pack containing a map, a dining schedule, and a warning about shaking out shoes.

By the second evening, she had rearranged the room.

Her laptop sat on the desk with a folding stand beneath it. His notebooks had moved to the shelf. The kettle had been placed near the socket that did not spark. Her shirts hung by colour. His field trousers stayed rolled in the lower drawer. No one, she said, needed to look at that much beige.

The room was still narrow.

It now had a centre.

'You have dust in your keyboard,' she said.

'Everything has dust in it.'

'Not everything. My suitcase is making a heroic stand.'

'Give it time.'

She was sitting cross-legged on the bed, laptop open, headphones around her neck. A client deck glowed on the screen. The words strategic capability framework sat above a diagram of boxes connected by arrows.

Thomas sat at the desk with his own laptop angled away from her.

Olesya's hospital invoice was open beside the spreadsheet.

The asthma attack had not ended cleanly. Nothing medical did. There had been discharge medication, one follow-up appointment, a nebuliser rental, and a school note stating that Anya was excused from physical exercise until reviewed. Each item came with paper. Each paper came with an amount.

The final hospital balance had cleared.

The room had existed. Anya had slept in it. Olesya had sent the discharge slip with the blue stamp at the bottom and the hospital bracelet lying beside it, cut open and flattened.

After that, there had been three days without a request.

Then:

Need inhaler refill next week. I will cover.

He had read it during a field-status call and answered after the meeting:

Send amount.

Olesya replied:

No. I said I cover.

He typed:

This should be budgeted.

He did not send it.

Budgeted was not a word that belonged in her kitchen.

He opened the spreadsheet.

Rent. School. Medicine. Transport. Clinic. Hospital. Refill. Official letter.

The rows no longer behaved like emergencies. They had begun repeating. Rent returned. Medicine returned. Transport returned. School appeared in uneven pieces, then larger ones. He could predict the next month before it arrived.

That was the second danger.

He created a new sheet.

Monthly.

Sarah spoke without looking up from her screen.

'If you are working, I am judging you.'

'You are also working.'

'Yes, but I am a hypocrite with a deadline.'

'That is a recognised category.'

'In consulting, it is most of us.'

She clicked through a slide, frowned, and deleted a line.

'What are you doing?'

'Budget.'

'Our budget?'

'Mine.'

'That sounds ominous.'

'It is mostly boring.'

'Boring can be ominous. It is where the serious things hide.'

She returned to the deck before he had to answer.

Thomas entered categories into the new sheet.

Rent.

Food.

School.

Medicine.

Transport.

Contingency.

The last word sat at the bottom looking more honest than the others.

He used conservative amounts. Then realistic ones. Then the amounts Olesya's receipts had already shown him. The total came out lower than expected, then higher after he included fees, then manageable after he removed any attempt to clear the old debt.

He did not touch the debt column.

He selected the first day of the next month and set a reminder.

Then he opened the transfer platform and scheduled nothing.

Scheduling would have made it visible in a way he was not ready for. He entered the amount in his calendar under a name that sounded like work.

Baku admin.

He looked at it for a while.

Then he changed it.

O admin.

The shorter name gave away less and meant more.

At the end of the week, Olesya sent the inhaler receipt.

She had paid it herself.

The photograph showed the receipt, a box of cartridges, and Anya's school timetable beneath them. The timetable had been altered in pen. Physical education crossed out for two weeks. Library added in the margin. Olesya's handwriting, narrow and exact.

Thomas sent the inhaler amount anyway.

For next refill.

The reply came the following morning.

You do not listen.

He was in the convoy yard when it arrived, waiting beside a row of vehicles with engines running and dust already lifting under their tyres. The security man checked names against a list. A mechanic banged a spanner against a tow hook. Someone shouted for water bottles.

Thomas typed:

I listened. I made it monthly.

He did not send it.

He typed:

Keep receipt.

He put the phone away before the dots could appear.

The field day ran long.

By the time he returned, Sarah had moved her laptop to the dining hall after the room's connection failed during a client call. He found her at a corner table under a television showing football without sound. She wore a white shirt, jeans, and the expression she used when pretending fluorescent lighting did not exist.

'There you are,' she said.

'Here I am.'

'You smell like machinery.'

'That is better than some options.'

She closed her laptop halfway.

'Eat.'

'I need to shower.'

'Eat first. You get strange when you are running on coffee and moral tension.'

'Moral tension?'

'It was either that or hydraulic fluid. I chose kindly.'

He sat opposite her with a tray of rice, lentils, chicken, cucumber, and a small orange still cold from storage. She watched him until he picked up the fork.

'Better.'

'You have become very compound.'

'I have become very bored. Different thing.'

The dining hall filled and emptied around them. Men in dusty boots. Engineers with badges turned backwards. Security staff in black shirts. Two women from finance carrying salads and speaking quietly about school fees in Aberdeen.

Sarah looked towards the television.

'I checked the joint account today.'

Thomas kept the fork in his hand.

'For what?'

'The Edinburgh hotel double-charged the card and I wanted to see if they had reversed it.'

'Had they?'

'Yes.'

She looked back at him.

'Your personal account is lower than usual.'

The fork touched the tray.

'I have had some Basra costs.'

'Basra reimburses.'

'Eventually.'

'How much eventually are we talking about?'

'Enough to be irritating.'

She opened the orange with her thumbnail, stripping the peel in one long piece.

'Do you need to move money from joint?'

'No.'

'Thomas.'

'No.'

'Fine.'

She placed half the orange on his tray.

'But do not subsidise a multinational out of your personal account. It offends my politics and our mortgage.'

'I will claim it.'

'Good.'

She resumed eating. The conversation closed under her hand. That was one of Sarah's mercies. She could leave a door unforced if the hinges looked strained.

Thomas ate the orange after the rice.

It was sharper than he expected.

On the first of June, the reminder appeared at 06:00.

O admin.

Sarah was asleep. Her breathing had adapted to the air conditioner's rattle. One hand lay open on the sheet, palm up. Her wedding ring had turned slightly on her finger during the night.

Thomas took the phone to the bathroom.

The amount was already in the spreadsheet. He opened the transfer platform and entered it without recalculating.

Recipient: Olesya Petrova.

Reference: June.

Purpose: Other.

Fee: accepted.

Delivery: same day.

He pressed confirm.

The screen gave him the reference number. He saved the PDF. He did not print it. Not with Sarah asleep in the next room and the compound printer in the office. He took a screenshot and sent it.

June.

The reply came at 09:18, while he was in the operations office reviewing pump vibration data.

What is this?

He stepped outside into a corridor that smelled of hot plastic and dust.

Monthly.

The dots appeared.

No.

He waited.

Then:

For what?

Rent. School. Medicine. Transport.

He could see her reading the list in the flat in Baku, or perhaps at the packing counter, phone held low so no one saw the screen.

The reply took eleven minutes.

This is not salary.

No.

What is it?

He typed:

Support.

He looked at the word and disliked its softness.

He deleted it.

Buffer.

He sent that.

Her answer:

For you.

He stood in the corridor with the phone in his hand. At the far end, a cleaner pushed a mop bucket through a strip of sunlight. The water inside it moved darkly.

For Anya, he typed.

This time the dots did not appear.

At noon, Olesya sent three photographs.

Rent receipt.

Bus card top-up.

Pharmacy balance.

Each had been placed on the plastic tablecloth, separated by a ruler, aligned for inspection. At the edge of one photograph, Anya's hand was visible, holding the ruler in place. The nails were bitten short.

Thomas entered the receipts into the June row.

The monthly system closed its first loop before lunch.

By the second month, the loop had a rhythm.

Reminder. Transfer. Screenshot. Receipts. Spreadsheet.

Olesya resisted in words and complied in paper.

July:

You sent too much.

Receipt attached.

The attachment showed three papers arranged on the tablecloth, each corner held down with something heavy. A chipped mug. A spoon. A small tin of tea. The rent receipt lay at the centre with its purple stamp pressed too hard into the paper. The bus-card slip sat below it. The pharmacy receipt had been folded and unfolded so often the print had begun to silver at the crease.

Thomas zoomed in.

The pharmacy total was less than she had expected. He knew that from the way the remaining cash was placed beside the receipt, notes aligned by size, coins gathered in a bottle cap. It was an answer before he had asked the question.

He entered the difference into the spreadsheet.

Under.

The word appeared in the variance column.

For three minutes he watched it sit there.

Then he changed it to:

Remaining.

In the evening, Sarah came back from the finance office with a brown envelope of petty cash and a printout stapled to it. She placed both on the desk and took off her badge.

'They reimbursed the hotel charge,' she said.

'Good.'

'And my taxi.'

'Also good.'

'And the airport lounge, which I had forgotten about and now feel rather fond of.'

She opened the envelope and tipped the notes onto the desk. The money lay between them with the loose confidence of money that had a proper route home.

'Do you need cash?' she asked.

'No.'

'Sure?'

'Yes.'

She counted the notes, wrote a figure on the printout, and clipped everything together. Her processes were not hidden. They were crisp and available, left in plain view amongst chargers, lip balm, and the compound map with her route to the gym marked in blue pen.

Thomas looked at the envelope.

Official money travelled with paperwork. It left marks in the correct places. It entered and left accounts wearing its own name.

On his screen, Olesya's July row glowed pale green.

Sarah leaned over his shoulder while the lid was still open.

'Spreadsheets after nine,' she said. 'That should be in the marriage contract.'

'I was checking one line.'

'That is how it starts.'

'What starts?'

'The decline. First one line, then tabs, then you are explaining conditional formatting at breakfast.'

He closed the laptop.

'Saved you.'

'You did.'

She kissed the back of his head and went to the bathroom. The shower pipes knocked inside the wall before water came through. A strip of steam appeared beneath the door.

Thomas reopened the laptop.

He moved the monthly sheet into a hidden tab.

August:

School books more this term.

I used contingency.

There were no photographs at first.

Only the message, sent at 22:41 Baku time. The hours between them had become another column in his head. When she sent late, he pictured the kitchen after Anya had gone to bed, plates soaking in the sink, the balcony door open a hand's width to let out the heat from the stove. When she sent early, he pictured the bus stop, the phone held in one hand, receipts folded inside the other.

Send receipt when you can.

Tomorrow.

The receipt arrived at 06:12 the next morning. Exercise books. Pens. A plastic geometry set. English reader. School uniform repair. Shoe glue.

The shoe glue made him stop.

It sat at the bottom, a small amount, nearly hidden by the pharmacy stamp from another shop on the reverse side of the paper. He enlarged the image until the pixels broke. The word remained legible enough.

Glue.

At breakfast, Sarah read emails on her phone whilst spreading jam on toast. She had stopped complaining about the dining hall bread and now treated it as a hostile material to be managed with butter.

'I have to extend a week,' she said.

Thomas looked up.

'Here?'

'Yes. Client moved the review. They want me in the same time zone.'

'Can you?'

'Already approved. Travel changed it. No drama.'

She said it whilst deleting a message with one thumb. A flight altered. A room extended. A badge kept active. The world rearranged itself for her with forms and confirmations, each one bearing her name.

'That works,' he said.

'Does it?'

'Yes.'

'You hesitated.'

'I was thinking about the seal replacement.'

'Romantic.'

'I do what I can.'

She smiled into the toast.

He nearly told her then.

The thought came without shape, more a movement in the mouth than a sentence. Olesya. Anya. Hospital. Rent. Monthly. The words lined up badly, none of them small enough for breakfast, none of them large enough for what had already happened.

Sarah's phone rang.

'London,' she said, and stood with the toast still in her hand.

The moment passed into the air-conditioning.

Thomas opened his phone beneath the table.

He sent the school amount.

September:

Anya needs dentist. I can use August left.

This time Olesya sent the receipt first.

It showed the dental clinic's headed paper and a small square of gauze still in its packet. At the bottom edge of the photograph, Anya's school shoe appeared, black leather cracked across the toe, one lace grey with dust.

Thomas added a note beside the September line.

Dentist.

Then he added:

Shoes soon.

He deleted it.

The spreadsheet should not predict everything. Prediction had a way of becoming permission.

In the operations office that afternoon, the printer jammed for the third time. Paper folded itself inside the tray with a dry tearing sound. The finance clerk stood beside it, holding a stack of expense forms and a paper cup of tea.

'Again,' she said.

Thomas opened the tray and freed the sheet.

'It needs servicing.'

'It needs retirement.'

She took the bent page and smoothed it against her hip. 'Yours?'

'No.'

'Then it might be approved.'

The men at the next desk laughed. The clerk did not. She wrote in red pen across the top of one form and stamped another. The stamp struck the paper with a hard little clap.

Approved.

Rejected.

Missing receipt.

Wrong code.

Every item had a box. Every box had a route. Even failure wore an official mark.

Thomas returned to his desk and opened the O monthly sheet. No boxes. No code. No line manager signature. Only a hidden tab and a woman in Baku sending proofs he had not asked her to learn.

When Sarah came in later with the revised travel documents, she placed them next to his keyboard.

'I printed yours too,' she said. 'Your next rotation.'

'Thanks.'

'You had three versions in your email. I picked the one that did not strand you in Istanbul for eleven hours.'

'You are a better logistics department than logistics.'

'Correct.'

She tapped the top page. 'Also, finance asked whether your Basra claim is still open. They say it has been sitting there for ages.'

'I will look at it.'

'You said that last week.'

'I know.'

'It is real money.'

He looked at the travel document. His name, route, departure, return. Each printed line clean and official.

'I will submit it tonight.'

'Good.'

She waited a moment, then left the office.

He did not submit the claim that night.

He reconciled September.

By October the calendar reminder no longer startled him.

O admin arrived amongst ordinary things. Generator inspection. Sarah London call. Water test. O admin. HSE review. Payroll query. The line sat there with the same grey text as everything else, neither larger nor smaller, absorbed into the machinery of the month.

He began doing it after tea, no longer before anyone woke.

The secrecy did not end. It changed posture.

At first he had taken the phone to the bathroom, held it low, kept one ear on Sarah through the door. In October he sat at the desk with Sarah reading on the bed and made the transfer whilst she turned a page. His hand covered the screen out of habit, then moved away. The transfer platform looked no different from banking, from bills, from anything a married man might do in the evening with numbers.

'You are quiet,' Sarah said.

'Just paying something.'

'Tax?'

'Worse.'

'Nothing is worse than tax.'

'School.'

The word came out before he had weighed it.

Sarah lowered her book.

'School?'

'An old colleague's daughter. They are in a tight patch.'

There it was.

A version small enough to live.

Sarah kept the book open against her knee. The cover had curled back in the heat.

'How tight?'

'Temporary.'

'How old a colleague?'

'Baku.'

'Oil world?'

'Adjacent.'

She looked at him in the light from the desk lamp. The air conditioner clicked, stalled, then resumed with a rougher note.

'You are helping them?'

'A bit.'

'With school fees.'

'Some costs.'

'Does this person have family?'

'Not useful family.'

Sarah's thumb moved along the page edge, bending paper and releasing it.

'You could have said.'

'It was messy.'

'Most decent things are.'

He nearly laughed, and did not.

She returned to her book, but not to reading. Her eyes stayed on the same line for a long while.

Thomas confirmed the October transfer.

The screen brightened.

Recipient: Olesya Petrova.

He angled the phone down.

No discussion. No announcement. He changed the number in the spreadsheet, then in the transfer. The increased sum moved through with its usual fee and reference.

Olesya noticed.

Why more?

Winter.

That is not an answer.

Heating. Medicine. School term.

You make seasons into lines.

He read the message twice.

Then he typed:

Lines pay bills.

Her reply:

Yes.

It was not agreement. It was a receipt for the fact.

The next day she sent photographs of the flat.

Not the whole flat. Never that. Parts of it, the room trusted only in pieces.

The heater against the wall, its enamel chipped at one corner.

The balcony door with a folded towel pushed along the frame.

Anya's school shoes on newspaper, newly blackened, the cracked toe still showing beneath the polish.

On the table lay the printed October confirmation with a pharmacy loyalty card on top of it. Olesya had weighed it down for the photograph. That was all.

Thomas saved the images to the wrong folder.

Pump Seals.

He saw the mistake and left it there.

Moving them would have required admitting that they had a correct place.

Sarah noticed again in November.

They were in the compound shop, a small windowless room that sold toiletries, batteries, chocolate, instant noodles, phone chargers, razors, and socks. Sarah had a basket over one arm. Thomas held a packet of coffee that had been imported at some cost from somewhere that did not drink it.

'We need to talk about your personal account,' she said.

He placed the coffee back on the shelf.

'Here?'

'No one is listening. They are all deciding whether toothpaste is worth twelve dollars.'

'What about it?'

'It is dropping every month.'

'I told you. Basra costs.'

'Basra costs do not arrive on the first of every month.'

He looked at the shelf. The labels were in English and Arabic. Mint. Whitening. Sensitive. Family pack.

'I am moving money into a reserve.'

'For what?'

'Contingency.'

She turned the basket handle against her wrist.

'That is the sort of word people use when they do not want a second question.'

'It is work-related.'

'Everything is work-related if you stand far enough away from it.'

He said nothing.

Sarah took the coffee from the shelf and put it in the basket.

'Fine. Keep your reserve. But if it touches joint money, tell me before it does.'

'It will not.'

'Good.'

She walked to the counter and paid with the joint card.

The receipt printed slowly. The cashier tore it off and handed it to her. Sarah glanced at it, folded it once, and pushed it into her pocket without looking.

Thomas watched the motion.

Some receipts proved nothing. Nothing had to be proved.

The stipend changed the messages.

Emergencies thinned. Updates took their place.

Anya passed English exam.

The heating works now.

Dentist says second appointment next month.

School wants parent meeting.

The money did not make Olesya expansive. It made her more exact. She sent fewer explanations, not more. Problems came already sorted, categorised, partially paid, waiting only for the column he owned.

Thomas began to answer less as well.

Good.

Send receipt.

Use contingency.

I will adjust next month.

The messages looked cold on the screen. They worked.

On Olesya's birthday in December, there was no reminder in his calendar.

The date arrived through a receipt.

She sent a photograph of a cake box on the kitchen table. The box was white, tied with red string, with the bakery name stamped in one corner. Beside it lay a receipt for a small amount and a handwritten note from Anya on lined paper.

Happy birthday Mama.

The writing had improved. The letters stood upright now, more certain in their spacing.

Olesya's message beneath it:

Anya used her own money.

Thomas was in a site meeting when it arrived.

The operations manager was explaining a pump seal failure. On the screen, a diagram rotated slowly, showing the part from every angle. Men argued about whether the replacement could wait until Sunday. Sarah sat two buildings away in the accommodation office, taking a client call with headphones on, her notebook open beside a cup of compound coffee she had stopped pretending to like.

Thomas looked at the cake box.

He had not known the date.

Or he had known once and let it sit outside the system until the system exposed it.

He opened the transfer platform under the table.

Supplementary amount.

Reference: Birthday.

The word looked wrong.

He changed it to:

December extra.

Then changed it back.

Birthday.

Purpose: Other.

Fee: accepted.

He pressed confirm.

The meeting continued around him. Seal. Pressure. Shutdown window. Procurement.

The confirmation arrived.

He took a screenshot and sent it to Olesya.

Happy birthday.

For dinner.

For seven minutes, nothing.

Then:

You did not have to.

He typed:

I know.

He deleted it.

He typed:

Enjoy.

He deleted that too.

In the end he sent nothing.

The screenshot sat above her message, brighter than the words beneath it.

That evening, when Sarah asked why he had skipped dinner, he said the pump seal meeting had run late.

'Did it?' she asked.

'Yes.'

She was brushing dust from the hem of her trousers with the side of her hand. The compound light made every thread visible.

'You have that face again.'

'What face?'

'The one that belongs to a calculation you have not shown me.'

'It is a pump seal.'

'Poor pump seal.'

She let it pass.

At midnight, Olesya sent a photograph.

The cake had been cut into three pieces, though only two plates were on the table. Anya's note had been moved beside the transfer confirmation. The confirmation was printed, folded once, and placed under the edge of the cake box, not with the receipts.

Olesya had written on the back in pencil. The pressure of the writing showed through the paper.

Birthday.

Thomas opened the spreadsheet.

December.

Rent. School. Medicine. Transport. Contingency.

He added one more line.

Birthday.

The cell accepted it.
