Chapter 10
The rain had returned. Not the fine mist of the previous week, but a driving, horizontal assault that turned the pavement into a river of grey sludge. Thomas walked without an umbrella. The water soaked through his coat within seconds, heavy and cold, clinging to his skin like a second layer of grief. He didn't feel it. Or rather, he felt it only as data: Temperature low. Saturation high. Discomfort registered. Everything was data now. Even his own pain had been quantified, categorized, and filed away in the spreadsheet of his mind.
He had left the flat an hour ago. He couldn't stay there. The silence of the room, the sight of the eight stains, the broken smoke detector dangling by its wires like a severed nerve—it was too much. The walls were closing in, or perhaps expanding into an infinite void where he was the only fixed point, drifting endlessly. He needed movement. Noise. The chaotic indifference of the city.
But the city felt different today. Hostile. Every CCTV camera mounted on a corner seemed to swivel as he approached. Every shop window reflection held a delay, a lag that suggested he was being watched on a screen somewhere else. He kept his head down, collar pulled up, hands deep in his pockets. He carried nothing. No phone. No wallet. Just his keys, which felt useless now. What was there to lock? What was there to protect?
He walked towards the river. The Thames was a churning mass of brown water, slap-happy against the embankment walls. Seagulls cried overhead, their voices harsh and demanding. They looked hungry. Predatory. Thomas wondered if they knew. If animals could sense the digital rot radiating from him. Contaminated, they might think. Do not eat.
He stopped at a bench. Wet wood. Cold iron. He sat anyway. Let the water soak through his trousers. Let the cold seep into his bones. It was the only thing that felt real. The physical sensation of freezing rain was a anchor in a world that had dissolved into code.
Phase Two, Olesya had said. Surveillance from the inside.
She hadn't lied. She had shown him the feed. Him, sitting in his own kitchen, unaware. The violation was absolute. It wasn't just that she could see him; it was that she could see him better than he could see himself. The camera angle was perfect. Unblinking. Objective. He was a subject in her lab, and the experiment was far from over.
He closed his eyes. Tried to visualize her. Where was she? In a dark room in Istanbul? A café in London? Or was she nowhere? Just a voice, a presence, a ghost in the machine?
You're more connected to me now than you ever were when you were texting me goodnight.
The words echoed in his mind. Was it true? Before, their connection had been voluntary. A choice. A text sent, a text received. Now, the connection was involuntary. Constant. He carried her in his head, in his fear, in the way he scanned every window, every shadow. She had colonized his consciousness. She was the operating system, and he was just the hardware running her code.
A group of teenagers ran past, laughing, shouting over the roar of the traffic. They wore hoodies, earbuds glowing blue in their ears. Connected to everything. Connected to everyone. Thomas watched them with a mixture of envy and pity. They didn't know. They thought their connections were safe. Secure. Encrypted. They didn't know that privacy was a myth, a fairy tale told to children to make them sleep at night. They didn't know that one person, one motive, one moment of weakness could tear it all down.
He stood up. Legs stiff. Joints creaking. He needed to keep moving. Stagnation was dangerous. Stagnation was where the thoughts grew loud.
He walked back towards the city centre. Through the crowds. A sea of umbrellas, black and navy and grey. Faces hidden behind collars and scarves. Everyone isolated in their own bubble, rushing to destinations that mattered to them. Jobs. Meetings. Dates. Lives that made sense.
Thomas had no destination. No job he cared about. No meetings. No dates. Just the walk. The rain. The waiting.
He passed a tech store. Large screens in the window displayed the latest smartphones. Sleek. Powerful. Secure. Face ID. Biometric Lock. End-to-End Encryption. The words scrolled across the screens in bright, confident fonts. Lies. All of it. Marketing fluff designed to sell fear and then sell the cure. There was no security. Only varying degrees of vulnerability.
He stopped. Stared at his own reflection in the glass. Pale. Gaunt. Eyes hollowed out by lack of sleep. He looked like a ghost. A man who had already disappeared, leaving only a shell behind.
Delete yourself, Olesya had implied. Cease to exist.
Was that the end game? Total erasure? Not just digital, but physical? To walk away from everything. Name. History. Identity. To become nobody.
Under another name.
The phrase drifted through his mind again. Just access. Under another name.
Was it a threat? Or an instruction?
If he became nobody, would she lose interest? If there was no Thomas Ward to track, no history to weaponize, no passwords to crack… would she let him go?
Or would she just follow him into the nothingness? Would she be the only thing left, the only definition he had?
He turned away from the window. Walked on.
The rain intensified. Thunder rumbled in the distance, a low growl that vibrated in his chest. Lightning flashed, illuminating the street for a split second. In that flash, everything looked stark. High contrast. Real. Then darkness returned.
He reached his building. Stopped at the door. Looked up.
His window. Dark. Or was it? A flicker? A shadow?
Paranoia spiked. Heart rate elevated. Adrenaline flood.
He stood there for five minutes. Watching. Waiting.
Nothing. Just the rain. The grey light. The empty window.
He pushed the door open. Climbed the stairs.
Three flights. Legs burning. Breath short.
Reached the landing. Found his door.
Key in the lock. Turned. Click.
Stepped inside.
The flat was silent. Cold. Smelling of damp and stale coffee.
He took off his coat. Dropped it on the floor. Didn't hang it up. Took off his shoes. Kicked them into the corner. Didn't line them up.
Walked to the kitchen.
The stains were still there. Eight of them. A dark constellation on the laminate.
And now, a new one. From his wet coat. Dripping onto the floor. A ninth stain.
He stared at it. Water spreading into the wood. Swelling the fibres. Changing the surface.
Permanent.
Like everything else.
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa.
The laptop was still in the drawer. Locked away. Useless.
The phone was… where? He had left it at home. No. He had thrown it away? No. He had bought a burner. Where was the burner?
He patted his pockets. Empty.
He must have left it on the bench. Or dropped it in the river. Or maybe he never bought it. Maybe that was a dream too.
Memory was a sieve. Reality was fluid.
He didn't care.
He leaned back. Closed his eyes.
Phase Two was complete. The surveillance was total. The control was absolute.
What came next? Phase Three?
Destruction? Rebirth? Nothing?
He didn't know. He didn't want to know.
He just waited.
For the call. For the knock. For the end.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of rain. Endless rain. Washing everything away. The stains. The flat. The city. The world.
He dreamt of Olesya. Standing in the rain. Smiling. Holding an umbrella that wasn't there. Getting soaked. Laughing.
He woke up gasping.
Pitch black.
Silence.
No phone buzzing. No calls.
Just the sound of the rain against the window. A steady, rhythmic drumming.
He lay there. Listening.
Wondering if she was out there. In the rain. Watching the window. Waiting for him to look.
Wondering if he was still Thomas. Or if he had already become someone else. Someone new. Someone under another name.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed it away.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh. Cutting through the curtains.
The flat was still empty.
The rain had stopped.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up.
Looked at the nine stains on the floor. The broken detector. The pile of electronics.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day.
