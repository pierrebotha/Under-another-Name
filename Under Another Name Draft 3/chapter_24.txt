Chapter 24

The deck light went off after thirty seconds.

The dark returned to the glass.

Thomas sat with the flight searches open and did not enter dates. Stavanger to Istanbul waited in one tab. Baku to Istanbul waited in another. The cursor blinked inside the departure field, clean and patient, asking for the one thing he had spent years supplying: sequence.

Sarah's words remained in the kitchen.

A chance requires another person reaching for it.

He closed the Baku tab.

Opened it again.

Not Baku. Tbilisi.

He typed Tbilisi to Istanbul and watched the options populate. Morning departures, afternoon departures, one evening flight with a connection too long to be useful. He opened a third tab and searched bus routes from the northern district to the Georgian border. The names appeared on the screen in a clean column: Qusar, Quba, Shamakhi, Balakan, Lagodekhi. Roads he did not know. Towns that held no memory of him. Borders that did not care what he had meant.

The investigator replied before midnight.

Crossing via Georgia theoretically possible. Subject would need to travel south-west, then onward. Not recommended without willingness.

Thomas wrote:

If subject agrees, can courier deliver documents and funds?

Possible.

Can local contact assist with border payment?

Need clarity.

Secondary facilitation fee.

Delay.

Then:

Possible. Risk high. Subject may still refuse at any point.

Thomas looked at the last line.

Subject may still refuse at any point.

He copied it into the Istanbul folder and left it there.

The next morning, Sarah found him in the study.

He had not slept. The curtains were open, and a thin winter light had reached the desk without warming it. On the screen were maps, flight options, hotel pages, visa notes, courier instructions, exchange rates, border names, a spreadsheet titled Istanbul route.

Sarah stood in the doorway with a mug in both hands.

'You are doing it.'

He did not turn.

'I am costing it.'

'No.'

He looked at her then.

'I need to know what is possible.'

'You know.'

She came into the room and placed the mug on the desk, away from the laptop.

'Have you written to her?'

'Not yet.'

'Then write before you build the world around her answer.'

He rubbed both hands over his face. His jaw rasped under his palms.

'If I write before the route exists, she will say no.'

'If the route is the reason she says yes, what is she saying yes to?'

He looked back at the screen. Istanbul hotels offered warm rooms, airport transfers, views over water, breakfast included. The photographs were full of white linen and brass lamps. He closed one.

'A way out.'

'Out of what?'

'The village. The debt. The men.'

'Towards what?'

He did not answer.

Sarah lifted the mug again, held it briefly, then set it back down.

'I am going to the office space in town,' she said.

'I thought you had no meetings.'

'I don't.'

She left.

When the house door closed, the study seemed to lose scale. The map on his screen filled the room. Roads, borders, airports, transfer points. Each line made the next line possible. Each point required the next instruction.

He opened the secure app.

Olesya's last message remained:

Stop.

He typed:

I know you asked me to stop. I will ask once. There is a route to Istanbul. No documents sent to company. No visa. No Norway. You choose after arrival. I can arrange ticket from Tbilisi, hotel, doctor, money, and a route for Anya after.

He read it.

Then deleted Anya after.

He typed:

Anya can follow if you decide.

He deleted that too.

In the end, he sent:

There is a route to Istanbul. No company. No visa. No forms. If you want it, I can arrange everything. You can say no.

The message delivered.

No blue ticks.

For three days, nothing moved.

He went to the office. He returned to the house. He answered calls. He signed off the Russian package consultancy. He rejected a cost forecast with wrong assumptions. He ate what Sarah placed on the table and sometimes what she did not. At night he left the app open beside the bed until Sarah turned over and said nothing.

The message remained delivered.

On the second night he opened the Istanbul folder and removed half of what he had put inside it.

Debt lawyer.

Norway route.

Document recovery.

Salary threshold.

He deleted the files, then restored two of them, then moved all four into a subfolder called Later. Later made them worse. He renamed it Holding. That was worse too. In the end he left the folder untitled, a blank icon beneath Istanbul, and closed the window.

Sarah came into the study with a basket of folded washing.

'There are three shirts here that need ironing,' she said.

'Leave them.'

'For Turkey?'

He looked at her.

She set the basket on the chair.

'I am not asking as a trap.'

'I know.'

'Do you?'

He turned the phone face down beside the laptop.

'The message has not been read.'

'And you are already packing.'

'I am not packing.'

Sarah lifted one shirt from the basket and shook it open. The fabric snapped once in the quiet room.

'This is what packing looks like before it admits itself.'

She laid the shirt over the back of the chair and left the basket there.

He did not iron the shirts.

On the third morning the investigator sent an unsolicited update.

Courier route remains possible for limited package. Subject still in district. No contact attempted.

Thomas wrote:

Do not approach unless I confirm.

Confirmed.

He stared at confirmed. The investigator used the word as a lock. Thomas used it as a bridge. Olesya had once used Received until even that had disappeared.

At lunch he stood in the office kitchen with a sandwich in its paper wrapper and watched snow collect on the outer ledge. The wrapper grew damp at one corner from his hand. He threw it away without opening it.

On the fourth morning, the ticks turned blue.

He was in a meeting with Hammerfest, camera on, whilst a man in a wool jumper argued about schedule float. The blue marks appeared at the bottom of the app window on his second screen. Thomas stopped writing mid-word.

'Thomas?' someone said.

He looked at the meeting screen.

'Repeat that.'

The man repeated nothing important.

Olesya's typing mark appeared.

Vanished.

Appeared again.

The meeting continued around him, a room of faces speaking through headsets.

Her answer arrived in two lines.

Why Istanbul?

Why not here?

He did not answer until the meeting ended. He closed the door, lowered the blinds, and placed both hands flat on the desk.

Istanbul is possible, he wrote.

Here is also possible.

You are not out of reach there.

No place is out.

I can meet you in Istanbul and arrange next step.

What next?

Doctor. Rest. Debt lawyer. Documents if you choose.

Choose what?

He leaned back.

The office light hummed over him. Outside the glass wall, people passed with coffee cups, access cards, winter coats, lives arranged by badge and calendar.

He wrote:

What you want.

Her reply came after eight minutes.

I wanted home.

The line did not move.

He read it once, then once more, then locked the phone.

At home that evening, Sarah found him printing the itinerary.

Not a final itinerary. A draft, he told himself. A set of options. Stavanger to Istanbul. Tbilisi to Istanbul. A hotel near the airport, then another in Karaköy with better medical access. Contact details for a cardiologist recommended by an expatriate forum. Courier instructions. Cash amounts. Emergency numbers. Clinic addresses. The pages came out warm from the printer and curled slightly in the tray.

Sarah picked up the first page.

'She answered.'

'Yes.'

'And?'

'She asked why Istanbul.'

'That is not yes.'

'It is not no.'

Sarah placed the page down.

'You hear absence as permission.'

'I hear a door not closed.'

'No. You hear hinges.'

He gathered the pages and tapped them into order. Sarah watched the gesture.

'Does Anya come?'

'Not first.'

'Then you know what she will ask.'

'I know.'

'And your answer?'

'Later.'

Sarah turned away.

'You should be done with that word by now.'

He put the itinerary into a folder.

The next morning Olesya wrote:

Anya will not leave.

He answered too quickly.

You can come first. Then we bring her.

No.

If you stay there, nothing changes.

Something changed. We are away.

You are in a village, working through Anya, hiding from men.

We are not in street.

You need medical follow-up.

There is doctor.

Where?

No.

He stopped.

The word still had its old power. No. Small, exact, no explanation trailing behind it.

He wrote:

I will not ask where.

Good.

Will you consider Istanbul?

No answer for the rest of the day.

At 23:18, she wrote:

If I come, what am I there?

He stared at the question.

The house was silent. Sarah had gone upstairs an hour earlier. On the table sat the Istanbul folder, a half-empty glass of water, and the investigator's invoice. The fjord outside had no visible edge.

He typed:

Free.

He deleted it.

Out of reach.

He deleted it.

With me.

He deleted that too.

He wrote:

You decide after you arrive.

Her answer came within seconds.

Always after.

He stood from the table and walked to the window.

Always after.

After Baku. After transfers. After debt. After hospital. After Norway. After Olesya first, Anya later. Every route he had built required her to enter the first half and trust him for the second.

He returned to the phone.

I can book Anya too.

She will not.

Then I will keep route open for her.

No answer.

The next afternoon, Olesya wrote:

I will try crossing.

Thomas read it standing in the office kitchen with a paper cup under the coffee machine. The cup overflowed whilst he looked at the screen. Coffee ran over the counter and dripped onto the floor.

A woman from finance reached for napkins.

'You all right?'

'Yes.'

He took the napkins from her and cleaned the counter.

I will try crossing.

Not I will come.

Try.

He wrote:

I will arrange everything. Do not travel if chest pain. Keep medicine with you. Courier will bring documents and cash. Tbilisi flight will be flexible.

Her answer:

No many messages.

He stopped typing.

I understand.

He deleted the line.

Yes, he sent.

The next forty-eight hours became itinerary.

Courier through district centre. Cash in two envelopes. A phone SIM with Georgian roaming. Printed flight confirmation Tbilisi to Istanbul, flexible fare. Hotel booking under his name and hers separately, one suite, one adjoining room he added and removed and added again. A clinic appointment that could be cancelled. A driver in Tbilisi. A driver in Istanbul. Emergency funds. A contact at the crossing arranged through the investigator, described only as secondary facilitation.

Each item produced a document.

Each document produced a folder.

The courier pack went first.

The investigator refused cash over the agreed limit, then accepted a smaller amount, then accepted the larger amount after Thomas split it between travel and medical. The distinction was false, but the form required a label. Thomas printed the ticket confirmation twice. One copy for Olesya. One copy sealed in a second envelope marked duplicate. He added a small card with the Istanbul hotel address and his phone number written by hand.

He did not write anything personal on the card.

Then he wrote one line on the back.

I will be there.

He looked at it for a long time.

The line made a claim larger than the route. He tore the card in half and wrote a new one.

Hotel:

Flight:

Driver:

Emergency:

Facts only.

He placed the SIM card in a small plastic sleeve, then placed the sleeve in the envelope with the ticket. The phone itself became an argument with the investigator.

Too visible.

Too expensive.

Too much to carry.

Thomas ordered it anyway through the courier contact: a simple handset, charged, sealed, number written on the outside. The invoice came through with a photograph of the device on a wooden table. Black phone. White charger. A hand at the edge of the frame holding the receipt down.

For an hour he watched the package move by messages.

Collected.

In transit.

Delivered to intermediary.

Subject notified.

Then nothing.

He waited at the dining table whilst Sarah read across from him. She had opened a novel but had not turned the page in twenty minutes.

'Has it reached her?'

'Intermediary.'

'That is not her.'

'No.'

The next message arrived after midnight.

Package accepted.

He put the phone down carefully.

Sarah closed her book.

'Accepted is not coming.'

'I know.'

'Say it like you know what it means.'

He looked at her.

'Accepted is not coming.'

The words did not help. They only proved he could repeat them.

Sarah watched him build it.

'You are not even hiding it now,' she said.

'No.'

'That is not honesty.'

'What is it?'

'The end of stamina.'

He looked up.

She stood at the kitchen island with the Istanbul folder open in front of her. Her finger rested on the hotel confirmation.

'A suite?'

'And another room.'

'For Anya?'

'If she changes her mind.'

'You booked a room for a girl who has refused to come.'

'I kept an option.'

Sarah closed the folder.

'Options are how you keep people inside the plan after they leave it.'

He took the folder from her.

'I am going to Istanbul.'

'I know.'

'I have to be there if she comes.'

'And if she doesn't?'

He did not answer.

Sarah nodded. It was a small movement, nearly formal.

'What will you tell me?'

'Supply chain meeting.'

'Don't.'

He looked at her.

'Please,' she said. 'Do not make me listen to the sentence.'

So he did not.

He packed two shirts, one jacket, the Istanbul folder, a document wallet, and cash divided between envelopes and a flat money belt he had last used in Iraq. Sarah watched from the bedroom doorway. She did not ask flight time. She did not ask hotel. She had seen enough paper.

At the airport, Stavanger was all pale wood and glass, people queuing without noise, children in padded coats, a man removing his belt before security with practised calm. Thomas moved through it carrying only hand luggage. His phone stayed in his palm.

Olesya sent one message before boarding.

Courier came.

All correct?

Yes.

Medicine?

Yes.

He stopped himself from writing more.

In Istanbul, the air met him warm and damp at the aircraft door.

The airport was too bright, too large, full of polished stone and perfume, voices carrying under the high ceiling, screens repeating destinations in rows of green and white. He passed through passport control, collected no baggage, and found the driver holding a sign with his name.

The city moved outside the car in wet evening light. Flyovers, mosques in the distance, blocks of flats, billboards, traffic splitting and rejoining with a discipline that looked like disorder only from far away. The driver kept one hand on the wheel and one on the phone mounted near the vents. Turkish radio murmured beneath the engine.

Thomas checked Olesya's flight.

Tbilisi to Istanbul. Booked.

Not checked in.

He checked again at the hotel.

Not checked in.

The suite overlooked water, though not the view in the photographs. The windows were wide and sealed. A sofa in cream fabric faced a low table. The bed was too large. In the bathroom, towels were folded with a band of paper around them. A small bowl of fruit sat on the desk beside a card welcoming him by name.

He moved the fruit bowl to the floor.

Then he laid out the envelopes.

Medical.

Food.

Travel.

Anya.

He had written the labels in block capitals on the flight, then regretted them. Now he turned the envelopes over so the writing faced down.

He placed the second room key beside them.

The second room was two doors down.

He went there after midnight, carrying the key card and nothing else. The corridor smelled of carpet cleaner and warm air from the lift shaft. A room-service tray stood outside another door, two glasses empty, a napkin fallen partly over the plate. He opened the second room and switched on the light.

Smaller. Two single beds. A desk. A chair. No view worth selling. The room had been made for transit, for people arriving late and leaving before the city could attach itself to them.

He stood between the beds.

For Anya, he had told himself.

Anya had refused. Olesya had refused for her. Or perhaps Anya had refused for herself. The distinction had been turned away at the door.

He placed the spare key on the desk and left the room open while the corridor air entered. Then he turned off the light and closed the door.

Back in the suite, he opened the minibar and removed everything: little bottles, chocolate, nuts, a tin of Turkish delight, two glass bottles of tonic. He placed them all inside the wardrobe. The fridge light exposed the empty shelves.

He walked to a shop near the hotel and bought water, plain bread, yoghurt, apples, crackers, tea bags, a packet of sanitary pads he picked up too quickly and then held properly, forcing his hand not to hide it in the basket. The cashier did not look at him. Outside, a man hosed the pavement though rain had already washed it clean.

In the room, he put the food in the fridge and the pads in the bathroom drawer. Then he opened the drawer again and moved them to the counter where they could be seen.

Every act arranged a woman who had not arrived.

At 21:40, the investigator wrote:

Subject departed village. Travelling towards crossing.

Thomas sat on the sofa with his shoes still on.

At 23:05:

Subject in vehicle. Local contact confirms movement.

At 01:12:

No update.

He did not sleep. He lay on top of the bedspread and watched the ceiling change with passing headlights from the road below. The room smelled of hotel linen, citrus cleaner, and the faint damp of a city near water. The air-conditioning clicked on and off with a polite mechanical breath.

At 02:30, he called the front desk and asked if the kitchen could prepare soup in the morning.

'For breakfast, sir?'

'For later.'

'We have room service.'

'Plain soup.'

'Lentil?'

'Yes.'

'What time?'

He looked at the flight status again.

'Keep it available.'

The clerk paused.

'Sir?'

'Never mind.'

He ended the call.

At 03:15, Sarah messaged.

Are you there?

Yes.

Is she?

Not yet.

Sarah did not answer for a long time.

Then:

Do not confuse waiting with being owed.

He placed the phone face down on the bed.

At 06:18:

Subject near crossing area.

He stood and put on the shirt he had hung in the wardrobe. His hands had begun to shake at the cuffs, so he left the top button undone and put on the jacket.

Olesya did not write.

At 08:03, the investigator wrote:

Secondary fee paid.

Thomas read the words three times.

Paid.

The edge had been reached. Not imagined, not mapped, not arranged in a folder. Olesya was at the crossing point with cash he had sent, medicine in a bag, a flight waiting in Tbilisi, a hotel room in Istanbul, a second key on the desk.

He opened the flight check-in page.

Passenger not checked in.

At 09:20:

Delay at crossing.

At 10:11:

Subject still at crossing.

At 10:38:

Local contact reports subject stepped away from queue.

Thomas stood by the window. The city below had entered morning. Ferries moved over the water. A man on the pavement arranged simit rings on a cart, each one placed by hand into a neat circle. A delivery van reversed towards a loading bay and a hotel porter lifted one palm to guide it.

At 11:07:

Subject not answering local contact.

Thomas called Olesya.

The app rang once.

No answer.

He wrote:

Are you at crossing?

Sent.

No blue ticks.

He called the investigator.

'Tell me exactly,' Thomas said.

The man on the line had background noise behind him, traffic or a café, voices he could not place.

'She arrived. She paid the secondary fee. She stood near processing. Then she moved away.'

'Moved where?'

'Back towards vehicle area.'

'Was she stopped?'

'No.'

'Did someone threaten her?'

'No report.'

'Did she cross?'

'No.'

The word did not echo. It removed the next room.

'Find her.'

'We are trying to confirm. Local contact cannot force engagement.'

'I did not ask him to force.'

'Then wait.'

Thomas ended the call.

At 12:02, the Tbilisi flight closed check-in.

At 12:40, it departed.

Thomas watched the status change from boarding to departed. The screen did it without drama. A line of text moving to its next correct state.

Olesya wrote at 13:16.

I went back.

He sat down on the edge of the bed.

The room had been prepared for arrival.

That was what altered it. Her absence stood among the evidence of his preparation. The water in the fridge. The spare key. The second room two doors away. The cardigan he had not bought and should not have imagined. The clinic number written on hotel paper. The envelopes face down on the desk, each one a small, labelled answer to a question she had not asked.

He stood and gathered the envelopes into one pile.

Then he spread them out again.

Medical.

Food.

Travel.

Anya.

The labels were visible now. There was no use turning them over. He read them as a clerk might read a file, as Sarah might read them if she entered the room, as Olesya would have read them had she crossed the glass and metal and passport booths and arrived at the door.

Not wife.

Not home.

He opened the second room on the hotel app and cancelled it. A warning appeared: cancellation fee applies.

He accepted.

The app thanked him for updating his booking.

He put the phone down and looked at the door between the suite and the corridor. No one knocked. No luggage wheels stopped outside. No woman stood there with medicine in her bag and the crossing behind her.

Why?

The message delivered.

Blue ticks.

No reply.

He wrote:

Are you with Anya?

No reply.

He wrote:

Are you all right?

He deleted all right.

Are you out of reach?

No reply.

He wrote:

Why?

The typing mark appeared.

Vanished.

Appeared again.

Her answer came after nine minutes.

You made room in Istanbul.

Then:

Not home.

He looked at the envelopes on the desk. Medical. Food. Travel. Anya. All face down, and still their labels seemed to rise through the paper.

He typed:

It could become home.

No.

You did not even come to see.

I saw.

What did you see?

No answer.

He stood and went to the bathroom. The mirror showed him in a shirt creased from the flight, hair flattened on one side, eyes cut by the white hotel light. Behind him, through the open door, the envelopes lay on the desk in a row.

His phone vibrated.

Olesya:

You make door. Always door. I stand outside with Anya.

He read it with one hand on the sink.

Then another line:

I go back.

He typed:

To what?

No answer.

He typed:

Olesya.

No answer.

He typed:

Please.

The ticks turned blue.

No reply.

At 15:30, the investigator confirmed she had left the crossing area in a vehicle heading back north. No exact destination. No contact. No further engagement recommended.

Thomas walked to the desk and picked up the envelope marked Anya. The paper had softened at one corner from his hand. He opened it and looked at the cash inside, counted and banded, useless in the room where it had waited.

He put it back.

Outside, Istanbul continued. Ferries. Horns. A call to prayer reached the sealed glass as a thin vibration in the frame. The hotel corridor clicked with doors opening and closing, luggage wheels passing, people arriving with keys that opened rooms prepared for them.

Thomas opened the secure app.

Why?

He typed the word again.

Then he stopped.

It was already there.

Blue ticks.

No answer.
