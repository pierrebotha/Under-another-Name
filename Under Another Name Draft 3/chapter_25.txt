Chapter 25

Thomas remained in Istanbul for three days.

He did not go to the clinic. He did not use the second driver. He did not call the investigator except once each morning, and each time the answer came back smaller.

No contact.

No engagement.

Subject reported returned north.

Reported.

The word sat badly in the room. It gave him distance and denied him shape.

The envelopes remained on the desk until the second morning. Medical. Food. Travel. Anya. At first he left them face up, then turned them down, then moved them into the hotel strongbox. The box required a four-digit code. He entered Olesya's birth year, noticed the number under his finger, cleared it, and used the room number.

The room number worked.

On the first night, he ordered soup and left it untouched beneath its silver cover. On the second, he ate bread from the packet he had bought for her and drank water from the fridge. On the third morning, he threw away the yoghurt. It had expired that day. The small printed date on the lid seemed more definite than anything else in the room.

He kept the suite.

There was no reason to. The second room had been cancelled. Olesya had gone back. The flight had departed. Yet the suite remained booked, paid for, warmed, cleaned, entered each morning by a housekeeper who knocked twice and called through the door in Turkish before letting herself in.

He also kept the driver on call.

The hotel concierge confirmed it each morning with a small card placed under the door.

Driver available.

Vehicle waiting time charge applies.

On the first card, Thomas wrote no longer required and left it on the desk. On the second, he folded it in half. On the third, he put it into the Istanbul folder beside the cancelled room confirmation. The driver never called. A car existed somewhere in the city, or did not. A man may have sat in it with a paper cup of tea and his phone on the dashboard. There was no way to know, and no reason to ask.

The clinic called once.

A woman speaking English asked whether the patient would attend.

'No,' Thomas said.

'Would you like to reschedule?'

He looked at the envelopes.

'No.'

'We will cancel.'

'Yes.'

The cancellation email arrived within a minute. Appointment released. No fee charged. The clinic gave up the room more easily than he did.

On the second morning he was sitting by the window when she came.

She saw the envelopes on the desk, the unopened soup, the water bottles lined beside the sofa, the folded towels in the bathroom still held by paper bands. She cleaned without looking at him directly. She made the bed he had not slept in, replaced the unused soap, emptied a bin containing only torn hotel stationery and the cardboard sleeve from the SIM card.

When she left, the room looked newly prepared.

That was worse.

At 11:20 on the third day, Thomas opened the secure app and wrote:

Are you with Anya?

The message did not deliver.

One grey clock.

He waited.

The clock remained.

He closed the app, reopened it, refreshed the connection, switched from hotel wireless to mobile data. The clock stayed beside the message, patient and useless.

He wrote:

Olesya.

The app froze.

Then a grey notification appeared at the bottom of the screen.

User not found.

He did not move.

The room continued around the message. Air-conditioning. Distant traffic. A door closing somewhere down the corridor. The small refrigerator humming inside the cabinet. Outside the window, a ferry crossed the water with a white wake unfolding behind it.

User not found.

He touched the screen once.

The conversation was still there. Years of fragments remained above the error. Baku. Receipts. Hospital. Received. Stop. Door. Anya. Why. The words existed in his device, stored in a place the app still allowed him to see. But the route had closed at the other end. He could read the corridor after the door had been removed.

He tried the old number outside the app.

The call failed before ringing.

He checked the contact card. Olesya's name, one old number, one newer number, an email she had not used in years, a note field containing bank details that no longer worked. The contact photograph was blank. He had no image in the phone that belonged to the present. Only documents, receipts, screenshots, reports.

He opened the note field and deleted the bank details.

Then he restored them from memory before saving.

He tried Anya's email.

No subject.

Please confirm your mother is well.

The message sent.

No reply came.

He tried the investigator.

Can you confirm welfare without contact?

The answer arrived at dusk.

No current issue observed. Further monitoring not recommended without subject consent.

Subject consent.

He put the phone on the desk beside the envelopes.

At reception, the clerk asked if everything had been satisfactory.

'Yes,' Thomas said.

'Will you require transport to the airport?'

'Already arranged.'

'Very good, sir.'

The clerk handed him the invoice in a white envelope. Accommodation. Room service. Cancellation fee. Driver. Laundry he had not used. The charges were exact. The failed room had a number. The uneaten soup had a number. The second key had no line of its own.

In the car to the airport, Istanbul moved in afternoon traffic, wet and bright after rain. Shop awnings dripped onto the pavement. Men carried trays of tea through narrow gaps between cars. A woman in a red coat lifted a child over a puddle and set him down on the other side. The driver listened to a football broadcast at low volume and cursed once under his breath at a taxi that cut across him.

Thomas checked the app again.

User not found.

At the airport, the departures screen showed his flight on time. He stood beneath it longer than he needed to, watching cities change rows: Oslo, Doha, Frankfurt, Baku, Tbilisi, London. The screen did not care which names belonged to him. It moved them all with the same clean authority.

He bought nothing in duty free.

On the flight back, he opened the Istanbul folder and deleted the clinic appointment, the driver contact, the hotel confirmations, the courier instructions, the duplicate ticket. The airline wireless failed twice. Each deletion waited, then completed.

He did not delete the message thread.

In Stavanger, the cold met him before the terminal doors opened. It pressed through the aircraft bridge, through the gaps at his collar, through the shirt he had worn too long. The air smelled of wet wool, coffee, and something metallic from the runway.

Sarah was not at the airport.

He had not asked her to be.

The taxi drove him home through a dusk that had already settled into the water. Houses glowed on the slopes above the fjord. Snow lay in old ridges along the road, grey at the edges, clean only where no one had touched it.

Sarah was in the living room when he entered.

She had a brief open on her lap, paper clipped at the top, pen resting across it. She looked up when he placed the suitcase beside the door.

'Did she come?'

He stood with one hand still on the suitcase handle.

'No.'

Sarah closed the brief.

'Did you see her?'

'No.'

The house held the two answers without echo.

Sarah set the pen on the table.

'Is it finished?'

He looked at the window. Their reflections sat apart in the glass.

'The app is gone.'

'Gone?'

'User not found.'

Sarah took that in. Her face did not change at once. Then she looked down at the brief on her lap, at the pen, at her own hands.

'I see.'

He wanted her to ask something else. He wanted an instruction, an accusation, a line he could push against. She gave him none.

'I am going upstairs,' she said.

'All right.'

She gathered her papers, tapped the edges into order, and left the room.

Thomas remained by the suitcase.

He carried it upstairs after the house had gone quiet.

The bedroom lamps were off. Sarah had closed the curtains, but a strip of fjord light showed beneath them, pale and restless. Her breathing came from the far side of the bed, measured, turned away. He placed the suitcase on the floor rather than the stand, opened it, then left it open.

In the morning she stepped around it without comment.

The next morning he unpacked.

Two shirts into laundry. Jacket onto hanger. Istanbul folder into the study drawer. Money belt emptied onto the desk. The envelopes went into a metal cash box he had used for project petty cash years earlier. Medical. Food. Travel. Anya. He removed the labels and put the blank envelopes back in the box.

The cash counted correctly.

That seemed improper.

He counted it twice more.

Then he stopped counting and separated it into two piles. One pile went into the metal cash box. The other stayed on the desk until noon, then went in as well. There was no category for returned rescue money. No field in any account for a sum meant to become a crossing, a room, a clinic, a second chance, and then becoming only paper again.

He opened the secondary account.

The balance had fallen to a figure too small to justify its own secrecy. Fees, couriers, investigators, brokers, Istanbul. Every hidden route had passed through it until the account looked like a room after furniture had been removed.

The bank portal offered options.

Transfer remaining balance.

Download statements.

Close account.

He downloaded the statements first.

The file names arrived in order: years, months, numbers. He saved them into Archive. Then he transferred the remaining balance to his main account with the description Personal reconciliation. The description looked absurdly clean.

Close account.

He clicked.

Reason for closure.

No longer required.

The bank accepted it.

Confirmation will be sent by email.

The email arrived within seconds.

Account closed.

There was no ceremony in it. The system had opened when asked and closed when asked. It did not know what had passed through it. It did not know which payments had reached a flat, which had fed debt, which had bought medicine, which had arrived too late, which had been refused, which still sat in blank envelopes inside a metal box.

He printed the closure confirmation.

Then he put it through the shredder.

The shredder jammed halfway.

He had fed the page in folded. The machine took the first half, strained, then stopped with the confirmation number still visible above the teeth. He switched it off at the wall, opened the head, and pulled the paper free in strips. Account closed became Ac, then count, then closed, separated in his hands.

He placed the torn pieces back into the bin.

An hour later he took them out again and tore them smaller by hand.

Spring came late to Stavanger.

Not as a change, at first. As a thinning. Snow withdrawing from stone. Water running under ice. A different light on the fjord, pale and brief, then gone. Sarah opened windows in rooms that had been closed all winter. The house began to smell of cold air rather than heating.

They lived in it carefully.

Breakfast. Office. Groceries. Calls. Laundry. Bills. Weather. A dinner invitation from Ingrid and her husband. A weekend drive Sarah suggested and Thomas accepted. They went to a village south of the city, ate fish soup in a café with steamed windows, walked along a harbour where ropes knocked against masts in the wind.

Sarah bought a wool blanket from a shop selling candles and ceramics.

'For the living room,' she said.

'Good.'

'You always say good when you have no opinion.'

He touched the blanket. Blue and grey.

'It suits the house.'

'That is not an opinion either.'

She bought it anyway.

At night, he sometimes opened the secure app.

The thread remained on his phone.

User not found.

He stopped writing messages. Then he stopped opening the keyboard. Then, for a time, he stopped opening the app at all.

The investigator did not write again.

Murad sent one message in April.

No more payout?

Thomas deleted it.

In May, Anya's email bounced.

He had sent one message only.

Medicine?

The reply came back with a mail delivery notice. Address unavailable.

He did not send another.

The days lengthened.

Sarah spent more time outside. She joined a walking group, then complained about the pace. She began learning enough Norwegian to order properly and correct him when he misread signs. Once, in the supermarket, she laughed with the cashier over a word he did not understand. He watched from beside the fruit display, holding a bag of apples, and for a second saw her as someone moving forward without asking the old room to come with her.

They did not discuss Istanbul.

Not directly.

It appeared in substitutions. Sarah asked whether he wanted the Istanbul folder kept in the study drawer or moved to storage. He said storage. She left it in the drawer. He asked whether she wanted to visit London in July. She said perhaps. Neither booked anything. She put the blue-grey blanket over the back of the sofa and he folded it each night before going upstairs.

One Sunday, she found the metal cash box while looking for tax papers.

He saw it on the kitchen island when he came in from the garage. Closed. Key beside it.

'I did not open it,' she said.

'I know.'

'Do you?'

He placed the car keys in the bowl.

'There is cash in it.'

'From Istanbul?'

'Yes.'

Sarah touched the key with one finger and moved it a little, not enough to pick it up.

'What will you do with it?'

'I don't know.'

'Return it to the bank.'

'I closed the account.'

'Then use another.'

He looked at the box.

'It feels wrong.'

'It is money, Thomas. It has no feeling.'

She stepped away from the island.

'That was the damage.'

The box remained there until evening. Then he carried it back to the study and put it on the floor by the desk.

He paid bills.

He attended meetings.

He stopped checking exchange rates.

For a while, he made small rules.

No app after dinner.

No investigator portal.

No exchange rate pages.

No old maps.

He wrote them on a notepad and placed the page under the paperweight Sarah had bought in Stavanger, a smooth piece of green glass with a bubble trapped near the centre. The rules looked childish there, pinned beneath something made for holding documents still.

The first rule lasted longest.

The app had nothing new to offer. User not found remained. The thread became a museum of old routes, and after a time the act of opening it began to feel less like contact than entering a room where dust had learned his shape. He stopped.

The second rule failed on a Tuesday.

He opened the investigator portal to download the final invoice for tax records. The case page still existed. Status: closed. Contact: inactive. Last note: no further action recommended. He downloaded the invoice, then clicked documents, then clicked photographs, then closed the browser while the thumbnails were still loading.

For the rest of the day, he saw the first photograph without opening it. The cinderblock wall. The snow. The taped window.

The third rule broke in the office.

A colleague mentioned currency movement against the manat during a finance call, a passing line with no relation to him. Thomas opened a rate page within seconds. Manat to kroner. Manat to dollar. Dollar to kroner. He looked at numbers he no longer had to use. The columns shifted with market movement, tiny changes inside a structure that had outlived its purpose.

He closed the page.

The fourth rule held until June.

No old maps.

Maps were the worst of it. They pretended to be honest. Road, river, district, border, station. They gave him distances that could be measured and routes that could be drawn. They never showed the refusal inside a person, the aunt's room, the bus station, the hand turning away from a crossing point.

One evening, he found the map still open from an old tab.

Qusar district.

He did not remember opening it.

He closed the tab and cleared the browser history. Then he opened the history panel to make certain it had gone.

Sarah saw the notepad a week later.

She lifted the paperweight, read the list, and put it back exactly where it had been.

'Are these yours?' she asked.

'Yes.'

'Do they help?'

'Sometimes.'

She looked at the rules again.

'You have made a system for stopping systems.'

There was no edge in it. Only fatigue and a strange accuracy.

'I know.'

'Do you?'

He picked up the notepad and tore off the page.

Sarah watched him.

'That was not what I asked you to do.'

He folded the page twice and put it in the bin.

The next morning he found it on his desk. Sarah had taken it out, smoothed it, and placed it beside the keyboard. No note. No comment. The green paperweight rested on top of it again.

He left it there.

Later that week, the walking group invited Sarah on a weekend trip. She packed a rucksack in the bedroom while he sat on the edge of the bed tying a shoelace he had already tied.

'You should go,' he said.

'I am going.'

'Good.'

'You don't need to approve it.'

'I wasn't.'

She zipped the rucksack.

'You were making yourself useful.'

He looked at the floor.

'Maybe.'

Sarah sat beside him, leaving a careful space between them.

'I am not leaving,' she said.

He turned towards her.

'I know.'

'No. You don't. You keep treating every distance as departure.'

Outside the bedroom window, the fjord moved under a weak sun. A boat cut across it, small and white, then vanished behind the line of the neighbouring house.

'What should I do with that?' he asked.

Sarah stood and lifted the rucksack.

'Nothing. I am telling you where the edge is.'

She left early the next morning.

The house changed with her absence. Not empty. Unwitnessed. The blanket stayed folded over the sofa. Her mug remained on the draining board. A note on the fridge said back Sunday, with the name of the lodge beneath it and no further explanation.

Thomas kept the rules that weekend.

No app.

No portal.

No rates.

No maps.

He cleaned the garage. He sorted old cables. He put the Istanbul folder into a storage box and carried it to the cupboard under the stairs. Halfway there, he opened it and removed the hotel invoice. He could not have said why. The invoice went into the study drawer. The rest went under the stairs.

On Sunday afternoon, before Sarah returned, he found the old phone from Iraq in a box of adapters.

It did not turn on until he charged it for twenty minutes.

When it woke, the date was wrong. The wallpaper was a photograph of the Baku office window taken from his desk: the Caspian beyond glass, a crane arm, the corner of a file cabinet. He had forgotten taking it. The phone had no SIM, no app access, no messages that mattered. Yet the object itself carried a version of his hand. Old scratches near the charging port. Dust in the case seam. A dent from a fall on a rig stair.

He switched it off and placed it back in the box.

That evening, Sarah came home with wet boots and a paper bag of rolls from a bakery on the road.

'You look rested,' he said.

'I walked until my knees complained.'

'That sounds useful.'

'It was not useful. It was a walk.'

She placed the rolls on the counter.

'You should try doing something with no result attached.'

He opened the bag. The rolls were still warm.

'I made soup,' he said.

Sarah looked at him.

'Did you?'

'Yes.'

'No spreadsheet?'

'No.'

They ate at the kitchen island, standing, tearing warm bread into soup from bowls too small for it.

For that hour, he did not check anything.

One evening in June, after Sarah had gone to bed, Thomas stayed in the study with the window open. The air carried the smell of water and cut grass. Somewhere down the road, someone was burning wood in a small garden stove. The sky had not gone dark, though it was close to midnight. Norway held light in the edges of things.

He opened a browser.

Then closed it.

Opened it again.

He did not use his usual profile. He opened a private window, then closed that too. Private still belonged to him. The search history could be cleared. The hand doing it would remain the same.

Weeks earlier, in a moment he had not named, he had made an account with an email that contained no part of his name. A string of letters. A number. A country setting chosen without thought, then changed. He had not used it.

Now he opened the site.

TikTok loaded with noise muted.

The page filled with faces he did not know, bright rooms, jokes, songs he could not hear. He dismissed them one by one. The search bar waited at the top.

He typed Anya's first name.

Too many results.

He added the village district in Cyrillic.

The results narrowed.

A bakery account appeared first. Then a school page. Then a profile with no surname, only a short handle and a photograph of a hand holding a paper cup.

He clicked.

The profile was public.

Seven videos.

No biography.

The first video showed bread coming out of an oven. The second, a road after rain. The third, a young woman stirring sugar into tea, face out of frame. The fourth had music attached and text in Russian across the top, too quick for him to read before it looped.

He opened the most recent.

Six seconds.

Anya stood in a small kitchen with the phone held too close. Her face filled the first second, then moved out of frame as she turned the camera. She was laughing at someone off-screen, her mouth open, hair tied high, flour on the sleeve of her jumper. The camera swung past a table covered with oilcloth, a stack of plates, a window with a plastic strip taped over one edge.

Then Olesya.

At the stove.

She stood in profile, stirring a pot with a wooden spoon. Her hair was covered with a scarf. The cardigan hung from her shoulders. One hand held the spoon, the other rested on the edge of the stove, fingers spread. Steam lifted into her face and clouded the lens for half a second.

Anya said something.

Olesya turned.

Not fully. Only enough.

The look went to her daughter, not the camera.

The loop ended.

Started again.

Anya. Kitchen. Table. Window. Olesya at the stove. Steam. The turn.

Thomas watched it once more.

The kitchen was poor. That was the first visible fact. Unpainted wall near the window. A cracked tile behind the stove. A shelf bowed under jars and tins. A towel folded over the back of a chair to hide a split in the vinyl.

The second fact came slower.

The surfaces were clean.

No paper stacks. No unpaid bills visible. No medicine scattered on the table. A pan lid hung from a nail. Cups stood upside down on a cloth. The stove had been wiped around the burners. Someone had placed two sprigs of greenery in a glass by the window.

The room had been kept.

He watched the video again.

On the fourth loop he noticed Anya's hand reaching into frame to adjust the heat. On the fifth, Olesya's shoulder dipping slightly before she turned. On the sixth, the way she did not look past the phone to find who else might be watching.

There was no message for him in it.

That was its force.

He moved the cursor near the heart icon.

Did not click.

The comment field waited beneath the video.

He did not open it.

The share arrow sat beside the others, bright and available. He did not touch that either.

The video looped.

Anya. Kitchen. Table. Window. Olesya at the stove. Steam. The turn.

In the hallway, a floorboard clicked. Sarah perhaps, or the house cooling around its own joints. Thomas lowered the laptop brightness until the screen no longer lit his hands.

He did not follow the account.

He did not save the video.

He did not write her name.

The account icon in the corner was blank, a small circle with no face inside it. The email behind it was not his. The window was private enough to vanish when closed, public enough to give him what no message could.

No route.

No claim.

No room.

Just access.

Under another name.
