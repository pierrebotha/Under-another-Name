Chapter 22

The message stayed on the screen after he sent it.

I cannot clear the debt.

The visa is cancelled.

The app gave him two grey ticks.

Delivered.

Thomas watched them until the phone dimmed in his hand. Outside, the fjord had turned from grey to black, and the last snow on the deck had hardened into a thin crust along the boards. The gull did not return. The railing held only a line of white where its feet had briefly marked the frost.

Sarah came back after seven.

He heard her key in the door, the pause whilst she took off her boots, the careful placement of them on the rubber mat. She had walked far enough for the cold to sit in her face. Her cheeks were raw. Her hair had flattened under the wool hat she carried in one hand.

'Did you send it?' she asked.

He looked up.

'Yes.'

She took off her coat and hung it in the hallway. Snowmelt darkened the hem.

'Did she answer?'

'No.'

Sarah came to the kitchen island and stood opposite him. The house lights reflected in the glass behind her, doubling the room, making a second Sarah stand faintly over the black fjord.

'What did you write?'

He turned the phone so she could see.

She read both lines.

Her mouth did not move.

'That is very little for what it ends,' she said.

'It does not end it.'

Sarah looked at him then.

'No?'

The phone vibrated on the table.

Olesya.

I know.

Two words.

He waited for more.

Nothing came.

Sarah saw the screen change. She stepped back from the island.

'I am making tea,' she said.

'Sarah.'

'Not now.'

She filled the kettle and stood with her back to him whilst it boiled. The kitchen was too quiet for a house with two people in it. Even the appliances seemed built to avoid disturbance. No rattle. No uneven compressor. No pipe knock in the wall. The kettle clicked off with one small sound and left the rest to them.

Olesya wrote again at 21:04.

You tried.

Thomas read the line and found no comfort in it. Tried belonged to failed machines and late payments, to men explaining why the result had not arrived. It had no place beside a hospital corridor, a debt paper, a daughter asked where husband was.

He typed:

There may be another way.

He deleted it.

He typed:

We can still clear part.

He deleted that too.

Sarah placed a mug beside him.

'Do not answer quickly,' she said.

He left the phone on the table.

In the morning, Olesya had written again.

I never expected you to clear it.

The message had arrived at 03:12. He found it whilst the sky over the fjord was still colourless and Sarah slept upstairs. The house held its warmth around him. Underfloor heat, sealed windows, a thermostat with a green light. He stood barefoot in the kitchen and read the line until the words separated.

I never expected you to clear it.

He answered:

Then why send the number?

The typing mark appeared, vanished, returned.

You asked.

He put the phone down.

The coffee machine ground beans. The sound filled the room with small violence. He placed a cup beneath the spout this time and watched the dark stream gather, obedient and hot.

Olesya sent:

You always ask for number.

He wrote:

Numbers can be solved.

Her reply came after a minute.

Not this.

Sarah came down in a grey jumper, hair tied back, feet in wool socks. She saw his face, then the phone.

'More?'

He nodded.

She poured coffee without asking him for one. Her hand moved slowly, not from sleep now, from caution.

'What is she saying?'

'That she never expected me to clear it.'

Sarah leaned against the counter.

'Did you?'

'Did I what?'

'Expect yourself to clear it.'

He looked at the water. The fjord sat under a low lid of cloud. On the road below, a cyclist passed in a yellow jacket, light blinking at the back of the helmet.

'I expected a route.'

Sarah closed her eyes briefly.

'Yes. You would.'

The phrase struck the soft scan in his head, absurdly, and then the moment moved past it.

'There is still money going out,' he said.

'Does she want it?'

'She needs it.'

'That is not the same.'

He opened the app again.

I will continue monthly support, he wrote.

Olesya did not answer for nearly an hour.

When she did, he was in the Stavanger office, sitting in a glass room with the door closed, watching snow move sideways across the harbour.

Keep.

One word.

He stared at it.

He typed:

For rent and medicine.

Keep.

You need recovery support.

No.

Olesya.

No.

He set the phone face down.

After lunch, she sent a photograph.

At first he thought it was a mistake. The image showed the corner of a room, badly lit, a cardboard box open on the floor. Inside were medicine packets, a folded cardigan, two cups wrapped in a towel, and the blood pressure cuff from the hospital photographs. The television wall appeared in the background, bare except for two hanging cables. On the windowsill stood a jar with coins in it and a dead plant in dry soil.

No caption.

He wrote:

What is this?

Packing.

For where?

No answer.

He enlarged the photograph. The box had once held cooking oil. The brand name ran across the side in red letters. Olesya had written MEDICINE on the flap in black marker, though the box held half the life of the flat.

He wrote:

Do not lift heavy things.

Anya lifts.

She has school.

Not today.

Why not?

Packing.

The answer did not accuse him. It did not need to. It placed Anya on the floor beside a box whilst he sat in a glass meeting room above the harbour, with a badge clipped to his belt and a calendar full of rooms reserved in his name.

He opened the recovery sheet he had once sent Anya.

Medicine morning.

Medicine night.

Blood pressure.

Rest.

No lifting.

No stress.

He closed it.

At three, Sarah messaged:

Do you want fish tonight?

He wrote:

Anything.

She replied:

That gives me nothing. I will buy fish.

When he came home, the kitchen smelled of butter, lemon, and the cold air that entered when Sarah opened the terrace door to cool the pan smoke. She had placed potatoes in a bowl and cut cucumbers with dill. The table was set for two.

'Before we eat,' he said.

Sarah turned from the stove.

'What now?'

'They are packing.'

The wooden spoon rested in her hand.

'Baku?'

'Yes.'

'To go where?'

'She won't say.'

Sarah turned off the stove and moved the pan from the heat.

'Then she has decided.'

'She is weak.'

'She may still have decided.'

'The debt men found the flat.'

'Then leaving the flat is not irrational.'

'Without telling me where?'

Sarah wiped her hand on a cloth and placed it beside the hob.

'Especially then.'

He looked at her.

'What does that mean?'

'It means she is removing the route.'

The words took a moment to enter him.

Sarah continued, quieter now.

'If the money brings the men, she has to break the path the money uses. That includes you.'

The fish cooled in the pan. Butter tightened around the edges.

'I am not the men.'

'No.'

'Then why treat me like them?'

Sarah took her time. She looked older in the kitchen light, not in the face exactly. In the way she stood without arranging herself for him.

'The path has the same shape from where she is standing.'

He picked up his phone.

Olesya had sent another photograph.

This one showed the hallway outside her flat. A suitcase stood near the door, old and soft at the corners. Beside it were two plastic shopping bags tied at the handles and a school backpack with a broken zip held closed by string. Anya's white trainers were on the threshold, toes pointing out.

Thomas wrote:

I can arrange proper transport.

No.

You cannot carry all this.

We carry.

Send address for pickup.

No.

Sarah watched him type and delete and type again.

'She is already answering you,' she said.

'She is refusing help.'

'She is refusing management.'

He looked at the phone.

The distinction was no use to him. It gave him nothing to do.

On the conference table in front of him lay the printed requisition decision from HR. After review, requisition not approved for progression. He had brought it to work without reason, folded into the back of his notebook. Now it sat there with the app beside it, two refusals in different languages.

At ten, the Hammerfest document control manager called.

'We are sourcing the Russian package recovery through consultancy,' he said. 'You know anyone legitimate?'

Thomas looked at the folded HR letter.

'No.'

'Unfortunate. Could have used that Baku person if compliance hadn't eaten it.'

'It was not only compliance.'

'No?'

'No.'

The manager waited, then moved on.

'I'll send you three vendors for comment.'

'Fine.'

After the call, Thomas sat in the empty room with the harbour cranes moving behind the glass. A loader reversed below with a white light flashing on its roof. The office made its small, controlled sounds: door seals, keyboard taps, coffee cups on tables, the soft ventilation running through the ceiling.

His phone remained face down.

At lunch he went outside.

The harbour air entered his coat through every gap. Snow had become rain by then, needling across the quay. Workers moved in marked lanes, heads low, their reflective strips catching light even in daylight. Across the water, houses climbed the hill in pale blocks, each one with a roof pitched against weather, each one with windows lit from inside.

He stood under an awning and opened the app again.

What do you mean keep?

The message delivered.

No answer.

He wrote:

Are you leaving Baku?

No answer.

He wrote:

Do not move until we discuss.

He did not send it.

He had no we left that would survive the sentence.

The reply arrived near four.

Landlord gave flat.

To who?

Other people.

Why?

We owe. Also men came.

When?

Yesterday.

Are you out of reach?

No answer.

He called.

The app rang. Once. Twice. Three times.

Then declined.

He wrote:

Answer please.

No answer.

He called Anya through the old email address, writing a subject this time.

Your mother.

He deleted it.

He wrote:

Please confirm you and your mother are out of reach.

He sent it.

Anya replied that evening.

We are packing.

Where?

North.

Which town?

Mother says not to write.

Are you with family?

My aunt.

Does she have space?

Some.

Is your mother well enough to travel?

No but she says better than here.

Thomas read the last line in the upstairs study, where the window faced a strip of dark garden and the neighbour's white fence. Sarah was downstairs making soup. The smell rose through the house, leeks and pepper, ordinary warmth.

He wrote:

Can aunt receive transfer?

No.

Why?

Mother says no.

Does aunt know about me?

No.

Will she help?

She helps. Family.

The word sat on the screen. Family. It required no affidavit, no conflict declaration, no salary threshold, no visa criteria. It had a door in the north, a room with some space, perhaps a mattress on the floor, perhaps a stove, perhaps a woman who would not ask for an explanation before boiling water.

He typed:

I can pay her.

No.

Why?

Then she knows.

Knows what?

No reply.

He put the phone down and opened a map of Azerbaijan.

Baku on the coast.

Roads north.

Sumgait.

Quba.

Khachmaz.

Villages he had no memory of crossing, though he must have driven near some of them during site visits years before. The map gave him names, distances, travel times, fuel stations, border roads, little blue symbols for pharmacies and hospitals. It made the country look open. A line could be drawn from one place to another with the finger.

Olesya would not give him the line.

He zoomed in until the screen filled with roads thin as scratches.

Sarah came to the study door.

'Food is ready.'

'In a minute.'

She looked at the map on his screen.

'You are not going to find them that way.'

'I am only looking.'

'No, you are searching.'

He closed the laptop halfway.

'They are moving while she is barely recovered.'

'And you are here.'

The line was factual. That gave it more weight.

At the table he ate three spoonfuls of soup and tasted none of it. Sarah did not make him eat more. She sat across from him, tearing bread into smaller and smaller pieces, crumbs gathering beside her bowl.

'If she sends a place,' Sarah said, 'what will you do?'

'Send money.'

'And if she does not want it?'

'Keep it available.'

'That is not doing nothing.'

'I cannot do nothing.'

'You may have to learn the shape of it.'

He looked at her.

'You think I should leave her.'

'No. I think she is leaving you.'

He pushed the bowl away.

Sarah did not reach for it this time.

He opened a transfer route.

Monthly support, full amount.

The old routes remained blocked or useless, so he sent it through Murad. Murad answered with his usual short confirmation.

Send card?

Same.

Bank still works?

Try.

Thomas sent.

The transfer failed.

Recipient declined.

He tried a smaller amount.

Rejected by recipient bank.

He wrote to Olesya:

Transfer rejected. Why?

Her answer came after dinner, when Sarah had placed bowls on the table and he had not touched his spoon.

I closed card.

Why?

Men know.

I can send new route.

No.

You need cash for travel.

No.

How will you move?

We have enough.

From where?

We sold things.

What things?

No answer.

Sarah put her spoon down.

'Eat.'

He looked at the bowl. Steam lifted from the surface and gathered on the underside of the pendant light.

'They are moving.'

'Where?'

'North. Sister or aunt. Anya will not say.'

Sarah's face did not change. She placed both hands flat beside the bowl.

'And Olesya?'

'She is closing accounts.'

'That sounds deliberate.'

'It is unsafe.'

'Both can be true.'

He pushed the bowl away.

'She rejected the transfer.'

'Then stop sending.'

'She needs travel money.'

'She is telling you not to send.'

'She is ill.'

'She is also the person living it.'

He stood.

The chair moved back too hard and struck the wall. The sound was small in the sealed house.

Sarah did not rise.

'Do not make me your obstacle,' she said.

He gripped the back of the chair.

'I am trying to keep them alive.'

'And she is trying to leave a place where your money can be followed.'

The line entered the kitchen and stayed.

Thomas looked at the phone.

Olesya had sent another message.

You cannot engineer this.

He read it once.

Sarah saw the screen from where she sat.

'There it is,' she said.

He did not answer.

Olesya wrote again.

This is our life. Not your project.

Project.

The word held.

It reached back through folders and spreadsheets, role descriptions, visa routes, debt notes, recovery schedules, hospital receipts, school fees, transfer references, every line he had built to make a life behave like something that could be managed from elsewhere.

Sarah stood slowly and took both bowls to the sink. She covered his untouched soup with a plate and placed it in the fridge.

'I am going upstairs,' she said.

He remained by the chair.

The app stayed open.

Olesya's last line sat bright on the screen.

This is our life. Not your project.

He typed:

I never meant to make you feel that.

He deleted it.

He typed:

I was trying to protect you.

He deleted it.

He typed:

Where are you going?

He sent that one.

The ticks turned blue.

No answer.

The next morning the transfer routes all failed.

Murad could not deposit to the old card. Cash pickup required a city and a name at a branch. Olesya would not provide either. The European platform remained closed. The bank transfer channel still demanded documentation. Crypto could move to Murad. Murad needed a recipient who would stand somewhere to receive it.

Thomas wrote:

Upstairs, the spare room held unpacked boxes from Delhi. Sarah had left them untouched. Linen. Books. A lamp wrapped in paper. On the floor near the window lay the Norway registration envelope, empty now, its contents submitted and accepted. Their tax numbers had arrived. Their residence cards would follow. The system had taken them in.

He stood by the window with the phone still at his ear.

Through the study window, a municipal worker pushed a snow shovel along the pavement though there was barely any snow left to move. The blade scraped over stone in a dry, repetitive sound. The man wore orange waterproofs and did not look at the houses.

Thomas wrote:

He looked at the line.

It carried no accusation. The absence made it harder. It was a receipt for an event already past.

The house kept the line overnight.

Thank you for hospital.

He saw it when he brushed his teeth, when he set the alarm, when he woke at 03:40 and reached for the phone before the room had settled into shape. It sat beneath unanswered questions and would not move. Hospital could be thanked. Transport could not be arranged. Debt could be counted. A location could be withheld.

At 04:12 Anya sent a photograph.

In the reflection on the glass, Anya's face hovered over the scene, half-transparent, with Olesya beside her reduced to a dark shape and a hand on a bag.
