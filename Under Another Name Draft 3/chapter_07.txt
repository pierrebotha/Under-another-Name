Chapter 7
The week did not begin with a bang, but with a low, humming static that seemed to vibrate in the fillings of Thomas’s teeth. It was Tuesday, or perhaps Wednesday; the days had lost their distinct shapes, blurring into a single, grey continuum of routine and vigilance. He sat at the kitchen table, the new laptop open before him. It was a sleek, silver thing, bought with cash from a shop three districts away where the assistant hadn’t looked him in the eye. A clean start. Or so he told himself.
The screen glowed with a spreadsheet he had created. Digital Audit – Phase One. It was a ridiculous document, overly formal, filled with columns for ‘Platform’, ‘Date Changed’, ‘2FA Status’, and ‘Risk Level’. He typed slowly, his fingers stiff against the unfamiliar keys. The tactile feedback was wrong; the click was too soft, lacking the satisfying snap of his old keyboard. Every sensation felt mediated, filtered through a layer of plastic and paranoia.
He had changed the password for his work email three times in two days. The first time, he had used a complex string of characters generated by a secure algorithm. The second time, he had decided the algorithm might be compromised, that the very concept of an algorithm was a trap laid by someone who knew how he thought. The third time, he had typed a sentence, a fragment of a memory he hoped no one else could access: The smell of wet wool in Marrakesh. It felt safer. Personal. Unhackable.
But was it?
Olesya had been in Marrakesh with him. She had held that scarf, the one that smelled of lanolin and dust. She had laughed when he sneezed, the sound echoing in the narrow alleyway of the souk. If she wanted to guess this password, she could. She knew the geography of his memories better than he did. She had mapped them, walked them, slept in them.
He deleted the sentence. Typed another: Blue tiles in the rain.
Deleted it.
Typed: 7#mK9!pL2$qR.
Confirmed it.
He added the entry to the spreadsheet. Work Email. Secure.
The word ‘Secure’ looked hollow on the screen. A lie printed in Arial.
He stood up, the chair scraping loudly against the laminate floor. The sound made him flinch. In the silence of the flat, every noise was an intrusion, a potential signal. He walked to the window. The view was the same as it had always been: the brick wall of the adjacent building, a sliver of grey sky, the occasional pigeon landing on the sill. But now, the window felt like a screen itself. A lens. He wondered if a camera could see him from across the gap. A drone? A long-lens setup in an opposite window? He pulled the curtain closed. Not all the way, just enough to break the line of sight. A compromise between light and safety.
The stains on the floor had multiplied. Five distinct dark patches on the laminate, a constellation of his unraveling. He avoided stepping on them, navigating the kitchen with a careful, dancing gait. Step over the wine. Sidestep the rain. Hop the spilled water. They were minefields. Markers of territory claimed by chaos. He thought about cleaning them. Getting a bucket, a cloth, some bleach. Scrubbing until the wood was bare, until the finish was stripped away. But the effort seemed insurmountable. And besides, if he cleaned them, what would be left to prove that anything had happened? Without the stains, the flat was just a flat. Empty, yes, but normal. And normalcy was the most dangerous illusion of all.
His phone buzzed on the counter.
He didn’t jump this time. The reaction had dulled, replaced by a cold, hard knot of dread in his stomach. He walked over slowly, looking at the screen.
Unknown Number.
It was the third call today. The second yesterday. A pattern was emerging, a rhythm of harassment that felt intentional. Was it her? Testing the new number? Or had she passed his number around? Sold it? A joke among friends in Istanbul? Here’s Tom’s new line. Call him and say nothing.
He let it ring. Six times. Seven.
Then silence.
No voicemail. Just a missed call notification.
He stared at it. The urge to call back was a physical itch under his skin. To dial the number, to hear a voice, to demand an answer. Who is this? What do you want? But he knew the game. If he called, he engaged. If he engaged, he lost. Silence was his only armor.
He turned the phone face down.
He needed to leave the flat. The air inside was stale, recycled too many times, thick with the scent of his own anxiety. He grabbed his coat, checked his pockets for keys, wallet, phone. He paused. Did he need the phone? If he left it here, he would be unreachable. Safe. But also blind. If something happened… what? What could happen? She was in Turkey. She wasn’t going to break in.
He took the phone. Shoved it deep into his pocket.
The street outside was busy. The lunchtime rush. People moved with purpose, heads down, shoulders hunched against the biting wind. Thomas merged into the flow, just another grey coat in a sea of grey coats. He kept his eyes on the pavement, watching the cracks, the discarded cigarette ends, the oily puddles. Don’t look up. Don’t make eye contact. Eye contact was an invitation. An acknowledgment of presence.
He walked past the newsagent. The headlines screamed about political scandals and celebrity breakdowns. Worlds collapsing in print. He didn’t stop.
Past the bakery. The smell of yeast and sugar hit him, sweet and cloying. It reminded him of Sunday mornings, before. Olesya buying a cinnamon bun, crumbs falling onto her jumper, laughing as she tried to brush them off. He pushed the memory away. It was a trap. Nostalgia was just another form of tracking.
He ended up in the park again. It was his default destination now. A neutral ground. The grass was muddy, churned up by dogs and children. The trees were bare skeletons against the low sky. He found a bench, far from the path, hidden by a cluster of rhododendrons. He sat down, pulling his collar up.
A dog ran past, a golden retriever, tongue lolling, eyes bright with joy. Its owner followed, shouting something about a stick. The sound was sharp, clear, human.
Thomas watched them. He felt a pang of something. Envy? Longing? It was hard to name. They existed in a world of simple causes and effects. Throw stick. Dog fetch. Happy dog. No hidden agendas. No digital leashes. No ghosts.
His phone buzzed.
He froze.
He didn’t want to look. He wanted to sit here, in this bubble of muddy reality, and pretend for five minutes that he was alone.
But he had to know.
He pulled the phone out.
Unknown Number.
Again.
He stared at the screen. The name blinked, mocking him.
He swiped to answer. Put the phone to his ear.
‘Hello?’
Silence.
Then, a click.
Then, breathing. Soft. Rhythmic. Female.
‘Olesya?’
No answer. Just the breathing.
‘I know it’s you.’
The breathing stopped. Then, a voice. Low. Distant. ‘Do you?’
‘Yes.’
‘How?’
‘The silence. The clicks. It’s your signature.’
A laugh. Short. Dry. ‘My signature? That’s flattering, Tom. I didn’t know I had one.’
‘Why are you calling? Why won’t you leave me alone?’
‘I’m not calling, Tom. You answered.’
‘You’re calling. Every day. Multiple times.’
‘Maybe I am. Maybe I’m checking to see if you’re still there. Still awake.’
‘I’m awake. Happy now?’
‘Are you? Are you happy, Tom?’
The question hung in the air, heavy and absurd. Happy? The concept felt alien. Like asking a fish if it was enjoying the water while the net was being pulled tight.
‘I’m surviving,’ he said.
‘Surviving is good. It’s a start.’
‘What do you want from me? Money? I don’t have any more. Apology? I’ve got nothing left to say.’
‘I don’t want anything. I told you. I’m just… observing.’
‘Observing what?’
‘The experiment.’
‘Experiment?’
‘See what happens when you remove the safety nets. When you cut the cords. Do you fly? Or do you fall?’
‘I’m falling,’ Thomas said. His voice cracked. ‘I’m falling apart.’
‘Good,’ she said softly. ‘That means you’re feeling gravity. That means you’re real.’
‘This is sick, Olesya. You’re sick.’
‘Maybe. Or maybe I’m the only one who’s honest.’
‘Where are you?’
‘Does it matter?’
‘Yes.’
‘Why?’
‘Because I want to find you. I want to look you in the eye and tell you to stop.’
‘You can’t stop this, Tom. It’s already done. The switch has been flipped.’
‘What switch?’
‘The one in your head. The one that says “safe”. It’s off now. Forever.’
Thomas gripped the phone tighter. His knuckles white. ‘I’m hanging up.’
‘Wait. One more thing.’
‘What?’
‘Check your email.’
‘What?’
‘Your work email. The one you changed three times today. Check it.’
‘Why?’
‘Just check it. Then hang up.’
The line went dead.
Thomas lowered the phone. Stared at the screen. Call Ended.
His heart hammered against his ribs. A frantic bird.
He opened the email app. Logged in.
One new message.
Sender: Unknown.
Subject: Marrakesh.
He opened it.
No text. Just an image.
A photo.
It was a picture of the alleyway in Marrakesh. The narrow souk. The blue tiles. The rain.
And there, in the corner of the frame, blurred but recognizable, was a scarf. The woolen scarf. Hanging on a hook in a shop window.
Taken recently. The light was current. The angle was from across the street.
She was there. Or someone was there.
Or she had sent it from a database. A memory file.
He didn’t know. He couldn’t know.
He dropped the phone onto the bench. It landed with a thud.
He put his head in his hands.
The world spun. The ground beneath him felt unstable. Nowhere was safe. Not his emails. Not his memories. Not even the past. She owned it all. She had colonized his history.
He sat there for a long time. The wind picked up, cutting through his coat. People walked past on the path. A jogger. A woman with a pram. A group of students laughing.
They didn’t see him. He was invisible. A ghost on a bench.
Eventually, he picked up the phone. Put it in his pocket.
Stood up.
Walked home.
The journey was a blur. He didn’t remember the streets, the lights, the people. Just the feeling of the phone in his pocket, burning like a coal.
When he arrived at the flat, he didn’t turn on the light. He dropped his keys on the table. Took off his coat. Kicked off his shoes.
Walked to the laptop.
Opened the spreadsheet.
Work Email. Compromised.
He typed it in. The keys clicked loudly in the silence.
He closed the laptop.
Walked to the sofa. Sat down.
Looked at the floor.
Six stains now. A new one from the rain on his trousers. A dark patch spreading on the laminate.
He leaned back. Closed his eyes.
The switch has been flipped.
Safe is off.
Forever.
The words echoed in his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed them away.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of emails. Thousands of them. Flying through the air like birds. Each one carrying a photo. Marrakesh. Istanbul. London. Him. Her. Everywhere.
He dreamt of switches. Rows of them. On a vast control panel. He tried to flip them back to ‘Safe’, but they were stuck. Glued. Broken.
He dreamt of Olesya. Sitting in the control room. Watching the screens. Smiling. Flipping switches. Turning off the lights. One by one.
He woke up gasping.
The room was pitch black.
He sat up. Disoriented.
The phone.
It was buzzing.
He fumbled for it. Knocked the glass of water off the table. It rolled across the floor. Spilled more water onto the carpet. A seventh stain.
He didn’t care. Grabbed the phone.
Unknown Number.
He answered.
‘Hello?’
Silence.
Then, a click. Then, silence again.
Then the recording ended.
No voice. No breathing. Just a call that connected and then said nothing.
He lowered the phone. Stared at it.
Was it her? Testing the line? Seeing if he would answer? Or just a wrong number?
It didn’t matter. The result was the same. Silence.
He put the phone down.
Lay back on the sofa. Pulled the blanket over himself.
Tomorrow he would change the email again. Delete the account. Create a new identity. Move to a new city.
But tonight, he just lay there. In the dark. Listening to the silence.
Waiting for a phone that wouldn’t ring.
Wondering if she was watching him right now. Through the app. Through the camera. Through the screen.
Wondering if she cared. Or if he was just a dot on a map. A starting point. Left behind.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed it away.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was still silent.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up.
Looked at the seven stains on the floor. A galaxy of failure.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day.
