Chapter 07

London received him in rain.

This rain was not Baku's hard weather, driven sideways across the harbour until the cranes vanished. It was soft, persistent English rain, settling on brick, black railings, plane trees, taxis, the shoulders of people leaving the Underground. It made the terrace house in Fulham look narrower than he remembered. The front step shone under the streetlamp. The brass letterbox had dulled while he was away.

Thomas let himself in with the key that still worked.

The hall smelled of polish, old wood, and Sarah's perfume caught faintly in the coats. Two suitcases stood by the stairs. One was hers, unzipped, half packed with folded shirts and a pair of running shoes wrapped in a hotel laundry bag. The other was empty, waiting for him, a black rectangle with its mouth open.

He stood there with his Baku bag in one hand and Olesya's blue folder under the other arm.

Sarah called from upstairs. 'That you?'

'Yes.'

'Do not move. I am coming down with a travel adapter in my teeth.'

He set the bag down.

She came down barefoot, hair clipped at the back of her head, phone wedged between shoulder and ear, adapter in one hand, passport in the other. Her shirt was half tucked, half loose. She was speaking to someone in Geneva about room blocks, arrival times, and a dinner no one wanted to attend but everyone needed to be seen attending.

She kissed him on the cheek while still listening.

'Yes,' she said into the phone. 'Put me near the exit. I have Lisbon the next morning.'

Thomas stood still until she finished the call.

She dropped the phone onto the small table by the stairs and looked at him properly.

'You look like an airport kept you overnight.'

'Several tried.'

'Baku released you?'

'Provisionally.'

'Very generous of it.'

She put both arms around him. Her body was warm from the house, from movement, from the life that had continued around his absence. He held her with the blue folder trapped between them.

Sarah leaned back and tapped the folder.

'What is that?'

'Travel copies.'

'You and your paper.'

'Paper survives more things than phones.'

'That sounds like something a man says just before losing his phone.'

She took his coat and hung it on the peg. No questions about who had made the folder. No reason for one. Sarah's world produced papers every day: boarding passes, contracts, visas, invoices, receipts from hotels that charged separately for water no one drank.

In the kitchen, she had left soup warming on the hob. The table held two bowls, two spoons, a loaf still in its paper sleeve, and her laptop open beside a stack of printed itineraries. Geneva. Lisbon. Berlin. Home, written in pencil across one weekend, then crossed out.

Thomas placed the blue folder on the sideboard with his passport.

Sarah served the soup.

'Basra dates?' she asked.

'First week of March.'

'How long in London?'

'Nine days, if the medical clears tomorrow.'

'Nine whole days. We should alert the press.'

'I assumed you were leaving.'

'Geneva tomorrow. Back Friday. Lisbon Sunday. Berlin after that if the client stops pretending they can decide by committee.'

She sat opposite him and tore bread with both hands. A crumb fell onto the table. She brushed it into her palm.

'You can come to Lisbon for the weekend,' she said.

'I have Basra prep.'

'Basra will still be there on Monday.'

'That is not a comfort.'

She smiled into her bowl. 'No.'

They ate. The house made its small winter noises around them: pipes settling, rain against the front window, a bus changing gear beyond the terrace. Sarah spoke about flights, clients, a hotel that had mistaken her booking for someone named Mrs Barton and upgraded the wrong woman. Thomas listened, answered in the right places, and watched her passport lying open beside the laptop.

Pages filled with stamps.

Movement reduced to ink.

After dinner, Sarah went upstairs to finish packing. Thomas washed the bowls, dried them, and opened his phone.

There were three messages from Olesya.

School balance paid.

Receipt attached.

Anya says English teacher gave her extra work for missing days.

A photograph followed. Three receipts lay on the kitchen table in Olesya's flat. Rent, medicine, school. The blue stamps had dried darker at the edges. One corner of Anya's exercise book was visible beside them, the word fractions written in her careful hand.

Thomas enlarged the photograph until the receipt numbers filled the screen.

Sarah came into the kitchen carrying a black dress on a hanger.

'Is that work?'

'Receipts.'

'Always glamorous.'

He locked the screen.

'Do you need the iron?' she asked.

'No.'

'That was not a philosophical question.'

'Still no.'

She took the iron from the cupboard and left him with the dress sleeve trailing behind her.

Thomas opened the message again.

Tell Anya good work, he typed.

He deleted it.

Keep the receipts, he typed.

He deleted that too.

In the end he wrote:

Good. Send school receipt Monday.

The reply came after four minutes.

Yes.

Nothing else.

He placed the phone face down beside the sink and finished drying the knife Sarah had used for the bread. The blade had a small notch near the tip. He had forgotten it. Sarah had not replaced it. It still cut.

The next morning, he woke before the alarm.

Sarah was already dressed. She stood at the bedroom mirror fastening an earring, her suitcase closed at her feet, passport tucked into the front pocket of her handbag. The rain had stopped. Pale light entered through the curtains and lay across the floorboards in long, cold strips.

'Car is ten minutes away,' she said.

'I will make coffee.'

'Already made. Yours is in the pot. Mine is in the travel mug and therefore inferior.'

'Geneva today.'

'Geneva tonight. Lisbon Sunday. If you ask again I will assume you miss me.'

'I can read the calendar.'

'That has never stopped you checking.'

She came to the bed and kissed him. Quick, familiar, warm at the corner of his mouth.

'Doctor this morning?'

'Medical at ten.'

'Do not fail it. I have become accustomed to you being employable.'

'I will try.'

Her phone buzzed. She looked at the screen, then at the door.

'Car.'

He followed her downstairs in his dressing gown. The suitcase wheels clicked over the hall tiles. At the door, she turned and pressed two fingers to his chest.

'Come to Lisbon if you can. Forty-eight hours somewhere with sun would improve you.'

'I will check.'

'That means no.'

'It means I will check.'

'Fine. Lie elegantly.'

She kissed him again and stepped out into the morning with her passport, phone, suitcase, handbag, coat, all of it arranged for departure before the car reached the kerb.

Thomas watched her through the front window. The driver lifted her suitcase into the boot. Sarah folded herself into the back seat, already on the phone, already leaving before the car moved.

The house became quiet by degrees.

He drank coffee standing at the counter. Then he took the blue folder from the sideboard and carried it to the study.

The room had been his once, then became both of theirs, then became mostly Sarah's during the months he was away. Her files occupied the lower shelf. His technical manuals held the upper one. A framed photograph from their wedding sat behind a stack of tax documents. Sarah in cream silk, wind lifting the edge of her veil. Thomas beside her, younger, darker-haired, looking at the camera with the face of a man holding confirmed tickets.

He opened Olesya's blue folder on the desk.

Passport copy. Visa letter. Hotel booking. Driver number. Emergency contact. Phrase sheet.

Thank you.

How much?

Where is the receipt?

He took out the phrase sheet and placed it beside Sarah's Geneva itinerary. Two papers. Two kinds of travel. One built from gates and hotel names. One built from copied documents and wrong spellings.

His medical took less than an hour. Blood pressure, eyesight, chest, questions about medications, signature on a form. He passed. The company stamped him fit for Iraq with the same clean confidence it had used to move him out of Baku.

On the way home, he passed a travel agency near the station.

Turkey. Greece. Cyprus. Winter sun. The posters had been laminated so many times that the colours looked sealed away from weather. Istanbul appeared in the corner of one display: domes, water, a bridge lit at night. The city offered itself as a meeting point, neither Baku nor London, neither his home nor hers.

Thomas stood outside long enough for a woman with a pram to steer around him.

At home, he opened the airline website.

London to Istanbul. Direct. Three hours fifty.

Baku to Istanbul. Direct. Three hours.

He chose a weekend twelve days away. Long enough to prepare, close enough to remain real. Sarah would be in Berlin, if Berlin happened. His Basra mobilisation would still be waiting. He could tell work he needed a private weekend before deployment. No one would ask him to prove the need.

He did not book.

He opened Olesya's message thread.

Can you travel to Istanbul next month?

He watched the words in the box. They looked larger than their letters.

He deleted next month and typed:

Can you travel to Istanbul before I leave for Iraq?

He deleted that too.

At last he wrote:

I have a long weekend before Basra. Can you come to Istanbul?

He sent it.

The message showed delivered.

No reply came for thirty-seven minutes.

Then:

Why?

He had not prepared for that.

To see you, he typed.

The cursor waited.

He added:

And to discuss what happens next.

He deleted the second line.

To see you.

He sent it.

This time the answer came quickly.

I need to check passport.

The sentence carried no warmth. No refusal either. He read it three times, then opened the airline website again.

That afternoon, he made a spreadsheet.

Not a formal one. Just dates, flights, hotel options, estimated costs, currency, documents required. Istanbul moved from idea to grid. He chose a hotel near the water, not too grand, not too cheap, with two rooms on the same booking at first, then one room, then two again. He looked at photographs of white sheets, small balconies, a breakfast room with copper lamps, a street where cats slept under parked cars.

He did not press confirm.

At six, Sarah called from Geneva.

'The hotel put me in a room directly opposite the lifts,' she said.

'Ask to move.'

'I did. They gave me a room beside the ice machine. I am climbing rapidly through the hierarchy of bad rooms.'

'Geneva is efficient.'

'Geneva is passive-aggressive with good watches.'

He heard her open a wardrobe, hangers knocking.

'Did the medical pass you?'

'Yes.'

'Tragic. I was hoping for early retirement in a cottage where you learn to make jam.'

'You would leave after a week.'

'I would leave after two days and send postcards.'

He smiled at the desk. The Istanbul spreadsheet remained open on the screen.

'What are you doing?' she asked.

'Travel admin.'

'For Basra?'

'Partly.'

'There is no partly with you. There is only a spreadsheet pretending to be a feeling.'

He looked at the columns.

'That may be fair.'

'Come to Lisbon,' she said. 'No spreadsheet. Just book a flight.'

'You are there for work.'

'Everyone is somewhere for work. Come anyway.'

Olesya's reply appeared on his phone as Sarah spoke.

Passport not with me. Need office.

Thomas read it, then put the phone face down.

'Thomas?'

'I heard you.'

'No you did not.'

'Lisbon may be difficult.'

'It may.'

Sarah made a small sound into the line, then moved on to the next thing.

'I have dinner in ten,' she said. 'Send me Basra dates when final.'

'I will.'

'And eat something that came from a pan, not a packet.'

'Yes.'

'Liar.'

The call ended.

Thomas turned the phone over.

What office? he typed.

The reply came at eight twenty.

Passport office. They took it before. I must ask.

He stared at the word took.

Before what?

No answer.

He waited until the house had settled into night. A bus exhaled at the stop. Someone laughed outside the pub on the corner. Rain began again, fine against the study window.

At ten, Olesya sent:

I will go tomorrow.

He typed:

Good.

Then:

If there is a fee, tell me.

The reply sat marked delivered for eleven minutes.

Then:

No.

He leaned back in the chair. The study smelled of dust warmed by the radiator. Sarah's Geneva itinerary lay beside the phrase sheet. Two papers touching at one corner.

That night he built the trip without booking it.

He printed the flight options first. London to Istanbul. Baku to Istanbul. Arrival times within forty minutes of each other if she took the morning flight. He circled the overlap and wrote feasible in the margin. Then he crossed the word out. Feasible belonged to load-outs and pipe routes. It looked indecent beside her name.

He chose a hotel with two entrances.

That mattered after he noticed it. One door opened onto the main street, another through the courtyard to a lane with blue tiles and a bakery. The photographs showed polished floors, brass lamps, a breakfast terrace with winter covers pulled over the chairs. He enlarged the room images. A desk. A bed. A second chair. A balcony too small to sit on. He imagined placing two passports on the desk, two cups near the window, two sets of shoes by the door.

Then he opened another tab and chose a second room.

The price changed. The meaning changed with it.

He closed the tab.

In the kitchen, the dishwasher clicked through its cycle. Sarah's absence occupied the house more efficiently than most people occupied rooms. Her suitcase had gone, but her movement remained: the adapter missing from the drawer, the coffee mug washed and upside down on the rack, a dry-cleaning ticket beside the fruit bowl. She had left a note on the fridge:

Bin night Thursday.

Geneva number on calendar.

Eat something green.

He took the note down and put it on the counter while he made toast. Then he put it back in exactly the same place.

At midnight, Olesya sent a photograph.

Not the passport office. Anya's school receipt. A narrow slip with a stamp at the top and Anya's name in blue ink. Beneath it, Olesya had written:

Monday paid early.

Thomas looked at the receipt and then at the Istanbul printouts. The school receipt was plain, complete, and possible. The trip was three pages of waiting.

He typed:

Thank you.

Then added:

Tomorrow, take whatever documents they ask for.

The reply did not come until morning.

They always ask for something not there.

He read it standing by the kettle, still in yesterday's shirt.

Then bring too many certificates, he typed.

For the first time since Baku, three dots appeared and stayed long enough to make him put the phone down.

Her reply came:

That was Rauf.

He could see the meeting room in Baku at once. The red folder. The weld photographs. Her thumb pressing the label flat.

Then Rauf was right, he wrote.

No answer.

The next day, he worked through Basra medical forms, tax declarations, security briefings, a hostile environment training refresher that told him to vary routes, carry emergency cash, and avoid local entanglements. The last phrase appeared in a paragraph about transport and subcontractors.

He read it twice.

At three, Sarah sent a photograph from Geneva: a conference badge on a table, her passport beside it, a coffee cup with lipstick on the lid. Her message read:

Proof I still exist.

Thomas replied:

Passport looks busy.

She sent back:

So do I.

At three twelve, Olesya sent:

Queue.

Then a photograph of a paper ticket held between two fingers. 146. Behind it, a tiled wall, a plastic chair, the blurred sleeve of a man in a black coat. No face. No request.

Thomas placed the phone beside the laptop and tried to finish the security briefing. The training video showed a man in a clean shirt explaining checkpoint conduct beside an animated roadblock. Do not argue with local officials. Keep copies of all documents. Do not assume a payment request is unofficial. The man smiled while saying it. Thomas muted him.

At three forty, Olesya sent:

Still waiting.

At four:

They sent me to another window.

He typed:

Which window?

He deleted it. The answer would not help.

At four fifteen:

They say archive.

He wrote archive in the margin beneath Exit and left a square around it. The word had mass. It meant a room no one entered quickly, a shelf with names in the wrong order, a clerk who could say tomorrow without lying.

He checked Baku time. Three hours ahead. Working day ending. Office closing. Doors becoming doors again.

At four twenty-eight, his phone rang.

At four thirty, Olesya called.

He answered from the study with the door closed.

'What happened?'

There was traffic behind her. Wind across the microphone. She was outside.

'They did not give it.'

'The passport?'

'Yes.'

'Why?'

She said something to someone away from the phone. A man's voice answered, bored and official even at a distance. Then the sound changed as she moved into a quieter place.

'There is a restriction.'

'What restriction?'

'Exit.'

He wrote the word on the corner of the airline printout.

Exit.

'From what?'

'Court.'

'What court?'

'Thomas.'

'I need to understand.'

'No. You want paper.'

'Yes.'

'There is paper.'

'Then tell me what it says.'

The line crackled. He heard a bus pass close to her, then voices, then her breathing.

'Bank debt,' she said.

'Yours?'

'In my name.'

'From when?'

'Before.'

'Before what?'

'Before you.'

The answer stopped him. It made him aware of the room he was in: the desk, the wedding photograph, Sarah's files on the lower shelf, the rain moving over the glass.

'How much?'

No answer.

'Olesya.'

'I do not know the full amount.'

'How can you not know?'

'Every year it changes.'

He pressed the pen harder into the paper. The word Exit darkened under the nib.

'Original amount?'

'Small.'

'Now?'

'Not small.'

'Give me a number.'

'Ten thousand manat. Maybe more.'

The number entered the room and stayed there.

He converted it before he meant to. Pounds. Dollars. Salary. Bonus. Joint account. Personal account. Cash. Tax. Sarah.

'Who was the debt with?'

'Bank.'

'For what?'

'Flat. Anya's father signed. I signed also. He left.'

She said it without shape. A series of facts laid down where argument could not reach them.

'And they took your passport?'

'No. They hold it. It is different.'

'It sounds the same.'

'Here it is different.'

He heard a lighter click near her. Someone smoking. Someone waiting beside her. The world around the call remained public.

'Can it be cleared?'

'Everything can be cleared with money.'

'How much to lift the restriction?'

'I told you.'

'The whole debt?'

'They say yes.'

'Who are they?'

'The people behind glass.'

He looked at Sarah's passport in the photograph still open on his laptop. Its burgundy cover. The worn edges. A document that opened gates with the right country printed on the cover.

'Can you appeal?'

'I can stand in another line.'

'With a lawyer?'

'Lawyer costs money.'

'How much?'

'Thomas.'

'I am asking.'

'I know.'

The sound of traffic thinned. He pictured her outside an office with a ticket number in her hand, the blue folder perhaps in her bag, Anya at school, the rent receipt already filed. Paper had done what it could and stopped.

'You should have told me.'

'When?'

'Before.'

'Before what?'

He had no answer.

'Before Istanbul,' he said.

'There was no Istanbul before you wrote it.'

That was true.

He stood and walked to the window. The street below held wet parked cars, a cyclist waiting at the light, a woman carrying flowers under her coat. London moved without showing documents.

'I can come to Baku.'

'No.'

'Why no?'

'You left.'

'I can come back.'

'For what? To stand in offices?'

'If necessary.'

'It will change nothing.'

'We do not know that.'

'I know.'

The same words from the office, the day he left. You will come back. I know. Then, certainty had reached for him. Now it shut a door.

'Istanbul is not possible,' she said.

'Not this weekend.'

'No.'

'We can choose another place.'

'Thomas.'

'Georgia. Turkey later. Somewhere else.'

'I cannot leave Azerbaijan.'

The sentence was plain. No crack in it. No space for planning.

He wrote it under Exit.

Cannot leave Azerbaijan.

The words looked excessive. Four words could do what flights, hotels, routes, money, and wanting could not.

'What about Anya?' he asked.

'She has school.'

'That is not what I meant.'

'I know what you meant.'

He turned from the window. The wedding photograph looked back from behind the tax documents. Sarah's face half lifted by wind. A woman free to leave a room before anyone had finished speaking.

'Let me think,' he said.

'Do not make a project.'

'I am not.'

'You are already writing.'

He looked at the pen in his hand.

'I need to see the paper.'

'No.'

'A photograph.'

'No.'

'Olesya.'

'If I send paper, you will make a plan. If you make a plan, you will think this is solved.'

'It may be solvable.'

'You like that word.'

'Most things have a route through.'

'Not all.'

He heard a door open near her, an official voice calling a number. Olesya did not move.

'We will find a solution,' he said.

The line stayed open. He heard the wind again.

'You say we.'

'Yes.'

'There is you. There is me. There is a court. There is a bank. There is Anya. There is your wife.'

Sarah's passport photograph remained on his laptop, casual as evidence.

'Those are conditions,' he said. 'Not the end.'

'For you.'

'For us.'

She said nothing.

'Olesya.'

'I have to go.'

'Wait.'

'No.'

'We will find a solution.'

This time the line clicked before he had finished hearing himself.

He stood with the phone against his ear until the empty tone stopped.

For a while, he did not move.

The airline website remained open on the screen. London to Istanbul. Baku to Istanbul. Two direct lines meeting over a city where neither of them lived. The hotel booking still waited, selected but unconfirmed. His spreadsheet showed dates, costs, transfer options, room categories.

He printed the spreadsheet before he closed anything.

The printer in the corner woke with a small complaint and pulled the paper through unevenly. Page one listed flights. Page two listed hotels. Page three held a column called documents and another called risk. He had not remembered adding risk. It sat there with empty cells beneath it, waiting to be made useful.

He took the pages from the tray and laid them beside the phrase sheet.

Thank you.

How much?

Where is the receipt?

The questions looked different now. They no longer belonged to taxis and office counters. They belonged to rooms behind glass, debts that changed size while no one watched, passports held in drawers he could not open.

Thomas opened his banking app and checked his personal account. Then the joint account. Then the savings account Sarah used for tax reserves and travel float. The numbers were available if one moved enough labels around them. He closed the banking app before the calculation could complete itself.

In the hall, rainwater ticked from his coat onto the tray by the door.

He went downstairs and picked up the coat. In the pocket he found the travel agency leaflet from the station, folded without remembering the act. Istanbul in glossy blue. A couple on a terrace with cups between them. No luggage visible. No paperwork. No daughter. No bank.

He tore the leaflet in half, then stopped with the two pieces in his hand.

The tear had gone through the bridge.

He placed both pieces in the recycling bin and returned to the study.

The spreadsheet waited.

He closed the Baku flight tab first.

Then the hotel.

Then London to Istanbul.

The spreadsheet stayed.

That evening, Sarah called from Geneva before dinner.

'I have twenty minutes,' she said. 'Tell me something that is not about work.'

Thomas looked at the paper on his desk.

Exit.

Cannot leave Azerbaijan.

'I passed the medical.'

'That is work.'

'I ate lunch.'

'That is biological maintenance.'

'I saw a travel poster for Istanbul.'

'Better. Are we going?'

'No.'

'Ah. I like Istanbul.'

'You have been?'

'Twice. Once for a conference, once for three days between contracts. I bought a scarf I never wear.'

'Naturally.'

'You say that like scarves are a moral category.'

He looked at the phrase sheet from Olesya's folder. Thank you. How much? Where is the receipt?

'Maybe after Basra,' Sarah said. 'We can go somewhere that is not connected to oil, consulting, or your habit of looking at cranes.'

'Maybe.'

'That is the sound of a man not booking a flight.'

He did not answer quickly enough.

'Thomas?'

'I am here.'

'Yes. And elsewhere.'

He folded the paper with the word Exit on it until the ink disappeared inside.

'Just tired.'

'Then sleep.'

'You have dinner.'

'I do. A long table full of people who think strategy is a personality.'

'Good luck.'

'To both of us.'

The call ended.

He remained in the study until the room cooled. Outside, the rain stopped and the street became bright with reflected lamps. He opened the spreadsheet and added a new column.

Restriction.

Then another.

Debt.

Then another.

Unknowns.

He did not enter the amount. Not yet. The number sat in his head with the rest of the unfinished things.

Near midnight, a message came from Olesya.

Do not send money for this.

He read it once. Twice.

Then he typed:

I did not offer.

He watched the sentence and heard how small it was.

He deleted it.

He wrote:

I understand.

The reply arrived after a minute.

No.

Then nothing.

Upstairs, the bedroom was still made on Sarah's side. Her suitcase had left a rectangle in the carpet near the wardrobe. Thomas brushed his teeth, turned off the lights, and lay down without undressing fully.

In the dark, the house held its shape around him. The radiator ticked. A car passed. Somewhere in the pipes, water moved through walls he did not open.

His phone lit the ceiling.

One more message.

School receipt Monday.

He placed the phone face down on the bedside table.

In Baku, the passport would still be behind glass. In Geneva, Sarah's would be in the front pocket of her handbag, ready for morning. In London, the Istanbul spreadsheet sat open in the study, waiting for figures.

Thomas closed his eyes.

The route had failed before anyone had packed.
