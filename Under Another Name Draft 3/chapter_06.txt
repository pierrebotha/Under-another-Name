Chapter 06

By the second week of February, the salary advance had become a folder.

Thomas found it in the shared drive under Payroll Exceptions, where the accountants placed anything that did not fit the ordinary rhythm of the month. The file name was bland enough to survive notice: O Petrova, advance request. Inside were four documents. His authorisation. Rauf's countersignature. A repayment schedule. A scanned copy of Olesya's receipt, stamped by payroll in blue.

He opened the receipt and looked at the amount.

The number had changed shape since the night in her kitchen. There, it had sat between the plates and the medicine bag. Here, it belonged to a column. It had a date, a reference, a line manager, a deduction plan. The company would take a little from her wages each month until the page repaired itself.

Outside his office, Olesya placed a courier envelope on the reception counter and pressed the flap down with the side of her hand. She was wearing the grey hat indoors. The heating had failed again on the north side of the floor. The torn cuff of her coat had been mended with darker thread. The repair was neat, visible, and meant to last.

Anya was back at school. The medicine line had disappeared from the note in Thomas's phone, and Olesya no longer left at four. She arrived at seven thirty, set the fern towards the window, removed the keyboard cover, checked the courier book, and restored the office one object at a time.

Nothing about her asked for acknowledgement.

That made acknowledgement harder to avoid.

At ten, Rauf came in without knocking.

'Your reassignment is confirmed.'

Thomas closed the payroll file.

'When?'

'End of month.'

'I thought they wanted me through commissioning.'

'They did. Then Iraq wanted you more loudly.'

Rauf dropped the printed transfer letter on the desk. Basra appeared twice in the first paragraph, once as location and once as cost centre. The language was clean and indifferent. Mobilisation date. Rotation close-out. Handover obligations. Travel itinerary to follow.

'You knew?' Thomas asked.

'Yesterday.'

'You waited until now?'

'I enjoyed one peaceful evening.'

Thomas read the letter again, though it contained nothing he had missed. Iraq had been possible for months. In oil work, possible often waited in a drawer until someone stamped it into fact.

'How long?'

'Six months minimum. Then it becomes a different six months.'

Rauf sat in the chair opposite him and looked through the glass.

Olesya was at the copier, feeding customs sheets through one by one. The machine pulled each page with a small shudder. She caught the copies before they had time to curl.

'Does she know?' Thomas asked.

'No.'

'Why would she?'

Rauf's mouth moved. Not quite a smile.

'Exactly.'

The copier jammed. Olesya opened the side panel, withdrew a crumpled page, and smoothed it on the glass with the flat of her hand. She did not call for help. She never called for help with machines after Thomas had shown her how to read the paper path.

Rauf stood.

'Handover plan by Friday. Inventory, open claims, customs issues, personnel notes. And do not leave your famous small disasters for me.'

'I do not have famous small disasters.'

'Everybody has them. Yours have labels.'

He left the letter on Thomas's desk.

Thomas turned it over so the printed side faced down.

At lunch, Sarah called from an airport.

The terminal noise reached him before her voice did: wheels over tiled floor, a gate announcement dissolving into static, someone laughing too close to the phone.

'You are officially moving again,' she said.

'News travels.'

'Company travel desk copied me on the itinerary request. Basra?'

'Looks that way.'

'Hotter than Baku.'

'Most places are one thing more than Baku.'

'And one thing less.'

He could hear her walking. Sarah always sounded clearer when she was in motion. Her breath adjusted to corridors, stairs, boarding queues, hotel foyers. Movement was the room she carried with her.

'Will you come through London?' she asked.

'Possibly for forty-eight hours.'

'Long enough to wash clothes and look troubled in the kitchen.'

'A useful skill.'

'You have many.'

Someone called her surname. She moved the phone away and answered, then came back.

'I have Geneva, then Lisbon again, then maybe home. We can overlap if the gods of scheduling are in a generous mood.'

'I will send dates when I have them.'

'Do that.'

There was a pause long enough for the terminal to enter it.

'You sound elsewhere,' she said.

'Office.'

'That is not what I meant.'

Thomas looked through the glass. Olesya had returned to her desk. She was writing numbers from a delivery note into the ledger, each digit narrow and exact. The torn cuff lay against the paper. Dark thread on dark wool.

'Handover,' he said.

'Ah.'

Sarah knew the word. It meant long evenings, boxes, passwords, unresolved problems disguised as lists. It did not mean what it meant at that desk outside his office.

'Do not carry Baku with you,' she said.

'I will carry the work.'

'That is not what I meant either.'

The announcement rose again behind her.

'Boarding,' she said. 'Send the dates.'

'Safe flight.'

'Always.'

The call ended.

Thomas left the phone face down beside the transfer letter. For several minutes he watched Olesya write.

On Friday, he began the handover.

He made the easy lists first. Open purchase orders. Cargo still in customs. Yard defects pending ultrasound. Spares delayed from Aberdeen. Vendor claims by severity. The system behaved when asked to describe steel, money, ships, valves. It became more resistant around people.

Personnel notes sat blank for half an hour.

At last he typed:

O Petrova. Administrative assistant. Strong physical records discipline. Weakness in digital systems and data entry. Requires review on spreadsheets before external issue. Reliable on courier, filing, translation, office logistics.

He stopped.

The sentence was accurate. It was also useless.

He added:

Good judgement under pressure.

Then deleted it. Too broad. Rauf would mock it. HR would not read it. Olesya would never see it. He wanted one piece of the file to hold something closer to the truth.

He typed:

Retain if possible.

The three words looked excessive on the page.

He left them.

By the following Tuesday, the office had begun to treat him as temporary. People knocked less. Documents arrived in small concluding stacks. Rauf came in twice a day with questions that were not questions.

'This claim from Sumgait.'

'Reject unless ultrasound attached.'

'This customs broker.'

'Do not let him call after four. He becomes theatrical.'

'This spare parts forecast.'

'Already padded by ten per cent.'

'You are learning local culture.'

'I am learning your handwriting.'

'Same thing.'

Olesya did not ask about the reassignment.

She took the transfer letter from the printer tray when he forgot it there, placed it face down on his desk, and said only, 'This was in the machine.'

'Thank you.'

Her eyes touched the Basra header. Then they moved away.

'Rauf needs the vendor file.'

'Which one?'

'The one he says he does not need until he needs it.'

'That narrows nothing.'

'Turkish valves.'

'Bottom cabinet.'

She nodded and went to retrieve it.

That afternoon, Thomas found a receipt on his desk.

It was from the clinic. The paper had been folded once. The blue stamp sat at the bottom, slightly smudged from wet fingers. Beside the amount, Olesya had written in pencil:

Deducted from advance.

No note. No thanks. No explanation.

He turned the receipt over. The back was blank.

For a moment he held a pen over it. Paid. Seen. Received. None of the words belonged there. He placed it in his top drawer with the chocolate from before winter and closed the drawer before the gesture could acquire a name.

At home that night, in the company flat, he took out his suitcase.

The flat had never settled around him. It had white walls, a sofa no one would remember buying, curtains that did not quite meet in the middle, and a kitchen equipped by someone certain all meals came from pans with lids. He had kept it efficient. He had never intended to belong to it.

Packing should have taken twenty minutes.

Work shirts. Field trousers. Boots. Passport. Yellow vaccination booklet. Laptop charger. Two notebooks. The stack moved from wardrobe to bed to suitcase in clean sequence. Then he stopped at the bedside drawer.

Inside were things that had avoided categorisation. A spare plug adaptor. Coins. An unopened packet of painkillers. Two receipts from the payment centre near Olesya's block. A school pharmacy slip folded into quarters. The old list from his phone, copied onto paper at some point without his remembering why.

Rent.

Medicine.

School.

He set the paper on the bed.

The categories had not ended. They had only waited.

The next morning, he went to the bank before work.

The branch sat behind mirrored glass on a road where traffic never stopped long enough to park cleanly. Inside, the air smelled of floor polish and paper notes. A security guard watched the queue with the expression of a man who had seen every version of need and no longer ranked them.

Thomas withdrew cash in two transactions to avoid a question from the clerk. The notes came out new and flat. He stood at the counter used for deposit slips and divided them beneath his hand.

Rent.

Medicine.

School.

Then he stopped and added more.

Not much. Enough to turn the envelope from calculation into something harder to defend.

He bought a plain white envelope from a kiosk beside the bank. The woman behind the counter sold cigarettes, chewing gum, batteries, and bus cards. She did not look at him while she counted his change.

In the office, he placed the envelope in the locked drawer of his desk.

That afternoon, Olesya asked to leave at three.

She stood in his doorway with the courier book against her chest and the question already prepared not to sound like a request.

'The school finishes early today.'

'Is Anya ill?'

'No. Teacher meeting.'

'Go.'

'I will finish the broker file from home.'

'No. Finish it tomorrow.'

'It is due today.'

'Tomorrow is fine.'

'You always say tomorrow is not fine.'

'Today it is.'

She looked at him. A rule had moved while she was using it.

'I can bring her here for one hour,' she said.

'You do not have to.'

'I have no one for one hour.'

The sentence came out flat, already stripped of any word he might try to soften.

'Bring her here.'

'She will sit in Meeting Room Three. She will not disturb.'

'Olesya.'

'What?'

'Bring her here.'

She nodded once and left before gratitude could be expected of her.

At four ten, Anya arrived with a schoolbag too heavy for her shoulders and a scarf wound twice around her neck. She had grown more serious since the night in the flat, or perhaps the office did that to children. Her eyes moved over the reception counter, the glass walls, the fern, the printer, the row of men passing with paper cups. She held herself carefully, reading the building for rules.

'Hello,' Thomas said.

'Hello, Mr Thomas.'

Olesya corrected her in Russian.

Anya looked at Thomas again. 'Hello, Thomas.'

'Meeting Room Three is free,' Olesya said. 'She has homework.'

'Good.'

Anya followed her mother through the office. The schoolbag brushed against her hip with each step. In the meeting room, Olesya set her at the far end of the table, away from the glass, and placed a bottle of water beside her exercise books. Then she returned to her desk, opened the broker file, and began typing with the same hard care she gave to all digital things that resisted her.

Thomas watched for longer than he should have.

Anya took out a mathematics book. The cover had been mended with clear tape at the spine. She sharpened a pencil over a folded tissue, gathered the shavings, and placed them in the bin. Then she bent over the page.

For half an hour, the office absorbed her.

Phones rang. Rauf swore softly at a supplier. The new engineer asked where a file was and received an answer from Olesya without her looking up. Anya worked through the noise, stopping only when a cough rose and settled again in her chest. The paper mask was gone. The inhaler sat in the open pocket of her schoolbag, its blue plastic cap visible.

At five, Thomas carried a stack of handover documents to the copier. On the way back, he passed the meeting room and saw Anya staring at one line in her book with the fixed stillness of someone refusing defeat.

He tapped lightly on the glass.

She looked up.

'Stuck?' he asked.

She covered the page with one hand.

'No.'

'Good.'

He moved on.

At his desk he lasted six minutes before standing again.

This time she did not cover the page quickly enough. Fractions. A train leaving one station at one speed, another leaving later from somewhere else, the kind of problem that pretended to be about trains and was really about time meeting itself.

'May I?' he asked.

Anya looked towards her mother.

Olesya's eyes were on the broker file, but she had gone still.

'It is homework,' Anya said.

'Yes.'

'Mama says homework is not for other people.'

'Your mother is usually right.'

'Usually?'

'Do not tell her I said that.'

Anya considered the bargain, then pushed the book a little towards him.

Thomas did not take the pencil from her hand. He drew two lines on a scrap piece of paper and marked the stations.

'Do not begin with the answer. Begin with what moves.'

She leaned closer.

'This train leaves first.'

'Yes.'

'Then it has more time.'

'Yes.'

'But this one is faster.'

'So the question is not where they are. It is when one catches the other.'

Anya looked at the page again. Her pencil hovered, then began to move. Olesya turned a page at her desk, though the file had not required it.

The new engineer came to the doorway.

'Sorry, where is the D bay ultrasound folder?'

Olesya answered before Thomas did. 'Third cabinet, orange tab, under current defects. Not historic defects.'

'Right. Thanks.'

Anya looked at Thomas. 'She knows all the files.'

'Yes.'

'But she says the computer hates her.'

'It does not know her.'

'She says it does.'

'The computer is poorly trained.'

Anya smiled down at the paper, small and private. Then the calculation required dignity.

She solved it three lines later.

'There,' she said.

'Good.'

'You are going to Iraq?'

He looked through the glass. Olesya's hands had stopped on the keyboard.

'Yes.'

'Is it far?'

'Far enough.'

'Will Mama still have this job?'

There was no polite way around the question. It had arrived from the place children watched from while adults pretended not to count money in front of them.

'Yes,' he said.

'Even if you go?'

'Yes.'

Anya wrote the answer to the train problem in the margin, then boxed it with a ruler.

'Good.'

Olesya stood and came to the meeting-room door.

'Anya, pack.'

'I have one more.'

'Pack.'

Anya closed the book without argument. Thomas stepped back.

'She solved it herself,' he said.

'Good.'

Olesya's eyes stayed on the schoolbag. She would not give the moment any more space than that. Anya packed the pencil, book, inhaler, water bottle. Each item went into the bag in order. Thomas saw where she had learned it.

At the lift, Anya turned.

'Goodbye, Thomas.'

'Goodbye, Anya.'

The doors opened. Mother and daughter stepped in together. Just before the doors closed, Anya reached for Olesya's hand. Olesya let her take it, but did not look down.

The office looked different after they were gone.

Not emptier. More exact.

The final week tightened around small things.

A replacement engineer arrived from Aberdeen with two suitcases, red cheeks, and a belief that the region would explain itself if spoken to slowly enough. Thomas walked him through the rig files, the vendor board, the customs delays, the map of personalities no one admitted was a map.

'Rauf knows every shortcut,' Thomas said. 'Listen when he pretends not to care.'

'And admin?'

The new engineer looked through the glass at Olesya's desk.

'Olesya manages the physical files.'

'Good with systems?'

Thomas glanced at the spreadsheet open on the man's laptop. Its columns were too wide, its headers mislabelled, its formulas already at risk.

'Define systems.'

The engineer laughed, assuming humour.

Thomas did not.

At four, Olesya brought in the close-out binder.

It was black, newly labelled, and heavier than it looked. She placed it on his desk with both hands.

'For Iraq.'

'This is Baku.'

'Yes.'

He opened it. Dividers. Printed contacts. Customs reference numbers. Vendor escalation paths. Rauf's mobile written twice, once under operations and once under people who answer if you call again. At the back, in a clear sleeve, she had placed copies of the receipts related to the salary advance.

Clinic.

Medicine.

Rent.

School shoes.

Each one stamped, dated, flattened.

'You did not need to include these.'

'They are part of the account.'

'Not this account.'

'Yes.'

She stood beside the chair, hands clasped in front of her. Her cuffs were mended now, both of them. The darker thread made two narrow bands around her wrists.

'Olesya.'

She looked at the binder, not at him.

'I deducted the first amount from salary,' she said. 'Payroll will deduct next month also. The rent receipt is there. Medicine also. Shoes also.'

'I saw.'

'There is no missing paper.'

'I know.'

Only then did she look at him.

'Then it is clean.'

He closed the binder.

'Clean is not always the same as finished.'

'No.'

The word was quiet. It did not agree with him. It marked that she had heard.

Rauf appeared in the doorway and took in the two of them, the binder, the closed drawer.

'We are pretending this is a normal Friday?'

'It is Thursday,' Olesya said.

'Even worse.'

Rauf entered, put a stack of yard photographs on Thomas's desk, and addressed Olesya without looking at her.

'We need tea.'

She did not move.

Rauf looked at her then. A small correction passed between them.

'Please,' he said.

She left.

When she had gone, Rauf lowered himself into the chair.

'You are leaving money somewhere.'

Thomas said nothing.

'Do not look surprised. You have the face of a man who has put cash in an envelope and thinks paper makes it less visible.'

'It is temporary.'

'Most expensive word in any language.'

'She has a child.'

'Yes.'

'She has rent.'

'Also yes.'

Rauf picked up the top photograph and studied a weld defect he had already seen.

'And you have a flight.'

The sentence landed without force, which made it harder to move.

'I know.'

'Good.'

Rauf put the photograph down.

'Then know both things.'

Olesya returned with tea before Thomas could answer. She placed Rauf's cup first, then Thomas's. No cup for herself. The old office order restored for one more afternoon.

On his final day, the sky over Baku was clear and hard with cold.

The city looked sharpened by it. The harbour cranes stood black against the morning. The Caspian held a pale metallic light. From his office window, Thomas could see the supply vessels at their berths, lined by function and timing. Movement prepared before movement began.

His desk had little left on it.

Laptop. Phone. Passport. The close-out binder. The white envelope inside the drawer.

Olesya arrived at seven thirty.

She removed her coat, turned the fern, took off the keyboard cover, checked the courier book, and placed a fresh paper cup on Thomas's coaster. Coffee. No sugar. The lid fitted badly, as usual.

'You are early,' he said.

'So are you.'

'Last day.'

'Yes.'

She did not ask when the car would come or what time the flight left. He suspected she knew both. The travel itinerary had passed through the printer. The driver had signed the movement sheet. The office calendar had a block marked T Ward departure, and Olesya managed the office calendar with the care of a person guarding doors.

At ten, the new engineer asked where the customs archive was. Olesya answered without looking up.

'Third cabinet, lower shelf, blue tabs. Do not pull the green folder. It is holding the shelf level.'

The engineer laughed.

She did not.

At noon, Rauf insisted on lunch in the conference room. Someone bought flatbread, grilled meat, pickles, tomatoes, too much parsley. The room smelled of lamb fat and printer toner. People came in, ate standing, made jokes about Basra heat, asked for favours that were not jokes, and left again.

Olesya stayed near the sideboard, passing plates, collecting used napkins, restoring order behind appetite.

Thomas caught her wrist as she reached for a stack of cups.

Not hard. Just enough to stop the motion.

'Eat.'

She looked at his hand.

He let go.

'There is work.'

'There is always work.'

'Yes.'

She took one piece of bread and folded it around nothing. Then she left the room with it still in her hand.

At three, the driver came up to say the car was waiting.

Thomas signed the final movement sheet, handed the replacement engineer his access card, gave Rauf the close-out binder, and stood in the office doorway with his coat over his arm.

The room continued.

Phones rang. The copier clicked. Someone laughed near procurement. The fern on Olesya's desk had turned a little towards the weak sun. The courier book lay open under her hand.

'That is everything,' Rauf said.

'No it is not.'

'No. But it is what fits in a binder.'

They shook hands.

Rauf held his grip a fraction longer than needed.

'Do not start wars in Iraq. They already have enough.'

'I will try.'

'Bad answer.'

'I will not.'

'Better.'

Thomas picked up his bag.

Olesya stood.

For a second he thought she would come to the lift with him. She opened the top drawer of her desk and took out a blue folder. She placed it on the counter between them.

'For the airport,' she said.

'What is it?'

'Copies. Passport. Visa letter. Hotel booking. Driver number. Emergency contact.'

'I have these.'

'Now you have them twice.'

He opened the folder. Everything was in order. Of course it was. The copies were clipped by category, labelled in pencil, each corner aligned. At the back she had added a sheet with useful Russian and Azeri phrases written phonetically, though he would not need them in Iraq.

'This is Baku,' he said.

'Yes.'

Neither of them moved.

He set his bag down and opened the drawer in his office for the last time.

The white envelope lay under the clinic receipt.

He took it out and returned to her desk.

'This is practical,' he said.

Her face changed by one small degree. Not surprise. Not acceptance. A tightening around the mouth, a thread pulled from inside.

'No.'

'For the next month.'

'No.'

'For rent. Medicine. School.'

'You made categories.'

'Yes.'

'Of course.'

She looked past him to the office, to Rauf pretending to read a message near the conference room, to the new engineer bent over a file at the wrong desk, to the copier with its side panel still slightly loose.

'This is for leaving.'

'Yes.'

'Then it is leaving money.'

'It is a buffer.'

'For you.'

'For you.'

'No.'

The word did not rise. It stayed low and exact.

He held the envelope out.

'Take it.'

She looked at his hand. Then at the envelope. Then at him.

'If I take, what is it called?'

Advance. Help. Nothing at all.

'Whatever you want to call it.'

'That is not an answer.'

'No.'

The lift opened behind him. Someone stepped out, saw them, and moved away down the corridor with sudden interest in the wall.

Olesya took the envelope.

She did not put it in her drawer. She held it against the blue folder and pressed both to her chest, the way she had once held the collection book.

'You will come back,' she said.

It was not a question.

Thomas heard the driver in the corridor, clearing his throat. He heard Rauf's phone vibrate on the conference table. He heard the copier restart itself with a hard internal clack.

'The project is closed.'

'You will come back.'

He looked at her hands around the folder and the envelope. The darker thread at her cuffs. The grey hat on the desk. The fern turned towards a sun that would be gone in an hour.

'I do not know.'

'I know.'

She said it without heat, without pleading. A fact placed on the counter beside the courier book.

He picked up his bag.

'Send the receipts,' he said.

For the first time that day, something moved in her expression.

'Receipts?'

'For the rent. Medicine. School. Whatever you use it for.'

She looked down at the envelope, then back at him.

'Yes,' she said.

He had meant proof. He heard, too late, that she had heard continuity.

The driver cleared his throat again.

No smaller gesture offered itself. Thomas nodded once and walked to the lift.

At the doors, he turned.

Olesya was still standing behind her desk. Rauf had stopped pretending to read his phone. The new engineer looked at the file in front of him and saw none of it.

The lift doors closed before anyone moved.

The airport road ran along the water before turning inland. Baku slid past in layers: glass towers, old stone, cranes, housing blocks, petrol stations, hoardings with families too clean for the dust beneath them. The driver listened to news radio at low volume. Thomas sat in the back with the blue folder on his lap.

At the terminal, he checked in, passed security, and bought coffee he did not drink.

The blue folder stayed in his hand through boarding.

On the aircraft, before take-off, he opened it again. Passport copy. Visa letter. Hotel booking. Driver number. Emergency contact. The phrase sheet.

Thank you.

How much?

Where is the receipt?

The phonetic spellings were careful and mostly wrong.

He closed the folder.

The plane rose over the Caspian after sunset. For a while there was only dark water below and the wing light blinking red against it. Then the city tilted away, its roads breaking into lines of amber and white. Somewhere inside that grid, an office would be closing. Somewhere above a courtyard, a taped window would be catching the last of the day's cold.

His phone buzzed as the seatbelt sign went off.

One message from Olesya.

Rent paid. Anya medicine bought. School balance Monday.

Then, below it:

Thank you.

No initial. No soft word. A list with one human line attached.

Thomas typed:

Keep the receipts.

He watched the message send over the aircraft Wi-Fi, the small progress circle turning until it disappeared.

Below him, there was no visible ground.

In the flat on the fourth floor, Olesya sat at the kitchen table after Anya had gone to bed. The envelope was empty beside her. Three receipts lay in front of her, their blue stamps still damp at the edges. Rent. Medicine. School.

She opened the tin on the shelf.

The older papers were already there, flattened beneath the lid. She added the new receipts one by one, aligning each corner before pressing them down.

Then she took a fresh clear sleeve, labelled it in her narrow hand, and made space for the next page.
