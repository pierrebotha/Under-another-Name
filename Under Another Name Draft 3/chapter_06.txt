The Baku rotation ended. Thomas was being reassigned. 

He packed his desk on a Friday in late February. Olesya stood in the doorway. 

"You are packing," she said. 

"My contract here is finished," Thomas said. "I am rotating out."

She stood perfectly still. The system was dismantling. 

"I have arranged a final advance," Thomas said. He handed her an envelope containing six months' rent. It was a severance package.

She looked at the envelope. She stepped closer and placed her hand flat against his chest. 

"You are a good man, Thomas," she whispered. 

He stiffened. He was an efficient man. 

He placed the envelope in her hand. "It is just practical."

She looked at him, stripping away the roles. "You will come back," she said. It was a demand. 

He walked out of the building. The Baku project was closed. 

As his plane banked over the frozen Caspian, his phone buzzed. 

*I have paid the rent for Anya. Thank you. - O.*

He typed a reply. *Keep the receipts.*

The system had not closed. The tether held. 

Olesya, in her apartment, carefully filed the receipt in a pristine folder. She was not accepting a severance; she was accepting a retainer. She desired the role of the wife, managing the home he provided. She believed the distance was temporary, a phase before the parallel lines finally converged.

Thomas, in the air, believed he could sustain both lines indefinitely without them ever touching. 

The structural inversion was set.
