Chapter 6 (Corrected & Expanded)
The new phone felt wrong in his hand. Lighter, smoother, devoid of the microscopic scratches and worn edges that had mapped the geography of his old life. It was a blank slate, a sterile object that hadn't yet learned the weight of his grip or the warmth of his pocket. Thomas held it over the bin, hovering there for a long moment, watching the grey light of the morning reflect off its pristine screen. The old phone lay inside the bin, buried under a layer of coffee grounds and tea bags. A burial. He had smashed the SIM card with the heel of his shoe before tossing it in, snapping the plastic into four jagged pieces. A small, violent act that had left him breathing hard, his heart hammering against his ribs as if he'd just committed a crime. He dropped the new phone onto the kitchen counter. It made a sharp, hollow click against the laminate. The sound echoed in the silent flat. 'Right,' he said aloud. His voice sounded strange, unaccustomed to speaking. 'Let's do it.'
He sat at the small table by the window. The laptop was open, the screen glowing with a harsh blue light. He had spent the night researching, reading forums about digital security, identity theft, and the invisible trails people left behind them. The information was overwhelming, a tidal wave of technical jargon and worst-case scenarios. Two-factor authentication. End-to-end encryption. Proxy servers. Phishing kits. Each term felt like a new door opening onto a room full of threats he hadn't known existed. Olesya had walked through those rooms effortlessly. She had navigated the digital landscape while he was still locking the front door and checking the windows. He started with the email. The gateway to everything else. He logged in, the familiar interface appearing like an old friend. For a second, he hesitated. This account held ten years of his life. Conversations with Olesya. Job applications. Birthday wishes from parents who were now dead. Tax returns. Booking confirmations for holidays they had taken together. To change the password was to lock himself out of that history. To sever the connection. But the history was tainted now. Every email felt like it might contain a hidden trap, a link she had clicked, a password she had seen over his shoulder. He clicked on Settings. Then Security. Then Change Password. The cursor blinked in the empty box. Enter current password. He typed it. His fingers knew the rhythm, a muscle memory formed over years. Enter new password. He paused. What constituted a strong password now? Eight characters weren't enough. Twelve? Sixteen? Needs uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols. No dictionary words. No birthdays. No names. He typed: Tr0ub4dor&3. Weak. Too common. He deleted it. Typed: H7$mK9!pL2#qR. Better. Random. Unmemorable. He typed it again to confirm. Password changed. A green tick appeared. A small victory. But it felt hollow. Was it enough? Had she saved the old one somewhere? Did she have a keylogger installed years ago that was still transmitting? The paranoia was a cold worm in his gut, twisting.
He moved to the bank. The site loaded slowly, the security certificate flashing green. He logged in with the temporary password Mr Henderson had issued. The balance stared back at him. The deficit was still there. -£5,000.00. A red wound in the middle of the screen. He looked away. Focused on the settings. Changed the password. Set up a new PIN. Enabled biometric login on the new phone, pressing his thumb against the sensor again and again until it beeped in acceptance. Security updated. He logged out. Cleared the cache. Closed the browser. One down. Ten to go. Social media. He hadn't used Facebook in months, but the account was still there, a digital ghost town populated by old photos and forgotten events. He logged in. The profile picture was of him and Olesya, standing in front of the Tower Bridge, smiling. He looked happy. She looked radiant. He clicked on the photo. Enlarged it. Stared at her face. The smile didn't reach her eyes. Not really. He had been too blind to see it then. Now, it was obvious. A mask. He changed the password. Then he clicked Deactivate Account. Are you sure? Yes. He was sure. He wanted to disappear. To stop being found. Account deactivated. The page refreshed. Blank. White. Gone. Instagram. Twitter. LinkedIn. One by one, he dismantled his online presence. Changing passwords, enabling two-factor authentication, deleting posts, removing tags. It was a slow, methodical suicide of his digital self. With each click, he felt lighter, yet also more isolated. He was erasing the traces of his existence, scrubbing the map clean so no one could follow him. Not even her. Especially not her.
By midday, his head throbbed. The blue light of the screen had drilled a hole behind his eyes. He stood up, stretching his stiff back. The flat was silent. The stains on the floor were still there, dark and accusing. He walked to the kitchen, poured a glass of water, and drank it standing up. The water was lukewarm. He didn't care. He looked at the new phone. It had been silent all morning. No calls. No messages. No notifications. The silence was deafening. He had expected... what? A message from her? A taunt? A confirmation that she knew what he was doing? Change your passwords, she had said. Delete the app. She had told him what to do. Was this part of her plan too? Was she watching him right now, nodding in approval as he ticked off her instructions? The thought made his skin crawl. He picked up the phone. Scrolled through the apps. There it was. The banking app. The icon was a simple blue circle with a white symbol. Innocuous. Harmless. He pressed and held the icon. Delete App? Delete. The icon vanished. The space closed up. Had she seen that? Did the app send a notification when it was uninstalled? User has removed the application. He imagined her phone buzzing in Istanbul. Her seeing the message. Smiling. Good boy, Tom. He threw the phone onto the sofa. It bounced softly on the cushions. 'Stop it,' he muttered. 'Stop thinking like that.' But he couldn't stop. The paranoia had taken root. Every silence was a watchful pause. Every noise was a footstep. He was living in a panopticon of his own making, where the guard tower was empty but he felt watched nonetheless.
He decided to leave the flat. The walls were closing in, the silence pressing against his eardrums. He needed noise. People. Movement. He grabbed his coat, checked his pockets for keys and wallet, and stepped out into the hallway. The stairwell smelled of boiled cabbage and damp concrete. The same smell as always. But today it felt oppressive. He walked down the stairs, his footsteps echoing. One flight. Two flights. Three. Outside, the rain had stopped. The sky was a uniform sheet of grey cloud, low and heavy. The air was cold, biting at his exposed hands. He shoved them into his pockets and started walking. He didn't have a destination. Just away. Away from the flat. Away from the screens. Away from the digital ghosts. He walked towards the high street. The pavements were wet, reflecting the dull orange glow of streetlamps that hadn't yet switched off. Puddles gathered in the cracks of the tarmac, oily rainbows swirling on the surface. People hurried past, heads down, collars up. Everyone seemed to be in a rush, fleeing the cold, fleeing the grey. Thomas walked slowly. He watched the shop windows. The bakery, smelling of yeast and sugar. The newsagent, cluttered with magazines. The off-license, neon sign buzzing. Normal things. Ordinary lives. He felt like an alien observing a species he used to belong to. He passed a café. Through the window, he saw a couple sitting at a small table. They were laughing, sharing a pastry. The woman wiped a crumb from the man's lip. A gesture of intimacy. Of care. Thomas stopped. Watched them. They looked happy. Real. Not like the hollow smiles in his deleted photos. He turned away. Couldn't watch anymore. It hurt too much.
He kept walking. Ended up in the park. The grass was brown, dormant. The trees were bare, skeletal branches reaching up like claws. A few dogs ran off-leash, chasing balls, barking. Their owners stood in clusters, talking, wrapped in scarves. Thomas sat on a bench. The wood was cold, damp. He didn't care. He watched the dogs. One of them, a terrier mix, ran up to him. Sniffed his hand. Thomas reached out, stroked the rough fur behind the ears. The dog wagged its tail, thumping it against Thomas's leg. 'Good boy,' Thomas said. The owner called the dog. It ran off, yapping. Thomas watched it go. He pulled out his new phone. Checked the screen. Still nothing. No messages. No calls. He opened the browser. Typed Istanbul weather. 12 degrees. Cloudy. Chance of rain. Same as here. He typed flight status TK1985. Departed on time. Of course it had. The world didn't stop because he was sitting on a bench in a park in London. Planes took off. People arrived. Lives continued. He closed the browser. A group of children ran past, kicking a football. It rolled towards his feet. He stopped it with his shoe. One of the boys ran back, breathless. 'Sorry, mister,' the boy said. Thomas kicked the ball back. 'S'alright.' The boy grinned, ran back to his friends. The game resumed. Shouts, laughter, the thud of the ball. Thomas watched them. He remembered being that age. The simplicity of it. The ball, the goal, the game. No complications. No hidden meanings. Just play.
His phone buzzed in his pocket. He froze. Heart skipping a beat. He pulled it out. Unknown Number. The words blinked at him. Mocking. Inevitable. He stared at it. Didn't answer. Let it buzz. Once. Twice. Three times. Then it stopped. Silence. He waited. A minute. Two minutes. No voicemail notification. No message. Just a missed call log. Unknown Number. Who was it? Her? A wrong number? A telemarketer? He wanted to call back. Needed to know. But he couldn't. If it was her, answering would mean giving her what she wanted. Attention. Reaction. If it wasn't her, it was just noise. He put the phone away. Stood up. The cold had seeped into his bones. He needed to move. Keep walking. He walked out of the park. Back towards the high street. The crowds had thickened. Evening rush. People hurrying, faces set, eyes forward. He passed a travel agency. Posters in the window. Sunny beaches. Blue skies. Smiling families. Escape the Winter. Book Now. He stopped. Looked at the posters. He could go back. Book another flight. Go to Istanbul. Find her. But how? The city was huge. Millions of people. She could be anywhere. And if he found her, what then? What would he say? Please come back? I miss you? She had made her choice. She had walked out of the flat. Zipped the bag. Clicked the door. He turned away from the window. Walked on. The phone buzzed again. Another call. Unknown Number. He didn't look. Didn't stop walking. Let it ring. Let it buzz. Let it scream. He kept walking. Head down. Shoulders hunched against the wind. He reached his building. Climbed the stairs. Two flights. Three. Unlocked the door. Stepped inside. The flat was exactly as he had left it. Dark. Cold. Silent. He took off his coat. Hung it up. Took off his shoes. Lined them up neatly by the door. He walked to the kitchen. Poured a glass of water. Drank it. He looked at the phone on the counter. It was dark again. Sleeping. He picked it up. Checked the call log. Two missed calls. Both Unknown Number. No voicemails. No messages. Just silence. He put the phone down. He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa. The same spot as before. He looked at the stains on the floor. Four of them now. A constellation of failure. He leaned back. Closed his eyes. Five thousand pounds. For you. You're the starting point. Not the destination. Change your passwords. The words echoed in his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted. He pushed them away. Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled. He dreamt of phones. Thousands of them. Ringing. Buzzing. Screaming. All unknown numbers. He tried to answer them, but they slipped through his fingers, turning to dust. He dreamt of Olesya. Sitting in a dark room. Surrounded by screens. Each screen showing a different version of him. Sleeping. Eating. Walking. Waiting. She was switching between them. Watching. Judging. Smiling. He woke up gasping. The room was pitch black. He sat up. Disoriented. The phone. It was buzzing. He fumbled for it. Knocked the glass of water off the table. It rolled across the floor. Spilled more water onto the already wet carpet. A fifth stain. He didn't care. Grabbed the phone. Unknown Number. He answered. 'Hello?' Silence. Then, a click. Then, silence again. Then the recording ended. No voice. No breathing. Just a call that connected and then said nothing. He lowered the phone. Stared at it. Was it her? Testing the line? Seeing if he would answer? Or just a wrong number? It didn't matter. The result was the same. Silence. He put the phone down. Lay back on the sofa. Pulled the blanket over himself. Tomorrow he would buy a new laptop. Maybe move house. Change his number. Disappear completely. But tonight, he just lay there. In the dark. Listening to the silence. Waiting for a phone that wouldn't ring. Wondering if she was watching him right now. Through the app. Through the camera. Through the screen. Wondering if she cared. Or if he was just a dot on a map. A starting point. Left behind. Just access. Under another name. The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted. He pushed it away. Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless. When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh. The flat was still empty. The phone was still silent. He got up. Showered. Dressed. Made coffee. Drank it standing up. Looked at the five stains on the floor. The wine. The rain. The spilled water. The new ones. A galaxy of failure. Left them. Opened the door. Stepped out. Locked it behind him. Walked down the stairs. Into the day.
