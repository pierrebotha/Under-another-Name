Chapter 01

The wind off the Caspian came in under the window seal before dawn, carrying salt, oil, and the faint mineral sourness that seemed to live in the stone of Baku itself. Thomas stood in the half-light of the rented flat, one hand resting against the cold glass, and watched the avenue begin to move.

Neftchilar Avenue ran below him in a silver line of headlights. Taxis idled at the kerb. A street cleaner pushed a barrow along the promenade, his shoulders lifted against the morning wind. Beyond him, the sea broke in short, dirty folds against the concrete. It was not the blue water of postcards. It was grey, worked over, carrying the sheen of fuel and winter light.

Out on the horizon, the offshore rigs held their positions.

That was what Thomas noticed first in any city. Not the monuments. Not the restaurants listed in the company pack, or the views people photographed from hotel terraces. He looked for the points that held. Roads, cranes, harbour lights, the passage of supply vessels, the schedule behind the visible world. Cities introduced themselves through their systems if one knew where to look.

His flat had been chosen by procurement. Two bedrooms, serviced weekly, walking distance to the regional office, secure parking, sea view. The furniture had the blunt neutrality of corporate accommodation: pale sofa, glass table, heavy curtains that smelled faintly of dust when drawn. On the kitchen counter sat a bowl of fruit he had not ordered and would not eat. Three apples. Two oranges. A banana already bruising at the stem.

He opened the fridge. Bottled water, yoghurt, cheese wrapped in waxed paper, a carton of eggs. Someone had stocked it from a list.

Efficient enough.

He closed the fridge and checked his watch. Six fifteen. The driver was due at seven.

Thomas had been in transit for almost nineteen hours: Heathrow to Istanbul, Istanbul to Baku, the final descent over a darkened coast where sodium lights marked the curve of the bay. At passport control, the officer had examined his work visa for longer than seemed necessary, then stamped the page without looking at him again. The driver from the company had been waiting beyond the barrier with a board that read WARD, T. He had taken Thomas's bag and led him into air that smelled of rain on hot electrical wiring.

The city had appeared in pieces through the car window. Glass towers. Dark blocks of Soviet flats. A lit petrol station where three men smoked beside a pump. The Flame Towers shifting colour above the hill, bright and theatrical against the older streets below. Thomas had watched without forming an opinion. Judgement was unnecessary on arrival. The first job was intake.

He made coffee from the machine on the counter. The capsule dropped into place with a small plastic crack. The cup filled too quickly, the liquid thin and bitter. He drank it standing by the sink and reviewed the schedule on his tablet.

Local office induction. Port access briefing. Meeting with operations manager. Review of drilling equipment manifests. Inspection schedule for the offshore installation. Contractor compliance pack.

The list settled him.

At seven, the driver called from downstairs.

The lobby was marble and brass, warmed by lights set too high into the ceiling. A young guard in a black suit stood behind the reception desk, watching the street through the glass doors. He nodded when Thomas crossed the floor. Outside, the wind pressed immediately against Thomas's shirt collar.

The driver opened the rear door.

'Good morning, Mr Ward.'

'Morning.'

The car smelled of leather polish and stale cigarettes hidden beneath something lemon-scented. Thomas sat back and watched the city lift itself out of night. Baku did not wake softly. Metal shutters rattled upwards. Buses exhaled at junctions. Men in dark jackets crossed between lanes with one hand raised, not asking permission so much as announcing their passage.

The office was on the sixth floor of a glass building that looked across the harbour. It had been fitted to an international standard: access cards, white desks, meeting rooms named after oilfields, framed safety posters in English and Azerbaijani. In the reception area, a television played muted footage of a platform supply vessel moving through rough water. The subtitles ran too fast to read.

Rauf Mammadov met Thomas by the lift.

He was shorter than Thomas had expected, compact in the shoulders, with a close beard and a suit that sat perfectly on him. He shook hands with both palms, not warmly, not coldly. A measured grip. His eyes moved quickly, taking in Thomas's shoes, watch, travel-creased jacket, the laptop bag held in his left hand.

'You found the flat?'

'Yes.'

'Good. The view is better than the plumbing, but procurement likes it.'

Thomas looked at him.

Rauf smiled as if to show the remark had not required a reply. 'Come. I will show you the office before everyone arrives and begins lying about what has already been done.'

He led Thomas through the open-plan area. Desks stood in long rows, each with a monitor, keyboard, company phone, and a small plastic tray for documents that no one had yet learned to stop printing. The air conditioning hummed with a dry, uneven sound. Behind the glass wall, the meeting rooms were empty and bright.

'Engineering there,' Rauf said, pointing with two fingers. 'Procurement at the back. Logistics opposite. Government liaison uses the small room because they shout on the telephone.'

'Who handles permit tracking?'

Rauf made a sound through his nose. 'In theory, logistics. In practice, whoever is least busy when the Ministry asks where the missing form is.'

They stopped outside Thomas's office. It had a desk facing the window, two visitor chairs, a low cabinet, and a whiteboard already carrying the ghost of previous marker lines. Someone had erased in haste. Faint grids remained.

Thomas set his bag down and opened the blind. The harbour lay below, the water torn white around the small workboats.

'You will need help,' Rauf said.

'With what?'

'Local filings. Customs stamps. Driver slips. Translation when the yard men decide English is beneath them.'

'I can handle documentation.'

'You can handle your documentation. That is different.'

Rauf leaned against the doorframe. He did not enter fully. 'We have been using a temp from procurement. She has resigned. Or her cousin has died. I am not sure which story we are on today. It does not matter. We need someone here, in the chair, every day.'

'Hire someone.'

'I have interviews this afternoon.'

'Then why are you telling me?'

'Because you will reject all of them if I do not tell you first that competence here is not always where you expect it.'

Thomas took out his notebook. The paper was squared, cream-coloured, the corners unbent. He wrote Permit support, then underlined it once.

Rauf watched the movement. 'You write everything down.'

'Most things.'

'Good. Then write this. A woman who can get a Ministry stamp before lunch is worth attention. A woman who can make yard engineers lower their voices is worth more. If Excel is the weakness, teach her.'

'Excel is not optional.'

'In Baku, many things that are not optional become optional before ten in the morning.'

From somewhere beyond the glass, a printer began its first mechanical cough of the day. One sheet came out, then another. Rauf looked towards it as if it had offended him personally.

'The interviews are at two,' he said. 'Use Meeting Room Three. It has the least broken chair.'

By eight, the office had filled. Men arrived in dark coats, shaking rain from their shoulders. A woman in a red scarf unlocked a cabinet and took out a stack of folders bound with string. Someone argued over the coffee machine. The language around Thomas shifted between Azerbaijani, Russian, and English, sometimes within a single exchange. He listened for function rather than meaning. Request. Refusal. Delay. Defence.

At nine thirty, a procurement clerk brought him the current equipment manifests.

The first file was wrong by the second page.

Thomas placed a ruler beneath the printed rows and began marking discrepancies in pencil. A casing order had been logged in kilograms in one sheet and tonnes in another. A pressure valve shipment had been duplicated under two supplier codes. Customs clearance dates were entered in European format on half the forms and American format on the rest, turning March into a month of impossible approvals.

By eleven, his pencil had gone blunt.

He sharpened it over the waste bin and looked out at the water. A supply vessel moved slowly between harbour walls. On deck, orange containers were lashed in two neat rows. Men in hard hats stood near the stern, small figures braced against wind.

Systems failed in small places first. A column heading. A unit conversion. A date typed by someone who wanted the task gone. Failure rarely announced itself. It waited inside ordinary documents until pressure arrived.

At one forty-five, Rauf put his head through the door.

'Meeting Room Three.'

Thomas gathered the interview forms. 'How many?'

'Five. One is late already, which is efficient. Saves time.'

The first candidate had a folder of certificates and too much perfume. She spoke polished English and referred to herself in the third person when describing achievements. Thomas wrote down Composed, overprepared, no field sense.

The second had worked for a shipping agent near the port. He knew customs procedures and had a habit of answering before the question finished. He asked about salary three times in fifteen minutes.

The third candidate did not bring identification.

'It is in the taxi,' she said.

Thomas looked at Rauf.

Rauf did not look back.

The fourth arrived at two fifty-six.

She paused just inside the doorway as if checking whether the room permitted her. She wore a dark blue dress with frayed cuffs and a black coat too warm for the overheated building. Her shoes were polished, though the leather had cracked across the toes. Her hair was pulled back so tightly that it sharpened the bones of her face.

'Olesya Sokolova,' Rauf said, reading from the page in front of him.

The surname landed in the room and stayed there.

Thomas saw Rauf notice it. A small pause, no more than the width of a breath. The old Russian ending, carried into a city that had spent years turning itself towards other names, other loyalties, other futures. Olesya sat before either man could invite her.

Her hands folded in her lap. Not loosely. The right thumb pressed into the left knuckle until the skin whitened.

'You have administrative experience?' Thomas asked.

'Yes.'

Her English was careful, each word set down separately.

'Where?'

'A freight forwarding office. Then a drilling support contractor. Temporary work.'

'Why did you leave?'

She looked at Rauf. Then at Thomas.

'The contract ended.'

Rauf made a note. The paper scratched beneath his pen.

'The role requires permit tracking, equipment manifests, filing, translation, and local liaison,' Thomas said. 'There are moving parts.'

'I understand moving parts.'

It was the first thing she said without the caution of a rehearsed answer.

Thomas looked up from the form. 'Tell me about the drilling support contractor.'

'They rented tools to yards. I prepared delivery notes. Sometimes invoices.'

'Did you manage spreadsheets?'

'Yes.'

'What level?'

A small movement passed across her face. It was not quite a flinch. More the body drawing a curtain.

'I entered the data.'

'Formulas?'

'Some.'

'Pivot tables?'

She stared at him.

Rauf coughed into his hand.

Thomas crossed out the line on his form. 'That is not essential on day one.'

Olesya's thumb eased away from the knuckle.

'Russian and Azerbaijani?' Rauf asked.

'Russian. Azerbaijani. English. Some Turkish if spoken slowly.'

'Drivers here do not speak slowly.'

'Then I ask them to repeat.'

Rauf smiled. 'And if they refuse?'

'I ask the dispatcher who wants to be paid.'

The room shifted a little. Rauf leaned back. Thomas wrote: practical under pressure.

He placed a sample manifest in front of her. Ten rows, deliberately imperfect. Wrong unit in column D. Two missing supplier codes. A delivery date that fell on a public holiday.

'Review this,' he said. 'Tell me what concerns you.'

Olesya took the paper. She did not begin with the numbers. Her eyes moved over the page edges first, then the stamped header, then the signatures at the bottom.

'This signature is not the same as this one,' she said, touching the lower corner.

Thomas had not altered the signature.

Rauf leaned forward. 'Why do you say that?'

'The first is written by someone sitting. The second is written standing up, perhaps on a car bonnet. See here. The pressure changes.'

Thomas looked. She was right.

'Anything else?' he asked.

She tapped the date. 'No deliveries on this day. Holiday. The yard gates will be closed unless someone pays security to open them.'

'And the tonnage?'

She looked at the column. Too long. 'It is heavy equipment.'

'The unit is wrong.'

Her face closed again.

'Kilograms,' Thomas said. 'The manifest requires tonnes.'

'Yes,' she said.

She had not seen it.

Rauf's pen stopped moving.

Thomas took the paper back. 'Thank you.'

Olesya stood, gathering the strap of her handbag. The bag was old, black, carefully repaired near the clasp with dark thread. Her chair made no sound when she pushed it in. She placed it exactly beneath the table.

At the door, she turned.

'The security guard at the yard will open the gate for tea,' she said.

'Tea?'

'Not money. Money makes him nervous. Tea makes him important.'

She left the room.

The door closed without catching.

Rauf looked at the manifest, then at Thomas. 'She missed the unit.'

'She saw the signature.'

'We need Excel.'

'We need fewer forged delivery notes.'

'So?'

Thomas put Olesya's form on the top of the pile.

Rauf made the same sound through his nose as before. 'You will teach her Excel.'

'I will correct what needs correcting.'

'That is not the same thing.'

'No.'

They interviewed the fifth candidate, who had excellent software skills and no Russian. He kept checking his phone beneath the table. Thomas thanked him and did not write anything after he left.

Olesya started the following Monday.

She arrived at seven forty-eight with two plastic folders, a small packet of wet wipes, and a pot plant wrapped in newspaper. The office cleaner had not yet finished the corridor. Lights flickered awake above the desks. Thomas was in his office reviewing the weekend drilling report when he saw her pass the glass.

She did not knock. She went first to her desk.

The desk had belonged to the temp whose cousin had died or not died. There were coffee rings on the laminate, adhesive marks where labels had been pulled away, and a drawer that did not close fully. Olesya stood over it for several seconds. Then she took off her coat, folded it over the back of the chair, and began cleaning.

Thomas watched without meaning to.

She wiped the desk from left to right, lifting the keyboard rather than pushing it aside. She cleaned the monitor frame, the telephone receiver, the inside of the drawer. From one folder she removed a pen, a pencil, a ruler, a small notebook, and a packet of paper clips still in their cellophane. Each object took its place. The paper clips went in the top right corner. The notebook aligned with the keyboard. The pen lay parallel to the desk edge.

The plant came last.

It was a small fern in a chipped ceramic pot. She unwrapped the newspaper, folded the paper twice, and put it into the bottom drawer. The fern went near the window, close enough for light, far enough from the radiator.

At eight precisely, she walked to Thomas's door with a cup of black coffee.

'Good morning,' she said.

He looked at the cup. 'Thank you.'

'No sugar.'

'No.'

'Rauf said.'

She placed it on the coaster beside his keyboard. There had not been a coaster there before.

For the first week, she was early every day.

For the first week, something in the system bent out of shape behind her.

On Tuesday, a customs form went into the folder for the vessel rather than the supplier. Thomas found it after lunch, wedged between two delivery notes, its corner folded down with unusual care. The filing was wrong. The fold was exact.

On Wednesday, she entered casing lengths as individual units and doubled the order volume. On Thursday, she translated a yard foreman's complaint accurately but left out the insult that explained why procurement had stopped answering his calls.

On Friday, she sent Thomas a permit register with the dates aligned, the fonts corrected, the cells colour-coded in pale blue and green. Three formula errors sat beneath the prettiness of the sheet.

Thomas opened the file at seven fifteen that evening.

Most of the office had gone. The air conditioning had been turned down after complaints from the engineers, and the glass held the last heat of the day. Outside, the city lights had begun to show along the promenade, each one trembling in the dark water.

He corrected the first formula. Then the second.

When he found the third, he sat back.

The spreadsheet was beautifully arranged. That was the problem. It gave the appearance of control before the control existed. The borders were consistent. The headings clear. The colours restrained. If he had skimmed it, as Rauf might have skimmed it, the error would have travelled into the weekly report and sat there until some later failure exposed it.

Thomas stood and walked to Olesya's desk.

The physical files were perfect. She had relabelled the drawers in English and Russian. Delivery notes sat in clear sleeves, ordered by yard and date. A small handwritten list was taped to the inside of the top drawer: stamps, signatures, driver copies, Ministry receipts. No flourish. No decoration. A working list.

Beside the keyboard, the fern's soil was dark from recent watering.

He returned to his office and fixed the spreadsheet.

On Monday morning, he called her in.

She stood rather than sat.

'The permit register had three calculation errors,' he said.

'I checked it.'

'You checked the presentation.'

Her mouth tightened.

He turned the monitor towards her. 'Here. Column F is multiplying the unit cost by the wrong quantity. It should reference column D, not C.'

She leaned closer. Her perfume was almost absent, something clean beneath paper dust and cold air.

'I copied the formula from the row above.'

'The row above was also wrong.'

She looked at the screen for a long time. 'I will redo it.'

'I have corrected it.'

'Then I will check the next one.'

'You need to understand why it failed.'

She nodded, but her eyes had moved to the floor.

Thomas opened a blank sheet. 'Sit.'

She sat.

He showed her the cell references, first slowly, then again. She watched his hand on the mouse rather than the formula bar. When he asked her to repeat the process, she clicked the wrong cell and immediately withdrew her hand from the mouse as if it had become hot.

'Again,' he said.

She did it again. Wrong.

He waited.

The office outside his door had grown louder. Someone laughed near the printer. A phone rang unanswered across the room.

Olesya placed both hands flat on her knees. The knuckles had gone pale. 'Rauf says I am useless.'

Thomas looked at the spreadsheet. The active cell blinked.

'Rauf says many things.'

'He thinks I do not hear.'

'He knows you hear.'

She turned her head towards him then. The line of her jaw held, but only just.

Thomas moved the mouse back to the correct cell. 'The issue is not intelligence. It is sequence. You are copying from a broken row. Start from the source data.'

'And if the source is wrong?'

'Then nothing after it matters.'

She considered that.

The sentence had entered her differently than he intended. He could see it in the way her hands loosened, then folded again.

'Show me once more,' she said.

He did.

At noon, she watered the fern. At twelve ten, she sent him the corrected register. One formula was still wrong. He fixed it without calling her back.

That became the shape of the days.

Olesya arrived before the office had gathered its noise. She cleaned the surface of her desk, placed coffee beside his keyboard, checked the physical files, spoke to drivers, called clerks, waited in corridors where Thomas would not have known which door to knock on. She came back with stamps. She came back with signatures. She came back with explanations written on scraps of paper and names spoken too quickly for him to catch.

Then the files came to him.

He repaired the numbers.

Neither of them named the exchange.

There were other things he began to notice because systems trained the eye to notice irregularities. She wore the same coat every morning, though the lining had torn near the cuff. She ate at her desk but never before one o'clock, and never anything that required heating. Bread, cheese, cucumber wrapped in foil. She kept tea bags in a tin but used each one twice. When the office ordered lunch from the cafe downstairs, she declined before anyone had finished asking.

On the Wednesday of the second week, three engineers from the yard came in before lunch and filled the open-plan office with wet coats and cigarette smoke. They spoke over Olesya's head at first, then around her, their Russian hard and fast, their Azerbaijani quicker still when Rauf appeared from the corridor. One of them placed a folder on her desk without looking at her.

She opened it, read the first page, and closed it again.

'This is not for us,' she said.

The man closest to her laughed. He had a wide face, badly shaved, with a gold ring on his little finger. He said something Thomas did not catch. The other two looked towards the window.

Olesya stood. She did not raise her voice. She picked up the folder and walked to Thomas's office.

'The pressure test certificate is copied from another site,' she said.

Thomas took it from her. The format looked correct. The company stamp sat where it should sit. The supervisor's signature was legible.

'How do you know?'

'The mud on the photograph.'

He looked again. A small image had been stapled to the back page, showing a section of pipe on packed ground. Nothing about it had caught his attention.

'That yard has red aggregate,' she said. 'This is grey.'

Thomas went to the door. The three engineers had stopped talking. Rauf was beside the printer with a cup of tea in one hand, watching.

'This certificate is not for the Sumgait test,' Thomas said.

The man with the ring spread his hands. 'Same pipe specification.'

'Not the same test.'

'The pipe passed.'

'Not on this certificate.'

The man's eyes moved past Thomas to Olesya, who had returned to her desk and was aligning the edge of a paper stack against the surface. He said something under his breath.

Thomas waited. The office waited with him.

Rauf put down his tea.

'Bring the correct certificate by four,' Thomas said. 'Or the payment release waits until Monday.'

The three men left with their wet coats unbuttoned and the wrong folder under the ringed man's arm.

When the lift doors closed, the office resumed too quickly. Phones were lifted. Keyboards began again. Rauf came to Thomas's doorway.

'You understand what he called her?'

'No.'

'Good.'

Thomas looked across the room. Olesya had taken a cloth from her drawer and was wiping the place on her desk where the folder had been.

'Will they bring it?'

'Yes,' Rauf said. 'They like being paid.'

At four twenty, a courier arrived with the correct certificate. Olesya checked the photograph first, then the stamp, then the signature. Only after that did she enter the file into the register. She sent the digital copy to Thomas at four thirty-one. The subject line read: Sumgait pressure test, corrected.

The formula in the tracking sheet was wrong.

Thomas fixed it before he approved the release.

On the second Thursday, the air conditioning failed.

By mid-afternoon, the office had become a sealed box of heat, printer toner, and stale coffee. Men loosened collars. Someone propped open the stairwell door and let in the smell of cigarette smoke from below. The glass wall of Thomas's office held the sun until the room seemed lit from inside the window itself.

Olesya came in with a stack of permit forms against her chest.

'The Ministry wants the environmental attachment with the stamp on every page.'

'It is already stamped on the first page.'

'They want every page.'

'Why?'

'Because today they want every page.'

Thomas took the forms. A drop of sweat ran from his temple to the hinge of his jaw. He wiped it with the heel of his hand.

Olesya stood by the door, not leaving.

'What else?'

'You are from England.'

'Yes.'

'It is cold there.'

'Sometimes.'

She looked through the glass towards the harbour. The rigs were faint in the haze, reduced to dark strokes against a white sky.

'You travel always?'

'For work.'

'That is not the same answer.'

Thomas placed the forms in a neat pile. 'It is the only answer that matters.'

She accepted this with a small tilt of the head. Not agreement. Storage.

'In Russia,' she said, then stopped.

He waited.

'My father travelled for work. Before he came home, my mother changed the flat around him. Moved a chair. Cleaned windows. Put out plates we were not allowed to use.'

She looked down at the forms in his hand.

'And when he left?'

'The cup stayed by his side of the bed.'

Outside, someone struck the side of the printer. The machine resumed with a shudder.

Olesya reached for the forms, but Thomas held them.

'I will finalise these tonight.'

'I can do them.'

'I know.'

She looked at him then. The heat had brought colour to her face. A strand of hair had loosened near her ear and stuck to her skin. She did not thank him. She did not argue.

'The stamp must be blue,' she said.

'Not black?'

'Blue means original. Black means copy. Even when it is original.'

'Of course.'

For the first time, he saw the edge of a smile. It appeared and vanished before it could be offered.

She left the forms on his desk.

The office emptied slowly that evening. Rauf stopped at Thomas's door with his jacket over one arm.

'You are staying?'

'The environmental attachment needs rebuilding.'

'Olesya gave it to you?'

'Yes.'

Rauf looked across the open-plan office. Olesya was at her desk, shutting down her computer. She wiped the keyboard before covering it with a thin plastic sheet she had brought from somewhere.

'She is not good with data,' Rauf said.

'No.'

'You chose her.'

'Yes.'

'Why?'

Thomas looked at the stack of papers on his desk, then at the row of physical folders she had reorganised along the cabinet. Before she arrived, he had lost ten minutes twice a day finding the right document. Now every file came to hand before he knew he needed it.

'She sees different failures.'

Rauf followed his gaze. 'That sounds expensive.'

'Not if I know which failures are mine.'

Rauf laughed once. 'Careful. That is how Baku gets you. First you think you understand the problem. Then the problem invites you to dinner and asks for a favour.'

Thomas capped his pen. 'Good night, Rauf.'

'Good night, Mr Systems.'

Rauf left.

At six thirty, Olesya came to Thomas's door with her handbag over her shoulder.

'I am going.'

'Good night.'

She hesitated. 'The driver slips from Sumgait are in the green folder. The foreman signed only two copies. I put a note.'

'I saw it.'

'And the courier for the Ministry comes at nine. He will ask for me.'

'I will tell him.'

'No. He does not like speaking English.'

'Then I will point.'

This time the smile almost stayed.

'Good night, Thomas.'

She used his first name as if testing whether it would be refused.

He looked at the permit forms. 'Good night, Olesya.'

The door closed behind her.

The office settled. Without the movement of people, the building revealed its smaller noises: the tremor of air through vents, the lift cables somewhere in the shaft, the faint tick of the cooling glass. Thomas worked through the attachment page by page. He corrected the margins. He rebuilt the table. He checked the stamp placement against the sample Olesya had left clipped to the folder. The blue ink had bled slightly into the paper fibres.

At eight, the cleaner came through with a vacuum cleaner and did not enter his office.

At nine, the harbour lights became the only visible order outside.

He printed the corrected document and laid the pages across the desk to dry. The stack looked clean, almost inevitable, as if it had always existed in that form. No one at the Ministry would know the first version had been unusable. No one offshore would know a permit had nearly stalled in a room above the harbour because a stamp needed to be blue on every page.

The paper cooled under his hand.

By morning, it would look untouched.

Thomas gathered Olesya's original pages and put them in the shredder tray. Then he stopped.

Her handwriting ran along the top corner of the first sheet, small and upright.

Blue stamp every page. He will reject black.

No explanation. No apology.

He placed the note in his drawer instead.

On his way out, he paused beside her desk. The fern had turned slightly towards the window, each frond holding the last of the office light. Her ruler lay exactly parallel to the keyboard. The chair was tucked in. Beneath the monitor, on a square of folded tissue, she had left a piece of wrapped chocolate.

Thomas looked towards the dark glass of his office, where the corrected permit forms waited in their clean stack.

He picked up the chocolate and put it in his coat pocket.

Downstairs, the night guard unlocked the front door for him. The street smelled of diesel, rain, and the sea. Across the road, the Caspian moved in the dark without showing its depth.

Thomas walked back towards the flat with the document bag under his arm.

In the morning, the courier would come for Olesya.
