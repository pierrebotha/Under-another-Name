Chapter 11
The silence in the flat had changed texture. It was no longer the heavy, waiting silence of the previous weeks, nor the charged, electric silence of surveillance that had hummed beneath the floorboards like a live wire. It was a hollow, ringing silence, like the inside of a bell after the strike has faded, leaving only the vibration in the metal. Thomas stood in the centre of the living room, staring at the nine stains on the floor. They looked different in the harsh morning light that cut through the gap in the curtains. Less like accidents, less like spills, more like coordinates. A map of where he had been, and where he had failed to go. A constellation of failure mapped onto the laminate.
He counted them again. One. Two. Three. Four. Five. Six. Seven. Eight. Nine.
The first was wine. The second, rain. The third, water from a knocked-over glass. The fourth, more rain. The fifth, a spill from a mug left too close to the edge. The sixth, mud tracked in from the park. The seventh, coffee. The eighth, more rain. The ninth, condensation from his coat last night.
Nine stains. Nine failures. Nine marks of a life unraveling.
They seemed to pulse in the grey light. Dark islands in a sea of pale wood. He felt an urge to clean them. To get a bucket, a scrubbing brush, bleach. To scour the floor until the laminate was raw, until the pattern was stripped away, until the wood beneath was bare and screaming. But the thought exhausted him. The effort required to bend down, to scrub, to rinse, to dry—it felt insurmountable. And besides, if he cleaned them, what would be left? What proof would there be that anything had happened? Without the stains, the flat was just a flat. Empty, yes, but normal. And normalcy was the most dangerous illusion of all. To clean the stains was to lie to himself. To say it hadn’t happened. To say he wasn’t broken.
So he left them. He stepped over them. Step-step-hop. A dance of avoidance he had perfected over the last week. A ritual of navigation. Don’t touch the wine. Sidestep the rain. Hop the coffee. If he stepped on one, he felt a jolt of wrongness, a violation of the order he had imposed on his chaos.
He had not slept well. The dream of the rain had left him feeling damp, chilled to the bone despite the dry, stale air of the room. He had dreamt of drowning in a room full of water that wouldn’t wet anything else. Just him. Soaked. Suffocating. He woke up gasping, sheets tangled around his legs, heart hammering against his ribs like a bird trapped in a cage.
He showered, but the water felt thin, ineffective. It slid off his skin without cleaning anything. The shampoo smelled of nothing. The soap left a film. He stood under the spray for twenty minutes, letting the hot water beat against his neck, his shoulders, trying to wash away the feeling of being watched. But the feeling wasn’t on his skin. It was inside. In the marrow. In the synapses. You can’t scrub off a ghost.
He dressed in the same clothes as yesterday: jeans, jumper, socks. The fabric was stiff, holding the shape of his body, smelling faintly of wet wool and stale coffee. He didn’t care. Hygiene was a ritual for people who believed in a future. For people who expected to be seen. Thomas didn’t expect to be seen. He expected to be overlooked. Ignored. Erased.
He walked to the kitchen. The kettle sat on the counter, unplugged. He had unplugged everything yesterday. The fridge hummed alone, a solitary sound in the dead room. A lonely, mechanical wheeze. He filled the kettle from the tap. The water ran cold, then lukewarm, then cold again. He watched it fill the glass, the bubbles clinging to the sides like tiny, desperate hands. He drank it standing up. It tasted of metal. Of pipes. Of nothing.
He needed to do something. But what?
The spreadsheet was locked in the drawer. The laptop was buried under a pile of wires. The phone was gone, lost or discarded, he couldn’t remember which. He was cut off. Analog. Invisible.
Or so he thought.
He walked to the window. Pulled the curtain back a fraction. Just enough to see.
The street below was busy. People rushing to work. Buses rumbling past. Life continuing in its straight, predictable lines. He felt like an observer from another planet. Watching a species he used to belong to. They had places to go. Things to do. Lives that made sense. Thomas had none of that. He had only the walk. The wait. The stains.
A van parked across the street. White. Unmarked. No logo. Just a blank white box on wheels.
He stared at it.
Had it been there yesterday? He didn’t think so. But memory was unreliable. Memory was a sieve. It held the big things—the fear, the pain, the voice on the phone—but let the details slip through. When had the van arrived? Who was inside? Why were they there?
Two men sitting inside. Drinking coffee. Looking at a clipboard.
Plumbers? Surveyors? Or watchers?
They weren’t looking at the building. They were looking at the clipboard. One of them pointed at something on the paper. The other nodded. They looked bored. Routine.
But boredom was a good cover. Watchers didn’t want to be seen. They wanted to be invisible. Just part of the background. Like a white van on a grey street.
He let the curtain fall back. Snapped shut.
It didn’t matter. If they were watching, they were welcome to it. There was nothing left to see. Just a man standing in a room with nine stains. A man who talked to himself. Who stepped over dark patches on the floor. Who dreamed of rain.
Let them watch. Let them take notes. Let them file their reports.
Subject: Thomas Ward. Status: Broken. Activity: Staring at floor. Threat level: None.
He turned away from the window. Walked to the door.
He needed to leave. The flat was shrinking. The walls were pressing in, inch by inch. The ceiling was lowering. The air was getting thick, hard to breathe. He needed space. Sky. Wind. Even if it was cold. Even if it was wet.
He grabbed his coat. Put it on. Didn’t check the pockets. Nothing there anyway. No phone. No wallet. Just keys.
Keys. He needed keys.
He found them on the table. Cold metal. Heavy. Jagged edges biting into his palm.
He opened the door. Stepped out into the stairwell.
The smell of boiled cabbage and damp concrete hit him. Familiar. Comforting, even. It was the smell of home. Of history. Of a thousand other people who had walked these stairs before him.
He walked down the stairs. One flight. Two flights. Three.
Each step creaked under his weight. A familiar complaint. A sound he had heard a thousand times. Now it sounded like a warning. Turn back. Turn back. Turn back.
He didn’t turn back.
Pushed open the front door.
The air outside was crisp. Cold. Smelling of exhaust and wet pavement. The sky was a uniform sheet of grey cloud, low and heavy. Rain threatened but didn’t fall. Not yet.
He started walking.
No destination. Just movement.
He walked towards the high street. Past the bakery. The smell of yeast and sugar was still there, sweet and cloying, but it didn’t tempt him. It smelled like a memory of hunger, not actual hunger. He wasn’t hungry. He was full. Full of dread. Full of silence.
Past the newsagent. The headlines screamed about wars and scandals. Politicians resigning. Celebrities divorcing. Markets crashing. The world ending in slow motion. He didn’t stop to read. It was all noise. Static. None of it mattered. None of it was real. The only reality was the flat. The stains. The voice on the phone.
Past the off-license. The neon sign buzzed, even in the daylight. A low, electric hum that vibrated in his teeth. Open. Open. Open. He didn’t go in. Alcohol was a temporary fix. A bandage on a bullet wound. He needed something else. Something permanent. Or maybe nothing at all.
He kept walking.
Ended up in the park.
The grass was muddy. Churned up by dogs and children. The trees were bare skeletons against the grey sky. Branches reaching up like claws. Begging for rain.
He found a bench. Sat down.
It was the same bench as before. The wood was still damp. Cold seeping through his trousers. He didn’t care. Let it cold. Let it chill. It was the only thing that felt real.
He watched the dogs. Running. Chasing. Barking.
Simple lives. Simple joys. Throw ball. Dog fetch. Happy dog. No hidden agendas. No digital leashes. No ghosts.
A dog ran up to him. A terrier mix. Rough fur. Bright eyes. Tail wagging.
It sniffed his hand. Wet nose. Cold leather.
Thomas reached out. Stroked the rough fur behind the ears.
‘Good boy,’ he said.
His voice sounded strange. Unused. Rusty.
The owner called the dog. A woman. Young. Wrapped in a scarf.
‘Come on, Buster!’
The dog ran off. Yapping. Happy.
Thomas watched it go.
He felt a pang of something. Loneliness? Envy? Grief?
It was hard to name. It was a mixture of all of them. A cocktail of emotions he couldn’t digest.
He sat there for an hour. Maybe two. Time had lost its meaning. It was a flat, grey expanse with no beginning and no end. Just the bench. The mud. The dogs. The people walking past.
Joggers. Mothers with prams. Old men feeding pigeons. Teenagers laughing. Couples holding hands.
None of them looked at him. He was invisible. A ghost on a bench. A stain on the park.
He wondered if Olesya was watching him now. From a distance? Through a camera? Or was she bored? Had she moved on to someone else? Another experiment? Another subject?
Subject: Thomas Ward. Status: Boring. Experiment terminated.
He hoped so. He hoped she was bored. He hoped she had forgotten him.
But he knew she hadn’t. She never forgot. She collected things. Memories. Passwords. Fears. He was just another item in her collection. A rare specimen. Man who broke under pressure.
He stood up. Legs stiff. Joints creaking.
He started walking back.
Towards the flat. Towards the silence. Towards the stains.
He passed the tech store again.
The screens in the window were still bright. Still scrolling their lies.
Secure. Private. Encrypted. Safe.
The words flashed in bright, confident fonts. Blue. Green. White.
Lies. All of it. Marketing fluff designed to sell fear and then sell the cure. There was no security. Only varying degrees of vulnerability. Only different ways to be watched.
He stopped. Stared at them.
A reflection moved in the glass. Behind him.
He turned.
A woman. Walking past. Looking at her phone. Typing. Smiling.
She didn’t look at him. She didn’t see him.
He turned back to the window.
His own reflection stared back. Pale. Gaunt. Eyes hollowed out by lack of sleep. Hair messy. Clothes wrinkled.
He looked like a stranger.
Like a man who had already disappeared.
Delete yourself, Olesya had said.
Cease to exist.
Was that the only way out?
To become nobody?
To walk away from the name, the history, the pain?
Under another name.
The phrase echoed in his mind.
Just access. Under another name.
Was it a threat? Or a promise?
If he became nobody, would she lose interest?
If there was no Thomas Ward to track, no history to weaponize, no passwords to crack… would she let him go?
Or would she just follow him into the nothingness? Would she be the only thing left, the only definition he had?
You’re more connected to me now than you ever were when you were texting me goodnight.
She was right. He was connected. By fear. By paranoia. By the constant scanning of every shadow. She was in his head. In his operating system.
He turned away from the window. Walked on.
The sky was darkening. Clouds gathering. Heavy with rain.
He walked faster.
Wanting to get inside. To hide.
He reached his building. Climbed the stairs.
Three flights. Legs burning. Breath short.
Unlocked the door. Stepped inside.
The flat was exactly as he had left it. Silent. Cold. Dark.
He took off his coat. Dropped it on the floor.
Took off his shoes. Kicked them into the corner.
Walked to the kitchen.
The stains were still there. Nine of them.
He stared at them.
They looked darker now. Deeper.
Permanent.
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa.
Closed his eyes.
Waited.
For what?
He didn’t know.
For the call? For the knock? For the end?
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of names. Thousands of them. Floating in the air.
Thomas. John. David. Michael. Robert.
He tried to catch them, but they dissolved in his hands. Turning to dust. Slipping through his fingers.
He dreamt of Olesya. Standing in a room full of mirrors.
Each mirror showed a different version of him.
One crying. One angry. One sleeping. One dead.
She was pointing at one. Laughing.
That one, she said. That’s the real one.
He woke up gasping.
Pitch black.
Silence.
No phone buzzing. No calls.
Just the sound of his own breathing. Loud. Ragged.
He lay there. Listening.
Wondering if she was out there. Watching. Waiting.
Wondering if he was still Thomas.
Or if he had already become someone else.
Someone under another name.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed it away.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh. Cutting through the curtains.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was silent.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up.
Looked at the nine stains on the floor.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day.
