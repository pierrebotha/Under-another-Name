Chapter 11
The silence in the flat had changed texture. It was no longer the heavy, waiting silence of the previous weeks, nor the charged, electric silence of surveillance. It was a hollow, ringing silence, like the inside of a bell after the strike has faded. Thomas stood in the centre of the living room, staring at the nine stains on the floor. They looked different in the harsh morning light. Less like accidents, more like coordinates. A map of where he had been, and where he had failed to go.
He had not slept well. The dream of the rain had left him feeling damp, chilled to the bone despite the dry air of the room. He showered, but the water felt thin, ineffective. It slid off his skin without cleaning anything. He dressed in the same clothes as yesterday: jeans, jumper, socks. The fabric was stiff, holding the shape of his body, smelling faintly of wet wool and stale coffee. He didn’t care. Hygiene was a ritual for people who believed in a future.
He walked to the kitchen. The kettle sat on the counter, unplugged. He had unplugged everything yesterday. The fridge hummed alone, a solitary sound in the dead room. He filled the kettle from the tap. The water ran cold, then lukewarm, then cold again. He watched it fill the glass, the bubbles clinging to the sides. He drank it standing up. It tasted of metal. Of pipes. Of nothing.
He needed to do something. But what?
The spreadsheet was locked in the drawer. The laptop was buried under a pile of wires. The phone was gone, lost or discarded, he couldn’t remember which. He was cut off. Analog. Invisible.
Or so he thought.
He walked to the window. Pulled the curtain back a fraction.
The street below was busy. People rushing to work. Buses rumbling past. Life continuing in its straight, predictable lines. He felt like an observer from another planet. Watching a species he used to belong to.
A van parked across the street. White. Unmarked. No logo.
He stared at it.
Had it been there yesterday? He didn’t think so. But memory was unreliable.
Two men sitting inside. Drinking coffee. Looking at a clipboard.
Plumbers? Surveyors? Or watchers?
He let the curtain fall back.
It didn’t matter. If they were watching, they were welcome to it. There was nothing left to see. Just a man standing in a room with nine stains.
He turned away from the window. Walked to the door.
He needed to leave. The flat was shrinking. The walls were pressing in, inch by inch.
He grabbed his coat. Put it on. Didn’t check the pockets. Nothing there anyway.
Keys. He needed keys.
He found them on the table. Cold metal. Heavy.
He opened the door. Stepped out into the stairwell.
The smell of boiled cabbage and damp concrete hit him. Familiar. Comforting, even.
He walked down the stairs. One flight. Two flights. Three.
Pushed open the front door.
The air outside was crisp. Cold. Smelling of exhaust and wet pavement.
He started walking.
No destination. Just movement.
He walked towards the high street. Past the bakery. The smell of yeast and sugar was still there, but it didn’t tempt him. Past the newsagent. The headlines screamed about wars and scandals. He didn’t stop to read. Past the off-license. The neon sign buzzed, even in the daylight.
He kept walking.
Ended up in the park.
The grass was muddy. Churned up by dogs and children. The trees were bare skeletons against the grey sky.
He found a bench. Sat down.
It was the same bench as before. The wood was still damp. He didn’t care.
He watched the dogs. Running. Chasing. Barking.
Simple lives. Simple joys.
A dog ran up to him. A terrier mix. Sniffed his hand.
Thomas reached out. Stroked the rough fur.
‘Good boy,’ he said.
The owner called the dog. It ran off.
Thomas watched it go.
He felt a pang of something. Loneliness? Envy?
It was hard to name.
He sat there for an hour. Maybe two. Time had lost its meaning.
People walked past. Joggers. Mothers with prams. Old men feeding pigeons.
None of them looked at him. He was invisible. A ghost on a bench.
Eventually, he stood up. Legs stiff. Joints creaking.
He started walking back.
Towards the flat. Towards the silence. Towards the stains.
He passed the tech store again.
The screens in the window were still bright. Still scrolling their lies.
Secure. Private. Encrypted.
He stopped. Stared at them.
A reflection moved in the glass. Behind him.
He turned.
A woman. Walking past. Looking at her phone.
She didn’t look at him.
He turned back to the window.
His own reflection stared back. Pale. Gaunt. Eyes hollow.
He looked like a stranger.
Like a man who had already disappeared.
Delete yourself, Olesya had said.
Cease to exist.
Was that the only way out?
To become nobody?
To walk away from the name, the history, the pain?
Under another name.
The phrase echoed in his mind.
Just access. Under another name.
Was it a threat? Or a promise?
If he became nobody, would she lose interest?
Or would she just follow him into the nothingness?
He turned away from the window. Walked on.
The sky was darkening. Clouds gathering. Heavy with rain.
He walked faster.
Wanting to get inside. To hide.
He reached his building. Climbed the stairs.
Three flights. Legs burning. Breath short.
Unlocked the door. Stepped inside.
The flat was exactly as he had left it. Silent. Cold. Dark.
He took off his coat. Dropped it on the floor.
Took off his shoes. Kicked them into the corner.
Walked to the kitchen.
The stains were still there. Nine of them.
He stared at them.
They looked darker now. Deeper.
Permanent.
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa.
Closed his eyes.
Waited.
For what?
He didn’t know.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of names. Thousands of them. Floating in the air.
He tried to catch them, but they dissolved in his hands.
He dreamt of Olesya. Standing in a room full of mirrors.
Each mirror showed a different version of him.
She was pointing at one. Laughing.
That one, she said. That’s the real one.
He woke up gasping.
Pitch black.
Silence.
No phone buzzing. No calls.
Just the sound of his own breathing.
He lay there. Listening.
Wondering if she was out there. Watching. Waiting.
Wondering if he was still Thomas.
Or if he had already become someone else.
Someone under another name.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed it away.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was silent.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up.
Looked at the nine stains on the floor.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day.
