Chapter 04

The Sumgait inspection was scheduled for a Thursday, which meant by Wednesday afternoon three people had already tried to move it.

The yard wanted Friday, claiming the weld team would still be finishing the final section. Procurement wanted Monday; the payment release had not been checked against the latest delivery note. Rauf wanted Thursday, while the defects were still visible and the money still mattered.

'Thursday,' he said, standing in Thomas's office with the inspection pack under one arm. 'Before they grind the ugly parts smooth and call it progress.'

Thomas took the pack. 'Who is translating?'

Rauf looked through the glass.

Olesya was at her desk, writing a courier reference into the collection book. Her grey hat lay beside the fern. The repaired cuff of her coat had opened again near the seam, a small dark mouth in the fabric.

'She knows the yard men,' Rauf said.

'Do they know her?'

'That is not always an advantage.'

Thomas looked through the glass again.

Olesya had opened the top drawer and was sorting the collection receipts by date. The drawer money, when it appeared, vanished without altering the order of anything around it. He had begun to notice this too. The absence left no mark. No shifted receipt book. No torn envelope. No note of thanks. The office simply continued the next morning with coffee on his coaster and a corrected physical file on her desk.

'What does Murat want?' Thomas asked.

'Payment.'

'Everyone wants payment.'

'Murat wants payment without inspection.'

'Then he will be disappointed.'

Rauf gave him a look. 'Disappointment is easy in an office. In a yard, it has tools.'

Olesya came back to the doorway with the collection book closed.

'What time tomorrow?'

'Seven thirty,' Thomas said.

'I will prepare the weld map.'

'Bring the photographs from last week. The missing section from D bay as well.'

She nodded.

'And the old test certificates,' Rauf said. 'Murat likes to claim memory has no paperwork.'

'Which certificates?'

'All of them.'

'That is not a number.'

'Then bring too many.'

She wrote it down in her notebook: too many certificates.

Thomas saw it and almost smiled. He did not.

That afternoon, she built the Sumgait pack on the long table in Meeting Room Three. Thomas found her there after five, sleeves rolled to her wrists, grey hat beside a stack of folders. She had arranged the documents in bands across the glass: current weld map, previous inspection photographs, pressure-test certificates, payment release, vendor correspondence, blank non-conformance forms.

Each stack had a paper label.

Thomas stood at the door.

'You have made a war room.'

'It is too small for war.'

'A skirmish room.'

She glanced up. 'That is closer.'

He walked to the table and checked the order. The current map was on top. The photographs had been marked in pencil at the corner, C seventeen, C nineteen, D two. The payment release sat beneath a red folder, visible but not accessible by accident.

'This is good.'

'It is complete.'

'Those are different things.'

'Today they are both.'

He picked up one photograph. The weld bead showed as a dark raised line beneath harsh yard light. Along the lower edge, mud had spattered the steel in small, dried flecks.

'This is the section from last week.'

'Yes.'

'The one the yard said was missing.'

'It was never missing.'

'Where was it?'

'Behind the wrong name.'

Thomas looked at her.

'The folder said auxiliary frame,' she said. 'The photograph said main support. They hoped the name would be enough.'

'And was it?'

'For someone who reads names, maybe.'

He put the photograph back.

The office outside had thinned to end-of-day sounds. A chair moved. A drawer closed. Somewhere near reception, the coffee machine expelled steam into its tray.

'You do not have to stay late for this,' he said.

'I know.'

'Then why are you?'

She aligned the edge of the red folder with the table.

'Tomorrow he will say we brought the wrong paper.'

'And if we bring every paper?'

'Then he will say paper does not build rigs.'

'Does it?'

'No.'

She pressed the label flat with her thumb. 'But it releases money.'

The red folder stayed under her hand.

Thomas watched her hand move across the page. Small letters. Firm pressure. No wasted space.

'Send her in.'

Rauf did not move at once. 'Sumgait is not the office.'

'I know.'

'No, you know the map. That is different.'

Olesya came to the doorway before Thomas could answer. She had the collection book still open in her hands.

'You need me?'

Rauf turned. 'Inspection tomorrow. Sumgait fabrication yard. You will interpret.'

The change in her face was almost nothing. A tightening near the mouth. Her fingers shifted on the spine of the collection book.

'Which foreman?'

'Murat.'

She looked down at the collection book.

'You know him?' Thomas asked.

'Everyone knows him.'

'That was not my question.'

'Yes,' she said.

Rauf took the book from her and closed it. 'Seven thirty. Company Land Cruiser. Bring the current weld map, the pressure-test photographs, and your warm coat.'

'My coat is warm.'

Rauf looked at the torn cuff.

Olesya put her hand over it.

'Seven thirty,' she said.

She left with the book held against her chest.

The next morning, Baku was still dark when Thomas reached the office car park. Rain had fallen during the night and left the concrete shining under the security lights. The Land Cruiser waited near the gate, white paint streaked with road dirt, tyres already carrying a skin of mud from some earlier trip. The driver smoked beside the bonnet with one hand tucked under his opposite arm.

Olesya stood by the rear door.

She wore the second-hand coat, the grey hat, and black gloves with the fingertips worn thin. A document sleeve was tucked inside the coat, protected from the damp. She had placed herself under the narrow shelter of the entrance canopy, close enough to the car to be ready, far enough from the driver that no conversation was required.

'Morning,' Thomas said.

'Good morning.'

The driver dropped his cigarette and crushed it beneath his shoe.

'Sumgait,' he said.

The word filled the car for a moment.

The city thinned as they drove north-west. Glass towers gave way to low blocks, repair yards, petrol stations, strips of waste ground where dogs moved through wet grass. The road carried buses, tankers, men in vans with fogged windows. Beyond the outskirts, the land flattened into a colourless industrial belt. Pipes ran beside the road on concrete supports. Smoke lifted from stacks and spread sideways under the low cloud.

Thomas sat in the front passenger seat with the inspection pack on his lap. Olesya sat behind him. In the rear-view mirror he could see her hands resting on the document sleeve. The gloves made them look smaller.

Rauf had not come.

That was deliberate. Rauf had left his familiarity at the office. A foreign engineer could say no in places where local refusals were folded into older obligations.

'Murat will say the welds are complete,' Olesya said from the back seat.

'Are they?'

'Some will be.'

'And the others?'

'He will say they are nearly complete.'

The driver laughed once.

Thomas turned a page in the pack. 'Nearly complete does not release payment.'

Olesya looked out of the window. 'He knows.'

The yard appeared first as a line of cranes against the grey sky. Then fencing, floodlights, stacked beams, the long ribs of unfinished structures, men moving between them in orange jackets. The Land Cruiser stopped at the security gate while the guard checked the entry list with a biro that would not write in the damp. Olesya spoke to him through the window. He answered without looking at Thomas.

Inside the yard, the ground was packed mud and crushed aggregate. Water gathered in ruts left by heavy tyres. Somewhere unseen, metal screamed under a grinder. The sound travelled across the yard in bright, hard bursts, stopping and starting like an argument.

Murat met them near Bay Three.

He was a large man with a shaved head, broad through the stomach, wearing a leather jacket beneath his high-visibility vest. His hard hat sat pushed back on his head, safety gear turned into ornament. Two welders stood behind him, both smoking, both looking at Olesya before they looked at Thomas.

Murat spoke in Russian.

Olesya listened without moving.

'He says the welds are complete,' she said. 'The inspection team is wasting time.'

Thomas opened the weld map. 'Tell him we will begin with section C seventeen.'

She translated.

Murat answered too quickly. The welders laughed.

Olesya did not translate.

Thomas looked at her.

'He says C seventeen is not accessible.'

'Why?'

She asked.

Murat pointed towards the bay and spoke again, longer this time. His hand moved through the air, cutting, dismissing, circling back towards Olesya and making her the obstruction.

'He says materials are stored there,' she said.

'Then the materials can be moved.'

She translated.

Murat laughed. He took a step closer to her. The mud sucked at his boot.

Thomas heard one word clearly. Russkaya.

Olesya's face did not change. Her thumb moved once against the edge of the document sleeve.

'Translate all of it,' Thomas said.

She looked at him.

'All of it.'

Her eyes returned to Murat. 'He says a Russian woman from an office does not tell yard men how to build for the sea.'

The welders stopped laughing before Murat did.

Thomas closed the inspection pack.

'Tell him the signature on the payment release is mine.'

Olesya translated.

Murat's mouth flattened.

'Tell him section C seventeen is cleared in ten minutes, or the release waits.'

She translated again.

Murat looked past her to Thomas and answered in broken English.

'You see weld. You pay.'

'I see weld pass inspection. Then payment is released.'

Murat spat into the mud, close enough to Olesya's boot for the splash to mark the leather.

Thomas stepped between them before deciding to.

The yard noise continued around them. Grinder, hammer, engine, wind through sheet metal. Murat looked at Thomas's chest, then his face.

'Ten minutes,' Thomas said.

The silence held for three breaths.

Then Murat turned and shouted towards the bay.

Men began moving steel.

Olesya looked down at her boot. A fleck of mud clung near the toe. She did not wipe it away.

They inspected C seventeen in a wind that drove grit against Thomas's cheek. The weld ran along a joint beneath a cross member, uneven in three places, with undercutting visible where the bead failed to meet the edge cleanly. Thomas crouched, wiped rainwater away with the back of his glove, and marked the defect on the sheet.

'Ultrasound,' he said.

Olesya translated.

Murat objected. She translated only the useful part. The equipment was at another bay. The technician was not available. The surface was wet. The test would delay work.

'The surface can be dried. The technician can be called. Work is already delayed.'

She repeated it.

The second weld failed visual inspection before they reached the end of the row. The third had been ground smooth. Too smooth. Thomas ran a finger along the cold metal, feeling for what had been hidden beneath the polish.

'Photograph this.'

Olesya took out her phone.

Murat said something to the welders.

Olesya's hand paused.

'What?'

'Nothing.'

'Olesya.'

She took the photograph. 'He says if I like pictures, I should photograph kitchens.'

Thomas looked at Murat.

Murat smiled.

'Take three photographs,' Thomas said.

She did.

At noon, the wind sharpened and the rain came in low from the sea. The yard office was a portable cabin with a heater that smelled of dust and hot plastic. Murat made tea for himself and the welders. No cup was offered to Olesya. Thomas declined before anyone could offer him one.

They stood around a metal table covered in drawings, inspection sheets, and old cigarette burns. Thomas marked the required retests. C seventeen. C nineteen. D two. Payment hold pending ultrasound. Photographic record attached. The pen dragged against damp paper.

Murat watched the marks accumulate.

'Too much,' he said in English.

'Enough.'

'You make problem.'

'No. I found one.'

Murat looked at Olesya. He said something low, almost conversational.

She translated with her eyes on the table. 'He says you should listen to local advice.'

'That is not what he said.'

She did not answer.

Thomas placed the pen down and looked at Murat. 'The release is held until the tests are complete.'

Murat's hand struck the table.

The drawings lifted at the corners.

Olesya did not step back. Only her shoulders changed, drawing in beneath the coat. Thomas knew the smaller signals now: the thumb on paper, the drawer click, the cup sound against the coaster.

'We are finished here,' he said.

Outside, the yard had become a blur of wet steel and moving jackets. The driver brought the Land Cruiser close to the cabin door. Olesya climbed into the back without speaking. Thomas took the front passenger seat again, the inspection pack damp at the corners.

No one spoke until the gate was behind them.

The driver turned up the heater. Warm air pushed dust from the vents. It smelled of old fabric and diesel.

Thomas looked through the side mirror. The yard receded behind fencing and rain.

'He knew I am Russian,' Olesya said.

The driver kept his eyes on the road.

Thomas turned slightly. 'Yes.'

'It matters there.'

'Only there?'

She looked out at the industrial land slipping past. Pipes, mud, low walls, men waiting under a shelter with cigarettes cupped in their hands.

'Some places say it. Some places only remember.'

The sentence ended there.

Thomas did not ask for more. The question would have required her to make a map for his benefit. She had already translated enough for one morning.

The road back towards Baku ran beside a drainage canal the colour of weak tea. Plastic bottles knocked against the concrete edges in the wind. Beyond the canal, housing blocks stood with their balconies enclosed in mismatched glass and metal, each one an attempt to claim a little more room from the weather. Laundry moved behind the panes. A woman in a red coat shook a rug over a railing and watched the Land Cruiser pass.

Olesya's reflection hovered in the side window, layered over the blocks and the canal and the low industrial sky.

'In school,' she said, 'they changed the language of the forms.'

Thomas waited.

'My mother had to fill everything again. Same address. Same child. Same building. New forms.'

'Because of Russian?'

'Because of many things.'

The driver adjusted the heater. The fan clicked louder.

'She made mistakes?'

'No.' Olesya looked down at the document sleeve on her lap. 'She made copies.'

The answer came with a small finality. A house rule from before Thomas knew her. If the city changed the form, you kept the old one. If a clerk denied a stamp, you produced the previous stamp. If a man in a yard pretended memory belonged to him, you brought paper.

Thomas looked at the inspection pack on his knees. Too many certificates.

Not too many.

Enough.

They drove through a district of repair shops and concrete walls painted with advertisements faded by salt air. The rain loosened and stopped. Puddles held the colour of the sky.

Olesya's phone rang.

The sound was thin in the heated car.

She looked at the screen and answered in Russian. At first she spoke quietly. Then the words came faster. Thomas caught only fragments: today, no, work, please, daughter, money. Dengi. Again. Twice more.

The driver glanced at her in the mirror.

Olesya turned her face towards the window.

When the call ended, she held the phone in both hands.

'Turn left here,' she said.

The driver looked at Thomas.

'Where?' Thomas asked.

'Please. There is a payment centre.'

'For what?'

She did not answer quickly enough.

The driver slowed at the junction.

'Turn left,' Thomas said.

The road narrowed into an older residential district, dense with low shops, balconies caged in metal, laundry hanging stiff in the damp air. Satellite dishes clung to walls, wet black bowls turned towards the cloud. The Land Cruiser moved awkwardly between parked cars and men pushing handcarts stacked with boxes. Children in dark school uniforms crossed through traffic without looking at the vehicle.

Olesya leaned forward and pointed. 'There.'

The payment centre sat between a pharmacy and a shop selling phone cases. Its sign was half-lit though it was only early afternoon. Inside, through the glass, Thomas saw a queue of people holding folded bills, plastic cards, scraps of paper. Fluorescent light flattened every face.

Olesya opened the door before the driver had fully stopped.

'Wait here,' she said.

She took her document sleeve but left the grey hat on the seat.

Thomas watched her enter the payment centre. She joined the queue near the back, then moved forward one place, then another. Her coat was dark against the pale walls. She held a paper in one hand and her phone in the other.

The driver lit another cigarette with the window cracked open.

Five minutes passed.

Then ten.

The queue moved. Olesya reached the counter. She spoke to the clerk, a woman behind scratched glass. The clerk shook her head. Olesya unfolded the paper, pushed it through the slot, then pulled it back when the clerk tapped the glass.

Thomas opened his door.

The driver looked at him. 'Problem?'

'Probably.'

Inside, the room smelled of wet coats, warm plastic, and the faint medicinal sweetness from the pharmacy next door. A wall-mounted television played a music video with the sound off. People in the queue watched Thomas enter, then looked away.

Olesya was still at the counter.

'What is the problem?' he asked.

She turned too quickly. The paper bent in her hand.

'Nothing.'

The clerk said something through the glass.

Olesya answered sharply.

Thomas took one step closer. 'Olesya.'

She looked at the paper, then at the clerk, then at the floor.

'Electricity.'

'What about it?'

'They will cut it today.'

The clerk tapped the amount printed on the bill with one polished fingernail.

Thomas read the figure. It was not large in the way corporate numbers were large. It was not an amount that required approval chains, cost codes, or a line in a budget. It sat there with the blunt force of a closed door.

'I can pay tomorrow,' Olesya said to the clerk in Russian.

The clerk shook her head.

'I said today,' Olesya said.

'How much?' Thomas asked.

'No.'

The word came out before he had finished the question.

The people in the queue had become still in the way people become still when pretending not to watch.

'How much?' he asked the clerk.

The clerk looked at Olesya first.

Olesya's face had gone pale beneath the harsh light.

Thomas took out his wallet.

'Please do not,' she said.

The clerk turned the bill towards him and pointed.

Thomas counted the notes and passed them through the slot.

Olesya did not move.

The clerk counted twice, stamped the bill, tore off a receipt, and slid it back. The stamp was blue. The same colour from the office had followed Olesya into this room.

Thomas picked up the receipt and handed it to her.

She took it by the edge.

Outside, the rain had stopped entirely. The street had brightened without sun. Water ran along the gutter, carrying cigarette ends, leaves, a strip of orange plastic from a shopping bag.

They walked back to the car.

Olesya opened the rear door, then stopped.

'I will pay you back.'

'No.'

'Thomas.'

'No.'

The driver watched them through the windscreen.

She folded the receipt once, then unfolded it. The paper had already softened from her fingers.

'This is not office.'

'Electricity affects work.'

'No.'

'If your electricity is cut, you do not sleep. If you do not sleep, you make errors.'

She looked at him. He had turned the gift into a work instruction.

'So it is for the spreadsheet?'

'Yes.'

The answer held.

Olesya looked down at the receipt. The blue stamp sat near the bottom, slightly crooked. Paid.

'I have money on Monday.'

'Then keep it.'

'For what?'

'The next thing.'

One corner of her mouth moved, then stopped.

'There is always a next thing.'

'Yes.'

They got into the car.

The grey hat lay on the seat beside her. She picked it up, smoothed it once, and held it in her lap for the rest of the journey.

Back at the office, Rauf was waiting by Thomas's door.

'Well?'

Thomas handed him the inspection pack. 'Three welds require ultrasound. Payment release held.'

Rauf opened the folder and read the first page. 'Murat will call me names.'

'He already did.'

'Good. Saves time.'

Rauf looked through the glass. Olesya had returned to her desk. She had taken off her coat and was placing the utility receipt inside the back cover of her notebook. Her movements slowed around the receipt.

Rauf closed the folder.

'Sumgait teaches quickly.'

'Apparently.'

'And slowly,' he said.

Thomas looked at him.

Rauf shook his head once and went back to his office.

The afternoon resumed around the held payment. Emails came from the yard before three. One from Murat, written in capital letters. One from the yard administrator, more polite and more dangerous; it copied senior people. Thomas replied to neither until he had attached the photographs, the weld-map references, and the payment clause.

Olesya translated the useful parts of each message and left the insults out.

'All of it,' Thomas said.

She looked at him across the desk.

'No.'

'Why?'

'Because this one is boring.'

He read the Russian line she had marked with her pencil. He knew enough now to recognise his own name and something about English hands.

'Boring?'

'Very.'

She took the printed page, placed it in the red folder, and aligned the corners.

At five, the ultrasound technician confirmed availability for the next morning. At five thirty, the yard accepted the hold in language that pretended it had been their idea. Rauf walked past Thomas's office and tapped the glass with two knuckles in approval.

Olesya stayed until six.

She did not bring the receipt to him. She did not mention the payment centre. When she shut down her computer, she opened the top drawer, placed the notebook inside, and closed it with the same small click as always.

The receipt was no longer visible.

Thomas passed her desk on the way to the printer and saw the notebook open beside the keyboard. Only the corner of the receipt showed from the back cover, the blue stamp cut in half by the paper edge. She had placed it there with the other things that had to be kept and not discussed.

He stopped long enough to read nothing.

On the top page of the notebook she had written the next morning's tasks:

Ultrasound confirmation.

Murat payment hold.

Driver slips.

The words sat in her usual narrow hand. Beneath them, separated by a line drawn with a ruler, she had written electricity.

Then she had crossed it out so completely that the paper had thinned beneath the ink.

Thomas went to the printer and collected the release memo. The machine was warm from use. The first page curled slightly in his hand.

When he came back, Olesya had closed the notebook.

At six ten, Sarah's name appeared on Thomas's phone.

He let it ring twice before answering. Olesya was still at her desk, winding the cord of her charger into a neat loop.

'You sound outside,' Sarah said.

'Office.'

'Then your office sounds like a road.'

Thomas looked towards the glass. The wind had risen again and was pushing against the building, making the seals murmur. 'Sumgait today.'

'Ah. The glamorous life.'

'Very.'

'Any casualties?'

'Three welds and one foreman's temper.'

'So a normal day.'

He could hear airport noise behind her: rolling suitcases, an announcement blurred by distance, the hollow acoustics of a terminal. Sarah was moving again. She always called between gates, between meetings, between one temporary room and the next.

'Where are you?' he asked.

'Geneva. Waiting for the late flight to London. They gave me an executive lounge voucher, which is how companies apologise without apologising.'

'Useful.'

'Barely. The wine is bad and a man beside me is explaining blockchain to someone trapped by politeness.'

Thomas watched Olesya lift her coat from the chair. The repaired cuff held.

'You sound tired,' Sarah said.

'Long day.'

'Come home for a weekend if you can. Or take one somewhere warmer. You do remember weekends?'

'In principle.'

'That means no.'

He smiled faintly. 'It means scheduling is complex.'

'Everything is scheduling with you.'

Olesya looked towards his office then, caught either by his voice or by the last small act of shutting down. She lifted one hand. Not a wave. A small practical acknowledgement, quick and exact.

Thomas lifted his hand in return.

Sarah said, 'Are you listening?'

'Yes.'

'No, you are looking at something.'

'Someone shutting down.'

'Important?'

The question was casual. It moved lightly through the line and left a small pressure at the base of his throat.

'A local administrator.'

'Then let her leave. You should too.'

'I will.'

'You will not.'

The terminal announcement rose behind her and swallowed the next words. She came back a moment later.

'Boarding. I will call tomorrow.'

'Safe flight.'

'Always.'

The call ended.

Olesya had gone back to her desk.

Her desk remained in its usual order. Fern turned towards the window. Keyboard covered. Receipt book absent from view. On the coaster beside Thomas's keyboard, the coffee cup from the morning still held a dark crescent at the bottom.

Thomas finished the release memo and saved it to the shared drive. The office lights caught his reflection in the window: white shirt, loosened tie, face blurred by the harbour darkness beyond the glass.

When he came out, Olesya was at the lift.

'Good night,' he said.

She turned.

'Good night.'

The lift arrived. The doors opened behind her.

She did not step in.

'The blue stamp,' she said.

'What about it?'

'At the payment centre. It was the same colour.'

Thomas thought of the Ministry documents, the yard pack, the electricity bill. Blue circles on paper. Permission attached to ink.

'Yes.'

'They stamp everything here,' she said.

'Most things.'

She looked at him for one second longer.

'Not everything.'

Then she stepped into the lift.

The doors closed.

Thomas returned to his office. On his desk, beside the keyboard, the weld photographs lay in a neat stack. C seventeen on top. Three angles. Mud at the edge of the frame. A dark line in the metal where the bead had failed to hold.

He placed the utility receipt number into his notebook, beneath the Sumgait release reference.

Then he crossed it out.
