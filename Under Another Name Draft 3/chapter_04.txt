Chapter 4
The rain had not stopped. It had merely changed its mind about how hard it fell, shifting from a drumming assault to a fine, persistent mist that coated everything in a slick, grey film. Thomas stood on the pavement opposite the police station, his shoes already saturated, the water seeping through the leather and soaking into his socks with a cold, clinging intimacy. He had been standing there for twelve minutes. He knew this because he had watched the digital display of his watch flip through the seconds, each one a small, silent judgement on his inability to move forward or turn back.
The building was a brutalist box of concrete and dirty glass, squatting on the corner like a forgotten bunker. The paint around the window frames was peeling in long, sunburnt strips, revealing the grey aggregate beneath. A sign above the heavy steel door read Metropolitan Police Service, but the 'S' in Service had fallen off, or perhaps had never been affixed properly, leaving it to read Metropolitan Police ervice. Thomas stared at the gap. It felt like a message. A missing letter in a word that promised order but delivered only fragmentation. Service. Service. The word echoed in his head, hollow and meaningless. What service was there for a man who had been picked clean by a ghost?
He shifted his weight from one foot to the other. The squelch of his shoe against the wet tarmac was loud in the quiet street. A bus rumbled past, its tyres hissing on the wet road, spraying a fine mist of dirty water onto the pavement. Thomas didn't flinch. He watched the droplets land on his coat, darkening the fabric, adding to the weight he was already carrying. He thought about walking in. He had rehearsed the walk a hundred times in his head during the journey from the bank. Push open the heavy steel door. Step into the warmth. Smell the floor polish and stale coffee. Walk to the desk. Speak to the officer. Say the words: I have been robbed. By a woman I loved. She took five thousand pounds and left me a note.
But the rehearsal always stopped there. At the moment of speaking. Because he knew, with a certainty that sat like a stone in his gut, what would happen next. He knew the script. He had seen it in films, read it in papers, heard it in the fragmented stories of colleagues. Domestic dispute. Civil matter. Insufficient evidence. Overseas jurisdiction. The phrases floated in his mind, disjointed and sharp. They were the walls of the maze, and he was the rat running into them again and again.
He looked at the door again. The steel was scratched, marked by thousands of hands, thousands of desperate people who had come here hoping for justice, for intervention, for someone to make it stop. How many of them had walked out feeling lighter? How many had walked out with the same heavy silence he carried now? He imagined the inside. The fluorescent lights humming with that low, electric buzz that vibrated in your teeth. The plastic screens separating hope from indifference. The young officers with tired eyes, typing reports that would vanish into a digital void, never to be read, never to be acted upon.
Five thousand pounds. The amount seemed absurd now. So much money. Enough for rent for months. Enough for a flight to Istanbul. Enough for a new start. And yet, to them, it would be nothing. A rounding error. A statistic in a spreadsheet that no one looked at. Resources are stretched, Mr Ward. He could hear the voice before he even heard the words. Polite. Professional. Dismissive. We can take a report, but without concrete evidence of coercion, without a recording, without a suspect within reach... there is very little we can do.
He closed his eyes. The mist settled on his eyelashes, blurring the world into a soft, grey wash. He thought about Olesya. Where was she right now? Sitting in a café in Sultanahmet? Walking along the Bosphorus? Watching him through the screen of her phone, seeing his location dot hovering outside the police station, smiling that tight, unreadable smile? You went to the police expecting them to save you, she had said. And they didn't. Did they? She hadn't known he was coming here. Not yet. But she would know. She always knew. The app. The tracking. The digital leash that he couldn't seem to cut.
If he went inside, he would give her exactly what she wanted. He would validate her narrative. See, Tom? You're just a victim. You need someone to save you. You can't do it yourself. She had called him a chain. A heavy, golden chain. Was going to the police just another link? Another way of binding himself to the system, to the rules, to the expectation that someone else would fix the mess she had made?
He opened his eyes. The door remained shut. The missing 'S' stared back at him. Metropolitan Police ervice. A broken promise. A fractured ideal. He took a step back. Then another. The decision wasn't a sudden epiphany; it was a slow, sinking realization, like watching a ship drift away from the dock, knowing you should jump but staying rooted to the spot. He couldn't go in. Not today. Maybe not ever. The shame of it burned in his chest, hot and acidic. Shame that he had been stolen from. Shame that he couldn't get the money back. Shame that he was standing in the rain, afraid to ask for help.
He turned away from the building. Started walking. He didn't have a destination. Just away. Away from the concrete box. Away from the missing letter. Away from the version of himself that believed in justice.
The streets were busy, despite the rain. Lunchtime rush. People hurrying under umbrellas, faces set in masks of determination. They had places to be. Things to do. Lives that moved in straight lines from point A to point B. Thomas walked among them, a ghost in his own city. He kept his head down, watching his feet. Squelch. Squelch. Squelch. The rhythm of wet shoes on wet pavement. A metronome counting down the seconds of a life that felt increasingly unmoored.
He passed a shop window. Caught his reflection. Pale. Haggard. Eyes sunken into dark sockets. Hair plastered to his forehead by the mist. He looked like a stranger. Like a man who had lost everything and was still waiting for the final blow. He looked away. Couldn't bear to see it. Couldn't bear to acknowledge that this was him now. This was the new normal. The man who stood outside police stations and turned away. The man who let thieves win.
His phone buzzed in his pocket.
The sound was sharp, sudden, cutting through the white noise of the traffic and the rain. Thomas froze. His heart hammered against his ribs, a frantic bird trapped in a cage. He didn't want to answer. He wanted to throw the phone into the gutter. Watch it sink into the dirty water. Smash it against the brick wall of the nearest building. But he couldn't. He had to know. He had to hear her voice. Even if it was to hurt him. Even if it was to remind him of his own weakness.
He pulled the phone out. The screen was wet, droplets distorting the light. Unknown Number. The words blinked at him, mocking, inevitable. He stared at it for a long moment. The rain dripped from his nose onto the screen. He wiped it away with his thumb. Swiped to answer. Put the phone to his ear.
'Hello?'
His voice sounded rough. Unused. Like he hadn't spoken in days.
Silence on the other end. Just the faint hiss of static. The sound of a connection waiting to be filled.
Then, a click. Soft. Deliberate.
'Tom.'
Her voice. Clear. Close. As if she was standing right behind him. No static this time. No distance. Just Olesya.
'Where are you?' he asked. He didn't mean to ask. The words just fell out.
'Does it matter?'
'Yes.'
'Why?'
'Because I want to know.'
'You want to know so you can come. So you can try to fix it.'
'I can't fix it.'
'No. You can't.'
The words hung in the air between them, heavy and final. Thomas gripped the phone tighter. His knuckles turned white. The rain was getting heavier now, soaking through his coat, chilling his skin. He didn't care. Let it rain. Let it wash everything away.
'I saw you,' she said.
Thomas stopped walking. 'Saw me? Where?'
'Outside the station.'
He froze. Blood turning to ice in his veins. 'What?'
'The app, Tom. The banking app. It tracks location. Remember? You set it up. "Find my device" for security. You wanted to know if I stole your phone. Now it tells me where you are.'
'You're watching me.'
'In a way. I got the notification. "Location update: Near Metropolitan Police Station." Cute, isn't it? That you went there. That you thought they could help.'
'I didn't go in.'
'I know. You stood there for twelve minutes. Then you turned away.'
Thomas felt a surge of nausea. Twelve minutes. She had been watching him for twelve minutes. Counting the seconds. Judging his hesitation. 'How long have you been watching?'
'Long enough.'
'Why? Why do you care?'
'I told you. I'm checking in. Making sure you're okay.'
'I'm not okay.'
'You will be. Eventually.'
'When?'
'When you stop waiting for someone to save you.'
The words hit him like a physical blow. He stumbled slightly. Caught himself on a lamppost. Cold metal against his palm. Rough texture. Wet with rain. 'You stole from me, Olesya. Five thousand pounds. You took everything.'
'I took what I needed. And I left you something.'
'What? A note? "For you"? Is that supposed to make it better?'
'It's supposed to make you think. To make you wake up. You were sleepwalking, Tom. Living in that flat. Working that job. Pretending everything was fine. Now you're not pretending anymore. Now you're awake.'
'I hate being awake.'
'That's because it hurts. But pain is real. Indifference is the lie.'
'I wish I was indifferent.'
'You can't be. Not anymore. I made sure of that.'
Thomas leaned against the lamppost. Head hanging. Rain running down his neck, under his collar. 'What do you want from me?'
'Nothing. I already took what I needed.'
'The money?'
'The money was just a symbol. What I really took was your attention. Your focus. For a few days, you haven't been sleepwalking. You've been angry. Scared. Alive.'
'That's twisted.'
'Maybe. Or maybe it's the only way to wake people up.'
'I loved you.'
Silence. Long. Heavy. The sound of traffic in the background. Horns. Engines. Distant voices. Istanbul. Still Istanbul.
'I know,' she said finally. Her voice was softer now. Quieter. 'And I loved you. But love wasn't enough. You taught me that.'
'I taught you?'
'Yes. With your silence. Your routines. Your fear of anything changing. You showed me that love without risk is just... habit. And I couldn't live like that anymore.'
'So you ran.'
'I flew.'
'To Istanbul.'
'To somewhere. Anywhere.'
'And now you're watching me. Through an app.'
'Just checking in. Making sure you're not doing anything stupid.'
'Like going to the police?'
'Exactly like that. You didn't go in, did you?'
'No.'
'Good. Because they wouldn't have helped. They would have called it a domestic dispute. Asked for evidence. Told you to go home and sort it out. And you would have walked out feeling smaller than when you walked in.'
Thomas said nothing. She was right. He knew she was right. The realization burned worse than the anger.
'See?' she continued. 'I know you better than you know yourself. I know what you're thinking before you think it. I know what you'll do before you do it. That's not stalking, Tom. That's intimacy. Twisted, maybe. But intimacy nonetheless.'
'We're not the same.'
'Aren't we? Both alone. Both trying to figure out what comes next.'
'I didn't choose this.'
'Didn't you? You chose to stay. To wait. To hope. That's a choice, Tom. A passive one, but a choice nonetheless.'
'What do you want me to do?'
'Live. Move on. Stop waiting for me to come back.'
'Will you come back?'
Silence. Longer this time. He could hear her breathing. Soft. Rhythmic. Alive.
'I don't know,' she said finally. 'Maybe. Maybe not. It depends.'
'On what?'
'On whether I find what I'm looking for.'
'And if you don't?'
'Then I'll keep looking.'
'And me? Where do I fit in all this?'
'You're the starting point, Tom. The place I left. But you're not the destination.'
The words echoed in his mind. Starting point. Not destination. He was the launchpad. The thing she pushed off from to fly away. Nothing more.
'Olesya...' he started.
'I have to go.'
'Wait. Please. Just tell me... are you safe?'
'I'm safe.'
'Are you alone?'
'That's not your business.'
'Please. Just tell me.'
'Goodbye, Tom. Change your passwords. All of them. And delete the app.'
The line went dead.
Thomas lowered the phone. Stared at the screen. Call Ended.
He stood there on the pavement. Rain pouring down now. Soaking him to the bone. Chilling him to the core.
People brushed past him. Hurrying. Ignoring him. A woman pushing a pram. A man on a bike. A group of teenagers laughing.
He felt invisible. Erased.
He put the phone in his pocket. Started walking again.
Towards home. Towards the flat. Towards the silence.
The walk back felt endless. Miles stretched into hours. Every step was a struggle. His legs felt heavy, filled with lead. His lungs burned with the cold, wet air. He didn't hurry. There was no point. The flat would be waiting. The silence would be waiting. The stains on the floor would be waiting.
He passed the same shops he had passed on the way out. The bakery. The newsagent. The off-license. Nothing had changed. The world kept turning, indifferent to his pain. Life went on. People bought bread. Read newspapers. Bought wine. Normal things. Things he used to do. Things he couldn't imagine doing again.
He reached his building. Stopped at the door. Looked up at the windows. His window. The third floor. The curtains were drawn. Dark. Empty.
He pushed the door open. Climbed the stairs. Three flights. Legs burning. Breath short. Each step creaked under his weight. A familiar complaint. A sound he had heard a thousand times. Now it sounded like a warning. Turn back. Turn back. Turn back.
He didn't turn back. Reached the landing. Found his door. Key in the lock. Turned. Click. The sound was loud in the quiet stairwell. Final.
He stepped inside.
The flat was dark. Cold. Smelling of nothing. Just air. Stale. Stagnant.
He took off his coat. Dropped it on the floor. Didn't hang it up. Took off his shoes. Kicked them into the corner. Didn't line them up.
Walked to the kitchen. Poured a glass of water. Drank it. The water was cold. Tasteless. It did nothing to quench the thirst that burned in his throat.
Looked at the phone on the counter.
It was dark. Sleeping.
He picked it up. Turned it over.
No new messages. No missed calls.
Just the time. Ticking forward. Indifferent.
He put the phone down.
Walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa.
Looked at the floor.
The stains were still there. The wine stain. The rain stain from his coat last night. And now, another one. From today. From the walk. From the standing outside the station. From the rain that had soaked through his shoes and trousers.
Three stains. Dark patches on the laminate. Records of spills. Of breaks. Of calls. Of walks in the rain.
Marks that something had happened. Even if nothing had changed.
He leaned back. Closed his eyes.
Five thousand pounds.
For you.
You're the starting point. Not the destination.
Change your passwords.
The words echoed in his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed them away.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of passwords. Thousands of them. Floating in the air. Letters and numbers swirling like snowflakes. He tried to catch them, but they dissolved in his hands.
He dreamt of the police station. The missing 'S'. The door opening to reveal not an office, but a vast, empty space. Filled with mirrors. Thousands of mirrors. And in every mirror, a reflection of Olesya. Watching. Smiling. Pointing.
He dreamt of the app. A map of the world. Covered in dots. Millions of dots. Each one a person. Each one being watched. And him, in the centre. A bright, blinking dot. Alone.
He woke up gasping.
The room was pitch black.
He sat up. Disoriented.
The phone.
It was buzzing.
He fumbled for it. Knocked the glass of water off the table. It rolled across the floor. Spilled more water onto the already wet carpet.
He didn't care. Grabbed the phone.
Unknown Number.
He answered.
'Hello?'
Silence.
Then, a click. Then, silence again.
Then the recording ended.
No voice. No breathing. Just a call that connected and then said nothing.
He lowered the phone. Stared at it.
Was it her? Testing the line? Seeing if he would answer? Or just a wrong number?
It didn't matter. The result was the same. Silence.
He put the phone down.
Lay back on the sofa. Pulled the blanket over himself.
Tomorrow he would change the passwords. All of them. Email. Bank. Social media. Everything. He would buy a new phone. Maybe a new laptop. Cut the digital ties. Sever the connection.
But tonight, he just lay there. In the dark. Listening to the silence.
Waiting for a phone that wouldn't ring.
Wondering if she was watching him right now. Through the app. Through the camera. Through the screen.
Wondering if she cared. Or if he was just a dot on a map. A starting point. Left behind.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed it away.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was still silent.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up.
Looked at the three stains on the floor. The wine. The rain. The spilled water.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day.
