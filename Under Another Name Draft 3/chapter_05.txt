Chapter 5
The rain had not stopped. It had merely changed its mind about how hard it fell, shifting from a drumming assault to a fine, persistent mist that coated everything in a slick, grey film. Thomas stood on the pavement opposite the police station, his shoes already saturated, the water seeping through the leather and soaking into his socks with a cold, clinging intimacy. He had been standing there for twelve minutes. He knew this because he had watched the digital display of his watch flip through the seconds, each one a small, silent judgement on his inability to move forward or turn back. The numbers glowed green in the gloom, 12:04, 12:05, 12:06. A countdown to nothing. A measure of cowardice.
The building was a brutalist box of concrete and dirty glass, squatting on the corner like a forgotten bunker. The paint around the window frames was peeling in long, sunburnt strips, revealing the grey aggregate beneath. A sign above the heavy steel door read Metropolitan Police Service, but the 'S' in Service had fallen off, or perhaps had never been affixed properly, leaving it to read Metropolitan Police ervice. Thomas stared at the gap. It felt like a message. A missing letter in a word that promised order but delivered only fragmentation. Service. Service. The word echoed in his head, hollow and meaningless. What service was there for a man who had been picked clean by a ghost? What justice could be dispensed in a building that couldn't even keep its own sign intact?
He shifted his weight from one foot to the other. The squelch of his shoe against the wet tarmac was loud in the quiet street, a wet, sucking sound that seemed to anchor him to the spot. A bus rumbled past, its tyres hissing on the wet road, spraying a fine mist of dirty water onto the pavement. Thomas didn't flinch. He watched the droplets land on his coat, darkening the fabric, adding to the weight he was already carrying. The water soaked into the wool, heavy and cold, pressing against his shoulders like a physical hand. He thought about walking in. He had rehearsed the walk a hundred times in his head during the journey from the bank. Push open the heavy steel door. Step into the warmth. Smell the floor polish and stale coffee. Walk to the desk. Speak to the officer. Say the words: I have been robbed. By a woman I loved. She took five thousand pounds and left me a note.
But the rehearsal always stopped there. At the moment of speaking. Because he knew, with a certainty that sat like a stone in his gut, what would happen next. He knew the script. He had seen it in films, read it in papers, heard it in the fragmented stories of colleagues over pints in pubs that smelled of stale beer and regret. Domestic dispute. Civil matter. Insufficient evidence. Overseas jurisdiction. The phrases floated in his mind, disjointed and sharp, like shards of glass he couldn't stop swallowing. They were the walls of the maze, and he was the rat running into them again and again, fur matted, eyes blind, tail dragging.
He looked at the door again. The steel was scratched, marked by thousands of hands, thousands of desperate people who had come here hoping for justice, for intervention, for someone to make it stop. How many of them had walked out feeling lighter? How many had walked out with the same heavy silence he carried now? He imagined the inside. The fluorescent lights humming with that low, electric buzz that vibrated in your teeth, a sound that got inside your skull and stayed there. The plastic screens separating hope from indifference, smudged with fingerprints, opaque barriers between the broken and the bureaucratic. The young officers with tired eyes, typing reports that would vanish into a digital void, never to be read, never to be acted upon, filed away under 'N' for 'No Further Action'.
Five thousand pounds. The amount seemed absurd now. So much money. Enough for rent for months. Enough for a flight to Istanbul. Enough for a new start. Enough to buy back a piece of his dignity. And yet, to them, it would be nothing. A rounding error. A statistic in a spreadsheet that no one looked at. Resources are stretched, Mr Ward. He could hear the voice before he even heard the words. Polite. Professional. Dismissive. We can take a report, but without concrete evidence of coercion, without a recording, without a suspect within reach... there is very little we can do. Little. Such a small word for such a large loss.
He closed his eyes. The mist settled on his eyelashes, blurring the world into a soft, grey wash. The traffic noise faded into a dull roar, like the sea heard from a great distance. He thought about Olesya. Where was she right now? Sitting in a café in Sultanahmet, sipping tea from a tulip-shaped glass? Walking along the Bosphorus, the wind whipping her hair, the seagulls crying overhead? Watching him through the screen of her phone, seeing his location dot hovering outside the police station, smiling that tight, unreadable smile that used to make him feel safe and now made him feel exposed? You went to the police expecting them to save you, she had said. And they didn't. Did they? She hadn't known he was coming here. Not yet. But she would know. She always knew. The app. The tracking. The digital leash that he couldn't seem to cut, the invisible tether that bound him to her even as she flew further away.
If he went inside, he would give her exactly what she wanted. He would validate her narrative. See, Tom? You're just a victim. You need someone to save you. You can't do it yourself. She had called him a chain. A heavy, golden chain. Was going to the police just another link? Another way of binding himself to the system, to the rules, to the expectation that someone else would fix the mess she had made? Or was it an act of defiance? A refusal to let her win? He couldn't tell anymore. The lines had blurred, the moral compass spun wildly, pointing nowhere.
He opened his eyes. The door remained shut. The missing 'S' stared back at him. Metropolitan Police ervice. A broken promise. A fractured ideal. He took a step back. Then another. The decision wasn't a sudden epiphany; it was a slow, sinking realization, like watching a ship drift away from the dock, knowing you should jump but staying rooted to the spot, feet glued to the concrete. He couldn't go in. Not today. Maybe not ever. The shame of it burned in his chest, hot and acidic, rising in his throat like bile. Shame that he had been stolen from. Shame that he couldn't get the money back. Shame that he was standing in the rain, afraid to ask for help, afraid of being told what he already knew: that he was alone.
He turned away from the building. Started walking. He didn't have a destination. Just away. Away from the concrete box. Away from the missing letter. Away from the version of himself that believed in justice, in order, in the idea that bad things happened to bad people and good things to good ones. That version of himself felt like a stranger now, a character from a book he had read long ago and forgotten.
The streets were busy, despite the rain. Lunchtime rush. People hurrying under umbrellas, faces set in masks of determination, eyes fixed on the pavement ahead. They had places to be. Things to do. Lives that moved in straight lines from point A to point B, predictable and secure. Thomas walked among them, a ghost in his own city, invisible and intangible. He kept his head down, watching his feet. Squelch. Squelch. Squelch. The rhythm of wet shoes on wet pavement. A metronome counting down the seconds of a life that felt increasingly unmoored, drifting away from the shore of normality into deep, dark waters.
He passed a shop window. Caught his reflection. Pale. Haggard. Eyes sunken into dark sockets, rimmed with red. Hair plastered to his forehead by the mist, dripping water onto his eyebrows. He looked like a stranger. Like a man who had lost everything and was still waiting for the final blow, the one that would knock him down for good. He looked away. Couldn't bear to see it. Couldn't bear to acknowledge that this was him now. This was the new normal. The man who stood outside police stations and turned away. The man who let thieves win. The man who loved a ghost.
His phone buzzed in his pocket.
The sound was sharp, sudden, cutting through the white noise of the traffic and the rain, a electric jolt that sent a spike of adrenaline through his veins. Thomas froze. His heart hammered against his ribs, a frantic bird trapped in a cage, beating its wings against the bone. He didn't want to answer. He wanted to throw the phone into the gutter. Watch it sink into the dirty water, submerged in the filth of the city. Smash it against the brick wall of the nearest building, shatter the screen into a thousand pieces, destroy the device that connected him to her. But he couldn't. He had to know. He had to hear her voice. Even if it was to hurt him. Even if it was to remind him of his own weakness, his own pathetic need for closure that would never come.
He pulled the phone out. The screen was wet, droplets distorting the light, making the words swim before his eyes. Unknown Number. The words blinked at him, mocking, inevitable, a headline he couldn't escape. He stared at it for a long moment. The rain dripped from his nose onto the screen, merging with the existing moisture. He wiped it away with his thumb, the skin rough against the glass. Swiped to answer. Put the phone to his ear.
'Hello?'
His voice sounded rough. Unused. Like he hadn't spoken in days, like his vocal cords had rusted shut from disuse. The word hung in the air, fragile and weak.
Silence on the other end. Just the faint hiss of static. The sound of a connection waiting to be filled, a void waiting for a voice. The background noise of the city seemed to fade, leaving only the static and the pounding of his own heart.
Then, a click. Soft. Deliberate. A sound of intention.
'Tom.'
Her voice. Clear. Close. As if she was standing right behind him, whispering into his ear. No static this time. No distance. Just Olesya. Just the sound of the woman who had dismantled his life with a few keystrokes and a single transfer.
'Where are you?' he asked. He didn't mean to ask. The words just fell out, tumbling from his lips before he could stop them, driven by a desperation he couldn't control.
'Does it matter?'
Her voice was calm. Detached. As if they were discussing the weather, not the wreckage of their relationship.
'Yes.'
'Why?'
'Because I want to know.'
'You want to know so you can come. So you can try to fix it.'
'I can't fix it.'
'No. You can't.'
The words hung in the air between them, heavy and final, like a verdict delivered by a judge who had already made up his mind. Thomas gripped the phone tighter. His knuckles turned white, the blood draining from his fingers. The rain was getting heavier now, soaking through his coat, chilling his skin, seeping into his bones. He didn't care. Let it rain. Let it wash everything away. The money. The lies. The memories. The hope. Let it all dissolve into the gutters and flow out to sea.
'I saw you,' she said.
Thomas stopped walking. His feet planted on the wet pavement, unmoving. 'Saw me? Where?'
'Outside the station.'
He froze. Blood turning to ice in his veins, a cold shock that spread from his chest to his fingertips. 'What?'
'The app, Tom. The banking app. It tracks location. Remember? You set it up. "Find my device" for security. You wanted to know if I stole your phone. Now it tells me where you are. Irony is a funny thing, isn't it?'
'You're watching me.'
'In a way. I got the notification. "Location update: Near Metropolitan Police Station." Cute, isn't it? That you went there. That you thought they could help. That you believed in the system enough to stand in the rain and wait.'
'I didn't go in.'
'I know. You stood there for twelve minutes. Then you turned away.'
Thomas felt a surge of nausea, rolling in his stomach like a wave. Twelve minutes. She had been watching him for twelve minutes. Counting the seconds. Judging his hesitation. Analysing his body language through the lens of a digital map. 'How long have you been watching?'
'Long enough.'
'Why? Why do you care?'
'I told you. I'm checking in. Making sure you're okay.'
'I'm not okay.'
'You will be. Eventually.'
'When?'
'When you stop waiting for someone to save you.'
The words hit him like a physical blow, a punch to the gut that doubled him over slightly. He stumbled. Caught himself on a lamppost. Cold metal against his palm. Rough texture. Wet with rain. The sensation grounded him, a tiny anchor in a spinning world. 'You stole from me, Olesya. Five thousand pounds. You took everything. You took my money, my trust, my... my life.'
'I took what I needed. And I left you something.'
'What? A note? "For you"? Is that supposed to make it better? Is that supposed to be some kind of gift?'
'It's supposed to make you think. To make you wake up. You were sleepwalking, Tom. Living in that flat. Working that job. Pretending everything was fine. Going through the motions. Existing but not living. Now you're not pretending anymore. Now you're awake. Now you feel something.'
'I hate being awake.'
'That's because it hurts. But pain is real. Indifference is the lie. You were living a lie, Tom. A comfortable, safe, suffocating lie.'
'I wish I was indifferent.'
'You can't be. Not anymore. I made sure of that. I broke the spell.'
Thomas leaned against the lamppost. Head hanging. Rain running down his neck, under his collar, trickling down his spine. A cold, uncomfortable sensation that he welcomed. 'What do you want from me?'
'Nothing. I already took what I needed.'
'The money?'
'The money was just a symbol. A catalyst. What I really took was your attention. Your focus. Your energy. For a few days, you haven't been sleepwalking. You've been angry. Scared. Alive. You've been present. That's worth more than five thousand pounds, isn't it?'
'That's twisted.'
'Maybe. Or maybe it's the only way to wake people up. Maybe people need a shock to the system to realise they're alive.'
'I loved you.'
Silence. Long. Heavy. The sound of traffic in the background. Horns. Engines. Distant voices. Istanbul. Still Istanbul. The distance between them felt infinite, yet her voice was right there in his ear, intimate and invasive.
'I know,' she said finally. Her voice was softer now. Quieter. A hint of something that might have been sadness, or maybe just fatigue. 'And I loved you. But love wasn't enough. You taught me that.'
'I taught you?'
'Yes. With your silence. Your routines. Your fear of anything changing. Your need for control. You showed me that love without risk is just... habit. A routine. A comfortable arrangement. And I couldn't live like that anymore. I needed more. I needed to feel something real.'
'So you ran.'
'I flew.'
'To Istanbul.'
'To somewhere. Anywhere. It didn't matter where. Just away from the routine. Away from the silence.'
'And now you're watching me. Through an app.'
'Just checking in. Making sure you're not doing anything stupid.'
'Like going to the police?'
'Exactly like that. You didn't go in, did you?'
'No.'
'Good. Because they wouldn't have helped. They would have called it a domestic dispute. Asked for evidence. Told you to go home and sort it out. And you would have walked out feeling smaller than when you walked in. Defeated. Humiliated. I saved you from that, Tom.'
Thomas said nothing. She was right. He knew she was right. The realization burned worse than the anger, a hot coal in his chest. She had manipulated him, stolen from him, and yet she had also protected him from the humiliation of institutional indifference. It was a paradox he couldn't solve.
'See?' she continued. 'I know you better than you know yourself. I know what you're thinking before you think it. I know what you'll do before you do it. That's not stalking, Tom. That's intimacy. Twisted, maybe. Broken, certainly. But intimacy nonetheless. We are connected. More connected now than we ever were when I was sleeping in your bed.'
'We're not the same.'
'Aren't we? Both alone. Both trying to figure out what comes next. Both lost in our own ways.'
'I didn't choose this.'
'Didn't you? You chose to stay. To wait. To hope. That's a choice, Tom. A passive one, but a choice nonetheless. You chose to be the victim. You chose to let me define your reality.'
'What do you want me to do?'
'Live. Move on. Stop waiting for me to come back. Stop looking at the door. Stop checking the phone. Just... live.'
'Will you come back?'
Silence. Longer this time. He could hear her breathing. Soft. Rhythmic. Alive. The sound of a person on the other side of the world, breathing air he couldn't taste, seeing sights he couldn't see.
'I don't know,' she said finally. 'Maybe. Maybe not. It depends.'
'On what?'
'On whether I find what I'm looking for.'
'And if you don't?'
'Then I'll keep looking.'
'And me? Where do I fit in all this? Am I just a footnote? A chapter you've closed?'
'You're the starting point, Tom. The place I left. The launchpad. But you're not the destination. You never were.'
The words echoed in his mind. Starting point. Not destination. He was the launchpad. The thing she pushed off from to fly away. Nothing more. A solid surface to gain momentum, discarded once the ascent began. The cruelty of it was absolute.
'Olesya...' he started. His voice cracked.
'I have to go.'
'Wait. Please. Just tell me... are you safe?'
'I'm safe.'
'Are you alone?'
'That's not your business.'
'Please. Just tell me. I need to know.'
'Goodbye, Tom. Change your passwords. All of them. And delete the app. Cut the cord. It's the only way.'
The line went dead.
Thomas lowered the phone. Stared at the screen. Call Ended. The words stark and final.
He stood there on the pavement. Rain pouring down now. Soaking him to the bone. Chilling him to the core. Water dripping from his hair, his nose, his chin. A river of cold misery.
People brushed past him. Hurrying. Ignoring him. A woman pushing a pram, shielding the baby with a plastic cover. A man on a bike, pedalling fast, water spraying from his wheels. A group of teenagers laughing, huddled under a single umbrella, sharing a joke he couldn't hear.
He felt invisible. Erased. A ghost haunting his own life.
He put the phone in his pocket. Started walking again.
Towards home. Towards the flat. Towards the silence.
The walk back felt endless. Miles stretched into hours. Every step was a struggle. His legs felt heavy, filled with lead, dragging against the pavement. His lungs burned with the cold, wet air, each breath a labour. He didn't hurry. There was no point. The flat would be waiting. The silence would be waiting. The stains on the floor would be waiting. Time had lost its meaning, becoming a flat, grey expanse with no beginning and no end.
He passed the same shops he had passed on the way out. The bakery, smelling of yeast and sugar, a scent that used to bring comfort and now felt cloying and sweet. The newsagent, cluttered with magazines and newspapers, headlines screaming about disasters and scandals he couldn't care less about. The off-license, neon sign buzzing in the window, promising oblivion in a bottle. Nothing had changed. The world kept turning, indifferent to his pain. Life went on. People bought bread. Read newspapers. Bought wine. Normal things. Things he used to do. Things he couldn't imagine doing again. The normalcy of it was offensive, an insult to his suffering.
He reached his building. Stopped at the door. Looked up at the windows. His window. The third floor. The curtains were drawn. Dark. Empty. A black eye staring down at him.
He pushed the door open. Climbed the stairs. Three flights. Legs burning. Breath short. Each step creaked under his weight. A familiar complaint. A sound he had heard a thousand times. Now it sounded like a warning. Turn back. Turn back. Turn back. But there was nowhere to turn back to. The past was gone. The future was a blank wall.
He didn't turn back. Reached the landing. Found his door. Key in the lock. Turned. Click. The sound was loud in the quiet stairwell. Final. The sound of a cell door closing.
He stepped inside.
The flat was dark. Cold. Smelling of nothing. Just air. Stale. Stagnant. The air of a place where no one lived, where time had stopped.
He took off his coat. Dropped it on the floor. Didn't hang it up. Took off his shoes. Kicked them into the corner. Didn't line them up. The disorder felt appropriate. A reflection of the chaos inside.
Walked to the kitchen. Poured a glass of water. Drank it. The water was cold. Tasteless. It did nothing to quench the thirst that burned in his throat, a thirst that had nothing to do with water.
Looked at the phone on the counter.
It was dark. Sleeping. A black mirror reflecting nothing.
He picked it up. Turned it over.
No new messages. No missed calls.
Just the time. Ticking forward. Indifferent. 14:32. The same time as the transfer. A coincidence? Or another joke?
He put the phone down.
Walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa. The springs groaned under his weight, a sound of protest.
Looked at the floor.
The stains were still there. The wine stain. The rain stain from his coat last night. And now, another one. From today. From the walk. From the standing outside the station. From the rain that had soaked through his shoes and trousers, dripping onto the laminate as he stood there, paralyzed.
Three stains. Dark patches on the laminate. Records of spills. Of breaks. Of calls. Of walks in the rain. Of moments of weakness.
Marks that something had happened. Even if nothing had changed. Even if he was exactly where he had been yesterday, and the day before.
He leaned back. Closed his eyes.
Five thousand pounds.
For you.
You're the starting point. Not the destination.
Change your passwords.
The words echoed in his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted. A chorus of voices telling him what he already knew.
He pushed them away. Tried to clear his mind. Failed.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of passwords. Thousands of them. Floating in the air. Letters and numbers swirling like snowflakes, dancing in a chaotic ballet. He tried to catch them, but they dissolved in his hands, turning to water, slipping through his fingers. He chased them, running through corridors that never ended, grasping at nothing.
He dreamt of the police station. The missing 'S'. The door opening to reveal not an office, but a vast, empty space. Filled with mirrors. Thousands of mirrors. Floor to ceiling. Wall to wall. And in every mirror, a reflection of Olesya. Watching. Smiling. Pointing. Laughing silently. He turned and turned, but there was no escape. Everywhere he looked, she was there. Trapped in a hall of mirrors with a ghost.
He dreamt of the app. A map of the world. Covered in dots. Millions of dots. Each one a person. Each one being watched. Each one tracked. And him, in the centre. A bright, blinking dot. Alone. Pulsing. Waiting. The only dot that wasn't moving.
He woke up gasping.
The room was pitch black.
He sat up. Disoriented. Heart racing. Sweat cold on his skin.
The phone.
It was buzzing.
He fumbled for it. Knocked the glass of water off the table. It rolled across the floor. Spilled more water onto the already wet carpet. A fourth stain.
He didn't care. Grabbed the phone.
Unknown Number.
He answered.
'Hello?'
Silence.
Then, a click. Then, silence again.
Then the recording ended.
No voice. No breathing. Just a call that connected and then said nothing. A ghost call. A reminder.
He lowered the phone. Stared at it.
Was it her? Testing the line? Seeing if he would answer? Or just a wrong number? A glitch in the system?
It didn't matter. The result was the same. Silence. The silence of the void. The silence of abandonment.
He put the phone down.
Lay back on the sofa. Pulled the blanket over himself. It smelled of detergent. Nothing else. No scent of her. No scent of life.
Tomorrow he would change the passwords. All of them. Email. Bank. Social media. Everything. He would buy a new phone. Maybe a new laptop. Cut the digital ties. Sever the connection. Burn the bridges.
But tonight, he just lay there. In the dark. Listening to the silence.
Waiting for a phone that wouldn't ring.
Wondering if she was watching him right now. Through the app. Through the camera. Through the screen. Wondering if she was laughing. Or crying. Or feeling nothing at all.
Wondering if she cared. Or if he was just a dot on a map. A starting point. Left behind. Erased.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted. A refrain from a song he couldn't forget.
He pushed it away. Tried to think of nothing. Failed.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless. A mercy.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh. Cutting through the curtains, illuminating the dust motes dancing in the air.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was still silent.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up. The taste bitter.
Looked at the stains on the floor. The wine. The rain. The spilled water. The new one from the night. Four stains. A constellation of failure.
Left them.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him. The click final.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day. Into the noise. Into the world that didn't care.
Into the life that was no longer his own
