Chapter 05

Three days after Sumgait, Olesya did not come to work.

At eight fifteen her desk was still exact. The fern had been turned towards the window. The keyboard cover lay square over the keys. The courier book sat closed with a pencil placed along its spine, sharpened to a point fine enough to pierce paper. Only the chair gave her absence away. It had been pushed in by the cleaner, not by Olesya. The angle was wrong.

Thomas noticed it before he noticed the missing coffee on his coaster.

He stood inside his office with his coat still on, looking through the glass. Outside, Baku had gone flat under low cloud. Rain made a pale sheet across the windows and drew the harbour into a grey smear. The rigs beyond the water were reduced to outlines, uprights and cranes fading in and out as the weather moved.

Rauf came in with a folder under one arm.

'She is late,' Thomas said.

'Who?'

Thomas looked at him.

Rauf followed his gaze to the empty desk. 'Ah.'

'Did she call?'

'Not me.'

The answer gave him nothing useful. Rauf placed the folder on Thomas's desk and opened it to a delivery query from the supplier in Turkmenbashi. A pump skid had cleared customs, then uncleared itself in the way cargo sometimes did when a stamp was smudged, a date misread, or a clerk wanted to be reminded that his desk existed.

'We need the import manifest,' Rauf said.

'Olesya has the physical file.'

'Then we need Olesya.'

Thomas took off his coat and hung it behind the door. 'Where is the digital copy?'

Rauf smiled without much warmth. 'You are asking the wrong question.'

By nine, the customs broker had called twice. By ten, the supplier had sent four emails with the subject line changing shape each time. Thomas found the digital manifest on the shared drive, but the scan cut off the lower third of the page. The missing section contained the signature, the stamp, and the part everyone would pretend was most important since it was the part they could not see.

He went to Olesya's desk.

The top drawer opened with its usual small click. Receipts, courier slips, spare staples, two wrapped sweets, a folded bus ticket, the red notebook. Everything had been placed with a care that made touching it feel like trespass. He found the key to the lower cabinet in a narrow paper envelope marked stationery.

The physical file was in the second drawer, behind a stack of expired customs forms tied with cotton string. He knew it by the blue strip along the spine. He took it out and carried it back to his office.

The signature was present. The stamp was clean.

He scanned the page himself, attached it to the broker's reply, and waited for the cargo to become legal again.

At eleven thirty, Rauf appeared in the doorway.

'Still absent?'

'Yes.'

'You called?'

Thomas had not. He reached for his phone and saw, before he touched it, how absurdly visible the motion was. Rauf watched him with his head tilted slightly to one side.

'No,' Thomas said.

'Then maybe call.'

'I was going to.'

'Of course.'

Rauf left before the silence could settle anywhere useful.

Thomas found Olesya's number in the office directory rather than in his personal contacts. The distinction mattered for the length of time it took him to press call. It rang nine times. No answer. He put the phone face down on the desk and returned to the import file.

At one o'clock, she called.

He answered before the second ring.

'Olesya?'

There was noise behind her. A kettle boiling too hard. A child's cough, dry at the edges and deep in the middle. Then a chair scraped across a floor.

'I cannot come,' she said.

'Are you ill?'

'No.'

The kettle stopped. The silence after it was worse. The cough remained.

'Anya is sick. The school sent her home.'

Thomas looked through the glass at her empty desk. The pencil still lay against the courier book, too perfectly placed to belong to an emergency.

'Do you need a doctor?'

'It is fine.'

The cough came again, closer to the phone this time.

'That does not sound fine.'

'It is a cough.'

'How old is she?'

'Twelve.'

He had known she had a daughter. The file had held the fact safely until the sound came through the phone.

'Can someone stay with her tomorrow?' he asked.

'Tomorrow she goes back to school.'

'If she is still coughing like that?'

'She goes back.'

The flat behind her went quiet. Someone had heard herself being discussed and was holding still.

'We needed the manifest,' Thomas said.

'It is in the cabinet. Second drawer. Blue spine.'

'I found it.'

'Good.'

Her relief was too quick. It did not belong to the manifest. It belonged to the job.

'Take the day,' he said.

'I have taken the day.'

'I mean, do not worry about the hours.'

'I will make them up.'

'That is not necessary.'

'It is necessary.'

Another cough. Then Olesya moved the phone away and said something in Russian, low and brisk. The voice that answered was younger than Thomas expected, roughened by the throat but edged with embarrassment. He could not make out the words.

Olesya came back. 'I must go.'

'Call if you need anything.'

'We do not.'

The line ended.

Thomas kept the phone in his hand for several seconds. On his screen, her number sat without a name attached to it. He added the name. Then he deleted it. Then he added it again.

In the afternoon the customs release came through. The pump skid regained its official existence and was assigned to a truck that would arrive late, dented, and accompanied by three men who would insist nothing had been dented. Thomas signed the release, updated the shipment tracker, and sent Rauf the revised schedule.

At four forty, Sarah called from London.

Thomas answered from the corridor outside the meeting room, where the glass reflected him in separate panels, each one narrower than the last. Behind her voice, he heard crockery and the bright confusion of a cafe. She had signed the Lisbon contract. Portugal for ten days, then probably Berlin. Her life arrived to him in public spaces, each one already leading somewhere else.

'Are you eating properly?' she asked.

'Yes.'

'That means no.'

Thomas looked through the glass wall. Olesya's desk had become shadowed by the late afternoon. The fern still faced the rain.

'One of the administrators has a sick child,' he said.

'She will manage,' Sarah said. 'People do.'

Someone near her called her name.

'I should go. Do not spend the night in the office.'

'I will not.'

'That tone is not legally binding.'

The line closed into the corridor hum.

Thomas returned to his office and finished the last two emails. At five twenty, he opened the personnel folder and found Olesya's address. It was written in her narrow hand on the emergency contact form, the letters careful, the block number corrected once in darker ink. Her emergency contact was Anya. The telephone number beside it was the same number he had heard coughing behind.

He copied the address onto a yellow note. Then he looked at it until the act became impossible to explain as work.

At five forty, he placed the import manifest in a folder, added two blank signature pages, and put the folder into his briefcase.

Rauf was still in his office.

'You are leaving early,' he said as Thomas passed.

'I have to confirm a signature.'

'At this hour?'

'It is on the way.'

Rauf looked at the briefcase, then at Olesya's empty desk. He picked up his tea and blew across the surface.

'Of course,' he said.

The taxi took twenty minutes to arrive and another thirty to reach the district. Traffic moved in the slow, uneven pulses of rain. Cars pressed into gaps that had not been offered. Buses breathed steam at the kerb. The city changed as they drove east: the office towers fell behind, the pavement broke, the shopfronts lowered their eyes.

Thomas sat in the back with the briefcase against his knees and the address open on his phone. The driver glanced at it twice.

'Old block,' he said.

'Yes.'

'Lift maybe no work.'

'Fine.'

The driver laughed softly. 'Fourth floor always fine for people not living fourth floor.'

They stopped beside a row of Soviet-era blocks, long and tired, their concrete darkened by weather and exhaust. Satellite dishes hung from balconies like metal leaves. Wires crossed the courtyard in low loops. The ground was mud, broken tile, puddles, and the pale ends of cigarette filters. A boy kicked a flat football against a wall painted with numbers for a gas service no longer operating.

Thomas paid the driver and stood under the rain for a moment longer than needed.

The entrance door had no handle. Someone had tied a strip of cloth through the hole where the handle had been, and the cloth was wet enough to darken his fingers when he pulled it. Inside, the stairwell smelled of damp plaster, cabbage, gas, and the mineral dust of old concrete. The lift doors were closed. A handwritten note had been taped across them.

Do not use.

The words had been written in Russian and Azeri. Someone had added a third warning in pen, harder pressed, underlined twice.

He climbed.

On the second floor, a woman opened a door and looked at him through the chain. On the third, a television played behind a wall, the sound thin and metallic. By the fourth, the briefcase felt foolish in his hand. He checked the number on the door and knocked.

Nothing.

He knocked again, softer.

Footsteps came close, stopped, then retreated. A lock turned. The door opened as far as the chain allowed.

A girl looked out at him.

She had Olesya's pale eyes and a face still unarranged by its own lengthening. Her hair was tied back badly, with strands caught under the elastic. She wore a school shirt under a cardigan too large in the shoulders. One sock was grey, the other white. Her cheeks were flushed, but the skin under her eyes had a bluish shadow.

'Hello,' Thomas said.

She did not answer.

'I am Thomas Ward. From your mother's office.'

The girl's eyes moved to the briefcase, then back to his face.

'Mama,' she called, and coughed halfway through the word.

The chain closed. More locks moved.

Olesya opened the door.

She wore a dark dress with a cardigan over it and a towel tucked through the handle of the oven behind her. Her hair had been pinned up, but pieces had escaped near her temples. Steam moved in the small kitchen at her back. The smell of boiled potato and lemon reached the hall.

'Thomas.'

The word was not a greeting.

'I brought the manifest,' he said.

She looked at the briefcase.

'To my home?'

'There is a signature discrepancy on the customs release.'

Anya stood behind her, one hand at her throat, watching both adults with the quick attention of a child who knew money and work could enter a room before they were named.

Olesya stepped back.

'Come in, then.'

The flat was small and warmer than the stairwell. Heat came from a portable radiator near the table. Shoes were arranged along the wall by size. Two women's coats hung on a hook, one Olesya's, one too small for her and too large for a child. The wallpaper had lifted near the window and been pressed back with clear tape.

No object lay where it had fallen. The poverty of the room had been organised until it could pass, at a glance, for a choice.

Olesya took the folder from him and placed it on the kitchen table. A pot simmered on the cooker. Beside it, a bowl of sliced lemon stood near the kettle. Anya sat on a low stool and folded her hands between her knees.

'This is Anya,' Olesya said.

'Hello,' Thomas said.

Anya nodded. 'Hello.'

Her English was careful. School English. Each sound placed in the mouth before being released.

'Your mother says you are unwell.'

Olesya looked at him.

Anya looked at the floor. 'It is just coughing.'

'Everyone says that.'

The corner of her mouth moved. The cough came before the smile could settle. It bent her forward, one hand pressed flat to her chest. Olesya crossed the room in two steps and put a cup into her hand.

'Drink.'

'It is hot.'

'Then hold it.'

Anya held the cup.

Thomas placed his briefcase against the wall and took the chair Olesya indicated. The table was covered with a plastic cloth patterned with faded cherries. The manifest folder sat between them like an official guest.

'Where is the discrepancy?' Olesya asked.

He opened the folder to keep the lie in place.

'The broker says the signature on the scan is not clear.'

She took the page, read it, and looked at him over the top edge.

'You already sent this today.'

'Yes.'

'Then there is no discrepancy.'

'No.'

The radiator clicked. Outside, a car passed through water in the courtyard.

Olesya lowered the paper.

'Why are you here?'

The question did not accuse him. It made the room smaller.

'I wanted to see if you needed anything.'

'I said no.'

'I heard her cough.'

Olesya's hand tightened once on the paper. 'Children cough.'

'Has she seen a doctor?'

'The school nurse looked.'

'A doctor.'

'There is a clinic.'

'Have you gone?'

She placed the manifest back in the folder and aligned its edge with the tablecloth pattern. Cherry to cherry. Corner to corner.

'Not today.'

'Why not today?'

Anya lifted her eyes to her mother. Olesya did not look at her.

'The private doctor is not there.'

'And the state clinic?'

'The state clinic is there every day.'

The answer carried no contempt. It carried experience.

Anya sipped the hot lemon water and made a face. Then she saw Thomas looking and straightened.

'I am not very sick,' she said.

'You sound sick.'

'It sounds worse than it is.'

'That is what your mother says about invoices.'

Anya looked at Olesya. Olesya looked away first.

For a moment, the room held something almost light. The pot lid trembled on the cooker. Rain tapped the window.

Thomas saw the schoolbooks stacked on the sill. There was no shelf. Mathematics, Russian grammar, English exercises, a science textbook mended in brown tape. A sheet of paper had been pinned above them with two drawing pins. Timetable. Homework. Chores. Medicine.

Medicine had been written in Olesya's hand, then crossed out, then written again.

'What medicine is she taking?' he asked.

Olesya moved to the cooker and lifted the lid. Steam rose, hiding her face for a second.

'Tea. Lemon.'

'That is not medicine.'

'No. It is tea.'

Anya looked into the cup.

Thomas leaned back. The chair legs were uneven. One had been repaired with tape wrapped tight around the lower part, building height where wood had worn away.

'I can pay for the clinic.'

'No.'

Olesya said it before the offer had fully entered the room.

'It is a doctor, not a favour.'

'It is both.'

'It can be work related.'

'My child is not work related.'

The words struck cleanly. Anya's eyes moved between them, quick and alert to the shape of adult things rather than their volume.

Thomas looked down at the manifest folder. The blue customs stamp sat visible at the top of the page. Permission attached to ink. Release granted by one square of colour in the correct place.

'No,' he said. 'She is not.'

Olesya turned off the cooker. The room became quieter at once.

'Then do not make her into work.'

He should have left. The right line had appeared in the room, and both of them could see it. A chair, a table, a child's cup, the distance from the office to here. It could still have remained intact if he stood, apologised, and took the folder back into the rain.

Instead, Anya coughed again.

It lasted too long. The cup shook in her hand and struck her tooth with a small sound. Olesya crossed to her and took the cup before it spilled. Anya tried to breathe through her nose. The effort made her eyes water.

Thomas stood.

'Where is the clinic?'

Olesya did not answer.

'Where?'

'Around the corner,' Anya said.

Her mother looked at her.

'It has a green sign,' Anya added, softer.

Olesya put the cup on the table. 'You should not have said.'

'He asked.'

'People ask many things.'

Anya lowered her head.

Thomas picked up his coat. 'I will wait downstairs.'

'Thomas.'

'Bring her coat.'

'No.'

'Then I will go to the clinic and bring the doctor here.'

Olesya stared at him. The colour had left the skin around her mouth.

'You do not get to order my home.'

He heard it then. Not refusal. Not pride. A boundary made from the last material she had.

He put the coat down.

'You are right.'

The radiator clicked again. Somewhere in the building, pipes knocked in the wall.

'I can give you the money,' he said. 'You decide.'

'And after?'

'After what?'

'After you give money, what is it called?'

He had no answer that would fit the room.

Anya coughed into her sleeve. Olesya closed her eyes for one beat, then opened them and took her bag from the hook.

'We will go,' she said. 'You do not come in.'

'Fine.'

'You wait outside.'

'Fine.'

'You do not speak.'

'Fine.'

Anya stood carefully, one hand pressed to her chest. Olesya wrapped the smaller coat around her, fastened each button, then took the grey hat from the hook and pulled it over her daughter's hair. It was too big for Anya. Olesya folded the edge twice.

Thomas looked at the table while she did it.

The manifest folder lay open beside the sliced lemon. The page did not belong there. Its stamp, signature, and printed clauses were absurd amongst the cup, the schoolbooks, the pot of potatoes. He had brought the office into the flat with no other way to enter.

The clinic was around the corner, as Anya had said. Its green sign flickered above a glass door filmed with condensation. A poster of lungs curled at the edges on the wall.

Olesya went in with Anya.

Thomas stayed outside under the narrow awning. Rain dripped from one broken corner onto the pavement in a steady line. People passed with bags pressed to their sides. Across the street, a bakery sold flatbread from a window fogged by heat. The smell reached him each time the door opened.

He checked his phone.

Sarah had sent a photograph from the Lisbon contract dinner: a long table, white plates, a glass of wine catching light, her face half turned towards someone out of frame. Beneath it she had written:

Proof of life.

Thomas looked through the clinic glass. Olesya sat with Anya beneath the lung poster. Anya had her head against her mother's shoulder. Olesya's thumb moved once over the seam of the coat.

He put the phone away.

Twenty minutes later, Olesya came out alone.

'Bronchitis,' she said.

'Antibiotics?'

'Yes.'

'Anything else?'

'Inhaler. Cough syrup. Rest.'

'Good.'

She held a folded prescription in one hand and the receipt in the other. The receipt was already damp along the edge.

'How much?'

'No.'

'Olesya.'

'No.'

The clinic door opened behind her. Anya came out wearing a paper mask. It made her eyes look larger. She held a small white bag from the pharmacy, but the shape inside was too thin for everything the doctor had named.

Thomas saw the omission before Olesya could hide it.

'Where is the inhaler?'

Olesya's hand closed around the receipt.

Anya looked at the street.

'They did not have that brand,' Olesya said.

'Which pharmacy?'

'It does not matter.'

'It matters if she needs it.'

'Thomas.'

He took the prescription gently from her hand. She let him with Anya watching. The inhaler was circled in blue by the doctor. Beside it, a price had been written in pencil.

Not large. Not small. A sum with teeth.

There was a cash machine at the corner by the bakery. Thomas withdrew the maximum it would allow in one transaction. The machine counted notes behind its metal face, slow and loud. He folded them once and placed them inside the prescription.

Olesya did not take it.

'For Anya,' he said.

'That does not make it simple.'

'No.'

'You think money makes things simple.'

'No.'

She looked at the folded prescription between them.

'You do.'

The rain thickened. Anya coughed behind the mask, smaller now, restrained by the effort not to draw attention from people passing on the pavement.

Olesya took the prescription.

She did not thank him.

They bought the inhaler from the second pharmacy. Thomas remained outside again. He watched through the glass as Olesya counted some of his money with her own notes folded around it, making the payment appear mixed, less clean, less entirely his. The pharmacist placed the box in a bag. Olesya checked the label before putting it into her handbag.

On the way back to the flat, Anya walked between them. She carried the medicine bag in both hands. At the block entrance she stopped and looked up at Thomas.

'Thank you,' she said.

Olesya's face closed.

'You do not need to say that,' she told her in Russian.

Anya answered in the same language. The sentence was too fast for Thomas, but the child's chin lifted at the end.

Olesya's eyes sharpened.

Anya lowered her chin.

'What did she say?' Thomas asked.

'Nothing.'

Anya looked at him through the paper mask. 'I said he bought medicine. People say thank you for medicine.'

Olesya opened the stairwell door.

'Inside.'

The climb was slower on the way up. Anya stopped on the second landing, breath catching. Olesya pretended to adjust the bag so the pause could belong to her. Thomas stood one step below them and looked at the paint peeling from the wall.

Back in the flat, Olesya made Anya sit by the radiator. She read the inhaler instructions twice, once in Russian, once in English, comparing the paper to the box. Thomas stood near the door with his coat still on. He could have left now. The clinic had been paid, the medicine bought, the immediate crisis reduced to instructions printed on paper.

But the flat had altered.

The heat seemed thinner. The tablecloth showed a tear near one corner. A bucket stood behind the curtain under the taped window, catching water from a leak he had not noticed before. Each drop landed with a soft, delayed tick. On the shelf above the cooker, two jars held rice and buckwheat. The rice was nearly gone.

Anya used the inhaler badly the first time. Olesya corrected her, too sharply. Thomas looked away until the second breath went in properly.

'Again tonight,' he said. 'And in the morning.'

'I can read,' Olesya said.

'I know.'

Anya leaned back against the chair and closed her eyes. The paper mask hung loose from one ear. The flush had begun to fade from her cheeks, leaving the skin pale and damp at the hairline.

Olesya folded the clinic receipt and placed it inside a small tin on the shelf. The tin already held documents: rent slips, school notices, payment receipts, folded forms with stamps in blue, purple, and black. She made room for the new paper by pressing everything flat with the backs of her fingers.

Thomas watched the gesture.

'Rent,' he said.

Olesya paused.

'What about it?'

'How much is it?'

'Paid.'

'For this month.'

'Yes.'

'And next?'

She closed the tin.

'Next month is next month.'

He heard Sarah's voice in that. Lisbon, Berlin, ten days, next month as a place one could enter with tickets and contracts already clearing a path. Here, next month waited behind a tin lid with the rent slips flattened beneath a child's prescription.

'Show me,' he said.

'No.'

'Olesya.'

'You are in my home.'

'Yes.'

'Do not inspect it.'

He stepped back from the shelf.

'I am sorry.'

The apology did not repair anything. It only sat between them, another object without a proper place.

Anya opened her eyes. 'Mama, he can sit.'

'He is leaving.'

'He brought papers.'

Olesya looked at the manifest folder still on the table. For the first time since he arrived, the absurdity of it touched her mouth.

'Yes,' she said. 'Very important papers.'

Anya looked at Thomas. 'Are you her boss?'

'No.'

'Rauf is boss?'

'Rauf thinks so.'

Anya considered this. 'Mama says Rauf talks too much.'

'Anya.'

'He does.'

Thomas took the chair by the door. Refusing would have made the girl's offer visible. Olesya served food once a person sat in her kitchen and the room demanded it. Boiled potatoes, a little butter, salt, black bread cut thin. She gave Anya the larger portion and placed a smaller plate in front of Thomas without asking.

'You do not have to feed me,' he said.

'Then do not eat.'

He ate.

The potatoes were plain and hot. The bread was dense, slightly sour. Anya ate slowly, measuring each bite against the cough. Olesya did not sit until both plates had been served.

'Do you like Baku?' Anya asked.

'Some days.'

'Mama says the wind has teeth.'

Thomas looked at Olesya. 'It does.'

Anya tore a small piece of bread and dipped it into the butter on her plate.

'Where are you from?'

'England.'

'London?'

'Sometimes.'

'How can you be from sometimes?'

Olesya's knife stopped against the plate.

'Work moves me,' Thomas said.

'Mama says men from rigs live in airports.'

'Your mother says many accurate things.'

Olesya lowered her eyes to her plate.

The phone on the shelf vibrated.

Olesya rose and checked it. Her face changed by not changing.

'School?' Thomas asked.

'Landlord.'

She put the phone face down.

It vibrated again.

Anya stared at her food.

Olesya did not touch the phone.

'Answer,' Thomas said quietly.

'No.'

'If it is about rent, answer.'

'It is not your call.'

'No.'

The phone stopped, then began again.

Anya pushed a potato into smaller pieces with the edge of her fork.

Olesya picked up the phone and went into the narrow hall. Her voice became low enough that the words blurred. Thomas heard the shape of Russian numbers. He heard tomorrow. He heard please, once. Then no again, harder.

The call ended.

She returned to the kitchen and took her seat.

'Eat,' she told Anya.

Anya obeyed.

Thomas looked at the tin on the shelf. Olesya saw him do it.

'Do not,' she said.

'How much?'

'Thomas.'

'How much?'

She pressed her hand flat on the tablecloth. The cherries beneath her palm distorted where the plastic had stretched.

'Two months,' she said. 'The school shoes and the doctor came together.'

'How much?'

She named the sum.

It was larger than the electricity bill. Still smaller than a single error in a procurement forecast. In this room, it sat at the table and took food from the plates.

Thomas opened his wallet.

Olesya stood so fast the chair struck the wall.

'No.'

Anya flinched.

Thomas stopped with his hand inside his jacket.

Olesya saw Anya's face and lowered her voice.

'No,' she said again.

'Let me solve this.'

'This is not a valve.'

'I know.'

'No, you do not.'

She took the plates to the sink though none of them had finished eating. Water ran hard over the metal, drowning the room. Her shoulders were straight. The tap splashed onto her sleeves. She did not move them back.

Thomas closed his wallet.

Anya looked at him. Her eyes above the mask were too old for the cardigan, the mismatched socks, the careful English.

'The landlord shouts in the stair,' she said.

'Anya,' Olesya said from the sink.

'Everyone hears.'

Olesya turned off the tap.

No one spoke.

Thomas took out his notebook instead of his wallet. He wrote the amount on a blank page, then the date, then rent, clinic, medicine. The words made a list. The list made the room manageable. That was the problem.

'I will not give it to you here,' he said.

Olesya watched him.

'Tomorrow, at the office, I will advance you two months' salary.'

'There is no form.'

'I will make one.'

'Rauf will know.'

'Rauf knows many things.'

'And accounting?'

'Accounting will see a salary advance.'

'It is not salary.'

'It will be.'

The room held the lie up to the light. It was less crude than the manifest, better shaped, almost usable. Olesya looked at the notebook, then at the tin on the shelf, then at Anya's medicine bag.

'And after?' she asked.

'After, you work.'

'I work now.'

'Then you keep working.'

'And I owe you.'

'You owe the company.'

'No.' Her voice was soft. 'I will owe you.'

He did not deny it.

'I can manage the paperwork,' he said.

'That is what you like.'

'Yes.'

Anya lifted the inhaler box from the table and read the English label aloud, stumbling over corticosteroid. Thomas corrected the pronunciation once. She repeated it, better the second time. Olesya watched them from the sink with her hands wet and empty.

Outside, someone shouted in the stairwell. A door closed. The building settled around them in small sounds: pipes, rain, footsteps, the lift refusing service behind its taped warning.

Thomas left at eight.

Olesya walked him to the door. Anya had fallen asleep on the narrow sofa with a blanket pulled to her chin, the medicine bag on the floor beneath her hand. The paper mask lay on the table beside the manifest folder. He had forgotten the folder. Olesya picked it up and gave it to him.

'Your important papers.'

'Thank you.'

She opened the door but did not step into the hall.

'Do not come here again without asking.'

'I will not.'

'And tomorrow you do not say hardship.'

'What should I say?'

'Advance.'

'No soft words.'

'No soft words.'

She looked back into the flat. Anya shifted in her sleep and coughed once, weaker now.

'You like simple,' she said.

'I like things that can be fixed.'

'Same thing.'

He had no answer.

She held the door with one hand. Her cardigan sleeve had slipped down, exposing the pale ridge of her wrist.

'Tomorrow I come to work,' she said.

'If she is still ill, stay home.'

'Seven thirty.'

'Eight is fine.'

'Seven thirty.'

The line was not about time. It was about the desk, the pencil, the fern, the job still waiting in the office where need could be renamed as procedure.

'Seven thirty,' he said.

She nodded once and closed the door.

The stairs smelled colder on the way down. On the second floor, the woman with the chain opened her door again, looked at him, and closed it without a word. Outside, the rain had thinned to mist. The courtyard lights shone on puddles broken by floating scraps of paper. The taxi he had called was not yet there.

Thomas stood under the entrance awning and opened his banking app. The numbers arranged themselves with obedient clarity. Rent, medicine, salary advance, repayment schedule, discretionary allowance. He made a note in his phone and titled it O admin.

The taxi arrived with its headlamps low in the mist.

Before he got in, he looked up at the fourth floor. One window glowed behind a thin curtain. A shadow crossed it once, carrying something small from one room to another.

In the taxi, his briefcase rested on his lap. Inside it, the manifest had picked up a faint smell of boiled potato and lemon.

He believed, as they drove back towards the office towers and the harbour lights, that he had prevented the next failure.

Upstairs, behind the taped window, Olesya opened the salary advance form he had left folded inside the manifest folder and read it twice. Then she took the money he had placed between the blank signature pages, smoothed each note against the table, and sorted it into three piles.

Rent.
