The black box began as a second account.

It had no card. No joint access. No paper statements. No branch he would ever visit. It existed inside the bank app as eight digits, a balance, and a name he changed three times before leaving it blank enough to live with.

Reserve.

Then:

Personal reserve.

Then:

Secondary.

The last word had no weather in it. No kitchen. No child. No woman on a balcony trying to photograph a receipt in light thin enough for a bank to accept. It sounded administrative, which made it useful.

Thomas set the payroll allocation on a Thursday morning, sitting in the operations office with a mask under his chin and dust in the corners of the keyboard. The form had passed from Nisha to payroll, from payroll to regional, from regional to a portal that rejected his first signature. The scanned image was too pale. He signed again with a thicker pen and pressed hard enough for the point to mark the page beneath.

By noon, the change appeared in the system.

Joint account: unchanged.

Personal account: reduced.

Secondary: active.

The line looked clean.

That was the first seduction.

Clean lines made dirty things look handled.

The compound remained under lockdown. By then, the first shock had gone stale. People had stopped treating the rules as temporary and begun arranging complaints around them. Meal boxes. Temperature checks. Restricted movement. Work calls with frozen faces and broken audio. Laundry slots negotiated like mineral rights. A rumour every morning and an official correction every afternoon.

Sarah worked in the side office when she could get it, in the room when she could not, and on the floor when the desk became too narrow for both their laptops. She had stopped packing and unpacking. The suitcase stayed under the bed, closed but not forgotten. Once a day she checked flight updates and then closed the page before the spinning wheel finished embarrassing itself.

Thomas watched her adapt.

That was one of the cruelties. She was good at it.

She learned which dining hall boxes travelled badly, which shower hour gave warm water, which corridor had signal after midnight, which security guard would allow an extra five minutes outside if no one else was around. Her world had shrunk, and she had mapped it.

His had shrunk too, though no map would admit it.

The less he knew, the faster he could move.

He began to prefer the messages that contained only sums.

That preference did not announce itself as cruelty. It arrived as lowered pulse, fewer open tabs, shorter delays between demand and payment. A message with a photograph took him into a room. A message with a number kept him at the door. The number could be handled. The room asked to be entered.

At first, he had wanted every receipt.

Order, he told himself. Documentation. Proof. A proper audit trail. He had asked for rent slips, pharmacy stamps, school notices, bus-card top-ups, doctor's notes, photographs taken in morning light with the total visible. Olesya had learned the discipline too well. The tablecloth, the ruler, Anya's hand at the corner, the careful framing of a life reduced to paper. Each image had arrived carrying more than the amount. It had brought chipped cups, balcony light, bad flooring, school shoes, the edge of a child's exercise book, a mother's thumb in the frame.
