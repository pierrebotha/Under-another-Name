Chapter 16

The end of Iraq came without farewell.

It came as a schedule change, then a rumour, then a line on the company tracker that moved Thomas and Sarah from locked-in to redeploying without asking either of them whether the word was accurate. Regional hub transfer. Delhi. Temporary corporate housing. Essential personnel and dependants. Travel corridor approved.

Dependants.

Thomas saw the word on the list before Sarah did.

She stood beside him in the operations office with a paper cup of tea and dust along the edge of her sleeve. The lockdown had held them in Iraq long enough for departure to become theoretical. Now it had returned wearing paperwork and no apology.

'Dependants,' she said.

'Standard category.'

'For wives, children, and women with laptops who overstayed a consulting trip by global event.'

'That may be too specific for the drop-down menu.'

'Their loss.'

She took the printed sheet from his hand and read the row again. Her name. His name. Passport status. Medical clearance. Destination: Delhi. Flight: charter. Date: provisional.

'Provisional means real until it isn't,' she said.

'Yes.'

'I dislike how easily I now speak logistics.'

'You are fluent.'

'Do not make this worse.'

Her voice was light, but her eyes stayed on the word destination.

Delhi.

Thomas had worked through Delhi before, never lived there. Airports, hotels, one office tower with over-watered plants, a driver who had known six routes to avoid six different kinds of delay. The city in his memory was heat, horns, wet dust, cardamom tea in paper cups, a hotel corridor smelling of polish and old air-conditioning. It had been transit then. A place crossed through.

Now it was the next container.

The compound spent three days disgorging people in batches.

No one called it leaving. They called it movement. They called it rotation adjustment. They called it hub consolidation. The words kept everything technical and therefore bearable. Bags appeared in corridors. Printer queues formed outside admin. Medical stamped forms until the ink pad dried. Security checked names against lists that changed every hour. Men who had wanted nothing more than to leave now distrusted the flight that allowed it.

Sarah packed with a precision that put the room under accusation.

Work clothes. Travel clothes. Documents. Chargers. Medication. Gifts still stranded from Scotland. The dead basil plant at home had become a joke in messages from her mother and then stopped being a joke. Sarah folded the printed lockdown form and slid it into the plastic sleeve.

'Do we need the room inventory?'

'Probably not.'

'That means yes.'

'Put it with passports.'

'Listen to us.'

She tucked it away.

'We have become the sort of people who travel with room inventories.'

Thomas closed his laptop.

'It is a stage of marriage no one warns you about.'

'The vows should mention laminated documents.'

She lifted the suitcase off the bed and set it upright. The zip held. Barely.

His phone lay face up on the desk.

Olesya had not replied to the 800.

The blue ticks remained. No message beneath them. The silence had become a surface he could touch without leaving a mark. He opened the thread less often now, or told himself he did. He knew the last line by position, by the exact amount of white space below it.

800 sent.

Read.

Nothing.

Sarah saw him look.

'Still no answer?'

'No.'

'And the next payment?'

'Due next week.'

'Can you send early?'

He looked at her.

'Why?'

'We are changing countries. Banks enjoy becoming stupid at borders.'

That was why she was good in crisis. She noticed the door before it closed.

'I can try.'

'Try before the airport.'

'It is monthly.'

'The month does not care where you are standing.'

He opened the banking app.

Secondary balance: enough.

Recipient: Olesya Petrova.

Amount: monthly plus buffer.

Reference: June.

Purpose: Other.

The compound connection flickered. The app paused, recovered, asked him to confirm.

He pressed confirm.

The circle turned.

Then the screen went white.

Session expired.

'Signal?' Sarah asked.

'Probably.'

'Try again.'

He did.

This time the app accepted the login, loaded the transfer page, then returned him to accounts without warning.

No payment.

He checked activity.

Nothing pending.

The air conditioner rattled above them with the loose screw he had never reported.

'Later,' he said.

Sarah looked at him.

'Before the airport.'

'Yes.'

He tried three more times before the convoy.

Each attempt failed differently.

Session expired.

Connection lost.

Unable to process.

Please try later.

At the fourth attempt, the app asked him to contact the bank.

That was new.

The convoy to the airport left at 05:40.

The compound was colourless at that hour. Floodlights, dust, men in masks, luggage lined beside armoured vehicles, a security supervisor reading names from a clipboard in the tone of a man who had lost all faith in names. Sarah stood beside Thomas with her backpack on both shoulders and her passport in the front pocket of her jeans.

'Did it clear?'

'No.'

'What does no mean?'

'It asked me to contact the bank.'

'That seems like a bad no.'

'Yes.'

'Thomas.'

'I know.'

He did not know. Not yet. He knew only the shape of the gate, not the lock inside it.

The road out of the compound ran past the perimeter wall, the empty clinic, the generator yard, the gravel space where men still smoked out of habit though the convoy was waiting. Beyond the gate, Iraq opened in hard morning light. Flat ground. Low buildings. Concrete barriers. Roadside dust lifting behind the lead vehicle and folding back over the rest of them.

Sarah sat beside him in the second vehicle.

She put her hand on his knee once when they passed the place where the mortar smoke had been visible weeks earlier. No speech. Just the pressure of her fingers through the fabric. Then she took her hand away and checked her passport again.

His phone had no service.

At the airport, service returned in fragments.

He opened the banking app while Sarah queued for document check.

Account under review.

The words sat at the top of the screen in a red banner.

He tapped details.

International transfer activity requires additional verification.

Please contact us.

He opened chat.

The queue in the terminal moved forward by inches. Around him, contractors held passports, boarding letters, medical forms, phone chargers, duty-free bags, all the small documents and objects required for a person to be allowed through a door. A child from another company's group slept across two seats with her head on a rucksack. The public address system announced a flight in Arabic, then English, then a burst of static.

Bank chat connected.

Automated assistant:

How can we help today?

Thomas typed:

Transfer blocked.

The assistant offered three buttons.

Card issue.

Fraud alert.

International payment.

He selected International payment.

The assistant asked him to describe the recipient relationship.

He stared at the question.

Sarah turned from the queue.

'Any progress?'

'Bank chat.'

'Do you need anything?'

'No.'

She looked at his screen from where she stood. Too far to read. Close enough to know he had angled it away.

The assistant repeated:

Please describe your relationship to the recipient.

The terminal moved around him. Wheels on tile. Shoes. Announcements. Security staff. A man laughing too loudly into a phone. A trolley with a squeaking wheel moving in circles around a pillar.

Thomas typed:

Friend.

The assistant replied:

Please provide supporting documentation establishing the relationship and purpose of ongoing transfers.

He deleted the chat.

Then reopened it.

The same red banner remained.

Sarah reached the document desk. The officer checked her passport, then his, then the company letter, then the list. Stamps. Screens. A second officer called someone over. The two men compared names. Sarah kept her face still. Thomas kept the phone low in his palm.

Olesya messaged at 06:32 Baku time.

Monthly?

He did not answer until they were through security.

Trying.

Her reply:

Need today.

He typed:

Bank review.

The dots appeared immediately.

Then:

What means?

Blocked until documents.

What documents?

He stood beside the departure gate with the phone in his hand and a boarding pass between two fingers. The gate windows showed the charter aircraft sitting in a haze of heat and exhaust. Men in high-vis vests moved around it with cones and hand signals. Sarah bought two bottles of water from a kiosk and placed one against his wrist.

'Drink.'

He did.

Olesya sent:

Send document then.

He closed his eyes for one second.

There is no document.

The message delivered.

One tick.

Two.

No reply.

At the gate, he tried the chat again.

The bank's human agent this time was called Priya, or said so in the small circle beside the message. Her replies came too neatly to feel human, though not neatly enough to be a machine. She asked for the recipient relationship again. Then proof of purpose. Then source of funds, even though the funds had come from salary paid into the same bank for years. Every answer opened a smaller door.

Recipient relationship:

Personal.

Please be more specific.

Long-term personal support.

Please upload supporting documentation.

There is no contract.

Please upload a written declaration.

Thomas opened a notes app and tried to make the sentence.

I provide regular support to Olesya Petrova for living costs.

He read it.

It was true in the way a skeleton was true.

He added:

This includes rent, food, medicine, and school costs for her daughter.

That was also true.

Then the document asked, silently and without mercy:

Why?

There was no field for why. That was the bank's genius. It wanted a relationship, not a reason. It could process wife, child, parent, invoice, employee, tenant, contractor, loan. It had boxes for the world's recognised routes. It had no box for a woman he had turned into an obligation without giving her a name the system would accept.

Sarah returned from the kiosk with water and a packet of almonds.

'You have gone very still.'

'Declaration.'

'Show me.'

He handed her the phone.

She read the two sentences.

'This is accurate.'

'It is not enough.'

'No.'

'What would be enough?'

She looked at the gate windows. Outside, a baggage cart moved beneath the aircraft with orange lights blinking.

'For a bank? Marriage certificate. Birth certificate. Rental agreement. Invoice. Employment contract. Something with a line they know how to read.'

'There is nothing.'

'There is something. There is no document.'

She handed the phone back.

'Those are different.'

The agent sent another message:

Please confirm whether recipient is family member.

Thomas looked at the words until the letters lost their place.

Sarah read them over his shoulder.

'Do not lie to a bank.'

'I know.'

'I mean it.'

'I know.'

He typed:

No.

The agent replied:

Please confirm reason for ongoing support to non-family recipient.

Boarding began.

Rows one to ten.

The queue formed badly at first, then corrected itself under the pressure of staff and tired people with printed boarding cards. Sarah stood and lifted her backpack.

'Thomas.'

'One minute.'

'They are boarding.'

He typed:

Personal humanitarian support.

The phrase looked like a notice on a charity box.

He sent it.

The agent replied:

Your case requires review by specialist team. Estimated response time: three to five working days.

He stared at the screen.

Three to five working days.

Olesya had written today.

The boarding line moved.

Sarah took his bag from the seat and handed it to him.

'Come on.'

'Three to five days.'

'I saw.'

'They do not have that.'

'No.'

She held his gaze for a second.

'Neither do we, if you miss this flight.'

He locked the phone.

The charter flight to Delhi had no ceremony either.

People boarded with the worn faces of evacuation and the thin manners of business travel. The cabin smelled of coffee, stale air, disinfectant, and bodies that had slept badly. Sarah took the window seat and pressed her forehead briefly to the plastic shade before lifting it. Thomas sat beside her, phone in aeroplane mode, the bank app useless behind the screen.

'Can you fix it from Delhi?' she asked.

'I can try from a proper connection.'

'That is not what I asked.'

'I know.'

'Can you?'

'I do not know.'

She nodded.

The plane taxied.

On the runway, his phone buzzed once before losing signal completely.

Olesya:

Electricity today.

Then another, arriving in the same breath:

If no payment they cut.

The phone went dark.

The aircraft lifted.

Iraq fell away beneath a film of dust, grids and roads and compounds flattening into colour and line. Thomas watched until cloud took the ground. Beside him, Sarah closed her eyes but did not sleep.

In the air, there was nothing to do.

That should have been impossible.

He had built his life around doing: transferring, categorising, replying, adjusting, moving funds from one account to another, naming things just enough for machines to allow them through. At altitude, cut from signal, his entire structure reduced to intention. Intention did not keep lights on. Intention did not satisfy banks. Intention did not stand in a queue, sign a form, explain a relationship, pay a bill.

Sarah opened her eyes.

'You are counting.'

'What?'

'Your thumb. On the armrest.'

He stopped.

'Sorry.'

'Do not apologise. Count something useful.'

'Such as?'

'Ways Delhi can be better.'

'Better than what?'

'Than a locked compound with box chicken.'

'That is a low bar.'

'Good. We need the win.'

He looked at the seatback in front of him.

'Company housing is supposed to be good there.'

'Define good.'

'More space. Better connection. Kitchen.'

'Kitchen sounds ambitious.'

'Regional staff. Drivers. Local admin.'

'You make it sound like a machine shop.'

'Maybe that would be useful.'

She turned her head towards him.

'Useful for us, or useful for the thing you need to fix?'

He did not answer.

She closed her eyes again.

'Fine. Count kitchens.'

The flight served breakfast in foil trays.

Sarah ate the yoghurt and left the eggs. Thomas ate nothing. He opened the in-flight magazine, looked at a map of routes, closed it. The line to Delhi curved neatly across the page. A clean arc. No blocked transfers. No child doing schoolwork under a failing bulb. No woman in Baku holding a phone up to a window for signal. Maps were dishonest in their politeness.

Halfway through the flight, the cabin lights dimmed.

Sarah slept at last.

Thomas took out the paper notebook from his bag.

Date.

Monthly transfer failed.

Reason:

He wrote:

Bank review.

Then:

Documentation required.

Then he stopped.

Relationship:

The pen rested against the page.

Friend.

Support.

Recipient.

None of them passed through the real gate.

He wrote nothing in that line.

The plane landed in Delhi in a white afternoon.

Heat came at the aircraft before the doors opened, visible through the windows in shimmer and exhaust. The terminal smelled of floor polish, coffee, damp fabric, and something floral from a cleaning solution sprayed too heavily near arrivals. Thomas's shirt stuck to his back before they reached passport control.

Delhi did not receive them as a new life.

It received them as processing.

Queue.

Form.

Stamp.

Thermal camera.

Company representative holding a printed sign.

Luggage delayed.

Phone signal strong and overloaded.

Sarah stood beside the baggage belt with both hands around the handle of her suitcase.

'Try now,' she said.

He did.

The bank app loaded at once.

Red banner.

Account under review.

He opened chat again.

This time a human agent joined after six minutes.

Good afternoon. I can help with your international payment query.

Thomas typed:

I need to release a payment urgently.

The agent replied:

We need additional information due to repeated transfers to Azerbaijan. Please upload documentation confirming your relationship with the recipient and the purpose of the funds.

He wrote:

Personal support.

Please provide documentation.

What documentation?

Examples include invoice, contract, tenancy agreement, family relationship document, or written declaration explaining payment purpose.

The baggage belt began moving. Suitcases emerged in a slow procession of other people's lives. Black cases. Blue cases. A cardboard box wrapped in plastic. A pushchair. Sarah's suitcase appeared with the laundry tag still tied to the handle. She pulled it off the belt and looked back at him.

'Well?'

He showed her the screen.

She read it.

'Written declaration.'

'Yes.'

'Can you write one?'

'Saying what?'

Sarah did not answer quickly.

That was the answer.

He closed the chat.

'Do not close it.'

'I cannot write it here.'

'You may not get a better place.'

The company representative approached, smiling with professional strain. 'Mr Ward? Mrs Ward? We have car ready. Bags only these?'

Mrs Ward.

Sarah looked at Thomas.

'Yes,' she said. 'Only these.'

The driver led them through arrivals into heat and horns.

Delhi moved around them with a force that made the compound seem like a diagram. Cars leaned into gaps that did not exist. Motorbikes slid between buses. Street vendors stood beside traffic with water bottles, phone chargers, masks, cut fruit under plastic. Stray wires looped between poles. Billboards rose above flyovers. A man pushed a cart stacked with green coconuts through a stream of vehicles that rearranged themselves around him without slowing.

Sarah sat beside Thomas in the back seat.

The air-conditioning worked hard and badly.

'Well,' she said.

'Yes.'

'It is not Iraq.'

'No.'

'That may be enough for today.'

He looked at his phone.

Olesya had sent four messages during landing.

Thomas?

They say today.

I asked Marina again.

She cannot.

Then:

Anya has school online. Battery low.

He typed:

I landed. Bank still blocked.

Her answer came while the car stopped under a flyover.

Then we are in dark.

No accusation. No punctuation after it.

Just the fact.

He typed:

I am trying another way.

She replied:

Trying is not light.

He held the phone low against his thigh.

Sarah watched Delhi through the window. A man on a scooter balanced a child in front of him, the child's hands resting on the handlebar between his. Both wore masks under their chins. At the roadside, laundry hung from a balcony above a pharmacy sign. A dog slept in the strip of shade beneath a parked truck.

'What did she say?' Sarah asked.

'They will be in the dark.'

Sarah did not look away from the window.

'Electricity?'

'Yes.'

'How much?'

'The monthly would cover it.'

'Yes.'

'Can you use a card?'

'Not for their utility.'

'A service?'

'Maybe.'

'Then find one.'

'I will.'

'Now?'

'In the flat.'

'Now in the car.'

He searched on his phone.

International bill payment Azerbaijan.

The first sites looked wrong. Fees hidden. Logos too bright. English badly arranged. He opened one, then another. The connection dipped under the flyover, returned, froze. The driver changed lanes with a sharp movement and Sarah's suitcase bumped his knee from the boot behind the seat.

He found a service that listed utilities.

Azerbaijan.

Electricity.

He asked Olesya:

Her reply:

The car moved into a wider road lined with trees whose leaves had turned grey at the edges from dust. Delhi heat pressed against the windows.

Thomas opened the secure gallery, searching old images.

Receipts. Rent. Pharmacy. School. Cake. Door. Red notebook. Lock. Food. Medicine. Blurred kitchen. Door tape. Nothing with electricity account number. He had never asked for it. Electricity had been a line in the sheet, not a document. It had arrived as amount, been paid, vanished.

The black box had kept him from seeing the number.

He watched the thread while traffic moved in bright jerks around the car. A bus leaned so close to the window that he could see handprints in the dust along its side. A boy stood in the open doorway, one hand around a rail, his shirt lifted by the heat.

Olesya replied:

I asked. No.

Too many people.

Then:

They do not want us sitting.

He looked at the line.

Not us. Not Anya. Us.

The driver braked hard. Sarah's shoulder touched his and moved away.

'Anything?'

'In Baku?'

'Yes.'

'Money is not the only block here.'

He knew that. The words made him look at it.

Hotel required passport, movement, permission, a woman arriving with a child and no husband, no local man, no clean explanation. It required crossing a city under rules that changed at each counter. It required Olesya to stand somewhere new and be assessed by someone with a pen.

He searched again.

Prepaid electricity Azerbaijan.

Pay utility Baku from abroad.

The results repeated themselves, each one either unusable without the account number or too polished to trust. One site asked for recipient phone number and promised bill lookup. He entered Olesya's number, then stopped before pressing submit. The site had no address listed, no company registration, no reason to be given her number except his need.

He closed it.

Need account number, he wrote.

Phone battery.

The car entered a road lined with trees whose leaves had turned grey at the edges from dust.

Sarah took his phone without asking.

He let her.

She opened the gallery and began moving through the images with her thumb. Receipts. Lock. School letter. Medicine. Food. The blurred kitchen. Door tape. Her face stayed still. Her breathing altered. The photographs passed one by one between them, each carrying a world she had only known through his fragments and his failures to name them.

'There are no utility papers here,' she said.

'Thomas.'

The line did not arrive hot. It arrived exact.

He looked at the screen. The cake remained there, small and neat, already from another life. He closed the gallery and opened the file folder where he had stored the renamed attachments: M-03-A, M-03-B, A-04-S, C-04. Maintenance codes for a family he had not permitted himself to call that.

No electricity.

No account number.

No route.

The connection dropped as the car passed under another flyover. Above them, traffic rolled over concrete, the sound pressing down into the back seat. When the signal returned, the message remained pending for eight seconds, then failed.

Sarah saw the red mark beside it.

'Send again.'

He did.

This time it delivered.

No answer.

The company flat was on the ninth floor of a serviced block near a road that did not stop sounding. Reception had marble too polished for the hour, a guard with a temperature scanner, and a bowl of green apples no one had touched. The flat itself had two bedrooms, a small kitchen, a sitting room, a balcony with a view of construction cranes and traffic, and air-conditioning that worked in several moods of loud.

Sarah stood in the doorway and looked at the space.

'This is obscene.'

'Compared with Iraq?'

'Compared with most things.'

The floor was cool underfoot. The fridge held bottled water, milk, eggs, butter, bread, and three small jars of jam. A welcome card sat on the counter with both their names printed correctly. In the bedroom, the bed was made with white sheets and four pillows. The bathroom had towels folded into a stack.

Thomas put his bag on a chair and opened his laptop.

Sarah did not empty her suitcase.

She stood beside the kitchen counter with one hand on the welcome card.

'Housing,' she said.

'What?'

'On the plane. You said Delhi housing would be better.'

'It is.'

'Yes.'

Her voice had gone flat.

He looked at the card.

Thomas Ward.

Sarah Ward.

Welcome.

His phone lit.

Olesya:

Lights off.

Then:

Anya keeps asking.

The second word hit the room and broke there.

Thomas opened the bank chat again.

Upload documentation.

He opened a blank document.

Bank compliance declaration.

He stopped.

He could write the money. He could write support. He could write recipient. He could write education, food, medicine, rent, electricity. He could not write the relationship in a way that would satisfy a bank without making a claim he had no right to file or exposing a truth his own life could not hold.

He tried anyway.

I have provided regular financial support to Olesya Petrova since...

Since when?

Since the hospital. Since the first transfer. Since the moment he had discovered money could cross the distance faster than any answer he was willing to give.

He deleted the line.

I am responsible for supporting...

Responsible.

The word looked legal, nearly marital, nearly paternal, nearly everything he had avoided becoming in public. He deleted it.

The payments are charitable.

That was worse. That took Olesya's kitchen, Anya's notebook, the lock, the medicine drawer, and pushed all of it into a box marked benevolence.

He deleted it.

Sarah stood behind him, silent enough to make the keyboard sound larger.

'You cannot write it,' she said.

'I can write something.'

'Something is what the bank rejected.'

He did not turn.

On the balcony, Delhi traffic continued below them, horns rising through the glass. The city was lit in pieces. Flats. Offices. Food stalls. Headlamps. A construction crane with red lights blinking at intervals. Electricity everywhere he looked.

We are in dark.

Thomas looked at the message.

The cursor blinked, asking for a name.
