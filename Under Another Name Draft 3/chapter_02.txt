Chapter 2
The routine began at six. Not because Thomas needed to be awake, but because the body, once trained, refuses to unlearn the rhythm of obligation. The alarm did not sound; he was already staring at the ceiling when the digital clock flipped from 05:59 to 06:00. The red numbers glowed in the gloom, a tiny, pulsing wound in the darkness.
He lay still for a moment, testing the weight of his limbs. They felt heavy, detached, as if belonging to someone else who had slept in this bed, woken in this room, and now faced this day. The silence of the flat was different in the morning. Less accusatory, more indifferent. It was the silence of a machine waiting to be switched on.
Thomas sat up. The mattress creaked, a familiar complaint. He swung his legs over the side, feet finding the cold floorboards. The stain from last night’s wine was still there, a dark, irregular patch on the laminate near the sofa. He looked at it, then looked away. Let it stay. A record of the spill, the broken glass, the call. A mark that something had happened, even if nothing had changed.
He walked to the kitchen. The phone sat on the counter where he had left it, face down. He did not turn it over. He filled the kettle, the sound of rushing water loud in the quiet room. He watched the level rise in the transparent plastic, stopping just below the max line. He clicked the switch. The red light illuminated, a small beacon in the grey dawn.
While the water boiled, he opened the cupboard. Tea bags, coffee jars, sugar, biscuits. All in their places. He took out the coffee jar, the one she had bought, the one she liked. ‘Proper coffee, Tom. None of that instant sludge.’ He had smiled, nodded, bought it. He had drunk two cups since she left. The rest would sit there, slowly losing its aroma, until it was just bitter dust.
He spooned instant granules into a mug. Added boiling water. Stirred. The smell was sharp, chemical, nothing like the rich scent she had praised. He drank it standing up, scalding his tongue, feeling the heat travel down his throat into his stomach. It tasted of nothing. Or rather, it tasted of everything he had become: functional, bland, enduring.
He showered again. The water was hot, the steam filling the small bathroom, fogging the mirror. He shaved, the razor scraping against his stubble, removing the night’s growth. He looked at his reflection in the cleared patch of glass. Pale eyes, hollow cheeks. The face of a man going through the motions. He dressed in the same clothes as yesterday: jeans, jumper, socks. Clean ones from the drawer. The routine demanded it.
At seven-fifteen, he left the flat. The stairwell smelled of cabbage and damp, unchanged. The street outside was grey, the sky a uniform sheet of cloud. The air was cold, biting at his exposed hands. He pulled his coat tighter, shoved his hands into his pockets, and walked towards the station.
The commute was a blur of motion and noise. The train was crowded, bodies pressed together, faces hidden behind phones and newspapers. No one spoke. The only sounds were the rumble of the tracks, the automated announcements, the occasional cough or rustle of paper. Thomas stood near the door, holding the overhead rail, letting the sway of the carriage rock him gently. He stared at the back of a woman’s head, her hair dyed a unnatural shade of blonde, tied back with a scrunchie. For a second, his heart stuttered. Then the woman turned to check her reflection in the window, and it was not her. Just another stranger.
He got off at his usual stop, walked the three blocks to the office. The building was a nondescript block of glass and steel, one of dozens lining the canal. He swiped his card at the turnstile, the green light flashing, the barrier clicking open. He took the lift to the fourth floor, stepped out into the corridor, and walked to his desk.
‘Morning, Tom,’ said Sarah from the next desk. She didn’t look up from her screen.
‘Morning,’ Thomas replied. His voice sounded rough, unused.
He sat down. Switched on his computer. The screen flickered to life, displaying the company logo, then the desktop. Icons arranged in neat rows. Emails waiting in the inbox. Forty-three unread. He clicked on the first one. Deleted it. Clicked on the next. Filed it. Clicked on the third. Replied with a standard template.
The work was mindless. Data entry, report generation, email correspondence. Tasks that required attention but no thought. Thomas let his hands move across the keyboard, fingers finding the keys without direction. His mind was elsewhere. Drifting. Thinking about the call last night. The voice on the line. I’m not the person you think I am. The girl in the photos is gone.
What did that mean? Was it a metaphor? A dramatic flourish? Or was it literal? Had she changed so much that she was no longer herself? Or had she simply decided to stop playing the part he had assigned her?
He stopped typing. Stared at the screen. The cursor blinked in the empty text box. Dear Mr Henderson, regarding your query…
He deleted the sentence. Closed the email. Opened a new tab. Typed Istanbul hotels into the search bar.
Hundreds of results appeared. Prices, ratings, locations. He scrolled through them. The Grand Hotel. The Bosphorus Palace. The Old City Inn. Pictures of lobbies, rooms, views. All looking clean, sterile, impersonal. None of them looked like the place in the photo. The balcony with the glass railing. The view of the minarets.
He clicked on a map. Zoomed in on the area near the Galata Tower. Scrolled through the street view. Narrow streets, old buildings, bustling markets. Nothing matched. She could be anywhere. In a hotel he hadn’t found. In an apartment rented for a week. In a hostel. With someone else.
The thought hit him like a physical blow. He gripped the edge of the desk, his knuckles turning white. With someone else. It was possible. Probable, even. She had wanted space. Freedom. To be seen. Why not find that with someone new? Someone who didn’t know her history. Someone who didn’t expect her to be the girl in the flat.
He closed the tab. The image of her with a stranger, laughing on a balcony, holding hands by the Bosphorus, burned in his mind. He pushed it away. Focused on the screen. The blinking cursor. The empty email.
He typed: Dear Mr Henderson, regarding your query, we are unable to process your request at this time.
It was a lie. He could process it. He just didn’t want to. He sent the email anyway.
Lunchtime came and went. He didn’t eat. He stayed at his desk, staring at the screen, watching the minutes tick by on the clock in the corner. One o’clock. Two o’clock. Three o’clock.
At four, his phone buzzed in his pocket.
Thomas froze. His hand hovered over the mouse. He waited. The buzzing stopped. Then started again. A long, sustained vibration against his thigh.
He looked around. Sarah was on a call. The others were focused on their screens. No one noticed.
He pulled the phone out. Checked the screen.
Unknown Number.
His heart hammered against his ribs. He looked at the phone. Then at the screen. Then back at the phone.
He slid his thumb across the screen. Answered. Put the phone to his ear.
‘Hello?’
Silence.
Then, a click. Then, the sound of breathing. Soft, rhythmic. Female.
‘Olesya?’ Thomas whispered.
No answer. Just the breathing.
‘Olesya, are you there?’
The breathing stopped. Then, a voice. Low, distant. ‘I saw you.’
Thomas frowned. ‘Saw me? Where?’
‘Online.’
‘Online? What do you mean?’
‘Your profile. You looked… tired.’
Thomas felt a chill run down his spine. ‘You checked my profile?’
‘Once.’
‘Why?’
‘Curiosity.’
‘Olesya, please. Talk to me. Tell me where you are. Tell me what’s happening.’
‘I can’t.’
‘Why not?’
‘Because if I tell you, you’ll come. And I don’t want you to come.’
‘Then let me come. Let me see you. Just once.’
‘No.’
‘Why?’
‘Because it won’t help. It will only make it harder.’
‘Harder for who?’
‘For both of us.’
The line went quiet. Thomas heard the faint sound of traffic in the background. Horns. Engines. Distant voices. Istanbul. It had to be Istanbul.
‘Are you in Istanbul?’ he asked.
‘Maybe.’
‘Just tell me.’
‘Does it matter?’
‘Yes. It matters.’
‘Not to me.’
The words hung in the air, sharp and final. Thomas felt a surge of anger, hot and sudden. ‘How can you say that? Don’t you care? Don’t you care about what you’ve done? About what I’m going through?’
‘I care,’ she said softly. ‘But caring doesn’t fix anything.’
‘Then what does?’
‘Time.’
‘Time?’ Thomas laughed, a harsh, brittle sound. ‘Time doesn’t fix anything. It just makes you forget.’
‘Maybe forgetting is the point.’
‘I don’t want to forget.’
‘That’s your problem, Tom. Not mine.’
The line crackled. The connection fading.
‘Olesya? Olesya, wait!’
‘Goodbye, Tom.’
The line went dead.
Thomas held the phone to his ear, listening to the dial tone. A steady, monotonous beep. He lowered the phone. Stared at the screen. Call Ended.
He sat there for a long time. The office around him continued as normal. Keyboards clacking. Phones ringing. Voices murmuring. Life going on.
He looked at his computer screen. The email he had sent earlier. Dear Mr Henderson, regarding your query, we are unable to process your request at this time.
He opened a new tab. Typed bank transfer into the search bar.
He logged into his online banking. The homepage loaded, showing his balance. A healthy sum. Enough to live comfortably for months. Enough to book a flight to Istanbul. Enough to rent a car. Enough to find her.
He clicked on Recent Transactions.
The list appeared. Direct debits. Standing orders. Card payments. Grocery stores. Petrol stations. Online subscriptions.
And then, he saw it.
A transaction from yesterday.
Transfer to: O. Volkov.
Amount: £5,000.
Reference: For you.
Thomas stared at the screen. His breath caught in his throat.
Five thousand pounds.
He hadn’t authorised it. He hadn’t initiated it.
He clicked on the transaction. Details appeared.
Date: Yesterday.
Time: 14:32.
Method: Online Banking.
IP Address: 88.245.xxx.xxx.
He didn’t recognise the IP address. He didn’t recognise the time. He had been at the airport yesterday at 14:32. Waiting for a flight that never boarded. Waiting for a woman who never arrived.
How?
How had she done it?
He didn’t share his password. He had changed it recently. He was sure of it. Or was he? Memory was a slippery thing. Maybe he had written it down. Maybe she had guessed it. Maybe she had watched him type it.
It didn’t matter how. What mattered was that she had done it.
She had taken five thousand pounds from his account.
And left a reference: For you.
Thomas felt a wave of nausea rise in his stomach. He gripped the edge of the desk, his knuckles white.
She had stolen from him.
After everything. After the silence. After the leaving. After the calls. She had stolen from him.
He wanted to scream. To throw the computer across the room. To smash the screen. To break something.
Instead, he sat there. Trembling.
He looked at the transaction again. For you.
What did it mean? Was it a joke? A taunt? A gift?
He didn’t know. He couldn’t know.
He logged out of the banking site. Closed the tab.
He looked at the clock. Five past five. Time to go home.
He stood up. Packed his bag. Put on his coat.
‘Leaving early, Tom?’ Sarah asked, looking up from her screen.
‘Not feeling well,’ Thomas said. His voice was flat.
‘Hope you feel better.’
‘Thanks.’
He walked to the lift. Pressed the button. Waited.
The lift arrived. He stepped inside. Pressed the ground floor button.
The doors closed. He was alone.
He leaned against the wall. Closed his eyes.
Five thousand pounds.
Gone.
Just like that.
The lift reached the ground floor. The doors opened. He stepped out. Walked through the lobby. Out onto the street.
The air was cold. The sky was dark. Rain had started to fall, a fine mist that coated his face, his coat, his hair.
He walked towards the station. Head down. Shoulders hunched.
He didn’t think about the money. He didn’t think about the call. He didn’t think about her.
He just walked.
One foot in front of the other.
Through the rain.
Towards the train.
Towards home.
Towards the silence.
When he arrived at the flat, he didn’t turn on the light. He dropped his bag by the door. Took off his coat. Hung it up. Took off his shoes. Lined them up neatly.
He walked to the kitchen. Poured a glass of water. Drank it.
He looked at the phone on the counter.
It was dark. Sleeping.
He picked it up. Turned it over.
No new messages. No missed calls.
Just the time. Ticking forward. Indifferent.
He put the phone down.
He walked to the living room. Sat on the sofa. The same spot as before.
He looked at the stain on the floor. The dark patch from the wine.
It was still there.
He leaned back. Closed his eyes.
Five thousand pounds.
For you.
The words echoed in his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed them away.
Sleep came eventually. Fitful. Dream-filled.
He dreamt of banks. Endless corridors of safes. Doors opening to reveal empty boxes. Money flying out of windows. Disappearing into the wind.
He dreamt of her. Standing on a balcony. Holding a handful of notes. Letting them go. Watching them fall. Smiling.
He woke up gasping.
The room was pitch black.
He sat up. Disoriented.
The phone.
It was buzzing.
He fumbled for it. Knocked the glass of water off the table. It didn’t break this time. Just rolled across the floor, spilling water onto the carpet.
He didn’t care. He grabbed the phone.
Unknown Number.
He answered.
‘Hello?’
Silence.
Then, a click. Then, silence again.
Then the recording ended.
No voice. No breathing. Just a call that connected and then said nothing.
He lowered the phone. Stared at it.
Was it her? Testing the line? Seeing if he would answer? Or just a wrong number?
It didn’t matter. The result was the same. Silence.
He put the phone down.
He lay back on the sofa. Pulled the blanket over himself.
Tomorrow he would call the bank. Report the fraud. Cancel the card. Change the password.
But tonight, he just lay there. In the dark. Listening to the silence.
Waiting for a phone that wouldn’t ring.
Wondering if she was spending the money right now. On a hotel room. On a meal. On a ticket to somewhere else.
Wondering if she thought about him. Or if he was already erased.
Just access.
Under another name.
The thought drifted through his mind. Unbidden. Unwanted.
He pushed it away.
Sleep took him again. Deep. Dreamless.
When he woke, the sun was up. Bright. Harsh.
The flat was still empty.
The phone was still silent.
He got up. Showered. Dressed.
Made coffee.
Drank it standing up.
Looked at the wet patch on the carpet.
Left it.
Opened the door.
Stepped out.
Locked it behind him.
Walked down the stairs.
Into the day.
