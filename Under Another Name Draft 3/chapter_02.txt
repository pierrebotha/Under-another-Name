Chapter 02

The courier came at nine carrying a plastic folder under one arm and a cigarette behind his ear.

He stopped beside Olesya's desk before looking anywhere else. That was the first thing Thomas noticed from his office. The man did not ask reception. He did not go to Rauf. He crossed the open-plan floor with the confidence of someone who knew which person in the room could make a document move.

Olesya stood before he reached her.

'Blue stamps,' she said.

The courier lifted the folder. 'All pages.'

'Show me.'

He made a sound of complaint, but opened the folder. Olesya turned the first page, then the second. Her finger paused on each stamp. She did not hurry because he wanted her to hurry. The office moved around them: phones, chairs, the click of keyboards, Rauf's voice from the corridor, the low cough of the printer coming to life again. Olesya stood in the middle of it and counted blue circles one by one.

When she reached the final page, she closed the folder and placed it on her desk.

'Wait.'

'I have other collections.'

'Then wait quickly.'

The courier laughed, but he stayed.

Thomas looked down at his screen. The permit register was open. Column H still held the wrong formula from the day before. He had corrected the live copy after Olesya left, but the version on her machine would still carry the error. If she updated the permit number from the courier's document and sent it to Rauf, the weekly report would pull through a false completion date.

He stood.

By the time he reached her desk, Olesya had taken out a receipt book. She wrote slowly, pressing hard enough for the carbon sheet to catch beneath. The courier leaned over her shoulder.

'Do not stand so close,' she said.

He stepped back.

Thomas placed one hand on the edge of the desk. 'Send me the register before you update Rauf.'

Olesya glanced up. 'I checked it.'

'Send it anyway.'

Her mouth tightened. Not refusal. Record.

She tore the receipt free and handed it to the courier. He tucked it into the folder, touched two fingers to his forehead in a half-salute, and left with his cigarette still behind his ear.

Olesya opened the register. Thomas waited beside her while the file loaded, the blue wheel turning at the centre of the screen. Her desk smelled faintly of lemon wipes. The fern had already been watered. A bead clung to the lip of the chipped pot.

'Column H,' he said.

'It is the completion date.'

'It is reading from the wrong cell.'

She looked at the formula bar. Her hand hovered over the mouse, then withdrew.

'I thought you corrected it.'

'On my version.'

'Then there are two versions.'

'Yes.'

'That is stupid.'

'It is common.'

She looked at him then, almost sharply. 'Common is not better.'

Thomas did not answer. He took the mouse and repaired the cell. Then he copied the formula down the column, checked the row references, and saved the file under the shared drive, where both machines would open the same version.

'Use this one now.'

'The other one?'

'Delete it.'

She moved the old file to the recycle bin with a decisiveness that pleased him.

At ten, Rauf came out of his office with the corrected permit pack in one hand.

'It passed.'

Olesya looked up from her desk. 'All pages?'

'All pages.' Rauf held the folder towards Thomas. 'The Ministry clerk asked who prepared it.'

'Why?'

'He said it looked like someone had finally learned.'

Olesya lowered her eyes to the keyboard.

Thomas took the folder. The blue stamps had dried darker at their edges. His rebuilt table sat exactly inside the margins. Olesya's handwritten note had done its work without being seen.

'Good,' he said.

Rauf watched him for a moment. 'There is another problem.'

'Of course there is.'

'Drill bits from Aktau. Customs hold. The yard is shouting because they need them by Friday.'

Olesya had already opened the logistics mailbox.

Rauf saw this and smiled without softness. 'Excellent. Everyone is awake.'

The shipment had been entered under the wrong tariff category. The vendor had used a general machinery code, the customs broker had copied it, and the local system had flagged the consignment as requiring secondary inspection. The drill bits were sitting in a bonded warehouse near the port, close enough for the yard men to point at and too far for anyone to use.

Olesya printed the documents and brought them to Thomas.

'The broker says we need the Ministry of Taxation to confirm the classification.'

'The broker should have checked it before the shipment arrived.'

'He says the vendor made the mistake.'

'He would.'

She placed the papers on his desk. The top sheet had already been marked with small pencil ticks beside the invoice number, supplier address, and shipping weight. She had missed the tariff code. She had seen the missing signature on the packing declaration.

Thomas pointed to the code. 'This is the hold.'

'I thought it was the signature.'

'The signature will delay release. The code prevents it.'

'Both matter.'

'Yes.'

The word hung between them longer than it needed to.

Rauf appeared in the doorway. 'Can we release the shipment?'

'Not yet.'

'That was not my preferred answer.'

'The vendor used the wrong tariff code. The broker copied it. Customs caught it. We need a correction letter from the vendor and a local classification note.'

Rauf looked at Olesya. 'Call the broker.'

'I called him.'

'And?'

'He says he sent an email.'

'Of course he did.'

'To the wrong address,' she added.

Rauf closed his eyes for one second. 'Find him.'

Olesya took the papers and returned to her desk.

Thomas watched her dial. Her Russian changed when she spoke to men who expected apology. It became lower, flatter, each sentence shorter than the last. She did not argue at first. She let the broker explain the wrong thing twice. Then she interrupted him.

The office quietened around her in patches.

'No,' she said. 'You sent it to procurement Azerbaijan. We are Baku regional support. Different mailbox. Different floor. Different people. Send it now.'

The broker spoke long enough for Thomas to hear the irritation in his voice through the receiver.

'No,' Olesya said again. 'Now.'

She ended the call and wrote something in her notebook.

Rauf looked across the room at Thomas. 'That is useful.'

The email arrived six minutes later.

It was addressed to the wrong person, attached to the wrong thread, and missing the correction letter.

Thomas stood behind Olesya's chair while she opened the attachments. There was a faint line of sweat along her hairline. The office air had not recovered from the previous day's heat. Outside, a low grey sky pressed down over the harbour, flattening the light.

'He forgot the letter,' she said.

'He did not forget.'

'No.'

'Call him again.'

She did.

By noon, the correction letter had arrived from the vendor in poor English and worse formatting. Thomas rewrote it. Olesya called the broker again to confirm the required stamp. Rauf carried the first version to the printer, read three lines, and brought it back.

'This says the items are non-critical.'

'They are critical,' Thomas said.

'That is why I brought it back.'

Thomas deleted the sentence and rebuilt the paragraph.

At twelve forty, the document was ready.

Olesya stood beside the printer, waiting for the pages. Her lunch sat unopened on her desk: bread wrapped in foil, a small cucumber, a boiled egg in a paper napkin. She had placed them neatly beside the fern, as if the food belonged to the desk more than to her.

'Eat,' Thomas said.

'After.'

'After what?'

'After the courier.'

'The courier can wait.'

She looked at him, not quite understanding the sentence.

The printer gave a soft chime. She took the pages and checked them against her list. Tariff code. Vendor stamp. Signature. Broker reference. Ministry address. She slid the set into a clear sleeve and pressed the edges flat with the side of her hand.

Thomas watched the motion.

There were people who moved paper as if it were an obstacle. Olesya moved it as if it could be made obedient.

The courier arrived at one ten. Not the same man. This one wore a leather jacket and smelled strongly of aftershave. Olesya made him sign the collection book before he took the sleeve.

'It must go to the Ministry first,' she said. 'Then customs.'

'I know.'

'Say it back.'

He laughed.

She did not.

'Ministry first,' he said. 'Then customs.'

Only then did she release the document.

At two thirty, the courier called from the Ministry. The clerk required a secondary stamp from the vendor's local representative. The representative was out of the office until Monday.

Rauf swore in Azerbaijani from inside his office.

The sound carried through the glass.

Olesya stood by Thomas's desk, holding the phone away from her ear.

'He says Monday.'

'Friday is the latest release date.'

'Yes.'

'Where is the representative?'

'At a funeral.'

'Is he?'

She listened again. 'No.'

'Where?'

'Lunch.'

Thomas held out his hand. She gave him the phone.

The man on the other end spoke quickly, lazily, with restaurant noise behind him. Thomas let him finish. He did not raise his voice. He asked for the representative's full name, the office address, the company registration number, and the name of the person to whom the delay should be attributed in the weekly vendor performance report.

The restaurant noise changed.

The man came back to the office.

At four fifteen, the secondary stamp was applied.

At four forty, customs released the drill bits.

At five, the yard confirmed receipt for delivery the following morning.

Rauf walked into Thomas's office with both hands in his pockets.

'You see?' he said.

'See what?'

'Baku.'

Thomas saved the release confirmation. 'This was ordinary incompetence.'

'Yes. That is Baku.'

Olesya appeared in the doorway. She held the final delivery note.

'The warehouse sent this.'

Thomas took it. The shipment weight was correct. The tariff code had been amended. The delivery address matched the yard. One line still listed the wrong internal cost centre.

He looked at Olesya.

She saw it a beat later.

'I will fix it.'

'No,' Thomas said. 'I will.'

Rauf raised his eyebrows.

'It affects the weekly cost report,' Thomas said.

'Then you will fix it,' Rauf said.

Olesya remained in the doorway after Rauf left. The delivery note trembled slightly in her hand. Not much. Enough.

'I missed it.'

'You caught the broker.'

'I missed this.'

'Yes.'

She looked down at the page. 'You fix the things after me.'

Thomas opened the cost report on his screen. 'I fix the things before they become expensive.'

'That is not the same.'

'No.'

He changed the cost centre. The cell accepted the correction and the total shifted by a small amount at the bottom of the sheet.

Olesya watched the number settle.

'Rauf will know?'

'No.'

'Why not?'

'Because the report will be correct.'

She folded the delivery note once, then unfolded it. A crease remained through the centre of the page.

'Thank you,' she said.

Thomas did not look away from the screen. 'For what?'

'For not making a public lesson.'

The phrase was too formal. It sounded translated from another room, another life.

'Public lessons waste time,' he said.

That was true enough to stand between them.

At six, most of the office began to close. Men pulled jackets over the backs of chairs. Someone wrapped leftover pastries in a napkin and carried them towards the lift. The harbour darkened early beneath the low clouds. Rain moved across the glass in fine diagonal lines.

Olesya was still at her desk.

Thomas could see only part of her through the open door: one shoulder, the side of her face, the pale oval of her hand moving between keyboard and notebook. Her lunch remained unopened. The cucumber had rolled against the fern pot.

He finished the cost report and sent it to Rauf.

When he looked again, Olesya had put on her coat.

She stood too quickly.

The movement was small, but not clean. One hand went to the edge of the desk. Her eyes closed for half a second, then opened.

Thomas came out of his office.

'Are you unwell?'

'No.'

'Sit down.'

'I am late.'

'For what?'

She began to gather her folders.

'Olesya.'

The use of her name stopped her more effectively than the instruction had.

She looked at the clock on the wall. Six twelve.

'The clinic closes at seven.'

'Then go.'

'I have to take the metro.'

'The metro is three blocks.'

'From here, yes.'

She placed the folders into her bag, then took them out again, noticing she had put them in the wrong order.

'Which clinic?'

'It does not matter.'

'It does if it closes at seven.'

She pressed the folders flat against the desk. 'Near Narimanov.'

Thomas knew the district only from the map on his tablet. Far enough in evening traffic. Too far on foot from the metro if the rain held.

'Why did you not go at lunch?'

'The shipment.'

'The shipment could have waited.'

She gave him a look that almost became disbelief. 'No. It could not.'

Outside, Rauf's office door opened. He came out with his phone in one hand and his coat over his arm. He saw Olesya standing with her bag, Thomas beside her desk, the untouched food still wrapped near the fern.

'Problem?'

'No,' Olesya said.

'Clinic,' Thomas said.

Rauf looked at her. Something passed through his face and was gone before it could be named.

'Go,' he said. 'The office will not collapse because you leave before the cleaners.'

'I finished the register.'

'Then vanish.'

She nodded once. Not gratitude. Compliance.

Downstairs, the lobby smelled of wet wool and floor polish. The guard watched a football match on his phone with the sound turned low. Outside, the rain had thickened, turning the streetlights soft around their edges.

Thomas had intended to call the company driver.

Olesya was already walking towards the metro.

'Wait.'

She stopped under the awning.

'I will get you a taxi.'

'No.'

'You will miss the clinic.'

'I have money for the metro.'

'That is not the same.'

The sentence landed badly. He could see it at once. Her chin lifted. Something in her had been handled too often by other people.

'I can take the metro.'

'I know.'

'Then why?'

He looked at the road. Cars moved slowly through the rain, tyres hissing over the wet surface. A taxi came past with its roof light on, then another without stopping.

'Because the clinic closes at seven.'

She held his gaze long enough to reject the first answer and accept the second.

Thomas stepped to the kerb and raised his hand. The third taxi slowed. He opened the rear door and gave the driver the district name. The driver repeated it, then named a fare too high by half.

Olesya answered in Azerbaijani before Thomas could speak. The driver shrugged. She answered again, shorter. He lowered the fare.

Thomas looked at her.

'He thinks you do not know,' she said.

'He is often right.'

The almost-smile returned. Briefly.

She got into the taxi. Thomas took out his wallet and pulled free two fifty-manat notes. Olesya saw them before he could hand them to the driver.

'No.'

'For the taxi.'

'No.'

The driver watched them in the mirror.

Thomas placed one note on the passenger seat beside the driver. 'To Narimanov. Wait there. Bring her back if she asks.'

Olesya's hand tightened around the strap of her handbag.

'Thomas.'

He closed the door before she could return the money.

The taxi pulled away.

Thomas stood under the awning until its brake lights disappeared into the traffic. Rain blew in beneath the glass canopy and darkened the front of his shirt.

Rauf came out behind him.

'You will learn,' he said.

Thomas did not turn. 'What?'

'In this city, help is never only help.'

'It was a taxi.'

'Yes.'

Rauf opened his umbrella. 'That is how it starts.'

He walked away towards the car park, the umbrella tilting against the wind.

Thomas stayed a moment longer, then went back upstairs.

The office was nearly empty. A cleaner had begun moving between the desks with a black rubbish bag. Olesya's desk remained as she had left it: keyboard covered, notebook squared to the edge, lunch still folded in foil. The fern leaned into the window. Beside it lay the clinic receipt she had forgotten to put away.

Thomas did not mean to read it.

The paper was face up.

Consultation. Medication. Laboratory fee. The total sat at the bottom in a boxed line, larger than he expected. Not catastrophic. Not nothing. Beneath it, in the same careful hand he had seen on the permit note, Olesya had written three numbers in a column and crossed out the first two.

He stood with the receipt between two fingers.

The cleaner's vacuum started at the far end of the room.

Thomas put the receipt back exactly where it had been.

Then he took his own notebook from his office and wrote the amount on the back page.

He waited until eight thirty before his phone rang.

'I am back home,' Olesya said.

There was traffic behind her. Or rain against glass. The line carried the soft interference of distance.

'Did the clinic close?'

'No.'

'Good.'

'The taxi waited.'

'Good.'

She said nothing for a while. Thomas looked through his office window at her empty desk.

'I will pay you tomorrow,' she said.

'For what?'

'The taxi.'

'No.'

'Thomas.'

'No.'

Another pause. Not silence exactly. He could hear her breathing, then something small being moved on a table.

'Then I will bring coffee.'

'You already bring coffee.'

'Better coffee.'

He looked at the machine near reception, its chrome surface smeared from a day of hands.

'That would be useful.'

'Good night.'

'Good night.'

The call ended.

Thomas remained in the office until the cleaner switched off the lights in the open-plan area. Only his room and the corridor stayed lit. He finished the cost report, sent a note to the yard, checked the customs release again, then opened the drawer where he had placed Olesya's blue-stamp note.

The wrapped chocolate from the previous evening lay beside it.

He had not eaten it.

Sarah called at nine fifteen.

Her face filled the laptop screen from a hotel room in London, the background all white bedding, polished wood, and the edge of a framed print hung too high on the wall. She had a pen in her mouth and was fastening an earring with one hand.

'You look tired,' she said.

'Long day.'

'Same here. Frankfurt moved the call twice, then pretended the delay was mine.'

She took the pen from her mouth and looked away from the camera. Somewhere beyond the frame, papers shifted. Thomas saw a neat stack of documents beside a glass of water, a blazer hanging on the wardrobe door, black shoes placed side by side under the desk. Sarah's rooms always became efficient within an hour of her arrival. Not homely. Operational.

'The consulting contract?' he asked.

'Likely. They want me in Geneva next week if the board signs off.'

'You should go.'

'I intend to.'

She smiled then, quick and bright, already past the decision. Sarah moved through the world as if doors existed to confirm her direction. Airports, contracts, trains, border checks, conference rooms. They did not resist her. If one route failed, she found another before the failure had time to settle.

'How is Baku?'

Thomas looked through the glass at the empty desk outside his office. The fern was a darker shape against the window.

'Complicated.'

'That means badly managed.'

'Mostly.'

'Good. You like badly managed. It gives you something to fix.'

He watched her turn a page on the desk beyond the screen. The earring caught the hotel light.

'And the people?'

'Capable in uneven ways.'

'That sounds diplomatic.'

'It is accurate.'

Sarah laughed under her breath. 'You and accuracy. I miss that, oddly.'

'Only oddly?'

'Do not make me sentimental before a board call.'

He smiled because she expected him to. She looked well. More than well. Focused, sharpened by movement, animated by the prospect of another city and another room where she would not have to ask anyone for permission to enter. Thomas admired that in her. He always had.

'The flat behaving?' she asked.

'Well enough.'

'And you?'

'Well enough.'

She studied him for a second. Not long. Sarah did not linger where no useful answer would be produced.

'Sleep, Thomas.'

'After I finish this.'

'You always say that.'

'It is usually true.'

'Good night, then.'

'Good night.'

The screen went dark.

Thomas sat in the reflection of his own face for a moment before closing the laptop. Outside the office, Olesya's desk remained ordered around its small plant. The tissue square beneath the chocolate was still there, empty now, a pale mark below the monitor.

Two rooms. Two women who never met inside them.

He turned off the desk lamp and left.

The next morning, Olesya arrived at eight exactly.

Her hair was still damp from the rain. She wore the same coat. The tear near the cuff had been repaired with thread a shade darker than the fabric. She placed a paper cup on Thomas's desk, this one from the cafe downstairs instead of the machine.

'Better coffee,' she said.

'Thank you.'

She turned to leave, then stopped.

'The taxi was too much.'

'It got you to the clinic.'

'Still.'

Thomas opened his drawer. The receipt was not there. The note was. The chocolate. His notebook. He took out two fifty-manat notes and placed them beneath a paperweight before she could step fully away.

Olesya looked at the money.

'For what?'

'Taxis.'

'How many taxis?'

'Future ones.'

She did not touch the notes.

The office noise gathered slowly behind her. A phone rang. Someone laughed near the kitchen. Rauf's door clicked shut.

'I am not your expense,' she said.

Thomas moved the paperweight half an inch, aligning it with the edge of the desk. 'No.'

'Then what is this?'

'A buffer.'

The word belonged to his world. It came out cleanly.

Olesya looked at the notes, then at the paperweight holding them flat. The word had changed their shape.

'For when the system fails,' he said.

She stood very still.

Then she picked up the notes, folded them once, and placed them inside the back cover of her notebook.

No thanks.

No promise to repay.

At noon, the fern was watered.

At five, the permit register arrived in his inbox. The formulas were still wrong in two places. The physical file reference was perfect.

Thomas corrected the numbers and saved the shared version.

The week arranged itself around that exchange.

On Monday, a driver arrived with four delivery slips and only three signatures. Olesya sent him back to the yard before Thomas had finished reading the first page. The man returned at noon with the fourth signature, angry enough to slap the paper down on her desk. She took a ruler from the drawer and moved the sheet towards herself until it aligned with the edge.

'Now it can be filed,' she said.

The driver looked at Thomas's office, then at Rauf's, then left without speaking.

The file she sent afterwards had the correct slips scanned in the correct order. The spreadsheet attached to it placed the delivery against the wrong vessel.

Thomas corrected the vessel code.

On Tuesday, Olesya secured a customs appointment no one else had managed to obtain. She did it by calling someone named Leyla twice, then a third time after lunch, her voice patient in a way Thomas would not have been patient. By three, the appointment was confirmed for Thursday morning.

The calendar invitation she created was set for the wrong time zone.

Thomas changed it before Rauf accepted.

On Wednesday, she brought coffee and a packet of small pastries from the cafe downstairs. She placed one on Rauf's desk, one beside the printer for the clerk who shouted at couriers, and one on Thomas's coaster. The office softened around the sugar. Even the procurement clerk who disliked everyone took one and said nothing cruel for almost an hour.

The purchase order she prepared that afternoon was missing the tax exemption line.

Thomas inserted it before sending the file.

Thomas kept the corrected files in the shared drive and the marked copies in his drawer. The room ran more smoothly that way. Olesya made it habitable. Thomas made it accurate.

Each believed the other was doing the lesser magic.

On Thursday morning, rain came hard against the harbour windows. Olesya arrived with the hem of her dress darkened and her hair damp at the temples. She took off her coat and hung it carefully over the back of her chair. The repaired cuff had opened again. A strip of lining showed through.

Thomas saw it when she reached across his desk to place the coffee down.

'Your coat is torn.'

She looked at the cuff as if it belonged to someone else. 'It catches on the bus seat.'

'You should have it repaired.'

'I did.'

The answer closed the subject.

At eleven, she went with Rauf to the customs appointment. Thomas watched them leave through the glass wall: Rauf walking quickly, phone already to his ear, Olesya beside him with the document sleeve held close against her ribs so the rain would not reach the paper. She had put a plastic bag over the sleeve and tucked the open end beneath her palm.

They came back after two. Rauf was wet to the shoulders and furious. Olesya was quieter than when she had left.

'Appointment was real,' Rauf said, dropping into the visitor chair in Thomas's office. 'Clerk was not.'

'Meaning?'

'Meaning Leyla exists. Leyla's supervisor has decided he requires a registration certificate that expired last year because he likes old paper.'

Olesya placed the sleeve on Thomas's desk. It was dry.

'We have the old certificate,' she said.

'Expired,' Rauf said.

'Still stamped.'

Thomas opened the folder. The old certificate was there, copied on thin paper, the stamp faint but legible. Olesya had anticipated the obstruction without knowing its exact form.

'This will work,' Thomas said.

Rauf looked at Olesya. 'Why did you bring it?'

'Because he asked for it once before.'

'When?'

'Not him. Another man.'

'Same office?'

'Same smell.'

Rauf stared at her, then laughed in a single burst. 'Same smell. Fine. I accept this classification.'

They resubmitted the pack by courier. The clearance came through at four fifty-two.

At five ten, Thomas found three arithmetic errors in the cost allocation sheet.

He stayed until seven correcting them.

The first note he left in her drawer was not large.

He chose the amount by making it small enough to deny and large enough to solve something. Two twenty-manat notes and one ten. He placed them beneath the spare staples in the top drawer, where she would find them only if she opened it fully. Then he closed the drawer and stood for a moment with his hand still on the metal handle.

The office was empty except for the cleaner in the corridor.

He told himself it was not payment. Payment would have required a ledger. It was not charity. Charity announced itself. This was a practical measure against lateness, rain, clinics, taxis, torn coat cuffs, and the thousand small frictions that made useful people unreliable.

A buffer.

The word held.

The next morning, the money was gone.

Nothing else had moved.

The staples sat where they had been. The notebook lay squared to the keyboard. The fern had been turned a little towards the glass.

Olesya brought coffee at eight.

'Good morning,' she said.

'Morning.'

No change in her voice. No blush of gratitude. No offence. She placed the cup on the coaster and returned to her desk.

Thomas opened the shared drive and found the permit register waiting for review.

Two weeks later, she came in wearing a warmer coat.

It was second-hand, he could tell that much. Dark wool, carefully brushed, a little too large across the shoulders. The buttons were mismatched and sewn on firmly. She hung it over the back of her chair and smoothed the sleeves before sitting down.

Rauf noticed at once.

'Promotion?'

Olesya looked at him.

'The coat,' he said.

'It was cold.'

'Good reason.'

Rauf went into his office.

Thomas did not look at the drawer.

He did not have to.

The pattern became almost invisible after that. A small amount, never on the same day twice. Sometimes beneath the staples. Sometimes inside the back of the spare receipt book. Once under the fern pot, where the damp ring marked the top note and forced him not to do that again.

The money disappeared.

Coffee appeared.

Files arrived ordered and wrong in ways he could repair.

No one mentioned any of it.

When he passed Olesya's desk on his way out, the notebook was open beside her keyboard. The back cover lay flat. Empty.

The money had moved somewhere he could not see.

The next morning, she arrived at seven fifty-eight.

The coffee was waiting before he sat down.
