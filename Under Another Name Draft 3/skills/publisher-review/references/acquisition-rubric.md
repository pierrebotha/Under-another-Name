# Acquisition Rubric

Use this file when you need a more deliberate publisher-style framework instead of a quick read.

## Fast verdict ladder

- Pass: the current material does not make a persuasive acquisition case.
- Not ready yet: there is promise, but the draft or package is not currently submission-strength.
- Revise and resubmit: the project has a plausible acquisition path if a few high-leverage issues are solved.
- Request more: the excerpt or package is promising enough to justify reading further.
- Strong acquisition candidate: the concept, execution, and positioning look aligned enough to advocate for internally.

## Core acquisition questions

Ask only the questions that fit the material provided.

### 1. Concept and hook

- Can the project be pitched in one or two sharp sentences?
- Is the premise legible quickly?
- Does it contain a fresh angle, commercial tension, or emotional inevitability?
- Does it sound like a book people already know how to want?

### 2. Market position

- What shelf does it belong on?
- Is the age category and readership clear?
- Does the project match an existing buying audience?
- Is it chasing a fading trend, meeting a durable appetite, or creating a harder-to-sell hybrid?

### 3. Comparative titles

- Are the comps current enough to be useful?
- Do they describe readership and positioning rather than prestige only?
- Do they imply realistic scale?
- Do they clarify the blend of tone, hook, and audience?

### 4. Opening performance

- Do the opening pages cash the promise of the pitch?
- Is the reader oriented without flattening intrigue?
- Does narrative pressure arrive early enough?
- Is the prose publishable for the intended lane?

### 5. Story engine

- Is there enough conflict, escalation, mystery, desire, or emotional propulsion to sustain a full novel?
- Do the protagonist and premise generate future scenes naturally?
- Is there a visible engine for the middle, not only an opening setup?

### 6. Editorial burden

- Would revision be surgical, moderate, or foundational?
- Are the main problems positional, structural, tonal, or sentence-level?
- Would a publisher reasonably invest the editorial time required?

### 7. Internal advocacy

- What would an acquiring editor say in the room?
- What would sales or marketing worry about?
- What one sentence best explains the opportunity?
- What one sentence best explains the risk?

## Typical green flags

- Hook is easy to retell.
- Audience is visible.
- Opening pages deliver quickly.
- Voice and concept reinforce each other.
- Comps clarify the lane instead of muddying it.
- Revisions seem additive rather than reconstructive.

## Typical red flags

- Premise sounds competent but not urgent.
- The package signals one lane while the pages deliver another.
- The story engine looks thin after the setup.
- Comps are oversized, outdated, or purely aspirational.
- Editorial work appears foundational.
- The book is easier to describe by influences than by its own promise.

## Compact response template

Use or adapt this structure when the user wants a full publisher-style read:

1. Commercial read
2. Acquisition case
3. Main strengths
4. Main risks
5. Verdict plus confidence
6. Best next move

## Revision-path prompts

When the material is promising but not ready, point toward the shortest meaningful path:

- sharpen the one-line pitch before revising pages
- align the query promise with the actual reading experience
- strengthen the opening's immediate pressure
- clarify audience and shelf placement
- replace prestige comps with market-useful comps
- solve the structural issue that makes advocacy difficult
