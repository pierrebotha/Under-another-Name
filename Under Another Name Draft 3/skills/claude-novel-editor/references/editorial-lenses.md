# Editorial Lenses

Use only the sections that match the request.

## Developmental Lens

Check:

- Is the story making and keeping a clear promise?
- Does the protagonist want something specific, difficult, and escalating?
- Are obstacles forcing meaningful choices rather than delaying action?
- Do reveals arrive in the order that creates the most tension?
- Does each chapter change the situation, not just elaborate it?
- Are stakes visible at personal, relational, and systemic levels?
- Are character choices driving plot more than coincidence?
- Is the midpoint or major pivot genuinely changing the reader's understanding?
- Does the ending resolve the central dramatic question rather than only the logistics?

Common high-value notes:

- the first act explains too much before the reader is emotionally hooked
- scenes repeat the same pressure in different language
- key relationships are described more than dramatized
- the novel withholds answers but also withholds the question that would create suspense

## Scene Lens

Check:

- What changes from start to finish?
- What does the point-of-view character want in the scene?
- What resists them?
- Where does the scene turn?
- Is the emotional movement legible?
- Does exposition interrupt active pressure?
- Does the scene end on a shift, cost, decision, image, or question?

Useful diagnosis language:

- "The scene enters too early."
- "The conflict is present, but the objective is blurred."
- "The turn lands, but the exit explains it twice."
- "The emotional beat is strong; the setup needs compression."

## Line Lens

Check:

- over-explaining after the effect is already on the page
- repetitive sentence rhythms
- vague abstraction where a concrete image would work better
- filter words and unnecessary thought-tagging
- dialogue that sounds interchangeable between characters
- point-of-view slippage
- overwritten emphasis
- cliche or default phrasing

Useful diagnosis language:

- "The sentence tells the reader what the image already proved."
- "This paragraph carries the same meaning three times."
- "The prose wants one sharper noun here, not another modifier."

## Severity Guide

- High: structural or recurring problem that weakens the book's core engine
- Medium: meaningful weakness inside a chapter or scene
- Low: sentence-level drag, repetition, or polish issue

## Revision Roadmap Template

Use this order:

1. Protect what is already working
2. Fix structural sequencing and chapter purpose
3. Strengthen scene turns and emotional logic
4. Trim repetition and explanation
5. Polish sentences, dialogue, and transitions
