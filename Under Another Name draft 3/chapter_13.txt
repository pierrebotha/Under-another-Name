The containment strategy became Thomas’s primary secondary occupation. 

He synchronized his interactions with the Baku system to Sarah's schedule. When she was on conference calls, he processed the wire transfers. When she was at the gym, he read Olesya's messages.

One evening, a mortar shell landed outside the perimeter. The sirens wailed. Protocol required everyone to move to the bunkers. 

Thomas and Sarah sat in the dim bunker. Sarah leaned against him, gripping his knee. 

"I hate this," she whispered. 

"It hit outside the wire," Thomas said. 

His phone vibrated in his pocket. It was the secure app. He waited until Sarah engaged in conversation with the compound manager. He slid his hand into his pocket.

*Thomas. The landlord changed the locks. The bank rejected the transfer. We are outside.*

The banking error was a systemic failure. He could not fix it from the bunker. He was physically trapped with his wife under mortar fire, while thousands of miles away, the stability he had constructed was collapsing. 

He typed blindly. *Bank error. Go to a hotel. I will reimburse.*

*We do not have the cash,* the reply came. 

He closed his eyes. The mechanism was entirely broken. 

"Are you alright?" Sarah asked. 

"Fine," Thomas said. 

When the sirens ceased, Thomas did not go back to their house. He went to the operations center and bypassed the firewall to access his banking portal. A security protocol had flagged the transfer. 

He spent forty minutes clearing the block and initiated a new transfer. 

*The block is cleared. Go to the ATM. Find a hotel.*

*Got it. We are in a taxi. Thank you, Thomas.*

He logged out. He was trying to balance his belief in equal provision with the clumsy tools of remote finance. He was keeping Olesya safe from the cold, just as he kept Sarah safe in the bunker. He believed this parity was sustainable.
