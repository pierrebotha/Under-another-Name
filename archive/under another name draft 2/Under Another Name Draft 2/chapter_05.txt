In late November, Olesya did not arrive at work. 

At one o'clock, she called. "I cannot come. Anya is sick. The school sent her home."

Thomas needed the shipping manifests. He found the folder and generated them himself. He then left the office at five and took a taxi to the address listed on her employment forms.

It was a decaying Soviet-era block. He knocked on the door on the fourth floor. 

A thin girl with Olesya’s pale eyes answered. It was Anya. 

Olesya emerged from the kitchen, wiping her hands on a towel. "Thomas. What are you doing here?"

"I brought the manifests to confirm a signature," he lied.

She knew he was lying. Anya coughed, a deep, rattling sound. A small space heater hummed weakly. 

"Have you seen a doctor?" Thomas asked.

"We cannot afford the private clinic," Olesya said. 

Thomas pulled out a stack of manat notes, enough for a private doctor and groceries. He placed it on the table. 

"It is an advance," he said. "We need you back in the office by Thursday."

Olesya stared at the money. She accepted the system. "Thursday," she repeated softly.

He left the apartment. He felt a surge of satisfaction. He had secured her stability. 

Later that night, he spoke to Sarah on a video call. She was in a hotel room in Frankfurt, energized by her meetings. 

"The merger looks promising," Sarah said, pacing the room. "I might have to extend the trip by a week."

"Do it," Thomas said. "The opportunity is there."

He loved her ambition. He loved that she did not need him for her survival. 

Yet, an hour ago, he had felt profound love providing the means of survival for Olesya. He held both realities perfectly balanced in his mind. He was the infrastructure for Sarah's flight, and he was the foundation for Olesya's domestic survival. He saw no contradiction. The paradox was invisible to him.
